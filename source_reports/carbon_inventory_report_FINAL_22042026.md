## Executive Summary

This report presents the first comprehensive carbon inventory of Ghana's two principal mangrove-bearing Ramsar sites: the Keta Lagoon Complex (20,717.95 Ha) and the Songor Ramsar Site (465.76 Ha). Conducted under Phase 4 of the WACA ResIP 2 Blue Carbon Ecosystem Assessment, the inventory establishes baseline carbon stock estimates across above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, and soil organic carbon pools.

A total of 3,757 individual trees were measured across 11 sampling communities, representing the largest systematic vegetation structure assessment of Ghanaian mangroves to date. Nine tree species were recorded at Songor and ten at Keta, with *Avicennia germinans* dominating both sites and *Rhizophora racemosa* forming the structurally significant stands at Keta.

For Songor, total ecosystem carbon storage is estimated at 297,453 Mg (including grasses) or 288,892 Mg excluding grass contributions, of which soil organic carbon in the 0–30 cm horizon accounts for 93.6% (278,524 Mg). The mean carbon density at Songor is 159.66 ± 51.87 MgC/ha across all components. For Keta, the completed assessment establishes a total ecosystem carbon stock of 17,026,633 Mg (including grasses) or 16,850,323 Mg excluding grass contributions. Soil organic carbon at 0–30 cm depth alone accounts for 97.5% of the Keta total at 16,605,437 Mg, with a mean density of 801.50 ± 119.50 MgC/ha — one of the highest documented for mangrove soils in West Africa. Above-ground biomass carbon is 303,518 Mg (14.65 MgC/ha) including grasses, or 127,208 Mg (6.14 MgC/ha) excluding grasses. The mean carbon density for Keta across all pools is 205.46 ± 52.13 MgC/ha.

This inventory serves a dual purpose within the national blue carbon programme. First, it provides a baseline snapshot of carbon stocks across Ghana's most significant mangrove landscapes, establishing the evidence base for national climate finance positioning and Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) reporting. Second, it validates the field measurement workflow described in the companion National MRV Framework Report, demonstrating that the methodology can be executed at scale and replicated by project proponents operating within the national monitoring architecture.

Critically, this assessment employs Temporary Sample Plots appropriate to a national baseline survey. The companion MRV Framework specifies that any project proponent seeking to generate verified carbon credits must conduct a project-specific assessment using Permanent Sample Plots with full-depth SOC sampling (0–100 cm minimum), in accordance with IPCC Wetlands Supplement Tier 2 and Verra VM0033 requirements. The national baseline provides the proof of concept; the MRV Framework provides the rulebook for market-ready implementation.

## Chapter 1: Introduction and Mandate

### 1.1 Purpose of This Report

This report documents the carbon inventory and stock assessment component of the Ghana Blue Carbon Ecosystem Assessment, constituting the third of four integrated standalone technical reports within the assessment package. Its purpose is to establish first-order estimates of carbon stocks held in Ghana's principal mangrove ecosystems, validate the field measurement methodology at national scale, and provide the quantitative foundation upon which future carbon finance and conservation interventions will be designed.

The report is structured to serve three audiences simultaneously: government agencies (MEST, MLNR) responsible for NDC reporting and climate policy; development partners and carbon market actors requiring verifiable stock estimates; and community-level governance structures (CREMAs, Community Resource Management Committees) who will ultimately manage and monitor these carbon assets through the national MRV architecture.

### 1.2 Assignment Mandate

Phase 4 of the Blue Carbon Ecosystem Assessment, as specified in RFP No: GR-MESTI-441726 CS-CQS, requires that the boundaries of the assessment area be defined and documented, that carbon pools be explicitly selected and measured, and that the results serve as the baseline for all subsequent estimates of total carbon stock or carbon stock change. The Terms of Reference further require the consulting team to determine plot type, number, and location in agreement with MLNR and MEST through progress meetings.

This mandate is read together with the broader programme objective: to produce a national blue carbon evidence package that positions Ghana to access carbon finance mechanisms, fulfil NDC commitments, and establish a replicable monitoring framework for coastal wetland ecosystems.

### 1.3 Scope

This assessment covers two Ramsar sites:

- **Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site** (20,717.95 Ha classified mangrove extent): the largest continuous mangrove landscape in Ghana, comprising approximately 69% of the national coastal mangrove total. Sampling was conducted across five community areas: Tunu/Gblipe, Atiteti/Anyanui, Agbatsivi, Atorkor, and Galotse/Sota.

- **Songor Ramsar Site** (465.76 Ha classified mangrove extent): a severely degraded landscape representing approximately 2% of the national total, with mangrove-to-salt-pan conversion as the dominant trajectory. Sampling was conducted across six community areas: Wasa Kuse, Azizanya, Totope, Luhuese, Alavanyo, and Patukope.

Carbon pools assessed include above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (fine root carbon, coarse root carbon), and soil organic carbon (SOC) at 0–30 cm depth. Deadwood was assessed where relevant but does not constitute a primary reporting pool for these sites. The assessment year is 2026.

### 1.4 Relationship to the Four-Report Architecture

This report is one of four integrated standalone technical reports that together constitute the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment:

1. **National Blue Carbon MRV Framework Report** — specifies how carbon stocks are monitored, reported, and verified within a four-tier national institutional architecture. The MRV Framework is the downstream consumer of this inventory: the stock estimates reported here feed the national carbon accounting cycle.

2. **Drone Survey and EOSDA Continuous Monitoring Report** — provides centimetre-resolution spatial products (orthomosaics, topographic models) and a satellite-based continuous monitoring system for the four priority Areas of Interest within the Keta Lagoon. The spatial products from the drone survey support biomass stratification and canopy condition mapping that complement the plot-based measurements in this inventory.

3. **Wetland Ecosystem Health Assessment Report** — documents the physico-chemical condition of water and soil across 65 monitoring stations at Keta and Ada/Songor. Ecosystem health findings (particularly soil pH, dissolved oxygen, and oxidation-reduction potential) provide the environmental context for interpreting spatial variability in soil carbon stocks.

4. **Carbon Inventory and Stock Assessment Report** (this document) — establishes the quantitative carbon baseline.

These four reports are designed to be read together. Carbon stock estimates gain ecological meaning when read alongside ecosystem health findings; spatial precision when overlaid with drone-derived orthomosaics; and institutional durability when embedded within the MRV monitoring architecture.

### 1.5 Report Structure

This report is organised in eleven chapters. Chapter 2 establishes the strategic and ecological context. Chapter 3 describes the methodology, including the two-tier logic governing the relationship between this national baseline assessment and future project-specific inventories. Chapters 4 through 7 present results by carbon pool: above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, soil organic carbon, and total ecosystem carbon stock. Chapter 8 addresses deforestation baselines, degradation trajectories, and permanence. Chapter 9 treats uncertainty and quality assurance. Chapter 10 discusses carbon market readiness and the pathway from national baseline to project-level validation. Chapter 11 links the inventory to the MRV and continuous monitoring architecture.

## Chapter 2: Strategic and Ecological Context

### 2.1 Ghana's Blue Carbon Mandate

Ghana's engagement with blue carbon operates at the intersection of three international frameworks. Under the Paris Agreement, Ghana's updated Nationally Determined Contributions identify coastal wetland conservation and restoration as a priority mitigation pathway within the land use, land-use change, and forestry (LULUCF) sector. The WACA ResIP 2 programme, funded by the World Bank, provides the institutional and financial vehicle for translating this commitment into a field-verified evidence base. The IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement provides the methodological architecture for reporting coastal wetland carbon within national greenhouse gas inventories.

The convergence of these frameworks creates a specific requirement: Ghana must establish a credible, verifiable baseline of its coastal wetland carbon stocks before it can access carbon finance mechanisms, report meaningfully within the Enhanced Transparency Framework, or demonstrate the additionality required for carbon credit generation.

### 2.2 National Mangrove Distribution

The Geospatial Mangrove Extent Baseline Report, produced by the project's GIS team using 2024 Sentinel-2 imagery and Random Forest classification, established that Ghana's total coastal mangrove extent is approximately 30,025 hectares. This figure is distributed across three Ramsar sites with sharply unequal concentrations:

- **Keta Lagoon Complex:** 20,717.95 Ha (69% of national total). The dominant species are *Rhizophora racemosa* (forming the tall, structurally complex stands along creek systems and tidal channels) and *Avicennia germinans* (colonising the landward fringes and coppicing zones). This is the landscape where carbon stocks are concentrated and where blue carbon project potential is highest.

- **Songor Ramsar Site:** 465.76 Ha (2% of national total). The landscape is characterised by severe degradation, with historical and ongoing conversion of mangrove to salt pan driven by artisanal and industrial salt extraction. *Avicennia germinans* is the dominant surviving species, with *Avicennia marina* and *Rhizophora mangle* present in relict stands.

- **Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site:** Mangrove cover is ecologically insignificant in its current state, representing a restoration priority rather than a conservation or carbon stock landscape.

### 2.3 Why Carbon Stocks Matter

Mangrove ecosystems are among the most carbon-dense ecosystems on Earth. Global literature consistently reports mangrove soil carbon stocks in the range of 200–900 MgC/ha in the upper metre, with some sites exceeding 1,000 MgC/ha. The combination of high carbon density, rapid loss rates, and the permanence of soil carbon pools under intact canopy makes mangrove conservation one of the highest-impact climate mitigation strategies per unit area.

For Ghana specifically, the concentration of 69% of national mangrove area in a single landscape (Keta) creates both an opportunity and a vulnerability. The opportunity is that a single, well-designed carbon project at Keta could represent a nationally significant mitigation contribution. The vulnerability is that continued deforestation at the documented 2.15% annual rate translates directly into a measurable national carbon emission that Ghana would need to account for under its NDC commitments.

### 2.4 Deforestation Baseline and Additionality Context

The carbon inventory operates within a documented deforestation context. The Keta Lagoon basin experiences sustained mangrove loss driven primarily by the harvesting of *Rhizophora* wood for commercial fish smoking. The Socio-Economic Baseline Report and the Improved Cookstove Interventions Report together document the economic dynamics: mangrove fuelwood is the preferred fuel in the coastal fish-processing economy because the smoke from *Rhizophora* wood imparts a desirable colour and flavour to smoked fish, commanding a price premium in local and regional markets.

This deforestation rate of 2.15% per year provides the business-as-usual baseline against which additionality is assessed in carbon market methodologies. Under Verra VM0033 (Tidal Wetland and Seagrass Restoration), a project proponent must demonstrate that carbon stock preservation or restoration would not have occurred without the carbon finance intervention. The documented deforestation rate, together with the identified economic driver, provides the factual foundation for that demonstration.

At Songor, the degradation trajectory is different in character: mangrove loss is driven by salt pan expansion rather than fuelwood extraction, and the remaining 465.76 Ha represents a near-terminal state that is more appropriately framed as a restoration candidate than a conservation baseline.

## Chapter 3: Methodology

### 3.1 Conceptual Framework

The carbon inventory follows an IPCC Wetlands Supplement Tier 2 approach, which requires country-specific or site-specific data for at least the principal carbon pools. Tier 2 is distinguished from Tier 1 (which relies on IPCC default values) by the use of field measurements, species-specific allometric equations, and site-level soil sampling. This assessment meets the Tier 2 threshold through direct tree measurement (N = 3,757), species identification and wood density assignment, and site-level soil core extraction.

The assessment is designed as a national baseline snapshot: a first-order estimate of carbon stocks across Ghana's principal mangrove landscapes, executed at a scale and methodological rigour sufficient to validate the field workflow and produce defensible stock estimates. It is not designed as a project-specific carbon audit for credit generation; that role is addressed by the companion MRV Framework, which specifies the additional requirements (Permanent Sample Plots, full-depth SOC, tagged individual trees) that project proponents must meet.

### 3.2 Landscape Stratification

Sampling locations were stratified by community and land cover type, reflecting the ecological heterogeneity within each Ramsar site. The stratification captures the principal vegetation conditions encountered in the field:

**Keta Lagoon Complex:**
- Mangrove swamp with grassland (dominant land cover across most communities)
- Coppicing mangrove swamp with grassland (reflecting active or recent harvesting pressure)
- Partially harvested *Rhizophora* mangrove swamp with *Avicennia* and grass cover
- Sparsely distributed mangrove with *Sesuvium* and grass
- Mixed *Rhizophora* and *Avicennia* mangrove with grass understorey

**Songor Ramsar Site:**
- Mangrove swamp with grassland (dominant)
- Mangrove swamp (without grassland component)

The land cover descriptions were recorded at each sampling site and reflect the field team's direct observation of canopy structure, species composition, and understorey condition. These descriptions serve as the stratification basis for extrapolating plot-level measurements to landscape-level stock estimates.

### 3.3 Carbon Pools Assessed

Four carbon pools were assessed in accordance with IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance:

| Pool | Component | Method | IPCC Alignment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Above-ground biomass (AGB) | Trees, shrubs, palms, grasses | Allometric equations applied to DBH, height, and wood density measurements | Tier 2 |
| Below-ground biomass — fine roots | Roots < 2 mm diameter (CFR) | Root biomass sampling | Tier 2 |
| Below-ground biomass — coarse roots | Roots ≥ 2 mm diameter (CCR) | Root biomass sampling | Tier 2 |
| Soil organic carbon (SOC) | Organic carbon in soil matrix, 0–30 cm depth | Soil core extraction and laboratory analysis | Tier 2 (conservative depth; see Section 3.7) |

Deadwood was assessed visually where encountered but does not constitute a principal reporting pool for these sites, where the primary carbon reservoirs are in living biomass and soil.

### 3.4 Plot Design: Temporary Sample Plots and the National Baseline Logic

This assessment employs Temporary Sample Plots (TSPs) as appropriate for a national baseline inventory. The rationale is as follows.

The purpose of this assessment is to establish a first-order estimate of Ghana's coastal blue carbon stocks and to validate the field measurement workflow at a national scale. TSPs are the standard instrument for baseline snapshot surveys where the objective is to characterise current stocks rather than to track change over time through repeated measurement of the same individuals.

The companion National MRV Framework Report specifies that any project proponent, whether a CREMA, a conservation organisation, or a private carbon developer, seeking to generate verified carbon credits from a defined project area must conduct a project-specific carbon assessment using Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs). PSPs require:

- Permanently marked plot corners with durable markers
- Individually tagged and numbered trees within each plot
- Documented subplot corner coordinates (not site-level GPS only)
- Designed for re-occupation at 3–5 year measurement intervals
- Community ownership: the PSPs become the community's long-term monitoring asset

The re-measurement cycle (3–5 years) is a project-level obligation, not a national government commitment. It is the contribution of the communities and project proponents operating within the MRV Framework. The government's role is to provide the framework, the validated methodology, and the baseline against which project-specific assessments are benchmarked.

This two-tier architecture, national baseline via TSPs plus project-specific assessment via PSPs, is consistent with the programme's design intent: to produce both a national snapshot and a proof of concept, while establishing the framework within which any future carbon trading project must operate to meet international verification standards.

**Plot configuration:**
Plots were established at each sampling site with multiple subplots (designated a through e) arranged within the site area. At Keta, 85 subplot records were generated across 16 site clusters in 5 communities. At Songor, 69 subplot records were generated across 12 site clusters in 6 communities. GPS coordinates were recorded at the site level; subplot positions were established relative to the site anchor point.

### 3.5 Field Measurement Protocols

**Tree measurements:**
All woody plants with diameter ≥ 2 cm were measured within each subplot. Measurements included:

- **Diameter at breast height (DBH):** measured at 1.3 m above ground level, or above the highest prop root for *Rhizophora* species. For multi-stemmed individuals, each stem ≥ 2 cm was measured separately.
- **Total height:** measured from ground level (or substrate surface) to the highest live crown point.
- **Species identification:** each individual was identified to species level in the field, with voucher specimens collected where identification was uncertain.
- **Wood density:** species-specific wood density values were assigned from published reference databases (global wood density database).

**Soil sampling:**
Soil cores were extracted at representative locations within each site to a depth of 30 cm. Core samples were sealed, labelled with site and depth identifiers, and transported to the laboratory for analysis. The 30 cm depth protocol and its implications relative to the IPCC 100 cm recommendation are discussed in Section 3.7.

**Root biomass:**
Fine root (< 2 mm diameter) and coarse root (≥ 2 mm diameter) biomass was assessed through root sampling at representative plots within each site.

### 3.7 SOC Sampling Depth: 0–30 cm Protocol and IPCC Considerations

The soil organic carbon assessment for this baseline inventory was conducted to a depth of 30 cm. This depth is appropriate for the objectives of this national baseline for the following reasons, while acknowledging the implications for total stock estimation.

**Why 0–30 cm is defensible for this assessment:**
The primary purpose of this inventory is to establish a national snapshot and validate the methodology. SOC sampling to 30 cm is widely reported in peer-reviewed mangrove carbon literature, provides a reliable indication of relative stock differences across sites and strata, and is operationally achievable within the logistical constraints of a multi-site national survey.

**What the IPCC recommends:**
The IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement recommends SOC sampling to a minimum depth of 100 cm for mangrove soils, recognising that a substantial proportion of mangrove soil carbon is stored below the 30 cm horizon. Global meta-analyses indicate that mangrove SOC at 0–100 cm depth is typically 1.5 to 3 times higher than the 0–30 cm fraction alone, with the ratio varying by soil type, hydroperiod, and species composition.

**Implication for reported values:**
The SOC stock estimates presented in this report should be understood as conservative minimum estimates representing the 0–30 cm fraction only. Total soil carbon stocks to 100 cm depth are likely to be substantially higher than the values reported here. This is particularly significant for Songor, where SOC at 0–30 cm already accounts for 93.6% of the measured total ecosystem carbon.

**Requirement for project-specific assessments:**
The National MRV Framework specifies that any project proponent conducting a carbon assessment for the purpose of credit generation must sample SOC to a minimum depth of 100 cm, with depth intervals at 0–15, 15–30, 30–50, and 50–100 cm, bulk density determination at each interval, and laboratory TOC analysis. This full-depth protocol is a non-negotiable requirement for Verra VM0033 compliance and IPCC Tier 2 reporting within a verified carbon project. The 0–30 cm baseline reported here provides the starting reference; project-level assessments must extend to the full depth profile.

### 3.8 QA/QC Procedures

Quality assurance and quality control procedures were applied throughout the field campaign:

- **Field data verification:** measurements were recorded on standardised data sheets with species, DBH, height, and plot identifiers. Data entry was cross-checked by a second field team member.
- **Species identification:** uncertain identifications were resolved through voucher specimen comparison and expert consultation.
- **Instrument calibration:** diameter tapes and height measurement tools were checked at the start of each field day.
- **Soil sample chain of custody:** all soil cores were sealed, labelled, and transported under controlled conditions to the analytical laboratory.
- **Outlier review:** extreme DBH or height values were flagged and field-verified before inclusion in calculations.

### 3.9 Uncertainty Treatment

Uncertainty in carbon stock estimates arises from multiple sources: measurement error in DBH and height; allometric equation prediction error; spatial variability within strata; wood density database uncertainty; and laboratory analytical precision for SOC.

For this baseline assessment, uncertainty is reported as the standard error of the mean and the 95% confidence interval for each carbon pool at the site level. The coefficient of variation (CV) is reported as an indicator of within-site spatial heterogeneity.

Keta's AGB carbon density (14.65 MgC/ha including grass, 6.14 MgC/ha excluding grass) reflects the younger, shorter, and more disturbed canopy structure at this site relative to the larger-stemmed trees recorded at Songor. When using species-specific allometric equations instead of the generalized Kauffman and Donato (2012) model, Keta AGB increases to 13.4 ± 5.04 MgC/ha (95%CI: -0.87 to 27.69), reflecting the stronger biomass representation of *Rhizophora racemosa* under species-calibrated parameters.

## Chapter 6: Results — Soil Organic Carbon

### 6.1 Songor SOC (0–30 cm)

**Table 7: Soil organic carbon — Songor Lagoon**

| Component | Mean ± S.E. (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SOC (0–30 cm) | 598.00 ± 49.33 | 471.20 to 724.80 | 30.62 | 278,524 |

Soil organic carbon at 0–30 cm depth is by far the dominant carbon pool at Songor, accounting for 93.6% of the total measured ecosystem carbon. This finding is consistent with the global mangrove carbon literature, which consistently reports SOC as the primary carbon reservoir in mangrove ecosystems, typically comprising 50–90% of total ecosystem carbon depending on forest age, species, and soil depth.

The relatively lower coefficient of variation for SOC (30.6%) compared to AGB (129.3%) and root carbon (119–137%) indicates that soil carbon is more spatially uniform than biomass carbon across the Songor landscape. This spatial regularity is characteristic of mangrove SOC globally: while tree biomass varies sharply with disturbance history and species composition, the organic-rich soil matrix accumulates over centuries and varies more gradually across the landscape.

The Songor SOC density of 598 MgC/ha at 0–30 cm places this site within the upper range of global mangrove SOC values for this depth interval, comparable to values reported from Southeast Asian and Caribbean mangrove systems. If the standard 0–100 cm global ratio of approximately 2 to 2.5 times the 0–30 cm value applies at Songor, total SOC to 1 metre depth could be in the range of 1,200 to 1,500 MgC/ha, a figure that would rank among the highest mangrove soil carbon densities recorded globally.

### 6.2 Keta SOC (0–30 cm)

**Table 7b: Soil organic carbon — Keta Lagoon Complex**

| Component | Mean ± S.E. (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SOC (0–30 cm) | 801.50 ± 119.50 | 519.00 to 1,084.00 | 42.16 | 16,605,437 |

Soil organic carbon at 0–30 cm depth at the Keta Lagoon Complex totals 16,605,437 Mg — the single most consequential number in this inventory. SOC accounts for 97.5% of all carbon stored in the Keta system, confirming that the conservation of Keta's mangrove soils is the primary intervention target for Ghana's blue carbon programme. The mean SOC density of 801.50 MgC/ha exceeds Songor's 598 MgC/ha and is consistent with the high clay content of the Keta Lagoon substrate documented by Finlayson et al. (2000), which supports strong positive clay-carbon associations known from mangrove literature (Li et al. 2024). The SOC estimate for Keta (16,605,437 Mg at 0–30 cm) is strikingly similar to a previous independent estimate (16,833,091 Mg at 0–30 cm) from the South Tongu District mangrove assessment (Addo-Danso and Amissah 2024), providing external validation of the current results.

A total of 110 soil samples were collected from Keta plots. Laboratory analysis was conducted using a LECO CN628 Elemental Analyzer (LECO Corporation, Minnesota, USA) for total carbon concentration. Bulk density was determined from oven-dried subsamples at 105°C for 4 hours, with stone-free corrections applied following the protocol of Kauffman and Donato (2012). SOC was calculated as: SOC (MgC/ha) = soil depth (cm) × bulk density (g/cm³) × carbon concentration (%).

## Chapter 8: Deforestation, Degradation, and Permanence

### 8.1 Keta Deforestation Rate and Drivers

The Keta Lagoon Complex experiences an annual deforestation rate of 2.15%, driven primarily by the harvesting of *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Rhizophora mangle* for commercial fish smoking. This rate is derived from multi-temporal analysis and corroborated by the Socio-Economic Baseline Report, which documents the economic dependence of coastal communities on mangrove fuelwood.

At this deforestation rate, the Keta mangrove system loses approximately 445 hectares of canopy cover per year. Using the completed AGB carbon density of 14.65 MgC/ha (including grasses, generalized equation), this translates to an annual above-ground carbon emission of approximately 6,500 Mg of carbon from biomass loss alone. However, the more consequential emission pathway is through SOC disturbance: at the measured SOC density of 801.50 MgC/ha and the documented deforestation rate, each year of unmitigated deforestation threatens to release on the order of 356,500 Mg of soil carbon — an annual carbon emission risk roughly 55 times larger than the above-ground loss alone. This asymmetry is the primary case for including SOC emissions in any Keta carbon finance project baseline, as required under Verra VM0033 and IPCC Wetlands Supplement Tier 2.

### 8.2 Songor Degradation Profile

Songor's degradation trajectory differs fundamentally from Keta's. The driver is salt pan expansion rather than fuelwood extraction, and the remaining 465.76 Ha of mangrove represents a near-terminal landscape. The community-level vegetation data (Section 4.3) shows that only Luhuese and Azizanya retain tree stands with diameter and height profiles suggestive of mature or recovering mangrove; the remaining four communities show vegetation structure consistent with young, disturbed, or recently colonising stands.

### 8.3 Permanence and Risk

Carbon permanence, the assurance that sequestered carbon will remain stored for the crediting period (typically 20–100 years for blue carbon projects), faces two categories of risk in these landscapes:

**Project-addressable risks:** fuelwood harvesting (addressable through improved cookstove interventions and alternative livelihood programmes); salt pan expansion (addressable through land-use planning and CREMA governance); unsustainable aquaculture development.

**Non-project structural risks:** the Akosombo Dam sediment deficit, which has fundamentally altered the sediment dynamics of the Volta estuary system, reducing the sediment supply that sustains mangrove soil accretion; coastal erosion driven by sea-level rise and reduced sediment input; and extreme weather events.

Any carbon project at Keta or Songor must address both categories in its project design document and may need to apply a permanence buffer (typically 10–20% of credits withheld in a buffer pool) to account for non-addressable reversal risk.

## Chapter 10: Carbon Market Readiness and Project Pathway

### 10.1 What This Baseline Establishes

This national baseline inventory establishes four elements required for carbon market development:

1. **Quantified carbon stocks** at two Ramsar sites, providing the evidence base for project-level feasibility assessment
2. **A documented deforestation baseline** (2.15% per year at Keta), providing the business-as-usual scenario for additionality determination
3. **A validated field methodology** demonstrating that the measurement workflow can be executed at national scale across multiple community areas
4. **Species and structural data** sufficient to inform allometric equation selection and stratification design for project-specific assessments

### 10.2 What Remains for Project-Level Validation

A project proponent (CREMA, conservation organisation, or private developer) seeking Verra VM0033 or Gold Standard certification within the Keta or Songor landscape must go beyond this baseline by:

1. **Establishing Permanent Sample Plots** within the project boundary, with permanently marked corners, individually tagged trees, and documented subplot coordinates
2. **Sampling SOC to 100 cm minimum**, with depth intervals at 0–15, 15–30, 30–50, and 50–100 cm, including bulk density determination at each interval
3. **Applying and citing specific allometric equations** appropriate to the species and conditions of the project area
4. **Conducting a formal uncertainty analysis** meeting the requirements of the applicable carbon standard
5. **Establishing the re-measurement protocol** (3–5 year cycle) as a community-owned monitoring obligation within the MRV Framework

This two-tier architecture ensures that the national baseline serves as a proof of concept and reference benchmark, while the project-specific assessment meets the full verification requirements of international carbon markets.

## Chapter 11: Linkage to MRV and Continuous Monitoring

### 11.1 How This Inventory Feeds the National MRV

The National MRV Framework Report establishes a four-tier institutional architecture for monitoring, reporting, and verifying blue carbon across Ghana's coastal wetlands. This carbon inventory occupies a specific position within that architecture: it is the T0 (time-zero) stock estimate against which all future measurements will be compared.

The MRV Framework specifies that:
- Carbon stock data from this inventory will be registered in the national MRV platform database
- Re-measurement data from project-level PSPs will be submitted through the same platform
- Discrepancies between baseline and re-measurement values will trigger the carbon accounting protocol described in the MRV Report (Section 9)

### 11.2 Integration with Drone and EOSDA Monitoring

The Drone Survey and EOSDA Continuous Monitoring Report establishes centimetre-resolution spatial products and a satellite-based alert system for the four priority Areas of Interest within the Keta Lagoon. These spatial products complement the plot-based carbon inventory in several ways:

- **Canopy condition mapping** from orthomosaics supports interpretation of AGB variability across the landscape
- **NDVI and NDMI time series** from EOSDA provide early warning of canopy disturbance between re-measurement cycles
- **Topographic models** from drone surveys support hydrological interpretation of spatial patterns in SOC

The TSP locations documented in this report (Annex A) provide a spatial reference that can be overlaid with the drone-derived products to assess whether plot-level findings are representative of the broader landscape condition.

### 11.3 Integration with Ecosystem Health Monitoring

The Wetland Ecosystem Health Assessment Report documents physico-chemical water and soil conditions across 65 monitoring stations at Keta and Ada/Songor. Several ecosystem health parameters directly influence carbon dynamics:

- **Soil pH and ORP** affect rates of organic matter decomposition and SOC preservation
- **Dissolved oxygen levels** indicate aerobic vs anaerobic soil conditions, with anaerobic conditions favouring long-term carbon storage
- **The pH 2.7 anomaly at Anyanui** (documented in the Ecosystem Health Report) has direct implications for SOC stability in that zone

The quarterly ecosystem health monitoring schedule (57 stations at Keta, 23 at Ada/Songor) provides the environmental context needed to interpret future changes in carbon stocks.

### Annex C: Laboratory Methods

**SOC Analysis:** Soil samples were oven-dried at 105°C for 4 hours to determine bulk density. Dried samples were crushed, sieved, and ground. Total carbon concentration was determined by elemental analysis using a LECO CN628 Elemental Analyzer (LECO Corporation, Minnesota, USA). SOC was calculated as: SOC (MgC/ha) = soil depth (cm) × bulk density (g/cm³) × carbon concentration (%). Stone and root volumes were corrected using water displacement. A total of 110 soil samples were processed from Keta plots and 90 from Songor plots.

**Fine root analysis:** Soil cores extracted to 30 cm depth along an X-shape in each subplot. Samples soaked, washed on 0.25 mm sieve, roots collected, oven-dried at 70°C. Fine root biomass expressed per unit ground area and scaled to per-hectare basis. Converted to carbon using a factor of 0.39.
