DRAFT DESIGN OF THE BLUE CARBON MRV SYSTEM

1. STRATEGIC CONTEXT AND PURPOSE

Ghana’s coastal wetlands and mangrove ecosystems represent critical national assets that provide climate regulation, biodiversity protection, shoreline stabilization, fisheries support, and livelihood security for coastal communities. Recognizing the strategic importance of these ecosystems within Ghana’s climate commitments and development agenda, the Government has initiated the development of a National Blue Carbon Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) Framework as part of the Basline Assessment Assignment.

This Framework establishes the institutional, technical, and governance architecture required to:

Monitor and safeguard mangrove and coastal wetland ecosystems;

Quantify and track carbon stocks and emission reductions in line with international good practice;

Detect and respond to environmental pressures that threaten ecosystem integrity;

Ensure inclusive and accountable governance structures;

Provide confidence to public, donor, and private sector investments in the blue carbon space;

Support Ghana’s Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) and broader climate reporting obligations.

The MRV Framework is informed by comprehensive national baseline studies, including mangrove extent mapping, carbon inventory assessments, ecosystem health analyses, environmental pressure assessments, governance readiness evaluations, and socio-economic profiling of coastal communities. This document serves as the national reference framework for monitoring blue carbon landscapes and is designed to guide future investments, policy decisions, and carbon market participation.

2. NATIONAL BLUE CARBON MRV ARCHITECTURE

The National Blue Carbon MRV Framework is structured as an integrated, multi-layered system operating across institutional levels.

2.1 Institutional Tiers

The system operates across four coordinated tiers:

Community Level

Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs) and Community Change Agents (CCAs) undertake field-based monitoring of restoration activities, environmental pressure signals, and governance indicators.

District Level

The Metropolitant, Municipaland District Assemblies (MMDAs) including the Wildlife Division (WD)consolidate and validate field data, facilitate conflict mediation, and support reporting processes.

National Technical Level

The Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR), Ministry of Environment, Science, and Technology (MEST), and Forestry Commission (FC) provide:

GIS and satellite analysis;

Carbon stock calculations and re-measurement oversight;

Quality assurance and quality control (QA/QC);

National-level data archiving and reporting.

Independent Review (When Applicable)

Independent verification may be undertaken where required for carbon market participation or external assurance.

2.2 Functional Layers

The MRV system operates through three functional layers:

Layer I – Ecological and Carbon Integrity

Ensures that mangrove extent, restoration performance, and carbon stocks are monitored and maintained in accordance with international best practice.

Layer II – Environmental Pressure Surveillance

Monitors drivers of degradation through structured field indicators and spatial analysis to provide early warning signals and trigger corrective action. This also looks at leakage management as part of the broader environmental pressure manageement protocols.

Layer III – Governance and Safeguard Assurance

Ensures inclusive participation, benefit transparency, conflict monitoring, and grievance redress mechanisms are functioning effectively.

These layers operate through an integrated digital platform enabling continuous monitoring and data transparency.

3. GUIDING PRINCIPLES AND DESIGN PHILOSOPHY

The National Blue Carbon MRV Framework is built on the following principles:

3.1 Institutional Permanence

The MRV system is designed as a long-term national framework, not a project-bound mechanism. The projects will use the framework together with site specific baselines if baselines are not availiable already to prepare an MRV system.

3.2 Technical Credibility

Carbon measurement and ecological monitoring are aligned with internationally recognized methodologies and IPCC good practice. Minimum Protocols and Manual of Procedures are provided to guide replication, validation and verification.

3.3 Digital Integration

A web-based spatial monitoring platform hosts:

Restoration potential areas;

Ecologically sensitive zones;

Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) locations;

Weekly satellite imagery feeds;

Spatial change detection analytics.

Access is managed at national level while allowing authorized MMDAs, WD and community-level access through smartphone-compatible interfaces.

3.4 Early-Warning Orientation

Environmental pressure monitoring is designed as a surveillance system to detect risk signals before irreversible degradation occurs. This systems extends beyound the project specific areas to ensure leakages are also detected and managed.

3.5 Transparency and Accountability

Monitoring outputs inform annual national reporting and investment-grade disclosure.

3.6 Progressive Enhancement

The MRV system is fully operational upon adoption and incorporates mechanisms for continuous improvement as investments and capacities expand.

4. ECOLOGICAL AND CARBON INTEGRITY FRAMEWORK

4.1 Objective

The Ecological and Carbon Integrity Framework establishes the technical architecture through which Ghana will:

Monitor the spatial extent and condition of mangrove and coastal wetland ecosystems;

Quantify and periodically re-measure carbon stocks;

Track restoration performance and ecological recovery;

Maintain long-term integrity of blue carbon assets;

Ensure readiness for voluntary and compliance carbon market participation.

This framework ensures that ecological monitoring and carbon accounting are conducted in accordance with international good practice while remaining institutionally anchored within national systems.

4.2 National Mangrove and Coastal Wetland Extent Monitoring

4.2.1 Baseline Reference

The 2025 Geo-Spatial Mangrove Extent Baseline, Ecological Health Report and Carbon Inventory serve as the national Year 0 reference for:

Mangrove spatial coverage;

Ecosystem classification;

Stratification for carbon measurement;

Fragmentation mapping;

Restoration potential zones.

All future measurements will be benchmarked against this baseline.

4.2.2 Satellite-Based Monitoring

Mangrove and coastal wetland extent shall be monitored annually using:

Sentinel-2 or equivalent satellite imagery;

Standardized classification algorithms consistent with baseline methodology;

Supervised land cover classification and change detection;

Ground-truth validation through CREMA and Wildlife Division field teams.

The web-based spatial monitoring platform shall host:

Baseline and annual extent layers;

Restoration zones;

Ecologically sensitive areas;

Detected land-use change polygons.

Outputs shall include:

Annual net mangrove area change (hectares);

Fragmentation trends;

Encroachment hotspots;

Restoration expansion verification.

This ensures spatial transparency and early detection of land conversion pressures.

4.3 Restoration Monitoring and Ecological Recovery

Restoration monitoring is essential to demonstrate ecological recovery and carbon enhancement.

4.3.1 Restoration Site Registration

All restoration sites shall be:

Georeferenced and mapped within the national web platform;

Assigned a unique site identifier;

Linked to responsible CREMA and district authority.

4.3.2 Survival and Establishment Monitoring

For each restoration site, monitoring shall include:

Seedling survival rate (%);

Canopy development progression;

Evidence of disturbance (grazing, clearing, re-cutting);

Hydrological suitability confirmation.

Monitoring frequency:

3 months post-planting;

6 months;

12 months;

Annually thereafter until establishment.

Minimum performance threshold:

≥ 70% survival after Year 1.

Sites failing to meet threshold shall trigger corrective restoration action.

4.3.3 Ecologically Sensitive Area Surveillance

Permanent ecologically sensitive areas shall be mapped and monitored for:

Disturbance signals;

Hydrological alteration;

Invasive species presence (where applicable);

Degradation trends.

These areas shall be integrated into the web platform for continuous observation.

4.4 Carbon Stock Monitoring and Accounting

4.4.1 Carbon Pools Covered

The national carbon monitoring system shall include:

Above-ground biomass (AGB);

Below-ground biomass (BGB);

Deadwood (where relevant);

Soil organic carbon (0–100 cm depth).

These pools are aligned with IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance and internationally recognized carbon accounting practices.

4.4.2 Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) System

Permanent Sample Plots established during the Carbon Inventory Assessment shall serve as the long-term monitoring backbone.

Each PSP shall:

Be georeferenced and digitally archived;

Be assigned a unique identification code;

Include plot stratification category;

Be hosted on the national web platform.

Community Change Agents shall support:

Plot access maintenance;

DBH measurement recording;

Visual disturbance documentation.

National technical agencies shall conduct:

Carbon stock recalculation;

Soil re-measurement;

Data validation and QA/QC;

Archiving and version control.

4.4.3 Re-Measurement Cycle

Carbon re-measurement shall occur in accordance with standard practice intervals:

Biomass re-measurement: every 3–5 years;

Soil carbon reassessment: consistent with methodological guidance and risk exposure.

Re-measurement frequency may be accelerated where:

Significant disturbance is detected;

Restoration scale increases substantially;

Market participation requires additional verification.

4.4.4 Quality Assurance and Quality Control (QA/QC)

Carbon accounting shall incorporate:

Field data verification;

Double-check measurement protocols;

Outlier detection procedures;

Methodological consistency with baseline inventory;

Secure data storage and audit trail documentation.

All carbon data shall be archived within a nationally managed database with controlled access rights.

4.5 Avoided Deforestation and Carbon Risk Detection

The system shall integrate spatial extent monitoring with carbon risk assessment.

Where annual satellite analysis detects:

Net mangrove loss;

Significant fragmentation;

Conversion hotspots;

These spatial signals shall be cross-referenced with:

Permanent Sample Plot data;

Environmental pressure indicators;

Conflict or land tenure records.

This ensures that carbon risk is detected early and that mitigation actions are initiated promptly.

4.6 Digital Integration and Real-Time Oversight

The Ecological and Carbon Integrity Framework is strengthened by the national web-based monitoring platform, which provides:

Weekly satellite imagery updates;

Automated change detection alerts;

PSP visualization;

Restoration tracking overlays;

Risk hotspot mapping.

Role-based access allows:

National agencies to manage data integrity;

District authorities to review area-specific trends;

CREMA members to access mapped zones via smartphone-compatible interfaces.

This digital backbone ensures continuous monitoring rather than periodic inspection alone.

4.7 National Integrity Statement

Through this Ecological and Carbon Integrity Framework, Ghana establishes:

A nationally anchored carbon accounting architecture;

Spatially transparent ecosystem monitoring;

Community-supported but technically verified field systems;

Re-measurement cycles aligned with international standards;

Continuous surveillance of restoration and degradation risks.

5. ENVIRONMENTAL PRESSURE SURVEILLANCE FRAMEWORK

5.1 Objective

The Environmental Pressure Surveillance Framework establishes Ghana’s national system for detecting, monitoring, and responding to drivers of degradation within blue carbon landscapes.

While ecological and carbon monitoring measure ecosystem condition and stock changes, environmental pressure surveillance functions as an early-warning system. It identifies risk signals before irreversible carbon loss or ecosystem degradation occurs.

The framework is designed to:

Detect emerging degradation pressures;

Monitor trends in human-induced ecosystem stress;

Link pressure signals to carbon risk;

Trigger timely institutional response;

Support leakage monitoring;

Provide investment-grade risk transparency.

The system prioritizes signal detection over complex quantification, ensuring long-term institutional sustainability.

5.2 Pressure Classification

Environmental pressures within Ghana’s blue carbon landscapes are classified into four categories:

Land Conversion Pressures

Biomass Extraction Pressures

Pollution and Physical Disturbance

Structural and Access-Related Drivers

Each category is monitored using standardized national indicators.

5.3 Core Environmental Pressure Indicators

To ensure operational feasibility and long-term continuity, the national system adopts five primary indicators.

5.3.1 Indicator 1: Land Conversion Monitoring (GIS-Based)

Purpose: Detect spatial conversion of mangrove and wetland areas.

Monitored variables:

Salt pan expansion (hectares/year);

Urban encroachment into wetland zones (hectares/year);

Wetland filling or drainage.

Methodology:

Annual satellite imagery analysis;

Change detection algorithms consistent with baseline methodology;

Web-based overlay comparison with restoration and protected zones.

Responsible entity:

MLNR / Wildlife Division.

Trigger threshold:

Any detected net loss within mapped sensitive or restoration areas;

Significant expansion of industrial salt pans into previously mapped mangrove zones.

Response protocol:

Immediate review by District and National authorities;

Integration with governance and land access review processes.

5.3.2 Indicator 2: Mangrove Cutting Signal (Field-Based)

Purpose: Detect active biomass extraction pressure.

Measured through quarterly CREMA transect walks within designated monitoring zones.

Field observations record:

Presence of fresh stumps;

Evidence of active cutting;

Presence of charcoal kilns.

Detection system:

Yes/No recording per transect;

Pressure Alert triggered if two or more signals are present in the same monitoring period.

This indicator prioritizes incidence detection rather than volume estimation.

Responsible entity:

CREMA Monitoring Teams;

Validation by MMDAs and WD.

Response protocol:

Community review meeting;

Engagement with responsible actors;

Escalation to enforcement authorities if persistent.

5.3.3 Indicator 3: Fuel Source Shift Monitoring

Purpose: Track behavioral shifts in fuel dependence.

Annual sample surveys (20–30 households per CREMA) record primary cooking fuel source:

Mangrove wood;

Woodlot wood;

LPG;

Improved cookstove usage;

Alternative fuels.

Trend interpretation:

Declining mangrove dependence = reduced extraction pressure;

Persistent high dependence = elevated carbon risk.

Responsible entity:

CREMA with District oversight.

This indicator supports leakage monitoring without requiring wood volume quantification.

5.3.4 Indicator 4: Charcoal and Extraction Hotspot Tracking

Purpose: Detect localized commercial extraction activity.

Quarterly recording of:

Number of active charcoal production sites;

Location mapping via GPS;

Evidence of expansion.

This indicator is particularly relevant in peri-urban and charcoal-prone zones.

Responsible entity:

CREMA and MMDAs/WD.

Response:

Dialogue with producers;

Promotion of alternative fuelwood sources;

Enforcement where necessary.

5.3.5 Indicator 5: Conflict and Access Pressure Index

Purpose: Identify structural drivers of degradation risk.

Quarterly reporting includes:

Number of land access disputes affecting wetland zones;

Number of unresolved cases;

Reported conflicts between artisanal and industrial actors.

Rising conflict levels correlate with increased ecosystem vulnerability.

Responsible entity:

District authorities with CREMA reporting support.

This indicator links governance instability to carbon permanence risk.

5.4 Pollution and Physical Disturbance Monitoring

In addition to the five core indicators, shoreline transects shall record:

Visible plastic accumulation clusters;

Waste dumping hotspots;

Physical disturbances affecting hydrology.

Monitoring frequency: Quarterly.

While not directly carbon-related, these factors affect ecosystem health and restoration viability.

5.5 Escalation and Response Protocol

Environmental pressure surveillance is actionable only if linked to response.

The escalation protocol operates as follows:

Detection at Community Level

Notification to CREMA Executive

District Review

Technical Assessment (if required)

Intervention (dialogue, enforcement, restoration, zoning review)

All actions shall be documented within the national MRV database.

5.6 Integration with Carbon Risk Monitoring

Where pressure indicators show:

Rising cutting incidence;

Expansion of land conversion;

Increased charcoal activity;

Escalating access conflict;

These signals shall be cross-referenced with:

Permanent Sample Plot locations;

Restoration areas;

Carbon stock projections.

This ensures that environmental pressure monitoring directly informs carbon integrity assessment.

5.7 Digital Platform Integration

The web-based spatial monitoring platform shall host:

Pressure alert overlays;

Charcoal hotspot mapping;

Land conversion polygons;

Conflict-linked zones.

Automated alerts may be generated where satellite change intersects with protected or restoration zones.

Community-level access via smartphone enables real-time reporting.

5.8 National Integrity Statement on Environmental Pressure Monitoring

The Environmental Pressure Surveillance Framework:

Avoids overcomplex quantification that undermines sustainability;

Prioritizes trend detection and early warning;

Links directly to carbon permanence risk;

Enables timely governance response;

Maintains institutional feasibility.

Through this framework, Ghana ensures that blue carbon investments are not only measured in carbon terms, but protected through proactive monitoring of degradation drivers.

6. GOVERNANCE AND SAFEGUARD ASSURANCE FRAMEWORK

6.1 Objective

The Governance and Safeguard Assurance Framework establishes the institutional mechanisms through which Ghana ensures that blue carbon investments are:

Socially inclusive;

Transparent;

Conflict-sensitive;

Accountable;

Equitably managed;

Protected against elite capture and governance instability.

This framework recognizes that long-term carbon integrity depends on stable, legitimate, and participatory governance systems at the landscape level.

6.2 Governance Integrity Structure

The governance assurance component operates through three interconnected mechanisms:

Institutional Representation Monitoring

Transparency and Benefit-Sharing Oversight

Grievance and Conflict Management Systems

These mechanisms function at community, district, and national levels.

6.3 Institutional Representation Monitoring

Inclusive governance reduces social risk and enhances carbon permanence.

Each CREMA and blue carbon governance structure shall maintain:

A minimum of 40% female representation in executive leadership;

Youth representation within governance committees;

Inclusion of marginalized or vulnerable groups where applicable;

Documented meeting frequency and attendance records.

Annual reporting shall include:

Governance composition statistics;

Changes in leadership structure;

Evidence of inclusive decision-making processes.

This ensures that benefits and responsibilities are broadly distributed and reduces the risk of social resistance or disengagement.

6.4 Transparency and Benefit-Sharing Oversight

Transparent management of blue carbon revenues and associated livelihood benefits is essential to maintaining trust and preventing elite capture.

Monitoring shall include:

Documentation of benefit allocation mechanisms;

Public disclosure of financial flows where carbon revenues are generated;

Record of livelihood support distribution (disaggregated by gender and youth participation where feasible);

Evidence of community consultations regarding allocation decisions.

Where carbon finance mechanisms are activated, benefit-sharing frameworks shall be formally documented and archived within the national MRV system.

6.5 Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM)

A functional Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) is mandatory for maintaining safeguard integrity.

Each blue carbon landscape shall maintain:

A documented grievance submission pathway;

Designated focal persons for grievance handling;

Escalation procedures to district and national levels;

Documentation of grievance resolution outcomes.

Monitoring indicators shall include:

Number of grievances received;

Nature of grievances;

Resolution rate;

Time taken to resolve cases.

Unresolved or repeated grievances shall trigger district-level review and, where necessary, national oversight.

6.6 Conflict and Access Risk Monitoring

Land tenure disputes, access conflicts, and resource competition can undermine ecosystem protection and carbon permanence.

The MRV system shall track:

Number of land-use conflicts affecting blue carbon areas;

Conflicts between artisanal and industrial actors;

Access restrictions affecting traditional users;

Mediation efforts undertaken.

Conflict escalation is treated as an environmental pressure risk and shall be cross-referenced with ecological monitoring outputs.

6.7 Free, Prior and Informed Participation

Sustained community consent and participation are foundational to long-term ecosystem stewardship.

The system shall document:

Annual consultation meetings;

Participation records;

Updates on restoration and monitoring activities;

Community validation of key decisions affecting land use.

Participation documentation shall be archived digitally within the national MRV database.

6.8 Safeguard Integration with Ecological Monitoring

Governance and safeguard indicators are not isolated from ecological monitoring.

The system integrates:

Conflict data with land conversion alerts;

Benefit-sharing transparency with restoration participation;

Grievance patterns with pressure hotspots;

Governance instability signals with carbon risk zones.

This integration ensures that governance breakdowns are identified as early-warning signals for ecological risk.

6.9 Digital Documentation and Transparency

The national web-based platform shall host:

Governance composition summaries;

Conflict mapping overlays;

Grievance tracking logs (with appropriate confidentiality safeguards);

Benefit-sharing documentation where applicable.

Access levels shall be managed to protect sensitive information while maintaining transparency for authorized stakeholders.

6.10 National Integrity Statement on Governance and Safeguards

Through this Governance and Safeguard Assurance Framework, Ghana establishes:

A socially grounded blue carbon monitoring system;

Mechanisms to prevent elite capture and inequitable benefit concentration;

Transparent processes that build donor and private sector confidence;

Conflict-sensitive landscape governance;

Institutional safeguards aligned with international good practice.

The integration of governance monitoring within the national MRV architecture ensures that blue carbon investments are not only environmentally sound but socially legitimate and institutionally secure.

8. CARBON ACCOUNTING PROTOCOL AND RE-MEASUREMENT CYCLE

8.1 Objective

The Carbon Accounting Protocol establishes the national technical procedures through which Ghana will:

Quantify blue carbon stocks;

Monitor stock changes over time;

Detect emissions and removals;

Maintain methodological consistency;

Ensure auditability and transparency;

Support participation in voluntary and compliance carbon markets.

The protocol is anchored in the national Carbon Inventory Baseline and is aligned with internationally recognized good practice, including IPCC Wetlands guidance and established carbon accounting standards.

8.2 Carbon Pools Covered

The national blue carbon accounting system includes the following carbon pools:

Above-Ground Biomass (AGB)

Below-Ground Biomass (BGB)

Deadwood (where applicable)

Soil Organic Carbon (0–100 cm depth)

These pools were quantified under the national Carbon Inventory Assessment and serve as the Year 0 benchmark.

8.3 Stratification Framework

Carbon accounting is conducted using ecological stratification based on:

Mangrove species composition;

Canopy density classes;

Hydrological zones;

Degradation intensity categories.

Stratification ensures:

Representative sampling;

Reduced uncertainty;

Accurate extrapolation to landscape scale.

The stratification schema established during the baseline inventory shall remain the reference structure for future re-measurement cycles.

8.4 Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) System

8.4.1 PSP Registry

All Permanent Sample Plots established during the baseline inventory shall be:

Georeferenced;

Digitally archived;

Assigned unique identifiers;

Integrated into the national web-based spatial platform.

PSPs represent the long-term carbon monitoring backbone.

8.4.2 Field Measurement Responsibilities

Community-level support:

Plot access maintenance;

Tree Diameter at Breast Height (DBH) measurements;

Visual disturbance reporting.

National technical agencies:

Biomass calculations using approved allometric equations;

Soil core sampling and laboratory analysis;

Data verification and validation;

Uncertainty assessment;

National carbon database management.

This separation ensures technical integrity while maintaining community engagement.

8.5 Re-Measurement Cycle

Carbon stock re-measurement shall follow standard practice intervals:

Biomass Re-Measurement

Every 3–5 years.

Soil Carbon Reassessment

In accordance with methodological guidance and risk exposure, typically aligned with biomass re-measurement cycles unless disturbance events require earlier reassessment.

Re-measurement may be accelerated in cases of:

Significant disturbance detected through satellite monitoring;

Large-scale restoration expansion;

Carbon credit issuance cycles requiring updated verification.

8.6 Emissions and Removals Accounting

Carbon stock changes shall be calculated through:

Comparison of re-measured carbon stocks against baseline;

Estimation of removals from restoration growth;

Estimation of emissions from detected land conversion or degradation events.

Where degradation or loss is detected through spatial monitoring, affected stratification units shall be assessed for carbon impact.

All calculations shall incorporate:

Conservative assumptions where uncertainty exists;

Clear documentation of applied equations;

Transparent methodological notes.

8.7 Leakage and Risk Consideration

Leakage risk shall be assessed through integration of:

Environmental pressure indicators;

Fuel source shift monitoring;

Charcoal hotspot tracking;

Conflict and access signals.

Where pressure increases outside restoration zones, national carbon accounting shall assess potential displacement effects.

This ensures that carbon gains are not undermined by externalized degradation.

8.8 Quality Assurance and Quality Control (QA/QC)

The national carbon accounting protocol incorporates the following QA/QC measures:

Double-entry field measurement verification;

Random plot re-checks;

Data consistency audits;

Outlier detection procedures;

Version-controlled database updates;

Secure archiving of raw field sheets and laboratory reports.

All carbon calculations shall be traceable from plot-level data to national totals.

8.9 Data Archiving and Audit Trail

Carbon data shall be:

Stored within a nationally managed database;

Backed up under secure digital storage protocols;

Time-stamped and version-controlled;

Accessible for independent verification when required.

The web-based platform shall provide visualization access, while detailed datasets remain under authorized institutional control.

8.10 Carbon Reporting Outputs

The Carbon Accounting Protocol produces:

Five-year carbon stock update reports;

Emission reduction or removal summaries;

Uncertainty estimates;

Risk-adjusted carbon projections;

Landscape-level carbon balance assessments.

These outputs support:

National climate reporting;

Investment due diligence;

Market participation readiness.

8.11 National Carbon Integrity Statement

Through this Carbon Accounting Protocol, Ghana establishes:

A scientifically robust blue carbon accounting system;

Clear re-measurement discipline;

Institutional ownership of carbon data;

Transparent QA/QC safeguards;

Market-compatible accounting architecture.

The protocol ensures that Ghana’s blue carbon assets are quantified, monitored, and protected in accordance with international standards while remaining anchored within national institutions.

The National Reporting and Disclosure Framework establishes the structured outputs of Ghana’s Blue Carbon MRV System and defines the mechanisms through which ecological, carbon, environmental pressure, and governance data are consolidated, reviewed, and communicated.

Institutional accountability;

National transparency;

Climate reporting integration;

Investment-grade disclosure;

Continuous adaptive management.

Restoration survival updates;

Mangrove cutting signals;

Environmental pressure alerts;

Reduced duplication;

Sustained monitoring performance;

Investment confidence.

The MRV system operates through a coordinated, multi-level governance structure.

10.2 National Oversight Institutions

10.2.1 Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR)

MLNR serves as the primary technical custodian of the National Blue Carbon MRV System.

Responsibilities include:

Oversight of ecological and carbon monitoring systems;

Management of the web-based spatial monitoring platform;

Consolidation of national-level monitoring data;

Coordination with Wildlife Division and Forestry Commission;

Preparation of the Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report;

Oversight of carbon stock re-measurement cycles.

10.2.2 Ministry of Environment, Science, and Technology (MEST)

MEST provides strategic and policy-level oversight.

Responsibilities include:

Alignment of MRV outputs with national climate reporting;

Integration into NDC and adaptation reporting;

Cross-ministerial coordination;

Investment and donor engagement support;

Safeguard policy alignment.

10.2.3 Wildlife Division (WD)

Wildlife Division provides technical and field oversight.

Responsibilities include:

Validation of ecological monitoring outputs;

Field verification of land conversion alerts;

Supervision of restoration monitoring;

Technical review of environmental pressure reports;

Support to CREMAs in monitoring activities.



10.2.4 Forestry Commission (FC)

The Forestry Commission provides carbon accounting expertise.

Responsibilities include:

Oversight of carbon inventory data;

Application of allometric equations;

Soil carbon analysis coordination;

QA/QC for carbon calculations;

National carbon database management;

Preparation of Carbon Stock Update Reports.

10.3 District-Level Responsibilities

Metropolitant, Municipal and District Assemblies serve as the intermediate validation and coordination layer.

Responsibilities include:

Consolidation of CREMA monitoring reports;

Validation of environmental pressure signals;

Conflict mediation support;

Escalation of land-use disputes to national authorities;

Annual submission of district blue carbon summaries to MLNR.

Districts act as both data validators and conflict response facilitators.

10.4 Community-Level Responsibilities (CREMAs and CCAs)

Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs) and Community Change Agents (CCAs) serve as the primary field monitoring actors.

Responsibilities include:

Restoration survival monitoring;

Mangrove cutting signal reporting;

Charcoal hotspot identification;

Fuel source shift survey participation;

Conflict reporting;

Governance participation documentation;

Engagement in adaptive management discussions.

CREMAs do not conduct carbon calculations but support data collection under national technical supervision.

10.5 Digital Platform Management

Managed centrally by MLNR;

Technically maintained by designated national IT/GIS personnel;

Access-controlled through role-based permissions.

Access categories:

National administrators (full data access);

District users (regional data access);

CREMA users (designated zone access);

Observer access (restricted summary dashboards where applicable).

Data integrity controls remain at national level.

10.6 Independent Review and Verification (When Applicable)

Independent review shall:

Review environmental pressure integration;

Long-term asset protection.

Restoration survival tracking;

Ecologically sensitive area surveillance;

Real-time land conversion alerts via digital platform.

QA/QC procedures;

Integration of environmental pressure signals into carbon risk assessment.

Institutionally controlled.

13. CONCLUSION

Ghana’s coastal mangrove and wetland ecosystems represent critical national natural capital assets that contribute to climate mitigation, biodiversity protection, shoreline stability, fisheries productivity, and community livelihoods.

The National Blue Carbon Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) Framework establishes, for the first time, a comprehensive, nationally anchored system for safeguarding these ecosystems while enabling sustainable investment and carbon asset development.

Through the integration of:

Ecological and carbon integrity monitoring;

Environmental pressure surveillance;

Governance and safeguard assurance;

Digital spatial oversight;

Structured carbon accounting protocols;

Institutional role clarity;

National reporting architecture;

Ghana has established a blue carbon monitoring system that is:

Scientifically robust;

Institutionally grounded;

Digitally enabled;

Socially inclusive;

Risk-aware;

Investment-ready;

Scalable to additional landscapes.

The framework moves beyond project-based monitoring and establishes a permanent national architecture capable of supporting both voluntary and compliance carbon market participation while maintaining sovereign institutional control.

By combining community-based monitoring with national technical oversight and continuous digital surveillance, the MRV system ensures that blue carbon assets are:

Measurable and verifiable;

Protected from degradation;

Transparent to stakeholders;

Managed through adaptive governance;

Positioned for long-term climate and economic value.

This framework provides the foundation upon which future blue carbon investments, restoration programs, policy reforms, and carbon finance initiatives can confidently operate.

The Government of Ghana, through MLNR and MEST, affirms its commitment to implementing and strengthening this National Blue Carbon MRV Framework as a central instrument of coastal ecosystem stewardship and climate action.
