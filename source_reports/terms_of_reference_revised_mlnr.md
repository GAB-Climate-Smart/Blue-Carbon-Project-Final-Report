# PROJECT BACKGROUND



The West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA ResIP 2) was developed in response to countries’ requests for solutions and finance to help protect and restore the ecological, social, and economic assets of coastal areas in the subregion. In recent years, West Africa’s coastal communities have become highly vulnerable to the impacts of coastal erosion, flooding, pollution, and coastal retreat resulting in the loss of housing, assets, land, and other important ecosystem services. 

Degradation of these coastal and marine ecosystems is leading to significant and potentially irreversible loss of critical ecosystems such as beaches, wetlands, and mangroves which are key for livelihoods, food security, and storm protection. 

These challenges are often transboundary, multisectoral, and beyond the ability of one country or development partner to finance or effectively tackle. Consequently, to address these shared regional challenges, WACA ResIP 2 was approved by the World Bank in December 2022. 

The project supports site-specific grey, green, and hybrid physical investments, and social subprojects at the community level to achieve measurable increases in protection from coastal erosion and flooding, pollution control, and to promote climate-resilient and nature-based coastal development. 

Project activities are expected to directly benefit coastal communities and protect/enhance livelihoods in the three beneficiary countries, i.e. Ghana, the Gambia and Guinea Bissau. 



In Ghana, the Project is designed to address the challenges to unlocking the potential of its blue economy and to ensure a resilient seascape for the communities who depend on it for their livelihood and wellbeing. Notable among the challenges in Ghana are coastal erosion, flooding, pollution; ecosystem degradation; and inadequate institutional coordination of policies and programs. The overall objective of WACA in Ghana is to strengthen the resilience of coastal communities. The project’s overarching strategy to achieve this outcome is to focus on strengthening Ghana's blue economy and to restore and protect critical coastal ecosystems which provide essential services to nearby communities, including income generation and protection against natural disasters.



WACA ResIP 2 in Ghana Project is being led by the Ministry of Environment Science Technology and Innovation (MESTI) with support from the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources and its Technical Agencies (Wildlife Division of the Forestry Commission), Ministry of Works and Housing (MWH) together with Ghana Hydrological Authority (GHA), the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) of Ghana and other partners. Details of the project design and implementation arrangement is available in the Project Appraisal Document which will be provided as part of the Client’s Input to the assignment.



# CONTEXT OF THE ASSIGNMENT



Blue Carbon is the carbon stored specifically in seagrasses, salt marshes and mangrove forests. Blue carbon ecosystems are among the most productive on Earth and although blue carbon ecosystems cover less than 2% of the area of the global ocean, they are critical carbon sinks and are a key component of the global carbon cycle.

One of the most valued ecological roles of blue carbon ecosystems is carbon storage and sequestration. Blue carbon ecosystems can store up to 10 times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial ecosystems. In addition to their carbon sequestration benefit, blue carbon ecosystems also provide considerable biodiversity and socio-economic value and are prioritized for conservation. 

Blue carbon ecosystems are also considered the most vulnerable ecosystems on the planet as they are disappearing at an alarming rate in the tropical and subtropical to warm- temperate regions worldwide, through development pressure and habitat destruction. As climate change continues to escalate, it is unlikely that emissions reductions alone will be sufficient to curb the current trends in global warming, and therefore measures implemented towards enhancing carbon dioxide (CO2) removals will be necessary.

As a signatory to the Paris Agreement, Ghana has made commitments towards reducing emissions, and aiming to achieve a just transition towards a resilient, low-carbon economy. While the largest sources of CO2 emissions in the country are from the energy sector, the land sector has the largest potential to contribute towards CO2 removals and shall include blue carbon ecosystems. Ghana is also signatory to the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and therefore is imperative that we position ourselves to meet the Kunming/Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF). 

Understanding the ecological dynamics of blue carbon ecosystems is key to the identification and implementation of appropriate climate change interventions as it can be used to determine in which blue carbon ecosystems can restoration measures and carbon stock enhancement be carried out. There is therefore a need to undertake a national assessment of blue carbon ecosystems to understand their vulnerability, ecological functioning and to identify the potential contribution blue carbon ecosystems can make to the conservation and national restoration targets for ecological infrastructure.





# PURPOSE OF THE 



The purpose of this Terms of Reference (ToR) is to recruit a Consulting Firm to support Ghana in assessing and prioritizing its blue carbon ecosystems potential for designing and implementation of measures towards the sustainability, adaptation and resilience of its coastal landscape.



# SCOPE OF THE ASSIGNMENT



The geographical scope of the assignment will encompass the entire coast of the country. The Consulting Firm would be expected to undertake the assignment in line with the objectives stated and the tasks thereof.



# OBJECTIVES OF THE ASSIGNMENT



The overall objective of the assignment is to assess and prioritize the Blue Carbon Ecosystem potential of the Country and provide the baseline data necessary to develop blue carbon projects that meets both the compliance and voluntary carbon markets.



The specific objectives include:





To determine the various Blue Carbon Ecosystems available in the country (Mangrove and Salt Marsh) 

To determine the extent of each of the Blue Carbon Ecosystems and map their geographic location.

To determine the drivers of degradation of these ecosystems and propose measures to address them.



To produce base maps and carbon inventories for these ecosystems to form the basis of decision making and development of subprojects.

To prepare ecosystem and site-specific solutions for the mapped Blue Carbon Ecosystems

To develop a strategy for mangrove and salt marsh restoration based on site-specific information collected during the assessment.

To develop monitoring protocols for the various ecosystem types identified in the study area.



To develop the capacity of officers and key stakeholders throughout the execution of the assignment so that they can continuously monitor and improve the data to be generated.

# TASKS TO BE PERFORMED BY THE CONSULTING FIRM



The Firm will undertake a national assessment of blue carbon ecosystems to quantify the extent, drivers of degradation over time and determine which blue carbon ecosystems should be prioritized for protection, conservation and restoration measures. 



This work will be organized in seven (7) phases. They are:

Phase 1: Project inception. 

Phase 2: Literature review of blue carbon ecosystems in Ghana. 

Phase 3: Generation of a national blue carbon ecosystem cover map, and a report on the current state of the identified blue carbon ecosystems.

Phase 4: Blue Carbon Inventory 

Phase: 5 Monitoring Protocol for the Blue Carbon Ecosystem types

Phase 6: Strategy for Mangrove and Salt Marsh Restoration 

Phase 7: Consolidating and report writing of the findings in the form of a Final Report. 



The final report must use the baseline data to provide advice on an approach for implementation of policy and management in the different blue carbon ecosystems, as well as contain a table with spatial locations of prioritized blue carbon ecosystems, in addition to where implementation can be initiated in the short, medium, and long-term. The Firm must adhere to the structure as set out in the ToRs.

The Consulting Firm will provide the following services:



## Phase 1: Project Inception 

The service provider is expected to participate in an inception meeting as would be scheduled by the Project Implementation Unit (PIU) at MESTI and the Focal Point Unit (FPU) of Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources. Participants of the inception meeting will be agreed among the PIU at MESTI and the FPU at MLNR. In this session, the Firm will be expected to present on the methodology, approach, and timelines proposed for the achievement of the assignment. The Firm will further document the discussions (in minutes) as well as decisions taken during the inception meeting.

Following the inception meeting, the Firm will prepare an inception report for consideration and sign-off by the MLNR’s FPU before submission to MESTI. The inception report must reflect decisions taken during the inception meeting, the project timelines and proposed knowledge transfer to Officials and Project Stakeholders who will be expected to implement and continuously monitor to improve the outputs of the assignment.



OUTPUT: A project inception report that captures the decisions and timelines agreed upon during the inception meeting.



## Phase 2: Literature Review

The Firm must undertake a literature review of blue carbon ecosystems in Ghana to provide secondary baseline information. The review must document deforestation, forest degradation, as well as biodiversity loss occurring in blue carbon ecosystems. Specifically, the literature review should also document degradation and biodiversity loss occurring in mangrove and salt marshes.

The literature review must provide detailed information of the state of blue carbon ecosystems with focus on aspects as listed below:

### Carbon benefits:

Carbon storage in the above ground biomass (AGB), below ground biomass (BGB) and the soil.

Description of GHG emissions and carbon sequestration within blue carbon ecosystems.

Carbon storage potential of intact blue carbon ecosystems.

Area available for restoration, expansion, and protection.

### Biodiversity Conservation

Drivers of human induced biodiversity loss.

Description of patterns and trends of biodiversity loss along the coast.

Other disturbances causing biodiversity loss in blue carbon ecosystems.

### Socio-economic benefits

Benefits of intact and healthy blue carbon ecosystems.

Threats to livelihoods resulting from degradation of mangroves and salt marshes.

Socio-economic development opportunities linked to conservation and restoration.



OUTPUT: A secondary baseline report on the state of blue carbon ecosystems in Ghana.



## Phase 3: Development of baseline Land Cover /land use change Maps for blue carbon ecosystems

The Firm must develop a baseline Land Cover /land use change map for monitoring of changes that would occur in blue carbon ecosystems during and after the project interventions. This is to support the development of policies and strategies for the conservation and enhancement of the restoration efforts of these ecosystems. 

The data sources, assumptions and methodologies used for this activity should be clearly explained and documented for replication by officials of MESTI & MLNR who would support the Firm.

The baseline assessment must cover the following aspects of the blue carbon ecosystems:

Land cover of each Blue Carbon Ecosystem (True extent): This is to address the inconsistent information on the extent of blue carbon ecosystems as shown in various literature in the country. It will also enable monitoring and evaluation of changes on the extents of blue carbon ecosystems by the MLNR over time.

The Firm will generate a national land cover map of mangroves and salt marshes within the marine and estuarine ecosystems to ascertain the total area coverage and changes between the 1990, 2000, 2010, 2020 and the present to allow for trend analysis. 

The Firm will annex to the baseline land cover maps, a drone shots or aerial photographs of the current state of the areas carefully put together in a collage and stored in a database. 

The Firm will collate information on drivers of degradation, transformation, and biodiversity loss for blue carbon ecosystems in the different estuaries, lagoons and locations on the cover map produced. 

The list of drivers must include natural or anthropogenic sources such as the extent of alien invasive plant infestation, encroachment for socio-economic activities. The details should include industries and sectors that are the main causes driving the degradation.

Specific detailed cost-benefit analysis on mitigation measures to address the drivers in the different locations should be outlined.

The Firm must prepare and present the results of the baseline studies during expert review meetings, stakeholder consultations and validation workshops. In addition, the Firm is expected to prepare and submit formal meeting minutes.



OUTPUTS: (1) A comprehensive blue carbon ecosystems dataset that can be integrated with the national land cover map, (2) Blue Ecosystem Cover maps showing the extent and geographic location of the mangroves and salt marshes, (3) Report on drivers of degradation, transformation and biodiversity loss including which industries and sectors are the main drivers of the degradation, as well as the social and economic costs of reducing the drivers of degradation and biodiversity loss. 



## Phase 4: Carbon Inventory for the Blue carbon Ecosystems

The Firm will prepare a blue carbon inventory for the identified and mapped blue carbon ecosystems in the country. Among others, the Firm must at least follow the steps below.

### Step 1: Define the boundaries in line with the land cover maps generated. 

Defined boundaries must be well-documented to serve as the baseline for any estimates of total carbon stock or carbon stock in a specific area.  

Ensure that the area to be assessed adequately represents the range of species and growth forms found in that ecosystem. 

### Step 2: Stratifying the Project Area

Depending on the baselines data collected on the nature of the ecosystem, large heterogeneous sites should be divided into smaller homogeneous areas to fit the purpose and objective of the assignment.

Depending on the nature of the ecosystem, stratification may be guided by:

Existing land use (tidal salt marsh areas being used for agricultural purposes). 

Potential land use (areas vulnerable to conversion for aquaculture or development). 

Variations in soil characteristics (soil depth or type, or sediment grain-size). 

Geomorphological features (proximity to geologic features, drainage features); and 

Proximity to the ocean (areas tidally flooded daily or areas only flooded at the highest high tides).  

### Step 3: Determine which Carbon Pools to Measure 

Based on the baseline data collected, a carbon pool should be measured if: 

it is a significant portion (e.g., > 5%) of the total carbon in the stratum. 

it is likely to or has changed significantly (either naturally due to climate change and extreme weather or due to human impacts such as land-use change or dredging); or 

the carbon pool is unknown.

The carbon pools expected to be measured in the assignment areas are indicated below:

Mangrove Carbon Pools 

Aboveground living biomass (trees, scrub trees, lianas, palms, pneumatophores)

Aboveground dead biomass (litter, downed wood, dead trees). 

Belowground living biomass (roots and rhizomes).

Soil carbon which includes the dead below-ground biomass.

Salt Marsh Carbon Pools 

Aboveground living biomass (shrubs, grasses, herbs, etc.).

Belowground living biomass (roots and rhizomes)

Soil carbon

### Step 4: Determine Plot Type, Number, and Location

The Firm would determine the minimum number of plots needed to ensure accuracy. 

This would be informed by the carbon pools present in the ecosystem and the assignment area stratification. 

Indicators on the plot type, number and location would be agreed with MLNR and MESTI in the monthly progress meetings before finalization to ensure that various objectives for each ecosystem are met.

### Step 5: Determine measurement frequency. 

Depending on the baselines data and the rate of expected change (natural disasters, land use change, climate change, etc.), in the ecosystem types, the Firm is to propose the frequency of measure to MLNR and MESTI through the monthly progress meetings for approval. 

The frequency should also be informed by requirements for participation in carbon markets, and the cost involved in sampling and laboratory analysis linked to resource availability.



OUTPUT: Blue Carbon Inventory for Ghana 



## Phase 5: Monitoring Protocol for the Blue Carbon Ecosystem types

The Firm would be expected to prepare a monitoring protocol for the ecosystem types identified in the assignment area which would be used by the Government to continuously monitor trends and changes.

The monitoring protocol would be linked to the baseline findings and the land cover maps prepared under phase 3 of the assignment. For example, monitoring protocol should be developed for the mangrove area identified and assessed in Keta Lagoon Complex or the salt marshes identified in Songhor Ramsar site. The protocol should clearly define the parameters to be measured, the frequency of measurement, institutional roles and responsibility, community roles and the equipment/resources required to undertake the task.

To help with real-time monitoring of the areas for which land cover maps have been generated, the firm is required to deplore a system that provides periodic satellite images to support quick decision making.



OUTPUT: Monitoring Protocol for various ecosystem types, list of equipment and a monitoring system that provides Realtime data on the project areas.



## Phase 6: Strategy for Mangrove and Salt Marsh Restoration

The Firm is required to prepare a strategy for mangrove and salt marsh restoration in the selected blue carbon ecosystems. This strategy would be informed by the information on drivers of degradation, transformation, and biodiversity loss for blue carbon ecosystems in the different estuaries and locations identified on the land cover map. 

The Strategy will include potential areas for restoration in the different geographical locations on the land cover map, identification and sources of viable restoration materials, institutional arrangement for the restoration, opportunities for community members to engage in the process, capacity needs of all actors to technically execute the restoration, cost benefits analysis for the proposed restoration methods and a system of continuous monitoring of investments among others.



The Firm would agree on the outlines, the various components of the strategy and the methodologies with MLNR and MESTI during the execution of the contract through the monthly progress meetings. This approach is adopted because it is envisaged that the initial analytical work which includes the ecosystem types and nature of degradation, and the sectors would be valuable in determining the appropriate restoration method. 

The Firm would be present at expert reviews, consultation workshops as well as the final validation of the strategy which would be facilitated by the MLNR and be responsible for documenting the inputs of subject matter experts that will be involved in the processes. The outputs from these reviews and consultations would form integral parts of the strategy.



OUTPUT: Strategy Document on Mangrove and Salt Marsh Restoration for Ghana



## Phase 7: Assignment completion and Final Report

There is a need to establish the extent at which blue carbon ecosystems fall within state-owned land, protected areas, private lands and community lands to be able to enhance their level of protection. The Firm must investigate the percentage split of blue carbon ecosystems that fall within protected areas, private lands, and communal lands.

The Firm must use the above information to provide advice on an approach for the protection, rehabilitation and expansion of blue carbon ecosystems, and the scope for implementation of policy and management of the different blue carbon ecosystems investigated. 

The strategic document on the approach for the protection, rehabilitation and expansion of blue carbon ecosystems must detail the cost benefit analysis of their implementation.

Each blue carbon ecosystem must be ranked according to a level of priority from the highest priority to the lowest priority. The criteria used to rank the ecosystems as well as for choosing the measures to be implemented must be clearly outlined.

The Firm must also generate a table with the spatial location of blue carbon ecosystems and recommendations where implementation can be initiated in the short, medium, and long-term, the nature of the interventions, as well as possible partners that can support with the implementation of the interventions.



OUTPUT: A Final strategic document containing policy, management advice, and a table with spatial locations where interventions can be implemented, level of priority, and possible implementation partners.
