# Chapter 3: National MRV Architecture

---

## 3.1 Design Philosophy

Ghana's National Blue Carbon Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) Framework was not designed to serve the life of a project. It was designed to serve the life of a coastline.

The WACA ResIP 2 programme (World Bank Project P175625), approved in December 2022, provides the investment foundation for the baseline assessment, the monitoring network, and the institutional capacity-building that the MRV system requires. But WACA funding is time-limited. The programme has a defined disbursement period, a defined set of deliverables, and a defined end point. Ghana's obligation to monitor 30,025 hectares of coastal mangroves and wetlands — to account for their carbon stocks, to detect threats to their integrity, and to report on their condition against its Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitments — is indefinite. The architecture of the MRV Framework was shaped by this fundamental asymmetry between project duration and national obligation.

This is why institutional permanence is the first design principle of the system. Every element of the Framework — the tiered institutional structure, the field monitoring protocols, the digital platform, the reporting cadence — has been designed to be operated by Ghanaian government institutions and communities using Ghanaian institutional authority and capacity, not by the consultancy team that established it. The Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI) are not participants in the MRV system. They are its custodians. The Forestry Commission's Wildlife Division is not an external validator. It is a structural component of the system's quality assurance architecture. Community Resource Management Areas (CREMAs) and Community Change Agents (CCAs) are not programme beneficiaries who contribute data to an external framework. They are the primary data-generating tier of the national system. This distinction — between a project mechanism and a national institutional architecture — is the foundational logic of every design decision documented in this chapter.

The second design principle is progressive expansion without structural redesign. At the time of this report, the MRV system operates across three Ramsar landscapes: the Keta Lagoon Complex, the Songor Ramsar Site, and the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site. These three landscapes account for the 30,025 hectares of coastal mangroves assessed in the 2025 geo-spatial baseline. The Keta Lagoon Complex alone holds 20,717.95 hectares — approximately 69% of the national mangrove total — and has been designated as the primary MRV pilot site, with the deepest monitoring infrastructure and the most fully developed baseline. Songor, at 465.76 hectares (2% of the national total), and Muni-Pomadze, where mangrove cover is ecologically insignificant, each require tailored monitoring approaches that reflect their distinct ecological and governance realities. As additional coastal landscapes complete their own baseline assessments in future programme phases, they join the national system by populating the established architecture with site-specific data — new PSP networks, new CREMA monitoring zones, new district reporting relationships — without requiring the framework itself to be restructured. The architecture was designed to accommodate Ghana's full coastal extent without being rebuilt to do so.

## 3.2 The Four Institutional Tiers

The MRV system operates across four coordinated institutional tiers. These are not parallel programmes running simultaneously; they are a vertically integrated chain of monitoring, validation, analysis, and oversight in which each tier performs functions that the others cannot, and in which the system fails if any tier is absent or non-functional.

**Community Tier — CREMAs and CCAs**

Field-based monitoring of mangrove ecosystems begins at the community level, delivered through CREMAs and the CCAs who serve as their operational frontline. A CREMA is a formally constituted community governance body with legal standing under the Wildlife Resources Management Act, 2023 (Act 1115), capable of entering into agreements, enforcing locally developed by-laws, and — critically — participating in carbon finance arrangements on behalf of its member communities. Across the 37 communities profiled in the 2025 governance and CREMA readiness assessment, readiness for CREMA formation varies substantially: Muni-Pomadze, anchored by the community of Sankor, shows the highest organisational cohesion and is the most advanced toward formal CREMA establishment; Keta presents a fragmented landscape where clusters such as Dzita and Agbatsivi have demonstrated high motivation and where emerging CREMA units are already mapping boundaries; Songor, where land tenure conflict between artisanal communities and industrial salt mining operations has fractured local leadership in communities including Pute and Elavanyo, requires conflict mediation as a prerequisite to formal CREMA formation.

Within the MRV architecture, CREMA monitoring responsibilities are specific and structured. CREMA members — supported by their designated CCAs — are responsible for maintaining access to Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) locations for periodic re-measurement; recording diameter at breast height (DBH) measurements as directed by national technical teams; documenting visible disturbance events such as cutting incidence, charcoal kiln presence, and evidence of land conversion; conducting quarterly transect walks within GIS-mapped monitoring zones to record pressure signals; monitoring restoration site survival rates at the intervals required by the restoration monitoring protocol; and recording governance indicators including meeting attendance, grievance submissions, and participation composition. CCAs operate within defined community monitoring zones that have been spatially delineated and uploaded to the national web platform, ensuring that field observations are georeferenced and directly linked to the sites they describe. The CCAs at Dzita, for example, were instrumental during the 2025 baseline in facilitating resolution of a dispute between landowners and youth over a potential restoration site — a function that illustrates both the social value of the CCA role and its practical importance to the integrity of the monitoring system they support.

**District Tier — MMDAs and the Wildlife Division**

Metropolitan, Municipal, and District Assemblies (MMDAs) and the Wildlife Division (WD) of the Forestry Commission form the district tier, which performs the consolidation and validation functions that transform community field data into verified district-level inputs to the national system. District authorities receive quarterly field data submissions from CREMAs, cross-check them for internal consistency, and identify observations that require technical follow-up or escalation. Where pressure thresholds are breached — for instance, where two or more mangrove cutting signals are recorded in the same monitoring period within a given CREMA zone — the district tier is responsible for triggering the response protocol: convening community review meetings, engaging with responsible actors, and escalating to enforcement authorities where cutting or conversion activity persists.

The Wildlife Division carries a specific and non-delegable role within the district tier: it is the primary statutory body responsible for Ramsar site management and CREMA establishment under Act 1115, and its field officers conduct the ground-truth validation that gives satellite-based extent monitoring its credibility. The 2025 geo-spatial baseline incorporated 319 participatory ground-truth reference points, validated through Wildlife Division field teams and community members. Ongoing satellite classification outputs — annual mangrove extent layers produced from Sentinel-2 imagery at 10–20 metre resolution — will be ground-truthed through the same institutional pathway, providing the continuity of methodology that is required for valid change detection over time. District authorities also submit consolidated annual monitoring reports to MLNR and MESTI, serving as the primary reporting link between field observation and national technical analysis.

**National Technical Tier — MLNR, MESTI, and the Forestry Commission**

The national technical tier, led jointly by MLNR and MESTI with technical support from the Forestry Commission, is responsible for the analytical, quality assurance, and archiving functions that national reporting requires. MLNR and MESTI receive consolidated district reports and integrate them with satellite analysis outputs and PSP re-measurement data to produce the national carbon stock picture. This involves GIS and satellite analysis of annual mangrove extent changes across the coastal landscape, carbon stock calculations from PSP re-measurement data on the 3–5 year cycle specified by the IPCC Wetlands Supplement methodology, quality assurance and quality control (QA/QC) procedures applied to field data, and the production and archiving of the Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report — the primary national reporting product that documents Ghana's progress against its NDC targets and provides investment-grade transparency for development partners and prospective carbon market participants.

MLNR serves as the MRV custodian: the institution with primary legal and administrative responsibility for the national data archive, the platform access governance structure, and the long-term integrity of the carbon accounting record. MESTI holds the lead ministry role for the WACA programme and serves as the primary interlocutor with the World Bank and with other development partners. The Forestry Commission's Wildlife Division connects the national technical tier to the district tier through its field officer network. This is not a communications link; it is an institutional quality assurance chain. Data moving upward through the system from community to national carries the Wildlife Division's validation at the district level, and the QA/QC procedures applied at national level are designed to detect anomalies that field-level validation may have missed.

**Independent Review Tier — When Applicable**

The fourth tier — independent review — is activated only under specific conditions: when the MRV system is being used to support carbon market participation under a verified standard such as Verra/VCS, Gold Standard, or Plan Vivo; when external donor audits require third-party assurance of the data record; or when other investment vehicles demand independent verification of reported carbon stock changes. Independent review at these moments provides the assurance that external counterparties require and that national institutions — whatever their credibility and rigour — cannot provide about themselves.

What independent review does not mean is the displacement of national institutional ownership of the data. Ghana's MRV system is a national system. The carbon stock data are Ghana's data. The Annual MRV Report is produced by MLNR and MESTI, not by a third-party auditor. Independent review examines the outputs and procedures of the national system; it does not operate the national system. This distinction is deliberate and important. MRV architectures that position external verifiers as the primary source of analytical authority create systems that cannot function when external funding for verification lapses. Ghana's architecture retains national primacy over data stewardship, with independent review as an external quality assurance layer that the system can engage when market or donor requirements demand it.

## 3.3 The Three Functional Layers

Alongside the vertical structure of institutional tiers, the MRV system operates through three horizontal functional layers. These layers define what is being monitored, not who is doing the monitoring. Each layer has a distinct analytical purpose, its own set of indicators, its own data sources and measurement cadence, and its own connection to the tier system through which monitoring responsibilities are assigned and escalation is triggered.

**Layer I — Ecological and Carbon Integrity**

Layer I is the foundational measurement layer of the system: it tracks the ecological condition of Ghana's coastal mangroves and the carbon stocks they contain. The primary unit of measurement is mangrove extent. Each year, satellite imagery from the Sentinel-2 constellation — processed at 10–20 metre resolution using the Random Forest classification algorithm on Google Earth Engine (GEE), consistent with the methodology used in the 2025 baseline — produces an updated national mangrove extent layer. Annual comparison against the Year 0 baseline reveals net mangrove area changes, fragmentation trends, encroachment hotspots, and restoration expansion across the coastal landscape. For the Keta Lagoon Complex, where the 2025 baseline documented 20,717.95 hectares and a historical deforestation rate of 2.15% per year, the annual extent update is the primary early-warning instrument for detecting whether conservation pressure is holding or intensifying.

Carbon stock monitoring operates on a longer cycle than extent monitoring, reflecting both the pace of biomass accumulation and the practical requirements of field-based measurement. The four carbon pools covered by the system — above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (BGB), deadwood where relevant, and soil organic carbon (SOC) measured to 100 centimetres depth — are quantified through the PSP network established during the 2025 carbon inventory, in alignment with IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance. Each PSP is georeferenced, digitally archived with a unique site identifier, and hosted on the national web platform. Biomass re-measurement is conducted every 3–5 years, with the cycle potentially accelerated where significant disturbance is detected or where restoration at scale requires more frequent verification. Community Change Agents maintain plot access and record DBH measurements in the intervals between full re-measurement exercises, providing a continuous presence at PSP locations that reduces the risk of undetected disturbance and supports data continuity between national measurement events.

Restoration performance monitoring is the third component of Layer I. All restoration sites — including the early action hubs at Dzita in the Keta Lagoon Complex, Pute in the Songor Ramsar Site, and Sankor in Muni-Pomadze — are georeferenced and registered within the national web platform with unique site identifiers and linked to the responsible CREMA and district authority. Survival rate monitoring begins three months after planting, with follow-up at six months, twelve months, and annually thereafter until establishment is confirmed. A minimum survival threshold of 70% after Year 1 must be met; sites falling below this threshold trigger corrective restoration action through the district tier. Canopy development progression, evidence of grazing or re-cutting disturbance, and hydrological suitability are also assessed at each monitoring visit, providing the ecological depth that carbon restoration accounting requires.

**Layer II — Environmental Pressure Surveillance**

Layer II is the early-warning layer of the MRV system. Where Layer I measures the condition of ecosystems, Layer II monitors the forces acting on them. The distinction matters operationally: by the time ecological degradation is visible in a carbon stock re-measurement or an annual extent update, the drivers that caused it have often been active for months or years. A surveillance layer designed to detect pressure signals before they produce measurable ecological harm gives the institutional tier system the lead time it needs to respond effectively.

The five core indicators that compose Layer II were selected to be operationally sustainable within the capacity constraints of Ghana's coastal communities and district institutions, while remaining analytically sufficient to detect the primary degradation threats documented in the 2025 environmental pressure assessment. The first indicator — land conversion monitoring — uses annual satellite imagery analysis to track salt pan expansion in hectares per year, urban encroachment into wetland zones, and wetland filling or drainage. At Songor, where industrial salt mining at Elavanyo and Obane has driven severe mangrove degradation, this indicator directly monitors the primary threat driver. The second indicator — mangrove cutting signal detection — is field-based: quarterly transect walks by CREMA monitoring teams record the presence of fresh stumps, active cutting evidence, and charcoal kiln activity within designated monitoring zones. Detection is binary — present or absent per transect — rather than volumetric, prioritising the detection of incidence over the quantification of volume, in recognition of the fact that volume estimation at the required spatial scale would exceed community monitoring capacity. A Pressure Alert is triggered when two or more signals are present in the same monitoring period, initiating district-level review.

The third indicator — fuel source shift monitoring — tracks annual household-level changes in primary cooking fuel dependence across a sample of 20–30 households per CREMA. At Keta, where fish-smoking operations drive intense and species-specific harvesting pressure on *Rhizophora racemosa* — the red mangrove preferred for its heat output — a persistent finding of high mangrove wood dependence in annual fuel surveys signals elevated carbon risk and supports the case for alternative fuel and cookstove interventions. A declining trend indicates that pressure-reduction strategies are working. The fourth indicator — charcoal and extraction hotspot tracking — records the number and GPS location of active charcoal production sites on a quarterly basis, enabling spatial trend analysis and the identification of sites where commercial extraction activity is expanding. The fifth indicator — the conflict and access pressure index — records the number of land access disputes affecting wetland zones, unresolved cases, and conflicts between artisanal and industrial actors. This indicator captures the governance-ecology interface that is particularly acute at Songor, where the 2025 CREMA readiness assessment found that long-standing land tenure disputes between local communities and commercial salt mining operations had paralysed local leadership structures in communities including Pute and Elavanyo, creating the conditions for passive ecosystem degradation in the absence of any active enforcement presence.

Each of these five indicators is connected to the escalation protocol that runs through the institutional tier system: detection at community level, notification to the CREMA Executive Committee, district review, technical assessment if required, and intervention — whether through community dialogue, enforcement action, restoration, or zoning review — with all actions documented in the national MRV database.

**Layer III — Governance and Safeguard Assurance**

Layer III recognises a claim that the governance and CREMA readiness assessment makes explicitly and with evidence: long-term carbon integrity depends on stable, legitimate, and participatory governance at the landscape level. A monitoring system that measures carbon stocks and detects ecological pressures but cannot verify that the governance structures responsible for ecosystem stewardship are functioning inclusively and accountably is a system that will fail to protect what it is measuring.

The first component of Layer III is institutional representation monitoring. Each CREMA must maintain a minimum of 40% female representation in its executive leadership — a threshold grounded in the baseline finding that women are the primary users of mangrove resources, particularly in fuelwood-linked fish-smoking value chains, yet are systematically excluded from the formal decision-making structures that govern those resources. The governance readiness assessment documented this exclusion across all three Ramsar landscapes: female respondents in communities including Salo reported that despite their daily dependence on mangrove access, they had no voice in resource management decisions. CREMA governance compositions are reported annually, including changes in leadership structure and evidence of inclusive decision-making, providing the data needed to track whether the 40% threshold is being met and maintained over time.

The second component is transparency and benefit-sharing oversight. Where carbon finance mechanisms are activated, benefit-sharing frameworks must be formally documented and archived within the national MRV system. Monitoring includes documentation of benefit allocation mechanisms, public disclosure of financial flows, records of livelihood support distribution disaggregated by gender and youth participation where feasible, and evidence of community consultations regarding allocation decisions. This is not administratively optional: elite capture — the concentration of carbon revenues within existing power hierarchies — is identified in the governance assessment as a significant risk in all three landscapes if CREMA formation is allowed to reproduce the exclusionary structures of traditional governance rather than correct them.

The third component is the Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM). Each blue carbon landscape is required to maintain a documented grievance submission pathway, designated focal persons for grievance handling, escalation procedures to district and national levels, and records of grievance resolution outcomes. The baseline finding that more than 80% of communities across the three landscapes rely on informal, unstructured grievance systems — oral complaint to the chief or elder, with no documentation, no defined process, and no escalation pathway — defines the gap this component is designed to address. The GRM monitoring indicators — number of grievances received, their nature, resolution rates, and time to resolution — provide the evidence base for assessing whether the governance layer is functioning as a legitimate safeguard or whether communities are experiencing unresolved conflicts that will, if left unaddressed, translate into disengagement from restoration and monitoring activities.

| Layer | Focus | Key Outputs |
|---|---|---|
| Layer I — Ecological and Carbon Integrity | Mangrove extent, restoration performance, carbon stock measurement and re-measurement | Annual extent change maps; restoration site survival reports; PSP carbon stock updates every 3–5 years |
| Layer II — Environmental Pressure Surveillance | Drivers of degradation, early-warning pressure signals, leakage risk management | Quarterly pressure indicator reports; Pressure Alerts and escalation triggers; annual fuel source shift assessments |

## 3.4 Integration — How the Layers Work Together

The three layers are not parallel silos that generate independent streams of data. They are an integrated system in which information produced by each layer informs the interpretation and response within the others. This integration is what makes the MRV architecture coherent rather than merely comprehensive.

The most operationally significant integration is between Layer III and Layers I and II. Governance instability is treated in this system not as a social outcome to be managed separately from ecological outcomes, but as a risk signal for carbon permanence. The fifth indicator of Layer II — the conflict and access pressure index — captures land tenure disputes and resource access conflicts that, when escalating, signal the breakdown of the social conditions under which ecosystem stewardship is possible. A community where land tenure disputes are unresolved is a community where restoration plantings may be cleared, where PSP sites may be disturbed or denied access, where the reporting relationships of the monitoring tier system may be disrupted by factional tensions. The governance assessment documented precisely this dynamic at Songor, where deep social fractures around salt mining rights had created conditions in which conventional CREMA formation — let alone functioning community-based monitoring — could not proceed without prior conflict mediation. In the MRV architecture, a rising conflict and access pressure index at Songor communities such as Pute is cross-referenced against the land conversion monitoring outputs of Layer I, with the expectation that land tenure instability and salt pan expansion will co-locate spatially and temporally.

The integration between Layer II and Layer I operates in both analytical and responsive directions. Environmental pressure data — rising cutting incidence, charcoal hotspot expansion, increasing fuel dependence — is cross-referenced against PSP locations and carbon stock projections, enabling the system to assess whether pressure signals are operating within the spatial proximity of carbon monitoring sites and whether they are likely to produce detectable carbon stock change before the next re-measurement event. This cross-referencing capability means that the re-measurement cycle can be accelerated in sites where pressure signals have reached threshold levels, rather than proceeding mechanically on a fixed 3–5 year schedule regardless of conditions on the ground. Conversely, Layer I ecological outputs feed back into Layer II prioritisation: where satellite analysis detects net mangrove loss or significant fragmentation in a given area, the pressure surveillance system is directed to intensify monitoring in that location to identify the causal driver.

Layer III governance indicators, similarly, inform Layer II pressure interpretation. A deteriorating grievance resolution rate in a community may precede an increase in reported cutting incidence, because communities that perceive governance as unresponsive or inequitable are less likely to enforce the collective norms that discourage extraction. A decline in female representation in CREMA leadership may reduce the effectiveness of fuel source shift monitoring, since women, as the primary fuelwood users, hold observational knowledge of extraction trends that male-dominated governance structures may not capture. These linkages are not abstract; they reflect causal pathways documented in the social-ecological analysis of the three Ramsar landscapes, and they are what distinguishes a genuinely integrated monitoring system from a collection of independently managed indicator sets.

## 3.5 The Digital Platform as the Connective Architecture

The institutional tiers and functional layers described in this chapter generate monitoring data that spans three Ramsar landscapes, four tiers of institutional actors, three distinct analytical domains, and monitoring cycles ranging from quarterly field observation to five-yearly PSP re-measurement. The digital platform described in Chapter 8 is the connective architecture that makes this integrated system operable at national scale.

The web-based spatial monitoring platform, developed and maintained by GAB Climate Smart Investment Ltd for MLNR and MESTI, aggregates data submissions from all four institutional tiers and makes them available through role-differentiated interfaces: national agencies manage data integrity and produce analytical outputs; district authorities review area-specific pressure trends and access CREMA submission records; CREMA members upload field observations and access their monitoring zone maps through smartphone-compatible interfaces. The platform hosts PSP locations, restoration site registrations, annual mangrove extent layers, pressure alert overlays, charcoal hotspot mappings, conflict-linked zone records, and governance composition summaries. It is the single point of integration that allows the Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report to be produced from a coherent, quality-controlled, nationally archived data record rather than from fragmented submissions reconciled manually at reporting time. The platform's design, data architecture, role management structure, and relationship to national reporting obligations are documented in full in Chapter 8.
