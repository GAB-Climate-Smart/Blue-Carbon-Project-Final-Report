# Chapter 1: Introduction

---

## 1.1 The WACA Blue Carbon Assignment

Ghana's engagement with blue carbon science and management is rooted in a formal international commitment. The West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA ResIP 2), financed by the World Bank under project number P175625 and approved in December 2022, was designed in direct response to the accelerating loss of coastal ecological and economic assets across the sub-region. Coastal erosion, recurrent flooding, marine pollution, and the progressive degradation of ecosystems that sustain millions of livelihoods had reached a scale that no single country could address in isolation. WACA ResIP 2 was therefore constructed as a regional platform, with Ghana, the Gambia, and Guinea-Bissau as the three beneficiary countries, each advancing site-specific investments within a shared framework of coastal resilience.

In Ghana, WACA ResIP 2 is led by the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI), which holds the primary mandate for climate and environment policy at the national level. Implementation is supported by the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) through its Focal Point Unit, which provides technical oversight of the assessment workstream; the Wildlife Division of the Forestry Commission, which carries statutory responsibility for wetland and wildlife management; the Ministry of Works and Housing together with the Ghana Hydrological Authority (GHA), responsible for coastal infrastructure; and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), which anchors the regulatory and compliance dimensions of project activities. This multi-ministry structure was not incidental to the design — it reflects the cross-sectoral character of blue carbon governance, in which land tenure, ecosystem management, water resources, and environmental regulation must operate in concert if monitoring and restoration outcomes are to be sustained beyond the project cycle.

The technical assessment work at the heart of this report was awarded to Ecological Nest Ltd under the Consulting Service for the Assessment of Blue Carbon Ecosystem Potentials of Ghana (Mangrove and Tidal Salt Marsh). The consultancy was charged with a specific and consequential objective: to assess and prioritise Ghana's blue carbon ecosystem potential and to produce the baseline data necessary to develop blue carbon projects that meet both compliance and voluntary carbon market standards. That objective is not simply a technical exercise. It places Ghana at the frontier of a global movement in which coastal ecosystem management generates measurable, verifiable carbon outcomes that can attract climate finance, discharge treaty obligations, and sustain the communities that depend on mangroves and salt marshes for their livelihoods. This report is the primary technical instrument through which those outcomes will be tracked, reported, and verified.

## 1.2 Ghana's Policy Context

Ghana's commitment to blue carbon management is not aspirational — it is formally obligated under three distinct international frameworks, each of which creates measurable reporting requirements that this Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system is designed to fulfil.

As a signatory to the Paris Agreement, Ghana submitted a Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) that identifies the land sector as the country's largest source of potential CO₂ removals. While energy-sector emissions dominate Ghana's greenhouse gas profile, it is the land sector — forests, wetlands, and coastal ecosystems — that holds the greatest national capacity to draw carbon out of the atmosphere and retain it at scale. Blue carbon ecosystems occupy a disproportionately large role within this potential: mangroves and tidal salt marshes store up to ten times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial forests, owing to the exceptional accumulation rates of below-ground organic matter in permanently waterlogged soils and the sustained absence of decomposition that oxygen deprivation creates. The translation of this ecological fact into NDC-aligned policy reporting is a direct purpose of the MRV framework this report describes.

Ghana's obligations under the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF), adopted in December 2022, add a second layer of formal accountability. The GBF's 30×30 target — requiring thirty percent of the world's land and ocean to be effectively protected by 2030 — places Ghana's three Ramsar-listed coastal wetland complexes at the centre of its biodiversity reporting commitments. The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, the Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, and the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site are not peripheral policy objects; they are designated sites at which the Ghanaian government has made formal international commitments to sustain ecological integrity. Any carbon project or monitoring system operating within or adjacent to these landscapes must demonstrate, not merely assert, compliance with Ramsar Convention obligations.

The third frame of obligation is the Ramsar Convention itself, which requires contracting parties to maintain the ecological character of listed wetlands and to report on threats and changes. All three of Ghana's coastal Ramsar sites fall within the assessment landscape covered by this consultancy. That these sites are simultaneously subject to intense and growing anthropogenic pressure — from fuelwood harvesting at Keta, salt pan expansion at Songor, and agricultural encroachment at Muni-Pomadze — makes the MRV system not an optional addition to conservation planning, but the primary instrument through which Ghana demonstrates that it is meeting its Ramsar commitments with evidence rather than assertion. The policy imperative, taken together across these three frameworks, creates an obligation structure that this MRV system was specifically built to serve.

## 1.3 The Seven-Phase Work Programme

The consultancy was structured around seven sequential phases, each producing a discrete output that built the analytical foundation for the next. This structure was not bureaucratic formality; it reflects the genuine logical dependencies of rigorous blue carbon assessment, in which field measurement cannot precede ecological characterisation, and monitoring protocol design cannot precede the inventory whose outputs it will track.

Phase 1, Project Inception, established the governance and operational foundations of the assignment. Through the inception meeting convened jointly by the MESTI Project Implementation Unit and the MLNR Focal Point Unit, the consultancy confirmed methodology, agreed timelines, and documented decisions that would govern the entire subsequent programme. The inception report produced in this phase set the framework within which all later outputs would be validated.

Phase 2, the Literature Review, provided the secondary baseline on which all field assessment would be calibrated. The review documented the history of blue carbon ecosystem degradation in Ghana — including rates of mangrove loss, drivers of ecosystem change, and the carbon science underpinning mangrove and salt marsh valuation — giving the assessment team the contextual foundation necessary to design field protocols appropriate to Ghana's specific coastal conditions.

Phase 3, Generation of a National Blue Carbon Ecosystem Cover Map, moved from secondary evidence to primary spatial data. Using harmonised Sentinel-2 satellite imagery at 10–20 metre resolution, processed through a Random Forest classification algorithm on Google Earth Engine (GEE) and validated with 319 participatory ground-truth reference points, the consultancy produced the most rigorous mangrove extent baseline Ghana has yet assembled, covering the national coastline and quantifying current ecosystem extent at each priority landscape.

Phase 4, the Blue Carbon Inventory, translated the spatial mapping into carbon accounting. Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs) established at priority sites enabled the measurement of above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (BGB), deadwood, and soil organic carbon (SOC) to one hundred centimetres depth, following the methodology of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Wetlands Supplement. This inventory provides the baseline carbon stocks against which all future change will be measured.

Phase 5, Development of a Monitoring Protocol, built directly on the inventory results to specify what would be measured, how frequently, by whom, and using what equipment at each ecosystem type. The protocol bridges the technical measurement framework of the inventory with the institutional architecture of the MRV system, specifying the roles of community monitors, district Wildlife Division officers, and national technical teams in sustaining the data collection that carbon market and policy reporting require.

Phase 6, the Strategy for Mangrove and Salt Marsh Restoration, identified priority areas for ecological recovery, assessed restoration feasibility and methods, and recommended the institutional arrangements necessary to govern restoration programmes at scale. Grounded in the drivers of degradation documented in earlier phases, the strategy connects ecological diagnosis to practical recovery action.

Phase 7, Assignment Completion and Final Report, consolidates findings across all phases into the integrated deliverable package required by MESTI and MLNR. The Final Report establishes the spatial prioritisation of blue carbon ecosystems for protection, restoration, and monitoring; provides the policy and management advice required under the Terms of Reference; and creates the evidence base on which follow-on carbon project development and long-term monitoring can be built.

## 1.4 Purpose of This Standalone MRV Report

This Standalone MRV Report serves three distinct and non-overlapping purposes, each of which shapes its design and content.

The first is government delivery. MESTI and MLNR require a standalone operational reference that Ghana's environmental institutions can use to implement, manage, and oversee the national MRV system independently of the consultancy that designed it. The report is written accordingly — in full, authored prose, with complete institutional guidance, indicator specifications, and procedural detail — so that it can function as a governing document for the national MRV system without reference to any external technical file. It is addressed to the government institutions that will own and operate the system, and it is written to be used, not merely consulted.

The second purpose is to serve as source material for the Final Report. The Final Report synthesises findings across all six baseline workstreams — geospatial mapping, carbon inventory, ecosystem health, environmental pressures, governance readiness, and socio-economic profiling — into a single integrated deliverable. The MRV Standalone Report provides the monitoring and verification architecture chapter of that synthesis: a technically complete account of how Ghana will track carbon outcomes, detect ecosystem change, maintain governance integrity, and report to national and international audiences. Where the Final Report summarises the MRV architecture, this report provides the full technical basis.

The third purpose is to function as a technical reference for carbon market engagement and third-party verification. Whether Ghana's blue carbon assets are advanced through the voluntary carbon market under standards such as Verra's Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) or Plan Vivo, or through compliance mechanisms aligned with Article 6 of the Paris Agreement, independent verifiers will need to assess whether the monitoring architecture is methodologically sound, institutionally grounded, and operationally realistic. This report is designed to satisfy that scrutiny. It provides the methodological justifications, institutional arrangements, data flow specifications, and quality assurance protocols that verification bodies require, in a format that carbon market reviewers can assess directly.

These three purposes create a document that is simultaneously operational guidance, synthesis input, and technical evidence. The MRV Standalone Report is not a planning framework or a concept note. It describes a system that has been designed, partially deployed, and grounded in the specific ecological and institutional realities of Ghana's coastal landscape.

## 1.5 Geographic Scope

The assessment covers a national coastal landscape of 1,068,361.48 hectares, encompassing 35 coastal districts along Ghana's 550-kilometre coastline from the Ghanaian-Ivorian border in the west to the Ghanaian-Togolese border in the east. This is not a sample or a selected sub-region; it is the full extent of Ghana's coastal zone, assessed at the national level for mangrove and tidal salt marsh distribution, and examined in site-specific depth at the three internationally designated Ramsar wetland complexes that contain the country's ecologically significant blue carbon resources.

The three Ramsar sites anchor the assessment landscape. The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, in the Volta Region, holds 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves — approximately 69 percent of the national total — and represents Ghana's principal blue carbon asset. Its ecological integrity is threatened by a combination of fuelwood harvesting driven by fish-smoking demand in communities including Dzita, Salo, Agbatsivi, Bomigo, Galotse, and Sota; by the progressive sediment starvation of the Volta delta caused by the trapping of more than 90 percent of the Volta River's sediment load upstream at the Akosombo Dam following its construction in 1964; and by a historical deforestation rate of 2.15 percent per year that has compressed the mangrove estate dramatically over recent decades. The Keta Lagoon Complex is the primary pilot implementation site for the national MRV system, and the full depth of the monitoring infrastructure described in this report is deployed there first.

The Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, located in the Ada East District, holds only 465.76 hectares of mangroves — approximately 2 percent of the national total — distributed as fragmented patches across a landscape dominated by industrial and artisanal salt production centred on Elavanyo, Obane, Totope, and Azizanya. Songor is a landscape of active conflict between competing land uses, where salt pan expansion has progressively displaced mangrove and salt marsh habitat and where Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) formation remains contested. It is a priority for restoration planning and governance development.

The Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site, in the Central Region, carries ecologically insignificant mangrove cover and is characterised not as a carbon stock conservation site but as a restoration and ecosystem re-establishment landscape. The community of Sankor, which has demonstrated strong interest in CREMA formation and exhibits a high degree of social cohesion around natural resource management, represents the primary community-level entry point for restoration programming at Muni-Pomadze.

The geographic scope of this MRV report operates at two levels simultaneously. At the national framework level, it provides the monitoring architecture, institutional arrangements, reporting cycle, and carbon accounting protocol that apply to all blue carbon ecosystems along Ghana's coastline. At the pilot implementation level, it describes in operational detail the specific monitoring infrastructure, station network, and field protocols deployed at Keta — the site where the deepest layered baseline, the most complete PSP network, and the most advanced governance structures make full MRV system deployment possible within the current project cycle.

## 1.6 Relationship to the Wider Baseline Package

The MRV Framework described in this report was designed to draw on, and give operational coherence to, six distinct baseline workstreams that were developed in parallel across the consultancy programme. Each workstream generated a standalone technical output; the MRV Framework is the instrument through which those outputs are operationalised as a functioning monitoring system.

The Geospatial Mangrove Extent Mapping workstream produced the spatial baseline — a land cover map of Ghana's mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems, validated with 319 ground-truth reference points, that establishes the area boundaries against which all future change in ecosystem extent will be detected. The MRV Framework uses this spatial baseline as the reference layer for annual mangrove extent monitoring via Sentinel-2 satellite imagery processed on GEE, triggering field verification when the automated change detection identifies area loss or degradation above defined thresholds.

The Carbon Inventory workstream established the carbon stock baseline — the measured quantities of above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, deadwood, and soil organic carbon at each priority site — from which emission reductions and carbon sequestration increments will be calculated. The MRV Framework specifies the PSP re-measurement cycle, the carbon accounting methodology, and the data quality protocols that maintain the scientific credibility of stock change estimates over time.

The Ecosystem Health Assessment workstream provided site-level diagnostic data on vegetation condition, species composition, water quality, and structural integrity at Keta and Ada. These findings inform the ecological indicators tracked within Layer I of the MRV system — the Ecological and Carbon Integrity Layer — and provide the ecological context that distinguishes between carbon changes attributable to project activities and those driven by background environmental change.

The Environmental Pressure and Community Dependency Assessment documented the human drivers of ecosystem degradation — fuelwood harvesting, salt pan expansion, coastal erosion, agricultural encroachment, pollution — and quantified the degree to which communities at each site depend on mangrove and salt marsh resources for their economic survival. This workstream feeds directly into Layer II of the MRV system, the Environmental Pressure Surveillance Layer, which monitors pressure indicators at each site and triggers escalation responses when pressure signals exceed defined thresholds.

The Governance Readiness Evaluation assessed the institutional capacity of existing community governance structures — CREMAs, traditional authority systems, and district management institutions — to participate meaningfully in monitoring, benefit-sharing, and grievance management. Its findings determine the calibration of Layer III, the Governance and Safeguard Assurance Layer, which tracks the health of governance institutions over time and flags risks of elite capture, exclusion, and benefit-sharing failure before they undermine the integrity of the carbon programme.

The Socio-Economic Profiling workstream characterised the livelihood systems, demographic profiles, and economic dependencies of the 37 communities assessed across the three Ramsar landscapes, establishing the social baseline against which changes in community welfare — for better or worse — attributable to the blue carbon programme will be tracked. More than 70 percent of households in the assessed communities depend on mangrove-linked activities for their primary livelihoods, a dependency that makes the social integrity of the MRV system not a secondary governance concern but a condition of the programme's operational sustainability.

The MRV Framework does not summarise these workstreams; it consumes them. Each layer of the monitoring architecture is populated with indicators, thresholds, and escalation protocols that are grounded specifically in what those six baselines revealed about Ghana's coastal landscape.

## 1.7 Structure of This Report

The twelve chapters that follow are organised to move the reader from strategic context and system architecture through to operational implementation and maturity assessment, building sequentially on each layer of the MRV system before describing how those layers function in practice at the Keta pilot site.

Chapter 2, Strategic Context and National Purpose, grounds the MRV system in Ghana's policy and treaty environment, explaining precisely how the monitoring architecture serves Ghana's NDC commitments, Ramsar Convention obligations, CBD/GBF reporting requirements, and the national blue economy agenda. It establishes why MRV is not a technical add-on to restoration planning but the mechanism through which the policy commitments made at international level translate into verifiable domestic action.

Chapter 3, National MRV Architecture, describes the full structure of the MRV system — its four institutional tiers (community, district, national technical, and independent review) and its three functional layers (Ecological and Carbon Integrity, Environmental Pressure Surveillance, and Governance and Safeguard Assurance) — explaining the logic behind each design decision and how the tiers and layers interact to create a system that is simultaneously sensitive to ecosystem change and resilient to institutional disruption.

Chapter 4, Ecological and Carbon Integrity Layer, provides the detailed specification of Layer I — the monitoring of carbon stocks, ecosystem extent, and restoration performance that forms the scientific core of the MRV system. It describes the PSP network, carbon pool measurement protocols, remote sensing cadence, and the methodological alignment with the IPCC Wetlands Supplement that qualifies the system for both national reporting and carbon market verification.

Chapter 5, Environmental Pressure Surveillance, describes Layer II — the monitoring of the human drivers of ecosystem degradation that the baseline documented in granular site-specific detail. It explains how surveillance of fuelwood harvesting pressure at Keta, salt pan dynamics at Songor, and agricultural encroachment at Muni-Pomadze is operationalised as a set of leading indicators that the system tracks ahead of carbon stock change, enabling management response before irreversible degradation occurs.

Chapter 6, Governance and Safeguard Assurance, describes Layer III — the monitoring of the institutional health of CREMAs, the implementation of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) requirements, the functioning of Grievance Redress Mechanisms (GRMs), gender inclusion standards, and benefit-sharing transparency. It explains how the system tracks governance integrity as a precondition for the social and environmental legitimacy of carbon credits.

Chapter 7, Wetland Ecosystem Monitoring (Keta and Ada), describes the physical monitoring station network — 57 stations at Keta and 23 at Ada — including the water and soil parameter suites measured at quarterly intervals, the operational protocols for field teams, and how the station network integrates with the three MRV functional layers.

Chapter 8, Digital Platform and Data Architecture, describes the web-based platform developed to aggregate MRV data, support institutional reporting, and provide accessible dashboards for MESTI, MLNR, the Forestry Commission, and development partners. It covers the current state of the platform, the hosting and satellite monitoring arrangements, and the data governance protocols that protect the integrity of data flows from community monitors through to national reporting.

Chapter 9, Carbon Accounting Protocol, provides the full methodological specification for calculating carbon stock changes, attributing emission reductions, managing permanence and leakage risks, and producing credit-ready accounting outputs aligned with the requirements of the Verra Verified Carbon Standard, Plan Vivo, and Article 6 compliance mechanisms.

Chapter 10, Reporting Framework, describes the full reporting cycle — quarterly field data reporting, annual National Blue Carbon MRV Reports, five-year comprehensive reviews, and the linkage between internal national reporting and the external reporting obligations to the World Bank, carbon market verifiers, and international convention bodies.

Chapter 11, Keta Pilot Implementation, describes in operational detail what the MRV system looks like in practice at Ghana's principal blue carbon site — the 57-station monitoring network, the PSP locations including Salo, the early action hub at Dzita, the CREMA formation status, and the specific landscape risks from Akosombo Dam sediment starvation and fuelwood pressure that make Keta both the most important monitoring priority and the most demanding stress test for the MRV design.

Chapter 12, Implementation Maturity Assessment, provides an honest, evidence-grounded account of where the MRV system currently stands — what has been built, what is partially operational, what remains to be resourced and commissioned — and what institutional and financial conditions must be met to move from a well-designed framework to a fully functioning national monitoring system. The chapter distinguishes between the completeness of the MRV framework design and the progress of MRV system operationalisation, giving MESTI and MLNR a clear map of the investments required to cross that threshold.

The Annexes provide the technical reference material that supports but does not appear in the main chapters: detailed indicator tables, data dictionaries, field protocol summaries, and the spatial data that anchors the monitoring network to specific coordinates within the assessed landscape.
