# Executive Summary

---

Ghana's 2025 national blue carbon baseline has produced the most accurate account of the country's coastal mangrove resource ever assembled, and its central finding is both an opportunity and an obligation. The assessment, conducted by Ecological Nest Ltd under the West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA ResIP 2, World Bank Project P175625, approved December 2022), mapped a national coastal mangrove extent of 30,025 hectares using harmonised Sentinel-2 satellite imagery at 10–20 metre resolution, classified through a Random Forest algorithm on Google Earth Engine (GEE) and validated against 319 participatory ground-truth reference points. This figure — 2.81% of the total 1,068,361.48-hectare coastal landscape across 35 districts and 550 kilometres of coastline — is substantially larger than any previous national estimate, which ranged from 7,240 hectares in 2013 to 18,100 hectares in 1980. The discrepancy is methodological, not ecological: the fringing stands and small fragmented patches that earlier Landsat-based analyses missed at 30-metre resolution are real forests, holding real carbon, and generating real climate obligations. Ghana now has a number it can defend.

The strategic significance of that number is sharpened dramatically by its spatial distribution. The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, in the Volta Region, holds 20,717.95 hectares — approximately 69% of the national mangrove total. No equivalent landscape exists elsewhere on the Ghanaian coast. The Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, in the Ada East District, retains only 465.76 hectares of mangrove, distributed as fragmented patches across a landscape where salt pan expansion has progressively replaced wetland vegetation over decades. The Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site, in the Central Region, carries mangrove cover so ecologically insignificant that it is characterised not as a conservation site but as a restoration landscape. This distribution means that the fate of Ghana's blue carbon strategy is, in functional terms, the fate of the Keta Lagoon Complex. Blue carbon ecosystems store up to ten times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial forests, owing to the accumulation of organic matter in permanently anaerobic soils that resist decomposition at rates terrestrial systems cannot approach. Applied to the Keta estate, this relationship concentrates an outsized share of Ghana's national climate potential in a single landscape whose integrity must be protected as a matter of national strategic priority, not merely local conservation interest.

That landscape is under serious, multi-front pressure. At Keta, the fish-smoking industry — an industry dominated by women in communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, Salo, and Dzita, and built on the processing and sale of fish harvested from the very lagoon the mangroves sustain — drives intensive demand for *Rhizophora racemosa* fuelwood, preferred by kilns across the lagoon fringe for its heat output and the distinctive flavour it imparts to smoked fish. This creates what the baseline characterises as a social-ecological trap: the livelihoods that the ecosystem makes possible depend on consuming the ecosystem that supports them, progressively degrading the nursery habitat that sustains fish stocks and the mangrove canopy that supplies the fuel to process them. Approximately 70% of the natural mangrove cover around the Keta Lagoon has already been degraded or fragmented, with the most severe losses near settlements like Bomigo, Galo, and Sota. Superimposed on this livelihood-driven pressure is a structural geophysical risk that no community governance mechanism can reverse: the Akosombo Dam, constructed in 1964 sixty kilometres upstream on the Volta River, traps more than 90% of the river's natural sediment load, starving the narrow coastal sandspit that separates the Keta Lagoon from the Atlantic Ocean of the material it needs to hold its position. The delta has shifted from a prograding system to an eroding one, exposing the mangrove estate to increasing wave energy and saltwater stress as the barrier thins. The measured consequence is a historical deforestation rate of 2.15% per year at Keta — a trajectory that, if uninterrupted, represents the loss of approximately 445 hectares of mangrove annually from Ghana's most important carbon store.

At Songor, the degradation profile is categorically different and, in important respects, further advanced. The 465.76 hectares of mangrove that survive at Songor are the remnant of a landscape largely converted to salt pan infrastructure by industrial and artisanal mining operations concentrated around Elavanyo and Obane. Communities at Totope and Azizanya have experienced direct coastal erosion as mangrove buffering has been removed; field teams documented residential displacement at Totope attributed to shoreline retreat that mangrove loss has accelerated. The land tenure disputes between private salt companies, the Ada Traditional Council, and traditional fishing communities create a governance environment so fractured that CREMA — Community Resource Management Area — formation at the focal community of Pute requires conflict mediation as a prerequisite, not a parallel process. At Muni-Pomadze, where charcoal production and agricultural encroachment at communities including Bewadze have reduced mangrove to ecological insignificance, the contribution to Ghana's carbon programme will come entirely from future restoration at the early action hub of Sankor, a community whose exceptionally high social cohesion and strong women's groups represent the most promising governance starting conditions of the three sites.

Ghana's National Blue Carbon Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) Framework was designed with full knowledge of these pressures, not in ignorance of them. The system operates across four institutional tiers — Community (CREMAs and Community Change Agents, or CCAs), District (Metropolitan, Municipal, and District Assemblies and the Wildlife Division of the Forestry Commission), National Technical (the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources — MLNR — and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation — MESTI), and Independent Review — and three functional layers: Layer I, Ecological and Carbon Integrity, which tracks mangrove extent, carbon stocks at Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs), and restoration performance; Layer II, Environmental Pressure Surveillance, which detects degradation drivers — fuelwood harvesting pressure, salt pan expansion, charcoal production, land tenure conflict — before they produce irreversible carbon loss; and Layer III, Governance and Safeguard Assurance, which monitors the institutional health of CREMAs, the implementation of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) requirements, the functioning of Grievance Redress Mechanisms (GRMs), and benefit-sharing transparency. These layers are integrated rather than parallel: governance instability as measured in Layer III is treated as a leading indicator of carbon permanence risk; pressure signals in Layer II are cross-referenced against PSP locations to determine whether field re-measurement should be accelerated; and ecological deterioration detected in Layer I is traced back through the pressure and governance layers to identify its institutional cause. The framework was designed for institutional permanence beyond the WACA programme cycle, with MLNR and MESTI as its national custodians and CREMAs as its community-level operational backbone, because Ghana's obligation to monitor its coastal blue carbon resource does not expire with a project disbursement period.

The 2025 baseline assessment phase has established Ghana's Year 0 MRV foundation with a depth that places the country at the frontier of national blue carbon programme development on the African continent. The geospatial baseline is complete, covering 30,025 hectares with the methodological rigour that the Verified Carbon Standard (Verra/VCS), Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo standards require as a precondition for project registration. Carbon inventories have been conducted and the Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1 in the Keta Lagoon Complex has been established, providing the biomass and soil organic carbon anchor against which all future stock changes will be measured on a three-to-five year re-measurement cycle. The 57-station wetland monitoring network at Keta and the 23-station network at Ada are operational, providing quarterly water quality and soil chemistry data across a parameter suite — pH, salinity, dissolved oxygen, conductivity, humus content, and six additional indicators — that gives Ghana's principal blue carbon site a real-time ecological surveillance capacity unique in the national programme. The digital platform, developed by GAB Climate Smart Investment Ltd on a Next.js/Supabase architecture at `blue-carbon-mrv-green.vercel.app`, provides the connective infrastructure through which monitoring data from all four institutional tiers flows to national reporting. The three early action hubs — Dzita in the Keta Lagoon Complex, Pute in the Songor Ramsar Site, and Sankor in the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site — have been selected, their social readiness validated, and CCAs trained and field-deployed during the December 2025 baseline assessment. CREMA formation is in active progress across all three sites, most advanced at Sankor where community cohesion is highest, in the process of boundary demarcation at the Dzita–Agbatsivi cluster at Keta, and dependent on prior conflict mediation at Pute.

What remains is the transition from a completed baseline to a routinely operating monitoring system. The most consequential enabling action is the finalisation and Parliamentary passage of the CREMA Legislative Instrument, whose absence creates legal uncertainty that blocks CREMA gazettement and bylaw enforcement at every site. The priorities for the next twelve months are clear: completing formal CREMA constitution and registration at all three sites, with minimum 40% female executive leadership as the governance standard that the Anloga district's 46.2% female-headed household rate makes empirically necessary rather than aspirational; operationalising the quarterly CCA monitoring cycle at Dzita, Pute, and Sankor as a standing community practice rather than an assessment-period activity; completing the digital platform build to support field data submission, district validation, and national archive access; completing EOSDA satellite monitoring configuration for continuous mangrove change detection; and producing Ghana's first Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report. When these steps are taken, Ghana will possess one of the most operationally mature national blue carbon MRV systems on the African continent — one grounded in a methodologically defensible baseline, anchored in national institutional authority, and capable of generating the credible, verifiable carbon accounting that both compliance reporting under the Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) and voluntary carbon market participation require.
