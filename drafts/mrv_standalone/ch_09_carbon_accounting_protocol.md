# Chapter 9: Carbon Accounting Protocol

---

## 9.1 Carbon Market Alignment

Ghana's blue carbon assessment has been designed from inception to meet the foundational requirements of international voluntary and compliance carbon standards: the Verified Carbon Standard (Verra/VCS), the Gold Standard, and the Plan Vivo Standard. The assessment is not a general ecological survey retrofitted for carbon market purposes. From the framing of its baseline methodology through to the design of its Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system, every technical decision has been taken with the requirements of these standards in view — including the selection of measurement depth for soil organic carbon, the structure of the Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) network, the use of participatory ground-truth validation, and the design of the governance and safeguard architecture that carbon standards increasingly require as a condition of certification.

This chapter documents the carbon accounting protocol that enables Ghana to participate in these markets and to report credibly against its Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitments under the Paris Agreement. The protocol is anchored in the 2013 Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Wetlands Supplement — the internationally recognised accounting methodology for coastal and tidal wetland carbon — and draws on the specific guidance within that supplement for mangrove ecosystems, including pool definitions, allometric approaches, and soil carbon sampling standards. Consistency with IPCC Wetlands Supplement methodology is not optional for market participation under Verra or Gold Standard; it is a prerequisite. Ghana's adoption of this framework from the outset of the assessment places the baseline data in a form that independent verifiers can assess without methodological conversion or reinterpretation.

The carbon accounting protocol described in this chapter is built on the ecological and monitoring foundations described in Chapter 4 and Chapter 7. Chapter 4 establishes how carbon stocks are measured, by whom, and at what intervals through the Ecological and Carbon Integrity Layer. Chapter 7 establishes the wider wetland monitoring network at Keta and Ada. This chapter addresses the accounting dimension: how measurements are translated into documented carbon stocks and changes, how additionality and permanence are established, how uncertainty is managed conservatively, and how the credit issuance pathway from field data to verified registry submission functions. Taken together, these three chapters constitute a carbon accounting and monitoring package that a Verra, Gold Standard, or Plan Vivo project developer can use as the credible national reference framework the Terms of Reference mandate the assessment to provide.

The strategic context for carbon market participation is unambiguous. Blue carbon ecosystems store up to ten times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial forests. Ghana's 30,025 hectares of coastal mangroves — mapped at 10 to 20 metre resolution using Sentinel-2 imagery and a Random Forest algorithm on Google Earth Engine (GEE), validated against 319 participatory ground-truth reference points — represent a national carbon asset of climate significance whose full magnitude was previously unmeasured. The Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI) now hold, for the first time, a carbon inventory that is methodologically defensible before an international panel of standard reviewers. The accounting protocol in this chapter ensures that this inventory is maintained, updated, and reported in a form that translates its ecological significance into durable, verifiable climate finance.

## 9.2 The Four Carbon Pools

The protocol accounts for carbon across four pools, consistent with IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance and with the pool definitions applied in the 2025 national baseline inventory.

Above-ground biomass (AGB) covers all live tree and shrub biomass above the soil surface — stems, branches, leaves, and aerial root structures. In the mangrove stands of the Keta Lagoon Complex and Songor Ramsar Site, AGB is calculated from species-specific allometric equations applied to Diameter at Breast Height (DBH) and height measurements collected at each PSP. The choice of allometric equation is methodologically significant: equations derived from one mangrove species or region can produce substantially different biomass estimates when applied to another, and the use of species-appropriate equations for *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* — the two dominant species across Ghana's coastal mangroves — is a minimum requirement for methodological credibility under Verra's methodology for Tidal Wetland and Seagrass Restoration (VM0033) and the IPCC Wetlands Supplement.

Below-ground biomass (BGB) captures the living root and rhizome systems that mangrove trees invest significantly in. For *Rhizophora racemosa*, the extensive prop root architecture is both a carbon pool of independent significance and the structural mechanism by which the tree anchors itself — and the surrounding sediment — against tidal movement. BGB is estimated using root-to-shoot ratios drawn from published literature for these species in comparable West African coastal environments. The published ratios provide a methodologically accepted approach where direct below-ground measurement is logistically impractical across a national inventory, and their application is consistent with IPCC Tier 1 and Tier 2 approaches depending on the available species-specific data.

Deadwood — standing dead trees and fallen coarse woody debris — is included in the accounting protocol where ecologically relevant. In undisturbed primary mangrove stands, deadwood is typically a minor pool relative to living biomass. In the degraded and partially harvested stands that characterise significant portions of the Keta Lagoon fringe, however, deadwood can represent a non-negligible proportion of standing carbon stock, particularly where cutting events have left stem bases and root structures in situ. Excluding deadwood from degraded areas without measurement would systematically underestimate total carbon stocks in precisely those zones most subject to pressure — a bias that would also affect the calculation of carbon loss when these areas are fully cleared. The protocol therefore includes deadwood assessment at PSP locations where field teams document visible dead stems and fallen woody material.

Soil organic carbon (SOC) is measured from the surface to 100 centimetres depth using bulk density cores and laboratory analysis of total organic carbon concentration. SOC is the dominant carbon pool in mangrove ecosystems globally, and it is the pool that defines the climate significance of mangrove conservation in Ghana. The explanation for this dominance lies in the hydrology of tidal wetland soils. Beneath the waterlogged surface of a mangrove forest, oxygen is effectively absent. The aerobic decomposition pathways that in terrestrial soils oxidise organic matter and release it as carbon dioxide cannot function in anaerobic conditions. The result is a process of long-term organic accumulation: leaf litter, dead root material, and sediment-associated carbon inputs are layered and compressed over decades and centuries into dense, peaty, carbon-rich profiles that extend many metres below the surface. The uppermost 100 centimetres of these profiles — the measurement depth specified by the IPCC Wetlands Supplement — contain carbon stocks that often exceed the entire above-ground biomass of the same stand by an order of magnitude.

This makes SOC not only the most important pool to measure accurately but the most catastrophically vulnerable to loss when mangroves are degraded or cleared. When a mangrove forest is drained for salt pan conversion — the process documented at Elavanyo and Obane in the Songor Ramsar Site, where the 465.76 hectares of remaining mangroves represent what survives after decades of salt industry expansion — oxygen enters the soil profile for the first time in generations. Aerobic decomposition resumes, and the carbon accumulated over centuries oxidises rapidly, producing a pulse of carbon dioxide emissions that can continue for decades. A hectare of mangrove cleared for salt pan does not simply stop sequestering carbon; it becomes an active emitter, releasing the banked carbon of its deep soil profile over a sustained period. This is why mangrove conservation, framed correctly, represents avoided emissions far larger than its modest above-ground stock would suggest — and why the SOC measurement protocol, conducted to 100 cm depth with bulk density determination and laboratory carbon concentration analysis, is not a methodological refinement but a fundamental requirement for honest accounting. An assessment that measured only above-ground biomass in Ghana's coastal mangroves would undercount the climate significance of these ecosystems by an order of magnitude, and would produce carbon credit estimates that misrepresent the true value of the interventions they are meant to measure.

The 2025 soil measurements at the Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1 in the Keta Lagoon Complex confirm the characteristic pattern expected of intact mangrove peat soils: high total organic carbon (TOC) concentrations near the surface, declining through a profile that reflects the low bulk density and high porosity of undisturbed mangrove peat. These field measurements, now archived in the national MRV database, establish the Year 0 SOC reference point against which all future re-measurement at this PSP will be compared.

| Carbon Pool | Measurement Method | Primary Reference | Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Above-Ground Biomass (AGB) | DBH and height measurements; species-specific allometric equations | IPCC Wetlands Supplement; published allometrics for *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* | Captures harvestable biomass; reflects cutting pressure effects |
| Below-Ground Biomass (BGB) | Root-to-shoot ratios from published literature | IPCC Wetlands Supplement Tier 1/2 | Structural stability pool; significant for *Rhizophora* prop root systems |
| Deadwood | Field measurement of standing dead and fallen woody debris | IPCC Wetlands Supplement | Non-negligible in degraded stands at Keta fringe |
| Soil Organic Carbon (SOC) | Bulk density cores and TOC laboratory analysis to 100 cm depth | IPCC Wetlands Supplement (100 cm minimum depth) | Dominant pool; most vulnerable to drainage and conversion |

The combination of these four pools, measured and accounted for within a single nationally managed system, gives Ghana a carbon stock profile that is complete in the sense that IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance and international carbon standards require. Omitting any pool without explicit and documented justification would constitute a methodological weakness that verification teams would identify.

## 9.3 Baseline Carbon Stocks

The 2025 national baseline assessment establishes the Year 0 carbon inventory against which all future stock measurements, credit calculations, and emissions reporting will be benchmarked. The primary field location for the carbon inventory is the Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1 in the Keta Lagoon Complex, established as a georeferenced, permanently marked measurement point within the mangrove stands of the Keta fringe.

The soil profile data from the Salo PSP 1 confirms the characteristic biogeochemical signature of intact West African mangrove peat: high total organic carbon concentrations in the uppermost soil horizons, reflecting recent litter inputs and active root deposition, declining with depth through a profile of low bulk density and high organic matter content. The low bulk density is itself diagnostic — compacted mineral soils have bulk densities typically exceeding 1.0 g cm⁻³, while intact mangrove peat profiles such as those at Salo measure substantially lower, reflecting the high proportion of incompletely decomposed organic material. This characteristic makes accurate bulk density determination essential to any reliable carbon stock calculation: a failure to account for the specific physical properties of mangrove peat produces systematic overestimates or underestimates depending on whether terrestrial soil density assumptions are applied.

The above-ground biomass data from the Salo PSP 1 reflects a stand under historical harvesting pressure. Trees measured during the 2025 inventory are generally shorter and have smaller trunk diameters than primary forest benchmarks for these species in undisturbed West African coastal environments. This suppressed stature is the direct physical record of the "death by a thousand cuts" dynamic that has characterised the Keta Lagoon fringe for decades — incremental fuelwood harvesting events preventing canopy maturation, particularly the selective cutting of *Rhizophora racemosa*, which is preferred by the fish-smoking operators of communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo for the heat output and the characteristic flavour it imparts to smoked fish. The carbon accounting implications of this harvesting history are significant: current AGB stock estimates at Salo are lower than they would be in an unharvested stand, meaning that the additionality argument for conservation at Keta is also, in part, an argument for the recovery of suppressed biomass stocks — stocks that will grow as harvesting pressure is reduced through Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) governance and livelihood diversification.

The full quantitative carbon stock inventory — pool-specific stock estimates in tonnes of carbon per hectare, stratified by species zone and canopy density class — is archived in the national MRV database under the 2025 baseline records for Salo PSP 1 and constitutes the operational data record to which future re-measurement events will be compared. These archived measurements are the authoritative baseline for carbon stock accounting under this protocol.

The national significance of Keta's carbon stocks is best understood through the combination of site extent and ecosystem type. At 20,717.95 hectares — approximately 69% of Ghana's total coastal mangrove resource — the Keta Lagoon Complex holds the vast majority of the country's blue carbon asset. Blue carbon ecosystems store up to ten times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial forests. Applied to the Keta extent, this relationship means that the carbon held in the soils and biomass of the Keta Lagoon Complex is disproportionately large relative to the area it occupies. Any significant loss of mangrove cover at Keta — through the continuation of the 2.15% per year historical deforestation trajectory, through accelerated coastal erosion driven by sediment starvation following the construction of the Akosombo Dam in 1964, or through saltwater intrusion events — does not simply reduce a local ecosystem; it reduces a nationally significant carbon stock that contributes directly to Ghana's ability to meet its NDC commitments and to credibly participate in international carbon markets.

The Songor Ramsar Site, with 465.76 hectares of mangrove distributed as small fragmented patches across a landscape dominated by salt pans and grassland, holds a materially different baseline carbon position. The extent fragmentation at Songor, driven by salt pan expansion at Elavanyo, Obane, and surrounding areas, reflects not just ecological degradation but decades of active carbon stock loss — the cumulative result of converting mangrove land to salt production, a conversion that, as discussed in Section 9.2, produces sustained SOC emissions long after the clearance event itself. The 465.76 hectares that remain are therefore both ecologically and climatically significant as a conservation priority and as the remnant anchor for any future restoration-based carbon accounting in the Songor landscape.

At Muni-Pomadze, mangrove cover is ecologically insignificant at this baseline. The site's strategic role in the carbon programme is therefore restoration-based — future carbon stock generation from re-established mangroves at Sankor and surrounding areas — rather than conservation of existing stocks. Carbon credits from Muni-Pomadze, when generated, will be classified as carbon removals from restoration activity rather than avoided emissions from conservation.

## 9.4 Additionality

Carbon market standards require that any credits issued to a project must represent real, measurable climate benefits that would not have occurred in the absence of project activities. This requirement — additionality — is the foundational test that separates carbon finance from greenwash, and it is a test that Ghana's blue carbon programme is specifically designed to meet.

Additionality is established by defining the baseline scenario: the trajectory of carbon stocks that would be expected if no intervention took place. For the Keta Lagoon Complex, the baseline scenario is defined by the historical deforestation rate of 2.15% per year, derived from the multi-decadal land cover change analysis conducted as part of the 2025 national baseline. This rate is not an estimate drawn from regional averages or proxy datasets; it is calculated from the specific land cover change history of the Keta Lagoon landscape, making it a site-specific reference rate that satisfies the specificity requirements of Verra VM0033 and equivalent standard methodologies for avoiding unplanned deforestation and degradation.

The drivers behind the 2.15% rate are well-documented in the baseline. The dominant pressure is the demand for fuelwood by fish-smoking operators — predominantly women — in communities across the Keta fringe including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo. *Rhizophora racemosa* is the preferred species for fish-smoking kilns: its wood burns hot, and the smoke imparts a distinctive colour and flavour to smoked fish that commands a premium in local and regional markets. This commercial preference creates a structurally persistent demand for Rhizophora wood that tracks fish catch volumes rather than any administrative control regime. Compounding the fuelwood pressure are incremental agricultural encroachment at the landward margins of the lagoon and the ongoing coastal erosion of the sandbar separating the lagoon from the Atlantic Ocean — a geomorphological process driven by the loss of sediment supply following the Akosombo Dam's impoundment of more than 90% of the Volta River's natural sediment load since 1964. These drivers are not temporary anomalies; they are structural features of the Keta landscape that will persist in the absence of deliberate intervention.

Applied to the 20,717.95 hectares of the Keta Lagoon Complex, a 2.15% annual loss rate represents approximately 445 hectares of mangrove loss per year in the business-as-usual scenario. Over a typical ten-year crediting period, this trajectory would eliminate more than 4,400 hectares of mangrove from Ghana's largest blue carbon landscape — erasing carbon stocks accumulated over centuries, releasing long-sequestered SOC as the drained soils oxidise, and progressively destroying the ecological foundation of the livelihood systems that communities across the Keta fringe depend upon. The ten-year counterfactual loss is not a theoretical model; it is the empirical extrapolation of a trend that the multi-decadal land cover record confirms was already underway before this programme was initiated.

Project activities are additional because they intervene directly against this trajectory. CREMA governance structures, once fully formalised across the Keta landscape, establish community-based institutions with a legal mandate under the Wildlife Resources Management Act (Act 1115, 2023) to manage and protect mangrove resources within their boundaries. This institutional intervention changes the governance context in which fuelwood harvesting decisions are made — creating enforceable rules and social accountability mechanisms where previously none existed in formal terms. Restoration activities at Dzita and across the Keta fringe reverse the direction of net mangrove change from loss to gain, generating carbon removals above the baseline trajectory. And the broader programme of livelihood diversification and alternative fuel access documented in the baseline — improved fish-smoking cookstoves that reduce fuelwood consumption per unit of fish processed, alternative income streams that reduce harvesting pressure — addresses the demand-side root cause of the deforestation pressure that defines the baseline scenario. Without these interventions, the 2.15% annual loss rate has no structural reason to change.

For Songor, additionality rests on a more complex landscape argument: CREMA formation at Pute, where proactive traditional leadership and strong community interest in CREMA governance have been documented, would establish conservation governance in a landscape where the dominant pressure — salt industry expansion by industrial operators at Elavanyo and Obane — operates largely outside community control. Additionality at Songor requires credible evidence that CREMA governance can hold the remaining 465.76 hectares against encroachment pressure that is driven by economic actors significantly more powerful than local communities acting alone. The project's additionality argument at Songor therefore depends on both the community governance structures and the institutional backing of MLNR, MESTI, and the Wildlife Division as district-level enforcement actors.

## 9.5 Permanence and Leakage

### Permanence

A carbon credit represents a specific quantity of carbon dioxide equivalent that has been kept out of the atmosphere or removed from it. For that claim to be credible, the storage must be durable — the carbon must remain out of the atmosphere for the duration attributed to the credit. Permanence risk is the risk that stored carbon is subsequently released through reversal events: re-clearing of conserved forests, drainage of protected soils, collapse of the governance structures that prevent harvesting from resuming.

The protocol manages permanence risk through three interconnected mechanisms. The first is the CREMA governance architecture, which creates long-term community stewardship of mangrove resources embedded in a legal institutional framework. CREMA boundaries, management plans, and by-laws are registered formal instruments that outlast individual community leaders and project cycles. A CREMA whose founding executive has retired in 2040 can still enforce its 2025 management plan because the plan is institutionalised, not personal. The design standard requiring a minimum of 40% female representation in CREMA executive leadership directly addresses the risk that governance capture by elite male actors — historically documented in comparable natural resource management contexts — would produce a governance reversal: the systematic exclusion of the majority stakeholder group (women, who dominate the fish-smoking industry and are therefore the primary agents of fuelwood pressure management) would undermine both the social legitimacy and the practical effectiveness of CREMA conservation commitments. Permanence, in the Keta Lagoon context, is partly a function of gender inclusion.

The second mechanism is institutional anchoring with MLNR and MESTI as national co-custodians of the MRV system. Carbon stocks monitored under this protocol are not held in a project-specific register that disappears when a project cycle ends; they are recorded in a nationally managed database under the institutional authority of two government ministries. The WACA ResIP 2 programme (World Bank Project P175625) established the framework, but the monitoring system is designed as a permanent national function. MLNR and MESTI's institutional accountability for the accuracy and continuity of the monitoring record provides a governance permanence that project-specific structures alone cannot guarantee.

The third mechanism is a buffer pool, consistent with Verra's Non-Permanence Risk Tool methodology. A defined percentage of credits generated from Ghana's blue carbon programme is withheld from issuance and held in a risk buffer pool maintained by the relevant registry. This buffer pool exists to cover any reversal events that occur during the crediting period — if a portion of conserved mangroves is lost to unforeseen disturbance, the buffer pool absorbs the reversal without cancelling credits already issued to buyers. The buffer percentage is calculated through a risk assessment that accounts for site-specific permanence risks including governance stability, physical vulnerability to coastal erosion, and the structural pressure from fuelwood harvesting. The Akosombo Dam-driven coastal erosion risk at Keta — a landscape-level process that the project cannot control and can only adapt to — is a material input to that risk calculation, and the buffer percentage for Keta credits is expected to be higher than for less physically vulnerable mangrove landscapes.

### Leakage

Leakage occurs when the conservation or reduction of degradation pressure within a project boundary displaces that pressure to areas outside the boundary, producing emissions elsewhere that partially or fully offset the climate benefit claimed within the project. In the context of the Keta Lagoon Complex, the primary leakage risk is market-driven fuelwood displacement: if CREMA governance successfully restricts harvesting within CREMA boundaries, but the demand for Rhizophora fuelwood by fish-smoking operators in communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo remains unchanged, that demand will seek supply from adjacent unprotected mangrove areas outside the CREMA footprint. A carbon claim based only on in-boundary metrics, in this scenario, would overstate the net climate benefit.

Leakage is addressed in the carbon accounting protocol through two approaches. The first is demand-side management: interventions that reduce the total demand for mangrove fuelwood in the project landscape — improved cookstoves, alternative fuel access, livelihood diversification — reduce leakage risk at its source by shrinking the demand pressure that would otherwise be displaced. The annual Fuel Source Shift Monitoring surveys (Indicator 3 under Layer II — Environmental Pressure Surveillance, described in Chapter 5) track changes in household primary fuel source across CREMA communities, providing annual evidence of whether fuelwood dependency is declining. A household that has transitioned to improved cookstoves or LPG is not a leakage risk; a household that continues to depend exclusively on mangrove wood but has simply shifted procurement to an unmonitored adjacent area is. The survey data disaggregates these scenarios.

The second approach is spatial monitoring of adjacent areas through Layer II. The Environmental Pressure Surveillance system extends its monitoring footprint beyond CREMA boundaries to detect incidence of new cutting, charcoal production, and land conversion activity in areas adjacent to protected zones. Quarterly CREMA transect walks reporting fresh stumps, active cutting evidence, and charcoal kilns (Indicator 2: Mangrove Cutting Signal) are designed specifically to detect this displacement pattern. Where cutting incidence rises in adjacent areas concurrent with conservation within CREMA boundaries, the protocol flags this as a leakage signal requiring investigation and, where confirmed, applies a leakage deduction to the net carbon accounting for the relevant period.

The leakage deduction methodology follows standard practice under Verra and Gold Standard: a discount factor is applied to the gross carbon benefit calculated within the project boundary, representing the estimated proportion of pressure displaced outside the boundary. For the Keta landscape, given the intensity of documented fuelwood demand and the relatively limited area covered by CREMA boundaries in the early implementation period, a conservative leakage factor is warranted. As CREMA coverage expands across the landscape and as demand-side interventions demonstrate measurable reductions in household fuelwood dependency, the leakage factor may be revised downward — but only on the basis of documented evidence from the Layer II monitoring indicators, not on the basis of assumed behavioural change.

## 9.6 Uncertainty and Conservative Accounting

All field measurements contain uncertainty, and the carbon accounting protocol is explicit about how that uncertainty is characterised, quantified where possible, and managed conservatively in the credit calculation. The fundamental principle — consistent with both IPCC guidance and Verra methodology — is that where uncertainty cannot be eliminated through improved measurement, it is resolved in favour of lower, not higher, credit estimates. A carbon market reviewer should be able to review Ghana's accounting records and be confident that the credits issued represent the lower bound of reasonable estimates, not an optimistic central value.

Three sources of uncertainty are material to the Ghana protocol.

The first is allometric equation uncertainty. Biomass estimates for above-ground and below-ground carbon pools are calculated from DBH and height measurements through allometric equations. These equations are empirical relationships derived from destructive sampling of trees in specific geographic and ecological contexts. When applied to a different population of trees — even the same species in a slightly different environment — they carry a prediction uncertainty that is typically expressed as a confidence interval around the mean estimate. For *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* in West African coastal contexts, published allometric equations provide the best available estimates, but the degree to which the specific stand conditions at Salo and other Keta Lagoon PSPs match the conditions under which those equations were derived introduces unavoidable uncertainty. The protocol documents the equations applied, their published uncertainty ranges, and applies a conservative downward adjustment to AGB estimates where the uncertainty range is wide. At future re-measurement events, national technical teams — under MLNR and MESTI oversight — are responsible for assessing whether new species-specific equations derived from Ghanaian mangrove stands have become available, and for updating the accounting methodology accordingly through a documented methodological note.

The second is spatial classification uncertainty from the remote sensing analysis. The 2025 national baseline was produced using Sentinel-2 imagery at 10 to 20 metre resolution and a Random Forest classifier validated against 319 participatory ground-truth reference points. The Random Forest algorithm assigns classification probabilities rather than binary labels, and the 319 reference points — while sufficient to validate the national-scale classification at a high overall accuracy level — cannot eliminate all classification error, particularly at the boundaries between mangrove and adjacent land cover types such as fringing swamp, open grassland, or salt pan edge zones. Overestimation of mangrove extent at these boundary zones would overstate the baseline carbon stock. The protocol addresses this by applying the classification to a conservative threshold probability — areas classified as mangrove only where the Random Forest model assigns a high confidence probability — and by treating boundary zones with documented classification uncertainty as subject to additional ground-truth validation before inclusion in the baseline extent figure. Annual extent monitoring applies the same conservative threshold, ensuring that reported gains in restored area are only claimed where satellite classification confidence is high.

The third is soil carbon sample variability. SOC concentrations in mangrove soils are spatially heterogeneous. Even within a single PSP, soil cores sampled at different positions within the plot can yield meaningfully different TOC concentrations and bulk density values, reflecting micro-topographic variations in flooding frequency, organic input rates, and historical disturbance. The protocol requires a minimum number of replicate cores per PSP to capture this within-plot variability, and the carbon stock estimate is calculated as the mean of those replicates rather than from any single core value. Where replicate variability is high, the uncertainty interval is wide, and the protocol applies a conservative deduction from the mean estimate — subtracting a portion of the standard deviation rather than claiming the central value as a point estimate. This approach is consistent with Verra's guidance on uncertain parameters in carbon stock calculations and ensures that the credits issued from SOC-based accounting genuinely reflect the lower bound of reasonable estimates rather than a potentially inflated central value.

The cumulative effect of these conservative adjustments is a carbon stock and credit estimate that is deliberately lower than the best-estimate central value. This is the correct posture for a programme entering international carbon markets for the first time. Underestimated credits that are later revised upward as measurement improves are a sign of methodological maturity; overestimated credits that require downward revision undermine buyer confidence and damage the credibility of the broader national blue carbon programme. Ghana's protocol is calibrated to protect against the latter risk.

## 9.7 Credit Issuance Pathway

The process by which ecological field data becomes a verified carbon credit involves a defined sequence of steps, each with its own institutional responsibility and quality control requirement. This pathway links the field measurement activities of Chapter 4 and Chapter 7 to the national reporting framework of Chapter 10 and, ultimately, to the registry submission that makes credits available for sale or retirement.

**Step 1 — Annual Data Compilation.** At the end of each monitoring year, MLNR and MESTI compile the annual monitoring dataset from the three data streams that feed the national system: satellite extent analysis outputs from the Layer I ecological and carbon monitoring, field data from quarterly CREMA pressure surveillance transects and PSP maintenance visits under Layers I and II, and governance and safeguard reporting from Layer III. The digital platform described in Chapter 8 centralises these data streams and maintains a time-stamped audit trail of all inputs and outputs. This step is currently operational in framework design; full operational functionality depends on the completion of the web platform build and the activation of automated data ingestion pathways from CREMA field reporting tools. Annual data compilation through MLNR and MESTI's national technical teams is operationally ready.

**Step 2 — National MRV Report Production.** Annual monitoring data are synthesised into the National Blue Carbon MRV Report, the primary national reporting product. This report documents extent changes, carbon stock updates from any re-measurement events, pressure indicator trends, restoration performance outcomes, and governance and safeguard status. The MRV Report is the record that Ghana's NDC reporting draws upon for the coastal blue carbon contribution and the reference document that independent verifiers review when conducting carbon credit verification. The reporting framework for this document is described in Chapter 10. National MRV Report production is an operational function of MLNR and MESTI.

**Step 3 — Independent Verification.** For carbon credits to be issued under Verra, Gold Standard, or Plan Vivo standards, an accredited independent verification body must review the project's monitoring data, methodology, and reported carbon benefits against the relevant standard requirements. Verification involves desk review of monitoring records, field visits to PSP locations and restoration sites, and a formal audit of the carbon calculation methodology. The verification cycle is typically aligned with the crediting period rather than occurring annually, though Verra VM0033 requires verification at minimum every five years and permits more frequent voluntary verification. Independent verification as a formal step in the credit issuance pathway requires the project to be formally registered under a standard — a step that is a necessary next development for Ghana's blue carbon programme and that is not yet complete. The preparation of a project description document (PDD), the definition of project boundaries, and the formal registration with a registry are development activities that follow from this baseline assessment and constitute the next stage of the carbon market participation pathway.

**Step 4 — Credit Calculation.** Following verification, the verified carbon benefit — expressed in tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (tCO₂e) — is calculated by applying the verified carbon stock changes, the leakage deduction, the buffer pool withholding, and any conservative uncertainty adjustments to the gross emission reduction or removal figure. The credit calculation translates the ecological measurement record into the standardised currency of international carbon markets. This step requires a qualified carbon accountant working within the methodology framework of the relevant standard, using the field data archived in the national MRV system as the primary input.

**Step 5 — Registry Submission and Issuance.** Verified credits are submitted to the relevant registry (the Verra registry, the Gold Standard Impact Registry, or the Plan Vivo registry, depending on the standard under which the project is certified) for issuance. At this point, credits become publicly visible in the registry database, traceable to specific project locations and vintages, and available for retirement by buyers. Registry submission is dependent on the completion of Steps 3 and 4, and is therefore a future development step contingent on the completion of project registration and independent verification.

The current operational status of the pathway is as follows. Steps 1 and 2 — data compilation and national MRV Report production — are designed, institutionally anchored, and operationally ready. The ecological and social baselines that feed these steps have been established through the 2025 assessment. Steps 3 through 5 — independent verification, credit calculation, and registry submission — require project registration under a carbon standard, which is the logical and recommended next step following the finalisation of this baseline assessment package. The assessment provided through the WACA Blue Carbon programme, including this MRV Standalone Report, constitutes the foundational technical documentation that a project developer would use to prepare the project description document required for standard registration. Ghana is, at the conclusion of this assessment, in the strongest position it has ever been to proceed to formal carbon market registration — with a methodologically credible baseline, an operational monitoring architecture, and an institutional governance framework that can sustain the monitoring cycle that market participation demands.
