# Chapter 4: Ecological and Carbon Integrity Layer

---

## 4.1 Purpose of the Ecological and Carbon Integrity Layer

Layer I of Ghana's National Blue Carbon Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) framework is the measurement foundation on which all subsequent carbon accounting, reporting, and market participation depends. Its purpose is precise: to ensure that the spatial extent of Ghana's coastal mangroves, the ecological health of restored and conserved areas, and the carbon stocks held in mangrove biomass and soils are continuously monitored, periodically re-measured, correctly attributed, and recorded in a form that meets the requirements of both national climate reporting and international carbon standard compliance.

The distinction between ecological monitoring and carbon monitoring is not merely technical. Mangrove ecosystems accumulate carbon slowly — above-ground biomass in the historically cut stands of the Keta Lagoon Complex develops over years and decades, not seasons — but they can lose carbon rapidly. Fuelwood cutting events, land conversion episodes, or saltwater intrusion caused by coastal retreat along the Keta sandbar can release stored carbon in a matter of weeks. A monitoring layer designed around annual or multi-year audit cycles alone would be structurally incapable of detecting these events in time to prevent or mitigate the losses they produce. Layer I therefore operates at two temporal scales simultaneously: continuous satellite-based surveillance of mangrove extent and annual extent change, and periodic field-based re-measurement of carbon stocks through the Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) network established during the 2025 carbon inventory. These two data streams are complementary rather than redundant. Extent monitoring provides the early signal; stock re-measurement provides the quantified account.

The Ecological and Carbon Integrity Layer operates within the institutional architecture described in Chapter 3. Community Change Agents (CCAs) and Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) members supply the field presence that sustains PSP access and validates spatial monitoring outputs between national measurement events. The Wildlife Division of the Forestry Commission provides district-level validation. The Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI) hold national oversight and analytical responsibility. This vertical integration is not administrative convention; it reflects the practical reality that no single institution possesses both the field density and the analytical capacity that continuous carbon integrity monitoring requires. The system functions because responsibilities are clearly stratified, not because any one tier attempts to perform all of them.

## 4.2 Carbon Pools Assessed

Ghana's national carbon monitoring system tracks four carbon pools, aligned with the methodology of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Wetlands Supplement — the authoritative international reference for blue carbon accounting in tidal wetland ecosystems. The four pools are above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (BGB), deadwood where ecologically relevant, and soil organic carbon (SOC) measured from the surface to 100 centimetres depth.

Above-ground biomass covers all living woody material above the soil surface, measured through diameter at breast height (DBH) and height measurements at each PSP. In the Keta Lagoon Complex, where *Rhizophora racemosa* (Red Mangrove) dominates the waterward fringes and *Avicennia germinans* (Black Mangrove) occupies the landward and more saline zones, DBH data from the 2025 carbon inventory indicate trees generally shorter than primary forest benchmarks due to the historical cutting pressure documented across communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo. This suppressed stature reflects the "death by a thousand cuts" dynamic characteristic of Keta — incremental harvesting events that prevent canopy maturation — and has direct implications for AGB stock estimates and for the carbon sequestration trajectory that restoration and conservation activities are intended to reverse. Below-ground biomass captures the living root and rhizome systems that mangrove trees invest heavily in: for *Rhizophora* species, the complex prop root architecture anchors both the tree and, crucially, the sediment, making BGB a pool of both carbon significance and structural importance to the physical stability of the mangrove system.

Deadwood — standing dead stems and fallen coarse woody debris — is included in the monitoring framework where site conditions make it ecologically relevant. In recently degraded or recovering stands, deadwood can represent a significant proportion of total carbon stock and should not be omitted from the accounting without justification.

The most analytically significant pool in mangrove ecosystems is soil organic carbon, and its treatment in this framework reflects both the ecological reality of tidal wetland soils and the methodological requirements of international carbon standards. Tidal wetland soils are fundamentally different from upland soils in their carbon dynamics. Anaerobic conditions below the waterlogged surface suppress the decomposition pathways that in terrestrial soils would oxidise organic matter and release it as carbon dioxide. Organic inputs — leaf litter, root material, sediment-associated carbon — accumulate in layers rather than cycling rapidly, producing dense, peaty, carbon-rich profiles that extend many metres below the surface. Sampling to a depth of 100 centimetres captures the most active zone of this accumulation and aligns with IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance on the minimum sampling depth required for credible tidal wetland carbon inventories. The 2025 soil measurements at the Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1 in the Keta Lagoon Complex confirm the characteristic pattern: high total organic carbon (TOC) concentrations near the surface, declining with depth through a profile that reflects the low bulk density and high porosity of undisturbed mangrove peat. The sheer volume of carbon held in this soil pool — orders of magnitude greater per hectare than the above-ground stock in many mangrove stands — makes SOC the dominant carbon pool in the Ghanaian assessment and the pool most at risk from drainage, saltwater regime disruption, or conversion of mangrove land to other uses such as the salt pan expansion documented at Elavanyo and Obane in the Songor Ramsar Site, where mangrove soil carbon accumulated over centuries can be lost within years of drainage and exposure to aerobic decomposition. The 100 cm depth protocol is not arbitrary; it is the minimum required to capture the soil carbon reality of the ecosystems this framework governs.

The combination of these four pools, measured and accounted for within a single nationally managed system, gives Ghana a carbon accounting framework that is both methodologically defensible under international standards such as Verra/VCS, Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo, and institutionally rooted in the government structures described in Chapter 3.

## 4.3 Permanent Sample Plots

The PSP network is the physical backbone of Ghana's carbon stock monitoring system. PSPs establish fixed, georeferenced measurement points within mangrove ecosystems where repeated biomass and soil measurements can be conducted over time, providing the long-term data record that carbon stock change detection requires. A single measurement — however rigorously conducted — can describe what carbon is present at one moment. Only a network of fixed plots, re-measured at consistent intervals using consistent methodology, can determine whether carbon stocks are increasing, decreasing, or stable, and attribute those changes to the drivers that produced them.

Each PSP is georeferenced and assigned a unique identification code, archived in the national web platform alongside its stratification category, plot boundary coordinates, and full measurement history. This digital archiving is not a convenience; it is a methodological requirement. If the physical markers of a PSP are disturbed — as is possible in the dynamic, tidally influenced landscapes of the Keta Lagoon fringe — the GPS coordinates and plot record held in the national system allow field teams to relocate the measurement points precisely. PSPs are linked to the responsible CREMA and district authority, ensuring that community monitoring teams know which plots fall within their operational zone and that national technical agencies can trace any data anomaly back to its source.

The Salo PSP — formally designated Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1 and georeferenced at its location within the Keta Lagoon Complex — exemplifies the operational logic of the PSP system. Salo is a community within the Keta Lagoon fringe that is both deeply integrated into the mangrove-dependent economy and proximate to the intensive harvesting pressure documented in the 2025 baseline. The plot captures the carbon dynamics of a stand subject to real-world pressure — not a protected reference forest, but a working landscape where conservation and use exist in tension. Tree measurements collected at Salo in 2025 include five sample trees representing the species composition typical of the site: the mixed *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* assemblage that characterises the Keta Lagoon fringes. These measurements — DBH, tree height, and species identification — feed directly into above-ground biomass calculations using established allometric equations, while soil measurements from the same plot provide the SOC profile against which future re-measurement will be compared.

At each PSP, the variables measured span the full ecological picture that carbon accounting and restoration performance assessment require. Species composition is recorded to track shifts in community structure over time — a change from *Rhizophora* to *Avicennia* dominance, for example, may signal altered hydrology or increased salinity pressure rather than random succession, and can only be detected through species-level records. DBH of all trees within the plot above the minimum threshold diameter is measured to allow biomass calculation and to track individual tree growth between measurement events. Tree height is recorded to complement DBH-based allometric estimates and to capture structural changes in the canopy. Canopy cover is assessed to provide an integrative measure of stand density that can be visually compared with satellite imagery. Recruitment and mortality are recorded to characterise the demographic health of the stand: a plot showing healthy sapling recruitment is a recovering stand; a plot showing elevated mortality with no recruitment is under active pressure. Taken together, these variables at each PSP provide not just a carbon number but an ecological narrative of each plot's condition and trajectory.

CCAs support PSP access maintenance and DBH recording in the intervals between full national re-measurement exercises. This community-level engagement at PSPs serves two purposes. It provides a continuous presence at measurement locations that reduces the risk of undetected disturbance — a CCA who visits the Salo PSP quarterly knows immediately if trees have been cut since the previous visit, whereas a national team visiting every three to five years would see only the outcome, not the event. It also builds the technical familiarity and institutional memory within communities that sustained long-term monitoring requires: a CCA trained in 2025 becomes a resource for training their successor in 2030.

The biomass re-measurement cycle for the PSP network is every three to five years, consistent with the IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance on re-measurement intervals for tidal wetland carbon inventories. This cycle reflects the rate of measurable biomass change in mangrove stands — sufficiently frequent to detect ecologically meaningful carbon stock shifts, but not so frequent as to make the field cost of national re-measurement prohibitive. Where significant disturbance is detected between scheduled re-measurement events — through CCA reporting, satellite extent analysis, or district-level pressure alerts — the re-measurement cycle may be accelerated at affected PSPs to ensure that carbon losses are quantified in the accounting period in which they occur.

## 4.4 Mangrove Extent Monitoring

Annual satellite-based monitoring of mangrove extent is the continuous surveillance instrument of Layer I. Where PSP re-measurement operates on a three to five year cycle and captures fine-grained carbon stock data at fixed locations, extent monitoring operates annually across Ghana's entire 550 km coastline, providing the spatial intelligence needed to detect conversion events, track fragmentation trends, and verify that restoration activities are expanding the total mangrove canopy as intended.

The methodology mirrors that of the 2025 national baseline, ensuring that annual updates are directly comparable to the Year 0 reference point. Harmonised Sentinel-2 multispectral satellite imagery, processed at 10 to 20 metre spatial resolution, is classified using the Random Forest algorithm on the Google Earth Engine (GEE) cloud computing platform. The choice of Sentinel-2 is methodologically significant: at 10 to 20 metre resolution, the imagery is capable of detecting and classifying the narrow fringing mangrove stands and small fragmented patches that dominate Ghana's coastal landscape outside of the Keta Lagoon. The 30-metre Landsat imagery that underpinned many historical national estimates produced systematic undercount of these smaller features — contributing to the discrepancy between pre-2024 national figures ranging from 7,240 hectares (2013) to 18,100 hectares (1980) and the 2025 baseline figure of 30,025 hectares. Maintaining Sentinel-2 as the monitoring instrument throughout the project ensures that annual change detection analyses are not corrupted by sensor-switching artefacts, and that extent gains attributed to restoration activities genuinely reflect ecological expansion rather than improved detection capability.

The Year 0 baseline against which annual extent monitoring is compared was established through the 2025 geo-spatial carbon inventory. It documents the national mangrove total of 30,025 hectares, representing 2.81% of the 1,068,361.48 hectare coastal landscape assessed across 35 political districts. Within this national picture, the site-level distribution is markedly uneven. The Keta Lagoon Complex holds 20,717.95 hectares — approximately 69% of the national total — in large, largely contiguous stands concentrated between the main lagoon body and the Volta River estuary. The Songor Ramsar Site holds 465.76 hectares (2% of the national total), distributed as small, fragmented patches across a landscape dominated by salt pans and grassland. Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site's mangrove cover is ecologically insignificant — a landscape of advanced degradation where restoration is the operating strategy rather than conservation of existing stocks. This three-site differentiation is not administrative shorthand; it is the empirical basis for tailored monitoring intensity and management response at each landscape.

Annual outputs from the satellite monitoring programme include three core spatial products: an extent change map showing areas of detected mangrove loss (deforestation and conversion), detected mangrove gain (restoration expansion and natural regeneration), and stable mangrove area that has neither been lost nor gained in the monitored period. These three categories are expressed in hectares and compared directly to the Year 0 baseline, yielding a net annual area change figure for each landscape and for the national system. Detected loss events are cross-referenced with the environmental pressure indicators of Layer II — particularly the land conversion and mangrove cutting signal indicators — to test whether field-level pressure signals predicted the observed spatial outcome. Detected gains are compared to georeferenced restoration site records to attribute canopy expansion to specific intervention activities. Areas that remain stable despite documented pressure signals are examined to assess whether conservation measures are holding.

Ground-truth validation of annual classification outputs follows the same participatory pathway used in the 2025 baseline: Wildlife Division field officers and CREMA monitoring teams cross-check satellite-detected change events against their direct field knowledge of the landscapes in their zones. The 319 participatory ground-truth reference points generated during the 2025 baseline establish both the methodological precedent and the institutional pathway for this validation — the same field teams, the same institutional relationships, the same protocol. This continuity is methodologically essential. Random Forest classifiers trained on one year's reference data may perform differently when applied to the following year's imagery, and field validation ensures that classification performance is assessed and documented annually. Any systematic degradation in classification accuracy is detected before it propagates into the carbon accounting record.

## 4.5 Restoration Performance Monitoring

At the three early action hubs — Dzita in the Keta Lagoon Complex, Pute in the Songor Ramsar Site, and Sankor in the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site — Layer I operates in its most active mode: tracking the ecological performance of restoration interventions from the point of planting through to confirmed establishment and, ultimately, inclusion in the carbon stock accounting system.

The three hubs reflect deliberately differentiated starting conditions. At Dzita, restoration is operating within Ghana's principal blue carbon landscape — a site where the conservation of 20,717.95 hectares of existing mangrove stands is the primary strategic priority and where restoration activities aim to arrest active degradation and expand mangrove cover at the margins of the existing forest. The social readiness profile at Dzita is well-documented: CCAs were actively engaged during the 2025 baseline fieldwork and played a substantive role in facilitating access and resolving at least one restoration site dispute between landowners and youth groups during the community consultation period. At Pute, restoration is taking place in a severely degraded landscape where only 465.76 hectares of mangroves survive across the Songor Ramsar Site as a whole. The land tenure conflicts between local artisanal communities and industrial salt mining operations at Elavanyo and Obane that have prevented coherent landscape governance make Pute's restoration monitoring particularly sensitive to disruption risk, and monitoring protocols at this site must account for the possibility that encroachment events can reverse planting gains rapidly. At Sankor, restoration is the defining activity of the Muni-Pomadze engagement: with mangrove cover ecologically insignificant, every seedling established represents not the protection of existing carbon but the beginning of a carbon accumulation trajectory that did not previously exist at landscape scale. Community cohesion at Sankor is the highest of the three hubs, and CREMA interest in both governance formation and active restoration has been consistently strong throughout the baseline period.

All restoration sites are georeferenced and registered in the national web platform with unique site identifiers, linked to the responsible CREMA and district authority. This registration is the entry point for a structured monitoring protocol that begins three months after planting and continues at six months, twelve months, and annually thereafter until ecological establishment is confirmed. The indicators tracked at each monitoring visit provide a complete picture of restoration trajectory. Seedling survival rate — expressed as the proportion of planted stems surviving at each monitoring interval — is the primary performance indicator. The minimum acceptable survival threshold is 70% at Year 1: a site that fails to meet this threshold triggers a corrective restoration action through the district tier, either replanting, hydrological assessment if water regime issues are suspected, or investigation of disturbance events such as grazing or re-cutting. A site that consistently meets or exceeds 70% survival and shows progressive canopy development, natural recruitment of seedlings beyond those deliberately planted, and appropriate species composition for the site's hydrological conditions is a site on a confirmed restoration trajectory.

Canopy cover development is assessed at each visit to track structural progression from an open, recently planted site to a functionally closed canopy. Canopy closure is ecologically significant not only as a carbon accumulation signal but as the point at which the mangrove stand begins to provide its broader ecosystem services — shade, root structure for sediment stabilisation, nursery habitat for fish. Natural recruitment — the establishment of seedlings from propagules produced by the restored stand itself or carried in by tidal flow — is the most reliable indicator that ecological function is recovering, since it demonstrates that the restored area is now acting as a source of new plants rather than depending entirely on human intervention for its regeneration. Evidence of disturbance — fresh cutting, grazing damage, evidence of charcoal production activity — is recorded at each visit alongside the restoration performance indicators, because the permanence of restoration is only as secure as the governance and enforcement conditions that surround it.

A restored plot formally enters the carbon stock accounting system when three conditions are met: canopy closure is confirmed as progressing; natural recruitment is documented; and the site has met the Year 1 survival threshold without triggering corrective restoration. At this point, the site is incorporated into the PSP re-measurement cycle, with a PSP established within the restored area to provide the long-term carbon stock record that market compliance requires. This transition — from restoration monitoring to carbon stock monitoring — is the point at which Layer I's ecological and market functions converge, and it is the threshold that determines when restoration investments begin generating a verifiable carbon return.

## 4.6 The Degradation Baseline and Additionality

Carbon credits generated through conservation and restoration of Ghana's coastal mangroves are only internationally credible if they can be demonstrated to be additional — that is, if the emission reductions or carbon sequestration they represent would not have occurred in the absence of the project. Additionality is established by comparing the carbon stock trajectory under the project against the baseline scenario: what would have happened to mangrove extent and carbon stocks if no intervention had taken place.

The reference degradation trajectory for the Keta Lagoon Complex is the historical deforestation rate of 2.15% per year, derived from the multi-decadal land cover analysis conducted as part of the 2025 national baseline. This rate reflects the cumulative effect of fuelwood harvesting — concentrated particularly on *Rhizophora racemosa*, which is preferred by fish-smoking operators in communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo for its heat output and the characteristic flavour it imparts to smoked fish — as well as incremental agricultural encroachment and the structural coastal erosion driven by sediment starvation of the Volta Delta following the construction of the Akosombo Dam in 1964. The dam has trapped more than 90% of the Volta River's natural sediment load, transforming the delta from a pro-grading system building landward from the sea to an eroding system retreating under Atlantic wave pressure. Applied to the Keta Lagoon's 20,717.95 hectares, a 2.15% annual loss rate represents approximately 445 hectares of mangrove loss per year in the business-as-usual scenario — a trajectory that, left uninterrupted, would eliminate a significant proportion of Ghana's largest blue carbon asset within decades.

The 2025 Year 0 baseline is the temporal anchor for Ghana's carbon accounting under this framework. All future measurements of carbon stock change — whether gains from conservation and restoration activities, or losses from detected degradation events — are calculated as departures from the Year 0 inventory conducted during the 2025 baseline assessment. The combination of the Year 0 baseline and the 2.15% per year reference deforestation rate defines the counterfactual: the carbon stock trajectory that would be expected if the WACA Blue Carbon programme had not been implemented. Project carbon credits are generated by the positive difference between the observed trajectory under the project and this counterfactual. A year in which extent monitoring shows zero net mangrove loss at Keta, in a landscape where the baseline scenario would have predicted a 445-hectare loss, represents a carbon benefit against the reference trajectory — one that can be quantified using the carbon stock data from the PSP network and the SOC inventory, and reported against Ghana's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitments under the Paris Agreement.

The additionality framework requires ongoing demonstration, not merely initial establishment. Each annual monitoring cycle — satellite extent analysis, CCA field reports, district-level validation — contributes to the rolling evidence record of project performance against the reference scenario. Where pressure monitoring under Layer II detects elevated fuelwood harvesting or land conversion activity, Layer I's extent monitoring provides the spatial corroboration. Where extent monitoring detects net mangrove loss at a landscape, the data are compared to the pressure indicators to determine causation and to assess whether the loss represents a temporary setback within an otherwise stable conservation trajectory or a systematic erosion of the carbon base that requires urgent intervention. This integration of additionality tracking with real-time ecological surveillance is what distinguishes Ghana's MRV framework from a periodic audit mechanism and makes it an operationally useful management tool.

At Songor and Muni-Pomadze, the additionality argument takes a different form. At Songor — where the 465.76 hectares of surviving mangrove are surrounded by the landscape-level evidence of what salt pan expansion has already achieved — the reference scenario is defined by the spatial pattern of documented loss rather than a single percentage rate, and the project's contribution is measured by the conservation of what remains and the restoration of areas where mangrove establishment is ecologically feasible. At Muni-Pomadze, where no meaningful baseline carbon stock exists to conserve, additionality is generated entirely through restoration: every tonne of carbon sequestered in a stand that did not exist at Year 0 is wholly additional, provided the restoration meets the monitoring and permanence standards the framework requires.

## 4.7 Data Flow from Field to National Record

The ecological and carbon data generated by Layer I do not become part of Ghana's national carbon record by moving automatically. They pass through a structured institutional chain that adds validation at each level, converts field observations into verified data products, and ultimately produces the annual carbon stock update that MLNR and MESTI require for national reporting.

At the community level, CCAs and CREMA members constitute the primary data-generating tier. Their responsibilities under Layer I include quarterly visits to PSP locations to confirm access and document any visible disturbance events; completion of standardised field data sheets recording DBH measurements where directed by national technical teams; quarterly monitoring of restoration sites against the survival, canopy cover, and recruitment indicators described in Section 4.5; and the recording of any anomalous ecological observations — significant tree mortality, evidence of flood events or saltwater intrusion, unusual wildlife die-offs — that fall outside the structured indicator set but are relevant to the ecological condition of the monitoring area. This community-level data collection is not informal or discretionary. CCAs operate within spatially delineated monitoring zones uploaded to the national web platform, and their data submissions are tagged to the specific sites those zones contain. The 2025 baseline fieldwork — which involved training CCAs across communities including Dzita, Salo, Pute, and Sankor in the use of monitoring equipment and data recording protocols — established the institutional foundation for this data collection capacity. Ongoing training, as recommended by the monitoring plan developed for the Keta and Ada wetlands, will be required as monitoring equipment is periodically updated and as new CCAs are brought into the system.

Field data collected quarterly are submitted to the Wildlife Division for district-level validation. The Wildlife Division's role here is not passive receipt but active checking: consolidating data from multiple CREMA zones within the district, cross-checking for internal consistency and for alignment with known site conditions, and flagging observations that require technical follow-up. Where a CCA's field record shows PSP disturbance, the district review determines whether corrective action is warranted at the community level or whether escalation to the national technical tier is required. The Wildlife Division also conducts the ground-truth validation of annual satellite classification outputs, field-checking detected change events against direct observation and cross-referencing them with community monitoring records. This district-level integration is what gives Layer I's spatial data its credibility: satellite-detected mangrove loss that is confirmed by a Wildlife Division field officer who has walked the same terrain in the same quarter is a verified observation, not a classification artefact.

At the national level, MLNR and MESTI receive district consolidated reports and integrate them with the outputs of GEE satellite analysis to produce the national annual mangrove extent update — the definitive statement of Ghana's coastal mangrove area at the close of each monitoring year. On the three to five year PSP re-measurement cycle, national technical teams conduct full carbon stock recalculations from PSP field data, soil re-measurement at designated depths, and the full QA/QC procedures that the IPCC Wetlands Supplement methodology requires before carbon stock figures can be used in national or market reporting. All carbon data are archived in the nationally managed database with controlled access rights and full audit trail documentation, ensuring that the data record supporting Ghana's NDC commitments and any future carbon market participation is complete, version-controlled, and institutionally owned. The Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report, produced by MLNR and MESTI, draws on the full cycle of community field data, district validation, and national analysis to report Ghana's carbon stock position, landscape-level changes, restoration progress, and additionality against the 2025 Year 0 baseline — a document that constitutes both a government record and a transparency instrument for development partners and carbon market reviewers. All monitoring data underpinning Layer I feeds into the digital platform described in Chapter 8, which provides the spatial and analytical interface through which national, district, and community stakeholders access the evidence base that Layer I continuously builds.
