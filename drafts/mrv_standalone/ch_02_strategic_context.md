# Chapter 2: Strategic Context and National Purpose

## 2.1 West Africa's Coastal Crisis and Ghana's National Response

Ghana's 550-kilometre coastline is one of the most ecologically productive and economically vital stretches of the West African littoral, yet it is also one of the most threatened. Over recent decades, the combined pressures of coastal erosion, recurrent flooding, marine and lagoon pollution, and the progressive degradation of mangrove, salt marsh, and wetland ecosystems have reached a scale that measurably undermines the livelihoods, food security, and physical safety of the communities that depend on them. These are not isolated incidents of localised environmental stress. They are symptoms of a systemic failure to manage the interface between a growing population, intensifying resource extraction, and a coastal landscape whose capacity to absorb pressure has been diminishing without replacement.

It was this recognition — shared across the sub-region — that motivated the design of the West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project (WACA ResIP 2). Approved by the World Bank in December 2022 under project number P175625, WACA ResIP 2 is a regional platform operating across three beneficiary countries: Ghana, the Gambia, and Guinea-Bissau. It was built on the premise that the scale of coastal degradation in West Africa exceeds what any single government or development partner can finance or effectively address in isolation, and that a regional framework combining national investments with shared knowledge and coordinated reporting offers a materially more durable response than country-by-country projects operating independently.

In Ghana, the project objective is specifically to strengthen the resilience of coastal communities by restoring and protecting the critical coastal ecosystems that provide essential services — storm buffering, fisheries nursery habitat, carbon sequestration, and livelihood support — to the millions of people who live within and adjacent to these landscapes. Responsibility for implementation rests with the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI), which holds the primary mandate for climate and environmental governance at the national level. MESTI is supported by the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) through its Focal Point Unit, which provides technical oversight of the assessment workstreams; by the Wildlife Division of the Forestry Commission, which carries statutory responsibility for managing the Ramsar-listed wetland complexes at the core of the blue carbon landscape; by the Ministry of Works and Housing and the Ghana Hydrological Authority (GHA), responsible for coastal engineering and hydrological management; and by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), which anchors regulatory compliance. This multi-ministry structure is not an administrative convenience. It reflects the genuine cross-sectoral character of coastal governance, in which land tenure decisions, ecosystem management authority, hydrological regulation, and environmental oversight all intersect in the same landscape, and all must be aligned for monitoring and restoration outcomes to persist beyond any single project cycle.

Blue carbon ecosystems — mangroves, tidal salt marshes, and seagrasses — occupy a distinctive position within this response framework precisely because they deliver simultaneously across three dimensions that coastal resilience programmes most often treat separately. They are climate mitigation assets, storing and sequestering carbon at rates that make them irreplaceable in national emissions accounting. They are biodiversity infrastructure, providing nursery habitat, foraging grounds, and ecological connectivity that sustain coastal fisheries and the livelihoods built around them. And they are community economic assets, supplying the fuelwood, fishing grounds, non-timber forest products, and storm protection that millions of households in Ghana's coastal zone depend on for their daily subsistence. The capacity to deliver across these three dimensions from a single landscape — rather than trading off mitigation against livelihoods or biodiversity against economic use — is what makes the strategic management of Ghana's blue carbon ecosystems a national priority, not merely an environmental one.

## 2.2 The Scale of Ghana's Blue Carbon Resource

Understanding the strategic stakes of the WACA Blue Carbon Assessment requires a clear account of what the 2025 baseline revealed about the scale of Ghana's coastal mangrove resource — and why that account represents a fundamental revision of previous understanding.

Prior to this assessment, estimates of Ghana's national mangrove extent ranged widely and inconsistently, from 7,240 hectares in a 2013 assessment to 18,100 hectares in a 1980 baseline. This variability was not evidence of ecosystem instability; it was evidence of methodological limitation. Coarser satellite imagery, lower-resolution land cover classification, and the absence of systematic participatory ground-truthing meant that previous assessments missed or misclassified the smaller, fragmented, and fringing mangrove stands that, in aggregate, constitute a substantial share of Ghana's actual resource.

The 2025 assessment resolved this ambiguity. Using harmonised Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery at 10–20 metre spatial resolution — a significant advance over the 30-metre Landsat data on which most historical estimates were based — and applying a Random Forest classification algorithm processed entirely on Google Earth Engine (GEE), the assessment produced a new national mangrove extent figure of 30,025 hectares, equivalent to 2.81 percent of Ghana's total coastal landscape area of 1,068,361.48 hectares, spanning 35 coastal districts. The classification model was trained and validated against 319 reference data points collected through a participatory ground-truthing process involving field visits with local community members and technical officers, supplemented by interpretation of very high-resolution imagery for areas inaccessible on foot. This was not merely a statistical exercise: community participants in the ground-truthing process identified previously undocumented mangrove stands that had been missed entirely by earlier global and regional datasets. The new figure of 30,025 hectares should therefore be read as the first accurate national count, not as evidence of a recent expansion.

The strategic importance of this revised figure lies not only in its size, but in the carbon implication of each hectare. Blue carbon ecosystems store up to ten times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial forests, owing to the exceptional accumulation of below-ground organic matter in permanently waterlogged soils and the sustained absence of aerobic decomposition that tidal inundation creates. Unlike a terrestrial forest, where much of the stored carbon turns over rapidly through decomposition, a healthy mangrove or tidal salt marsh holds centuries of accumulated carbon in its sediment profile. The corollary is stark: each hectare of mangrove lost to clearance, conversion, or degradation does not simply stop sequestering — it releases a disproportionately large pulse of previously sequestered carbon, drawing down a carbon stock that took generations to accumulate. This is why the 2025 national baseline is a matter of strategic national importance, not just ecological interest. Ghana's carbon market credibility, its NDC reporting obligations, and its capacity to attract blue carbon finance are all grounded, ultimately, in the credibility of this number and the rigour of the methodology behind it.

## 2.3 The Landscape Concentration Risk: Keta, Songor, and Muni-Pomadze

The national figure of 30,025 hectares distributes across Ghana's coastline in a pattern that is analytically more important than the headline number itself: it is not spread evenly, and it is not spread across many sites of comparable significance. The distribution is concentrated to a degree that creates a specific kind of strategic risk that must be understood before any monitoring or management system can be designed with adequate ambition.

The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, in the Volta Region, holds 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves — approximately 69 percent of Ghana's entire national coastal mangrove resource. This is not a site of local importance that happens to be large. It is the central pillar of Ghana's national blue carbon strategy. The spatial distribution within the site is characterised by large, continuous stretches of mangrove across the areas between the main lagoon body and the Volta River estuary, a configuration that supports both Red Mangrove (*Rhizophora racemosa*) and Black Mangrove (*Avicennia germinans*) across a landscape of exceptional ecological integrity relative to the rest of the national coastline. The scale and continuity of this resource explain the deep-seated socio-economic reliance of the surrounding communities — Dzita, Salo, Agbatsivi, Bomigo, Galotse, Sota, and Galo among them — and it equally explains why any deterioration of this single ecosystem is not a local environmental event but a national carbon accounting crisis. If the Keta Lagoon Complex loses mangrove cover at any significant rate, Ghana loses the majority of its blue carbon asset. There is no equivalent landscape elsewhere on the coast to absorb that loss.

In stark contrast, the Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, located in the Ada East District of Greater Accra Region, holds only 465.76 hectares of mangroves — a mere 2 percent of the national total, distributed as small, fragmented patches across a landscape that the 2025 land cover map depicts as overwhelmingly dominated by salt pans and grassland. This is not the Songor of historical record; it is the Songor of decades of documented degradation, in which the systematic expansion of industrial and artisanal salt mining centred on Elavanyo, Obane, Totope, and Azizanya has progressively displaced mangrove and tidal flat habitat. The 465.76-hectare remnant provides quantitative confirmation of what community consultations had long described: a landscape whose mangrove ecosystem has been largely replaced by a competing land use, leaving only isolated fragments where once there was connected forest. Songor is accordingly framed not as a carbon stock conservation site but as a restoration and land-use planning priority, where governance-level resolution of the conflict between salt production and traditional ecosystem-dependent livelihoods is a precondition for ecological recovery.

The Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site, in the Central Region near Winneba, presents a third and distinct condition. The 2025 assessment found the mangrove extent at Muni-Pomadze to be ecologically insignificant — small, degraded fringes that do not form a mappable, functional ecosystem at the scale of the national assessment. This finding does not diminish the site's cultural, biodiversity, or tidal wetland value, which remains substantial and is reflected in its Ramsar designation. But it reframes the site's role entirely within the blue carbon programme. Muni-Pomadze is a restoration landscape, not a conservation one. Its contribution to the national blue carbon account will come from the re-establishment of an ecosystem rather than from the protection of an existing carbon stock. The community of Sankor, which has demonstrated high social cohesion, active women's groups, and ongoing locally led mangrove nursery efforts supported by A Rocha Ghana, is the primary community-level entry point for restoration programming at this site.

The concentration of 69 percent of the national resource in a single site at Keta creates a vulnerability that any credible MRV system must be designed around, not simply noted. A monitoring architecture that treats the three Ramsar sites as equivalent in strategic weight — allocating equivalent attention and resources to Keta, Songor, and Muni-Pomadze — would misallocate the surveillance capacity the country has. The design of the national MRV system described in this report reflects the asymmetry explicitly: Keta receives the deepest monitoring infrastructure, the most complete field station network, and the first full MRV pilot deployment. Songor and Muni-Pomadze receive monitoring proportionate to their respective roles as a restoration governance site and a restoration re-establishment site, with the explicit intention of progressively deepening the monitoring architecture at each as CREMA structures and restoration programmes mature.

## 2.4 The Drivers of Degradation

The condition of Ghana's blue carbon ecosystems — degraded at Songor, ecologically absent at Muni-Pomadze, and under sustained pressure even at the nationally dominant Keta — is not the outcome of natural processes operating in the absence of human activity. It is the outcome of specific, documented human decisions made under specific economic and social pressures, compounded in the case of Keta by a large-scale hydrological disruption that long precedes the current project and continues to operate at a landscape scale far beyond the reach of community-level management.

Across the 37 communities profiled in the 2025 socio-economic assessment, more than 70 percent of households reported livelihood dependency on mangrove-linked activities — principally fish smoking and fuelwood harvesting, salt production, and artisanal fishing. This is not a uniform national figure; it is driven by the immediate proximity of the majority of profiled communities to the extensive mangrove stands of the Keta Lagoon Complex, where the resource has historically supported a tightly integrated social-ecological economy. Men fish the lagoon's waters; women smoke the catch for sale in local and regional markets; and the fuel that powers thousands of smoking kilns operating in communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, Salo, and Dzita is overwhelmingly mangrove wood, with *Rhizophora racemosa* preferred for its burning characteristics.

This creates what the baseline analysis identifies as a social-ecological trap: the practices required to maintain livelihoods progressively undermine the ecological foundations that make those livelihoods possible. The artisanal fishing industry depends on healthy mangrove nursery habitat to sustain fish stocks; the fish-smoking industry depends on the same mangroves for fuel to process those stocks; and the combined demand for fuelwood from thousands of households is the primary driver of the mangrove degradation that reduces both the nursery habitat and the future fuel supply. Field assessments confirmed that approximately 70 percent of the natural mangrove cover around the Keta Lagoon Complex is already degraded or fragmented, with particularly severe losses recorded near Bomigo, Galo, and Sota since the year 2000. The feedback loop does not resolve itself through individual action; it deepens with each harvest cycle.

Compounding the livelihood-driven pressure on the Keta Lagoon is a landscape-level hydrological disruption whose origin dates to 1964, the year of Akosombo Dam construction 60 kilometres upstream on the Volta River. The dam traps more than 90 percent of the river's natural sediment load, effectively starving the Volta delta of the sand and silt required to build and maintain the narrow coastal sandspit that separates the Keta Lagoon from the Atlantic Ocean. The consequence has been a documented regime shift from a river-dominated, prograding delta system to a wave-dominated, eroding one, producing significant and continuing coastal retreat along the sandspit and exposing the lagoon's mangroves to wave energy and saltwater intrusion at increasing rates. This sediment starvation cannot be reversed by project-level intervention; it is the persistent consequence of a permanent hydrological infrastructure change. What can be managed are the knock-on effects: the historical deforestation rate at Keta has been measured at 2.15 percent per year, a trajectory that, if sustained, would compress the national blue carbon estate significantly within a generation and foreclose the carbon market opportunities that the 2025 baseline has established. The MRV system is designed in part to detect whether that deforestation rate is accelerating, decelerating, or being reversed by management interventions, and to provide the evidence base for adaptive responses.

At the Songor Ramsar Site, the dominant degradation driver is categorically different: land conversion for salt pan expansion, a process driven by industrial and artisanal salt mining operations concentrated around Elavanyo and Obane. Unlike the Keta situation — where degradation accumulates through the cumulative impact of thousands of individual fuelwood decisions — Songor's mangrove loss is the direct and geographically legible outcome of systematic land-use conversion. The 2025 land cover map provides quantitative confirmation of what communities at Totope, Pute, and Azizanya have described in qualitative terms for decades: the near-complete replacement of mangrove and tidal flat habitat by salt production infrastructure. Communities including Totope and Obane remain almost entirely dependent on mangrove wood for fuel during the dry season, creating an additional extraction pressure on what little forest cover remains, and the loss of mangrove buffering has left these communities acutely exposed to the coastal erosion that direct observations confirmed at Azizanya and Totope.

At Muni-Pomadze, where mangrove cover has effectively ceased to function as an intact ecosystem, the degradation drivers are agricultural encroachment and charcoal production, concentrated heavily in the transition forests and wetland edges near communities including Bewadze and Mankoadze. A distinctive feature of Muni-Pomadze is that long-standing spiritual taboos and traditional governance structures have preserved the integrity of sacred groves — including in communities like Bomigo and Sankor — creating pockets of ecological resilience that persist despite the pressures operating on the broader landscape. But the buffer zones surrounding these groves are being actively degraded by the same communities that uphold the taboos, a paradox that the assessment characterises as cultural resilience acting as a fragile shield whose edges are eroding under modern economic pressures.

## 2.5 Ghana's Climate, Biodiversity, and Carbon Market Obligations

Ghana's engagement with blue carbon management is not elective. It is structured by a set of formal international obligations that create specific, measurable reporting requirements — and that place the management of the three Ramsar-listed coastal wetland complexes at the intersection of the country's most consequential environmental commitments.

Under the Paris Agreement, Ghana submitted a Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) that identifies the land sector as the country's largest potential source of carbon dioxide removals. While energy-sector emissions dominate Ghana's greenhouse gas profile, the land sector — forests, coastal ecosystems, and wetlands — holds the greatest national capacity to draw carbon from the atmosphere and retain it at the scale required to offset other emission sources. Blue carbon ecosystems occupy an outsized position within this potential, precisely because their per-hectare sequestration and storage rates are so much higher than those of terrestrial forests. The translation of this ecological potential into NDC-aligned carbon accounting and reporting is a direct function of the MRV system described in this report. Without a rigorous, evidence-based system for tracking changes in mangrove extent, carbon stock, and ecosystem condition, Ghana cannot credibly report its land-sector removals — and cannot access the climate finance that credible NDC implementation is increasingly designed to unlock.

Ghana's obligations under the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF), adopted in December 2022 — the same year WACA ResIP 2 was approved — add a second formal layer of accountability. The GBF's 30×30 commitment, which requires that thirty percent of land and ocean area be effectively protected by 2030, places the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, the Songor Ramsar Site, and the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site at the centre of Ghana's biodiversity reporting architecture. These are not peripheral sites; they are internationally designated landscapes at which the government has made formal commitments to sustain ecological integrity. The MRV system provides the evidence base on which those commitments can be assessed — moving Ghana's biodiversity reporting from assertion to verification.

The Ramsar Convention itself imposes a third set of obligations. As a contracting party, Ghana is required to maintain the ecological character of its listed wetlands and to report to the Ramsar Secretariat on threats, changes, and management responses. All three Ramsar sites fall within the assessment landscape, and all three are subject to the pressures documented in this baseline. The MRV system operationalises Ghana's Ramsar reporting obligations by providing, for the first time, a systematic, quantitative mechanism for tracking changes in ecological character at each site — replacing the ad hoc reporting that has characterised Ghana's wetland management oversight in previous cycles.

Beyond treaty obligations, the 2025 baseline was explicitly designed to meet the foundational data requirements of the principal voluntary carbon market standards. Verra's Verified Carbon Standard (VCS), the Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo each require, as a precondition for project registration, a robust, independently verifiable baseline that establishes current ecosystem extent, carbon stocks, degradation rates, and socio-economic conditions at the project start. The 2025 national baseline — integrating high-resolution geospatial mapping, carbon inventory through Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs), ecosystem health assessment, environmental pressure profiling, governance readiness analysis, and socio-economic community profiling — constitutes exactly that foundational dataset. The 319 participatory ground-truth reference points that validate the geospatial classification, the community-level participatory resource mapping that was cross-referenced with satellite data, and the explicit attention to Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) throughout the field programme collectively ensure that the baseline meets the participatory and social standards that Plan Vivo and the Verra Climate, Community and Biodiversity Standards require. Ghana is therefore positioned, at the close of the 2025 baseline programme, to proceed to carbon project design on a technically credible, market-compliant foundation — a position that was not available before this work was done.

## 2.6 The 2025 National Baseline as the Year 0 Foundation

The six baseline workstreams completed between June and August 2025 by Ecological Nest Ltd, under the WACA Blue Carbon Assessment commissioned by MESTI and MLNR, collectively constitute the Year 0 reference for Ghana's national blue carbon monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) system. Every future measurement of carbon stock change, every assessment of restoration progress, and every report submitted against Ghana's climate and biodiversity obligations will be benchmarked against what was found and documented during this programme. The integrity of that benchmarking depends entirely on the integrity of the baseline from which it begins.

The six workstreams were: geospatial mangrove extent mapping, which produced the national land cover baseline validated with 319 participatory ground-truth reference points; the carbon inventory, which established above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (BGB), deadwood, and soil organic carbon (SOC) stocks at Permanent Sample Plots across the priority landscapes; ecosystem health assessment, which characterised the physical and biological condition of the wetland systems at Keta and Ada across 57 monitoring stations at Keta and 23 at Ada; environmental pressure profiling, which documented the degradation drivers, community dependency patterns, and livelihood-pressure feedback loops across 37 profiled communities; governance readiness analysis, which assessed the institutional landscape — formal, customary, and emerging — within which a co-management system could be built; and socio-economic profiling, which established the demographic, livelihood, and social vulnerability context for benefit-sharing design and safeguard planning.

These workstreams were not parallel projects that arrived at separate conclusions. They were designed as an integrated baseline package in which each workstream's outputs cross-validate and contextualise the others. The geospatial mapping that found only 465.76 hectares of mangrove at Songor is validated by the environmental pressure assessment's documentation of salt pan expansion at Elavanyo and Obane. The carbon inventory's PSP network at Keta is calibrated against the ecosystem health monitoring station data at those 57 stations. The community dependency rate of more than 70 percent reported in the socio-economic profiling is geospatially validated by the spatial correlation between the highest-dependency communities — Dzita, Salo, Agbatsivi, Bomigo, Galotse — and the densest mangrove stands in the Keta land cover map. The whole is designed to be more than the sum of its parts, and its design logic is the logic of a credible MRV system: any claim about carbon stocks, ecosystem change, or community impact can be traced to multiple independent lines of evidence.

This baseline is the Year 0 reference in a precise technical sense. It establishes the area boundaries, carbon stock values, ecosystem condition metrics, pressure indicators, governance arrangements, and community profiles against which all subsequent MRV cycles will measure change. The date of the baseline — completed August 2025 — is the start date from which additionality will be demonstrated for any carbon project operating within the assessment landscape. Any restoration activity, any conservation intervention, and any change in mangrove extent, biomass, or soil carbon will be reported as a departure from what was documented in these six workstreams. The rigour with which the baseline was assembled — its methodological transparency, its participatory validation, its multi-workstream cross-referencing — is therefore not merely a quality standard for the 2025 programme. It is the precondition for the credibility of every carbon and biodiversity report that Ghana will produce from this landscape for years to come.
