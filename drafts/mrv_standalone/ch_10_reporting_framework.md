# Chapter 10: Reporting Framework

---

## 10.1 Purpose of the Reporting Framework

A Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system generates no practical value until its data becomes decisions. The ecological measurements, pressure surveillance records, and governance indicators described in Chapters 4 through 7 of this report exist to support a specific end: the production of credible, structured, and publicly accountable reporting outputs that translate raw monitoring data into governance intelligence, climate finance documentation, and evidence for national policy. The Reporting Framework described in this chapter defines how that translation happens — what is reported, when, by whom, to which audiences, and through which institutional channels — and establishes the quality assurance and transparency standards that give the reporting system its credibility.

The framework operates across four nested cycles, from the quarterly field returns submitted by Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) members and Community Change Agents (CCAs) in communities such as Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, Salo, Dzita, Pute, Elavanyo, Obane, and Sankor, through to the Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report produced by the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI) and the Five-Year Comprehensive Review that recalibrates the national baseline every three to five years. These cycles are not independent; each feeds the next. Community-level observations become district-level consolidated data; district data becomes national reporting inputs; national reports feed Ghana's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) obligations under the Paris Agreement and, where applicable, the audit-grade documentation required by international carbon market standards including Verra, Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo.

The design philosophy is that reporting serves multiple audiences simultaneously, and that designing the system for its most demanding audience — the independent carbon market verifier — produces a level of methodological rigour and documentary completeness that also satisfies the requirements of every less demanding user of the same data. A reporting framework robust enough to withstand a Verra audit is robust enough for a World Bank programme review, a national greenhouse gas inventory process, and a community feedback meeting. The reporting architecture described here is built to that standard from the community level upward.

## 10.2 The Reporting Cycle

### Quarterly Field Returns

The operational foundation of the reporting cycle is the quarterly field return submitted to the district Wildlife Division by CREMA members and CCAs across each blue carbon landscape. These standardised data sheets capture the three functional monitoring layers in their most granular form: pressure indicator records (fuelwood harvesting signals, fresh cutting evidence, charcoal production activity, salt pan expansion incursions), restoration survival checks at the early action hubs of Dzita in the Keta Lagoon Complex, Pute in the Songor Ramsar Site, and Sankor in the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site, and governance observations including CREMA meeting attendance records, benefit-sharing distribution confirmation, and grievance submissions.

Water and soil monitoring data from the 57 stations in the Keta Lagoon monitoring network and the 23 stations at Ada are compiled on the same quarterly schedule, following the standardised field protocols described in Chapter 7. Each station records water quality parameters — pH, temperature, conductivity, salinity, total dissolved solids (TDS), dissolved oxygen, and oxidation-reduction potential (ORP) — and soil health parameters including nitrate, phosphate, potassium, pH, moisture content, humus content, and temperature. The quarterly compilation of these data points across 80 stations provides a landscape-scale picture of ecosystem condition at a temporal resolution sufficient to detect emerging stress signals before they produce irreversible ecological change.

Quarterly field returns are not simply data submissions; they are the institutional record of the community monitoring system. The standardised data sheet format ensures comparability across quarters and across sites, enabling trend analysis that a narrative report alone cannot provide. The Wildlife Division's role at the district level is to receive, validate, and aggregate these returns — checking for internal consistency, flagging anomalies for field verification, and producing the consolidated dataset that feeds the annual district reporting process. The quarterly cadence also creates accountability: a community or CREMA that fails to submit returns triggers a welfare check and a capacity support response, not a punitive sanction, but a documented gap in the monitoring record that must be addressed before national reporting can claim complete coverage.

### Annual District Reports

The Metropolitan, Municipal and District Assemblies (MMDAs) and the Wildlife Division compile consolidated annual reports to MLNR and MESTI covering all three monitoring layers across their district area. These reports aggregate the four quarterly field returns received from each CREMA within the district, cross-reference pressure indicator trends against the ecological monitoring data from satellite analysis, and summarise governance performance including CREMA leadership composition (including compliance with the minimum 40% female representation standard), grievance redress outcomes, and benefit-sharing transparency records.

Annual district reports carry a specific analytical responsibility that goes beyond data consolidation. The Wildlife Division's district officers are positioned to observe trends that individual quarterly returns cannot capture — gradual shifts in pressure geography, seasonal patterns in harvesting intensity, emerging land tenure conflicts of the kind that characterise the boundary between salt pan operations and remaining mangrove cover at Elavanyo and Obane in the Songor district. The district report is therefore required to provide not just aggregated statistics but an interpretive narrative: an account of what the year's data means for ecosystem status and what, if anything, the district recommends by way of management response. District reports are submitted annually to MLNR and MESTI as the formal input to the national consolidation process.

### Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report

The Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report is the primary national reporting product of Ghana's Blue Carbon MRV System. It is produced jointly by MLNR and MESTI, drawing on district reports from all relevant districts, the annual satellite extent change analysis conducted on Sentinel-2 imagery at 10 to 20 metre resolution using the Random Forest algorithm on Google Earth Engine (GEE), and any Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) interim observations. The report is formally issued as a government document under the institutional authority of both ministries.

The Annual Report covers seven substantive components. The national mangrove extent change summary documents the year-on-year change in coastal mangrove area across all 35 coastal districts and 550 kilometres of coastline, derived from the annual Sentinel-2 classification update. At the landscape level, this summary disaggregates extent change for the Keta Lagoon Complex — which holds 20,717.95 hectares and approximately 69% of Ghana's national mangrove total — the Songor Ramsar Site's 465.76 hectares of fragmented mangrove, and the ecologically insignificant but strategically important restoration target at Muni-Pomadze. The carbon stock status section reports any interim carbon stock observations since the Year 0 baseline without claiming the precision of a full PSP re-measurement; it is a conditional assessment, noting any disturbance events or restoration progress that may have altered stocks and identifying the direction of likely change pending the next five-year re-measurement. The pressure trend analysis summarises the Layer II — Environmental Pressure Surveillance findings for the year: trends in fuelwood harvesting incidence, fuel source transition rates in community surveys, land conversion detections, charcoal production hotspot changes, and the status of known pressure concentrations including the fish-smoking fuelwood demand from communities across the Keta fringe. The governance and safeguard performance section reports against the Layer III — Governance and Safeguard Assurance indicators, including CREMA composition statistics disaggregated by gender and youth representation, grievance redress performance metrics, and conflict mediation outcomes. The restoration progress section reports survival rates, canopy cover establishment, and seedling recruitment at Dzita, Pute, and Sankor. The adaptive management actions section documents what management interventions were triggered during the year by monitoring signals and what their outcomes were. Finally, the strategic recommendations section provides the analytical conclusions that the year's monitoring record supports for policy and programme planning purposes.

The Annual Report is the document through which the entire monitoring architecture earns its operational justification. It is the product that government officials, development partners, and future carbon market reviewers will assess as the primary indicator of whether the MRV system is functioning as designed. The reporting cycle that produces it must therefore be completed with sufficient lead time to enable MESTI and MLNR review before publication — a process described in Section 10.5 of this chapter.

### Five-Year Comprehensive Review

The Five-Year Comprehensive Review is a full re-measurement and re-assessment cycle aligned with the PSP biomass re-measurement schedule, which occurs every three to five years as defined in the carbon accounting protocol (Chapter 9). The review produces a Carbon Stock Update Report that recalibrates the Year 0 baseline with measured PSP data — updated above-ground biomass (AGB) and below-ground biomass (BGB) estimates, re-collected soil organic carbon (SOC) cores to 100 centimetres depth, and a revised deadwood assessment at Salo PSP 1 and any additional PSPs established during the interval period. The Carbon Stock Update Report includes updated biomass and soil carbon estimates, an emissions and removals summary for the interval period, a revised uncertainty assessment, a risk-adjusted carbon balance, and a methodological consistency statement confirming that accounting methods remain aligned with IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance and the requirements of the relevant carbon market standards.

Beyond the carbon stock recalibration, the Five-Year Comprehensive Review also triggers a strategic review of MRV priorities. Monitoring systems require recalibration of their own design: indicators that were correctly designed for Year 0 conditions may need adjustment as the landscape evolves, as CREMA coverage expands, or as new pressures emerge that the original indicator set did not anticipate. The Five-Year Review is the institutionally scheduled moment at which MLNR, MESTI, the Wildlife Division, and community representatives formally assess whether the monitoring framework remains fit for purpose, and initiate any revisions that the accumulated monitoring record indicates are necessary. This adaptive management dimension is integral to the framework's long-term integrity and is not an optional supplement to the technical re-measurement process.

## 10.3 Reporting Audiences and Their Needs

Ghana's National Blue Carbon MRV System serves a diverse set of institutional and community audiences, each of which uses reporting outputs differently and requires information presented in ways calibrated to its specific decision-making context.

MESTI and MLNR are simultaneously the producers and primary users of national-level reporting. In their role as national policy institutions, they use Annual MRV Report outputs to assess programme performance, brief ministers and cross-ministerial coordination bodies, and populate the national greenhouse gas inventory update that feeds Ghana's NDC reporting obligations. MESTI's specific need is for climate reporting integration — the land sector contribution to Ghana's NDC commitments, including blue carbon conservation and restoration, requires an annual data feed that the MRV system is specifically designed to provide. MLNR's need is for operational and strategic management information: extent change summaries and pressure trend data are the inputs that inform decisions about where district Wildlife Division capacity should be concentrated, whether enforcement action is warranted at particular hotspot sites, and whether the restoration programme is achieving the performance benchmarks required to justify continued investment.

The Forestry Commission's Wildlife Division operates at the interface between national policy and community reality. Its reporting needs are operational rather than strategic: site-level pressure monitoring data, restoration survival metrics, and governance performance indicators that enable informed decisions about where field officer time and limited enforcement resources are most needed. The Wildlife Division uses district-level consolidation reports — which it has also helped to produce — as the primary management information source for planning the following year's field programme. A report that tells the Wildlife Division only what it already knows from its own officers has failed its audience; the value of the reporting system is that it aggregates community observations into patterns that no individual field officer can see from their single district vantage point.

The World Bank and the WACA programme have donor accountability and development effectiveness reporting requirements. World Bank missions and periodic implementation support reviews assess whether the investment represented by WACA ResIP 2 (Project P175625, approved December 2022) is producing the ecological, governance, and livelihood outcomes that the project appraisal committed to. The Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report provides the structured, institutionally validated evidence base for these assessments. The specificity of data required for World Bank review — measurable changes in mangrove extent, evidence of CREMA formation and governance function, restoration survival rates at the early action hubs — is exactly the content that the Annual Report's seven substantive components are designed to deliver.

Carbon market verifiers operating under Verra, Gold Standard, or Plan Vivo require the highest evidentiary standard of any audience the reporting framework serves. An independent auditor conducting a validation or verification assessment needs a complete chain of custody from raw field measurements to reported carbon stocks: traceable plot-level data, documented QA/QC procedures, archived laboratory reports from soil carbon analysis, version-controlled database records showing each data entry and any subsequent corrections, and a clear mapping between field measurements and the allometric equations and emission factors used in stock calculations. The Five-Year Carbon Stock Update Report, produced under the full re-measurement cycle, serves as the primary technical document for market verification purposes. The Annual Report provides the intermediate annual evidence that verifiers use to assess whether significant reversal events have occurred between full re-measurement cycles and whether the governance and pressure management systems are functioning as designed to protect permanence.

Communities and CREMAs occupy a foundational position in the reporting chain — as primary data collectors, they have both a practical stake in understanding what their data shows and a governance right to receive feedback on the outcomes their monitoring work supports. Reporting to communities is not a transparency add-on; it is a functional requirement for sustained community monitoring participation. A CREMA whose members submit quarterly field returns year after year without receiving meaningful feedback on what those returns reveal — about the status of the ecosystem they steward, about the governance performance of their own institutions, and about the benefit-sharing outcomes linked to carbon programme performance — will progressively lose the motivational engagement that makes community monitoring work. Community-level reporting therefore takes the form of accessible, locally relevant feedback sessions held at each landscape — at Dzita for Keta communities, at Pute for the Songor landscape, and at Sankor for Muni-Pomadze — providing summary ecological status updates, restoration performance figures, and confirmation of benefit-sharing distributions in forms accessible to community members regardless of formal literacy levels.

## 10.4 NDC Reporting Linkage

Ghana's third Nationally Determined Contribution commits the country to specific mitigation and adaptation outcomes across economic sectors. The land sector component of Ghana's NDC explicitly includes blue carbon ecosystem conservation and restoration as a contributing pathway toward national emission reduction and removal targets. The Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report is the data layer that makes this land sector contribution reportable — without it, the blue carbon component of the NDC commitment exists as a policy intention without a quantified evidence base.

The linkage between the MRV reporting cycle and the national greenhouse gas inventory process operates through MESTI, which coordinates Ghana's Biennial Transparency Reports (BTRs) to the UNFCCC under the Paris Agreement's Enhanced Transparency Framework. The BTR's land sector chapter draws on inventory data for wetland and forest cover change, and the Annual MRV Report's national mangrove extent change summary — derived from the Sentinel-2 annual classification update and validated against the 2025 Year 0 baseline — provides the annual input to the wetland land use change category within that inventory. The carbon stock status and pressure trend components of the Annual Report provide supplementary evidence for the inventory compiler's assessment of whether the coastal wetland sector is a net sink or net source in the reporting year.

This linkage has a structural significance that goes beyond data provision. When Ghana reports on its NDC progress under the Paris Agreement, the credibility of the blue carbon land sector contribution depends entirely on the quality of the underlying monitoring and reporting system. An NDC commitment to conserve 30,025 hectares of coastal mangrove and restore mangrove cover at Muni-Pomadze and Songor cannot be verified by international review teams unless there is a documented, methodologically rigorous system producing annual evidence of whether those commitments are being met. The National Blue Carbon MRV Report is that system's formal output. Its institutional production by MLNR and MESTI — government ministries, not project consultants — and its grounding in an IPCC-aligned methodology ensures that the land sector contribution to Ghana's NDC is reported through a chain of institutional accountability that meets the transparency requirements of the Enhanced Transparency Framework.

In practice, the Annual MRV Report's timing must be coordinated with MESTI's national inventory preparation cycle. Ghana's BTR submissions require finalised land sector data well in advance of submission deadlines, which in turn requires that the Annual MRV Report is completed and formally cleared by both ministries on a schedule that feeds into the inventory process rather than arriving after it. The institutional calendar for Annual MRV Report production — field data compilation, district report submission, national consolidation, internal review, and formal publication — is therefore structured with this NDC reporting dependency in view.

## 10.5 Quality Assurance in Reporting

The reporting system's credibility rests not only on the quality of the underlying monitoring data but on the rigour of the review processes through which that data is validated before it appears in a published report. QA/QC responsibility is distributed across the institutional tiers, with each level of the reporting hierarchy bearing responsibility for the quality of the data it submits upward.

At the community level, CCAs and CREMA members are trained in standardised data sheet completion, with double-entry verification for quantitative measurements and internal consistency checks built into the field return format. Community data that fails consistency checks — a survival rate figure that implausibly reverses a confirmed mortality trend, a water quality reading that falls outside the plausible range for the monitoring station's position in the tidal gradient — is flagged for Wildlife Division follow-up before being accepted into the district database. This first-line quality control is deliberately embedded in the field return format rather than imposed as a post-hoc check: it reduces the volume of erroneous data entering the system and builds data quality habits within community monitoring teams.

At the district level, the Wildlife Division conducts a formal data validation review before submitting district consolidation reports to MLNR and MESTI. This review includes cross-checking community-level field returns against the independent satellite analysis for the same period — if satellite imagery shows a new land conversion event in a CREMA monitoring zone, and no community return for that quarter flagged a cutting or conversion signal, the Wildlife Division investigates the discrepancy before finalising the district report. The independence of the satellite analysis from the community reporting system is precisely what makes this cross-validation meaningful: the two data streams are methodologically distinct, and agreement between them strengthens the credibility of both.

At the national level, MLNR and MESTI conduct an internal review of all national-level reporting before publication. The Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report is circulated for internal review by designated technical officers at both ministries — the technical custodians of the MRV system at MLNR and the climate reporting focal point at MESTI — before formal clearance and publication. This review assesses methodological consistency with prior years' reporting (including consistency with the approved numbers and framings in the baseline package), factual accuracy of stated statistics, and appropriate attribution of data sources. Any substantive change to a previously reported figure — for example, a revision to the Year 0 baseline extent following additional quality assurance of the Sentinel-2 classification — must be accompanied by a documented explanation of the revision rationale and recorded in the version history.

Document control and version management for the Annual Report follows a formal versioning protocol. Each Annual Report is issued with a version number, an issue date, and a summary of any revisions from the draft reviewed by district officers. Draft versions are clearly watermarked and distinguished from the final published document. The final published document is archived in the national MRV database under timestamped, version-controlled records alongside the underlying datasets from which it was produced. Previous years' reports are retained in full as part of the permanent audit trail. This version management discipline is not bureaucratic formality; it is the document control practice that carbon market verifiers will scrutinise when they assess whether Ghana's reporting record demonstrates the methodological consistency that Verra, Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo standards require across multi-year monitoring periods.

For carbon market reporting contexts, the QA/QC framework extends to include a capacity for independent review by a third-party verification body. Independent review is not a routine part of every Annual Report cycle; it is activated when a project developer is preparing for a formal validation or verification assessment under a carbon market standard, or when MLNR or MESTI determines that external assurance would strengthen the credibility of a specific reporting cycle for donor accountability purposes. The institutional separation between the data-collecting community and district tiers, the analytically independent satellite monitoring layer, and the version-controlled national database create the conditions for independent review to be conducted efficiently — a verifier can assess the full data chain from community field sheet to national report total without requiring the national institutions to reconstruct records that should already exist in the archive.

## 10.6 Public Disclosure and Transparency

Transparency in MRV reporting is not a supplementary governance principle; it is a functional requirement for the system's credibility. An MRV system whose outputs are known only to the institutions that produce them cannot serve as the basis for carbon market participation, NDC reporting, or community accountability — all of which depend on the ability of external parties to scrutinise and confirm the claims being made. The public disclosure framework establishes which reports are publicly accessible, in what format, and through which channels.

The Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report is publicly disclosed upon formal clearance by MLNR and MESTI. It is made available through the MLNR and MESTI government portals, ensuring that it is accessible to civil society organisations, academic institutions, development partners, and any international reviewer conducting due diligence on Ghana's blue carbon programme. The report is published in PDF format with a permanent document identifier, making it citable in subsequent publications and enabling year-on-year comparisons across the public archive. Summary versions of the Annual Report, translated into accessible formats suitable for community and district audiences, are distributed through the Wildlife Division's district offices and community feedback sessions at Dzita, Pute, and Sankor.

The web-based spatial monitoring platform, described in Chapter 8, provides the real-time and near-real-time transparency layer that static annual reports cannot deliver. Public summary dashboards on the platform display the most recent mangrove extent change analysis — current and historical Sentinel-2 classifications for the Keta Lagoon Complex, Songor Ramsar Site, and Muni-Pomadze — pressure hotspot mapping showing active monitoring alerts, and restoration progress visualisations for the early action hubs. These dashboards are designed for public access without authentication, providing any interested party — including community members in Agbatsivi or Galotse checking on their local landscape, a World Bank programme officer reviewing progress between missions, or a journalist reporting on Ghana's coastal management — with direct visual access to the current state of the monitoring evidence base. Restricted institutional data views, accessible only to authorised CREMA, district, and national users with assigned credentials, provide the full dataset behind the public summaries, including PSP measurement records, raw field returns, and laboratory reports.

For carbon market purposes, formal reporting to Verra, Gold Standard, or Plan Vivo registries follows the specific requirements of each standard's verification process. Project-level documents — Project Design Documents (PDDs), Monitoring Reports, and Verification Reports — are publicly posted on the relevant registry platforms in accordance with the transparency requirements of those standards. This registry-level disclosure provides the global carbon market community with access to Ghana's project-specific claims and the auditor findings that validate them. The chain from community quarterly field return to carbon registry posting is, in principle, fully transparent: each step is documented, each document is archived, and the registry posting is the publicly accessible terminus of a documented evidentiary chain.

Transparency is itself a governance safeguard. When benefit-sharing distributions, governance composition statistics, and restoration performance figures are publicly disclosed, communities retain the capacity to verify that the claims made on their behalf accurately reflect the reality they observe on the ground. A discrepancy between a published restoration survival rate and the condition of seedlings that Sankor community members can see with their own eyes is not merely a data quality problem; it is a governance failure that transparency enables to be identified and corrected. The reporting framework is therefore designed so that the people who generate the monitoring data — the CREMA members and CCAs who walk the transects, install the water quality sensors, and record the governance observations — can access the processed outputs that their work has produced, and can raise concerns if those outputs appear inconsistent with their direct experience of the landscape. This community verification function is the reporting system's most fundamental accountability mechanism, and it operates not through formal audit but through the social intelligence of people who know their own ecosystems.
