# Chapter 7: Wetland Ecosystem Monitoring (Keta and Ada)

---

## 7.1 Rationale for the Keta and Ada Monitoring Network

The decision to establish a dedicated, operational wetland monitoring network at Keta and Ada — before the equivalent infrastructure existed at any other project site — was not arbitrary. It followed directly from the ecological reality revealed by the December 2025 baseline assessment: that these two sites together define the strategic core of Ghana's national blue carbon programme, and that understanding how their ecosystems are behaving in real time is not optional for sound management. They are the two sites where ecological dynamics are both most significant and least understood.

The case for Keta is quantitative and unambiguous. The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site contains an estimated 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves — approximately 69% of Ghana's entire coastal mangrove resource. This concentration is not a gradual ecological transition from surrounding landscapes; it is a discrete, immense expanse of *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* stands extending in large, continuous stretches between the main lagoon body and the Volta River estuary, separated from the Gulf of Guinea by a coastal ridge as narrow as 0.92 kilometres at its narrowest point. A site of this scale, containing this proportion of the national carbon asset, requires its own monitoring infrastructure. No sampling approach designed for a smaller or more typical coastal wetland can adequately characterise the spatial variability in water chemistry, sediment conditions, and habitat quality across a 530 km² wetland complex. The 57 sampling stations established for Keta under the Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system represent the minimum spatial coverage necessary to detect ecologically meaningful variation across the site's distinct sub-zones: the Anyanui-Dzita area near the Volta estuary, the Salo-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi corridor where well-developed *Rhizophora* stands are concentrated, the proposed restoration zones at Aborlorvi and Norlopi, and the main lagoon bank stretching from Afiadenyigba through Havedzi, Vodza, and Woe to the Anloga area.

The case for Ada is structurally different but equally compelling. The Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve retains only 465.76 hectares of mangroves — 2% of the national total — distributed as fragmented patches across a landscape where salt pan expansion at Elavanyo, Obane, and surrounding areas has progressively displaced wetland vegetation. By area, Songor is ecologically depleted. But the Songor system is hydrologically connected to the Volta River estuary, encompasses three distinct habitat types — brackish lagoon, riverine floodplain, and marine coastal fringe — and is surrounded by communities including Pute, Totokpoe, Azizanya, and Wasakuse whose livelihoods intersect the wetland in complex and contested ways. The 23 monitoring stations at Ada are positioned to track the ecological condition of what remains, to detect the signals of further degradation before they become irreversible, and to provide the baseline evidence against which any future restoration investment can be assessed. At a site defined by its ecological pressures rather than its intact stocks, pressure surveillance and trend detection are the monitoring system's primary functions.

Together, Keta and Ada form the two-site nucleus of Ghana's national wetland ecosystem monitoring system. The combined 80-station network — 57 at Keta, 23 at Ada — provides the empirical foundation for monitoring Ghana's principal blue carbon conservation site alongside its principal blue carbon restoration opportunity.

## 7.2 The Monitoring Station Network

The 57 sampling stations at the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site were positioned using a supervised Geographic Information System (GIS) map of the study area, developed in consultation with Community Change Agents (CCAs) — community members trained in field data collection during the December 2025 baseline assessment. The positioning logic was spatial and ecological rather than administrative: stations were distributed to capture the full range of habitat types and pressure zones across the complex, rather than clustered around accessible entry points. Because the Keta complex is large — extending approximately 40 kilometres along the coast — and because its ecological conditions vary significantly between the fresher, Volta-influenced waters of the northwestern section and the more saline, evaporation-concentrated lagoon waters of the south and east, spatial coverage was designed to represent this gradient. Stations in the Anyanui-Dzita corridor, where the anomalous low pH of 2.7 recorded at Anyanui-Tunu road during the baseline assessment requires ongoing investigation, are among the highest monitoring priority in the network. Stations in the Salo-Galotse zone, where salinity values of 10.6 to 40 parts per thousand (ppt) support some of the best-developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands in the complex, provide the health benchmark against which degraded sub-zones are compared. The proposed restoration sites at Aborlorvi, adjacent to Norlopi, are covered by stations specifically positioned to detect whether hydrological conditions at those sites are suitable for mangrove re-establishment — a question that turns on soil nutrient availability, water salinity, and sediment moisture, all of which the monitoring protocol directly measures.

At the Songor Ramsar Site, the 23 stations were distributed across the three main habitat components of the system. The lagoon and creek network — including the Pute lagoon intake, Togbloku lagoon, Wasakusewayo restoration site, and Obane creek — is covered by stations whose primary function is to track salinity dynamics, dissolved oxygen, and oxidation-reduction potential in a water body that is largely closed off from the sea and therefore highly responsive to changes in freshwater inflow from the Volta. The estuary-proximate stations at Azizanya and Ayigbo provide the comparison baseline, registering the Volta River's diluting influence and distinguishing it from the higher-salinity, higher-conductivity conditions prevailing in the interior lagoon. The creek systems at Futuenya and Big Ada capture the intermediate hydrological zone, where the interaction between tidal exchange, river flow, and evaporation produces some of the most ecologically sensitive conditions in the landscape.

Under the approved quarterly monitoring schedule, field teams visit each of the 80 stations four times per year. This cadence was designed to provide temporal density sufficient to detect seasonal variation — the Keta lagoon in particular exhibits significant shifts in salinity and conductivity between the dry season, when evaporation concentrates lagoon water to hyper-saline conditions, and wet season periods when freshwater inflow dilutes the system. The December 2025 baseline data was collected during a period nominally classified as the dry season but in which unseasonal rainfall occurred, demonstrating that a single annual snapshot cannot capture the system's behaviour. Four sampling rounds per year, distributed across the seasons, allow the network to build a multi-year record of normal seasonal oscillation against which anomalies — pollution incidents, salinity spikes linked to altered freshwater inflow, sudden drops in dissolved oxygen — can be detected and attributed. The quarterly schedule also reflects the operational reality of community-based monitoring: CCAs trained during the baseline assessment can maintain the station network at this cadence with appropriate equipment and institutional support from the Wildlife Division, which sits within the national MRV institutional tier responsible for district-level validation.

## 7.3 Water Quality Parameters

At each of the 80 stations, water quality is assessed for nine physico-chemical parameters using calibrated field instruments. The December 2025 baseline assessment used the Apera SX751 multiparameter water testing device, with all station positions georeferenced using a Garmin Inreach Explorer. The parameters and their ecological relevance are as follows.

**pH** measures the acidity or alkalinity of the water column and directly governs the bioavailability of nutrients, the biochemistry of decomposition, and the physiological tolerances of aquatic biota. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, the baseline assessment found mean pH values ranging from 6.8 to 7.96 across sampling stations, with ecologically significant variation between sub-zones. The Salo, Galotse, and Genui area — where *Rhizophora racemosa* stands are most developed — recorded pH values of 6.5 to 7.6, within the optimal range for mangrove productivity. The main lagoon bank from Afiadenyigba to Anloga recorded values of 7.8 to 8.2, influenced by temperature variation and reduced freshwater input. The exceptional pH reading of 2.7 recorded at the Anyanui-Tunu road station during the baseline assessment represents a critical monitoring signal: highly acidic water at the edge of mangrove habitat indicates potential acid sulphate soil conditions, which can become actively toxic to *Rhizophora* root systems if exposed through drainage or hydrological disruption. Quarterly monitoring at the Anyanui stations is essential to determine whether this is a spatially isolated anomaly or an expanding condition. At Songor, Ada, pH values of 7.3 to 7.76 were recorded across most stations, with slightly elevated values of 8.1 to 8.6 at the Futue and Luhue creeks, reflecting the influence of wind-induced mixing and carbonate buffering in the open lagoon system.

**Water temperature** affects dissolved oxygen solubility, metabolic rates of aquatic organisms, and the rate of organic matter decomposition in sediments — all of which directly influence carbon cycling within the wetland. At Keta, mean temperatures varied between 30.24°C and 31.3°C, with an afternoon peak range of 31.19°C to 37.3°C reflecting solar heating in shallow water bodies. At Songor, the mean range was 31.2°C to 34.6°C. Temperature monitoring provides the seasonal context within which other parameters are interpreted: an elevated salinity reading in the dry season is expected and ecologically manageable; an elevated salinity reading combined with elevated temperatures and depressed dissolved oxygen in the wet season may indicate a hydrological disruption requiring investigation.

**Electrical conductivity** measures the water's ability to conduct an electric current and is a direct proxy for the total concentration of dissolved ions — primarily sodium, chloride, calcium, and magnesium — in the water column. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, the highest conductivity of 63.1 μS was recorded at Dzita, a station near the main lagoon body where evaporative concentration is highest. The lowest values, approximately 69.3 μS, were recorded at stations influenced by seasonal rainfall. At Songor, conductivity ranged from 31.4 to 50.4 μS, with the highest values in the interior creeks and lagoon and relatively low values of 6.23 μS at Azizanya and 2.64 μS at Ayigbo, close to the Volta River estuary where freshwater dilution is greatest. Conductivity trends over successive quarters at Keta will be among the most sensitive early indicators of changes in the hydrological regime, particularly those attributable to altered freshwater discharge patterns related to dam operations.

**Salinity** is the most ecologically informative single parameter in this monitoring network. In coastal mangrove systems, salinity determines which species can establish, how rapidly they grow, and how much organic carbon their below-ground biomass and associated soils accumulate. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, salinity values ranged from 4.3 ppt at stations under freshwater influence to 40 ppt at Dzita — a hyper-saline condition that reflects the Keta lagoon's partial isolation from the sea and its exposure to intense evaporation during the dry season. Sites in the Anyanui, Tunu, Akplotorkor, Saviatula, and Genui sub-zones recorded salinity values between 10.6 and 40 ppt, conditions that the baseline assessment confirmed are supportive of active mangrove development. At Songor, the highest salinity in the lagoon — 28.6 ppt at Pute lagoon and 28.2 ppt at the Pute creek lagoon intake — contrasts sharply with the near-freshwater conditions of 0.2 ppt recorded at the Futue creek bridge where the Volta River's influence is strongest. This salinity gradient is not ecologically neutral: the mangroves remaining at Songor are concentrated in the intermediate salinity zones between the hyper-saline ponds associated with salt production and the near-freshwater conditions of the Volta estuary. Monitoring salinity quarterly across the 23 Ada stations directly tracks whether the ecological space available to mangroves at Songor is expanding or contracting.

**Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)** complements conductivity by quantifying the aggregate mass of dissolved material per unit volume of water. At Keta, TDS peaked at 88.1 parts per million (ppm) at Dzita, declining to approximately 100 ppm at stations under rainfall influence. At Songor, TDS reached 57.3 ppm at Pute lagoon and fell to 1.95 ppm at Ayigbo bridge. TDS integrates contributions from all dissolved substances — including suspended organic material and biological exudate — making it a useful cross-check on conductivity measurements and a sensitive indicator of water clarity conditions that affect light penetration and aquatic primary productivity.

**Dissolved oxygen (DO)** is the parameter most directly indicative of aquatic ecosystem health. Adequate dissolved oxygen sustains fish populations, supports the aerobic decomposition of organic matter, and maintains the redox conditions in surface sediments that determine whether organic carbon is preserved or mineralised. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, DO values at stations such as Anyanui, Agblodome, Apklotorkor, Saviatula, and Salo were relatively high, influenced by shallow water depth, wind agitation, high water temperatures, and the photosynthetic activity of well-developed mangrove and aquatic plant communities. The relatively moderate DO of 13.23 mg/l recorded at Dzita alongside its high salinity and conductivity values reflects the complex interplay between evaporative concentration and biological oxygen demand in that sub-zone. At Songor, DO ranged from 5.51 to 7.13 mg/l across most stations, with the highest values of 6.09 mg/l and 6.91 mg/l recorded at the Futue and Luhue creek bridges respectively, benefiting from open lagoon conditions, high wind exposure, and the absence of significant algal mat growth on sediments. A sustained depression of DO values at any station — particularly in the mangrove creek stations — is one of the clearest early warning signals of organic pollution, eutrophication, or the onset of anaerobic conditions in the sediment column.

**Oxidation-Reduction Potential (ORP)** quantifies the water's capacity to act as either an oxidising or a reducing agent, and is directly related to the presence of dissolved oxygen and the thermodynamic conditions governing nutrient cycling, metal speciation, and decomposition. Higher positive ORP values indicate greater oxygen availability and self-cleaning capacity in the water. At Keta, the relatively moderate ORP of 160 mV recorded at Dzita alongside its high salinity reflects wetland waterlogged conditions typical of mangrove habitats, where the presence of anoxic micro-zones influences the availability of iron, manganese, and other elements critical to soil fertility. At Songor, ORP values in the lagoon and interior creeks ranged from 832 to 1,236 mV — substantially higher than at the Volta estuary stations, where values of 11 to 76.6 mV were recorded near the freshwater inflow. This strong ORP gradient across the Ada network signals a site where oxidation conditions vary dramatically across short distances, creating a mosaic of anaerobic and aerobic micro-environments that governs where mangrove regeneration is possible and where sediment carbon is stable.

**Resistivity** is the inverse of conductivity and provides a complementary measure of water purity: in low-salinity, low-conductivity water, resistivity is high; in high-salinity, high-conductivity water, resistivity is low. The high resistivity value of 15.8 KΩ recorded at Dzita, despite that station's high conductivity, reflects the specific ionic composition of hyper-saline lagoon water as distinct from, for example, a polluted or chemically contaminated freshwater source. Tracking resistivity alongside conductivity over successive monitoring rounds helps distinguish between salinity-driven changes in water chemistry — which are ecologically expected — and pollution-driven changes, which are not.

Taken together, changes in salinity, dissolved oxygen, and conductivity across consecutive quarterly monitoring rounds are the most powerful diagnostic combination for detecting altered hydrology at Keta. The Akosombo Dam, constructed in 1964, now traps more than 90% of the Volta River's sediment load before it reaches the coast. This sediment starvation is the landscape-level existential risk to the Keta Lagoon Complex: without the sediment and freshwater once delivered by the Volta, the lagoon is subject to progressive salinisation, the blockage of inter-connecting channels, and the disruption of the natural drainage system that historically regulated lagoon water levels. A sustained upward trend in salinity or conductivity at the Anyanui stations — closest to the former Volta influence — combined with declining DO in those same stations, would be an early and actionable signal of worsening hydrological stress. The quarterly monitoring network is designed specifically to provide enough temporal resolution to detect such trends before the ecological damage becomes irreversible.

## 7.4 Soil Parameters

At selected stations across both sites, soil conditions are assessed for seven chemical and biological indicators using the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD Ver 1.0 handheld quick-test platform. The December 2025 baseline collected 140 soil samples across 21 stations at Keta and 28 soil samples across four primary station areas at Ada, providing the first systematic soil chemistry dataset for these sites under the WACA Blue Carbon Assessment. The seven parameters and their significance to carbon stock monitoring are as follows.

**Nitrate** serves as the primary nitrogen source for mangrove growth and the microbial communities driving organic matter decomposition in wetland soils. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, nitrate values ranged from 1 to 23 mg/kg in upland stations and from 37 to 73 mg/kg in the moist, waterlogged stations typical of active mangrove habitat. At Songor, the contrast was extreme: nitrate reached 984 mg/kg at Wasakusengwa, driven by cattle grazing and organic waste accumulation, while Pute lagoon recorded only 3 mg/kg. Monitoring nitrate over successive quarters provides a direct signal of agricultural runoff and domestic waste inputs to the wetland — both identified as chronic pressure sources at Keta and Songor in the environmental pressures baseline. A progressive increase in nitrate at stations near community settlements or agricultural land margins signals eutrophication risk, which at high levels promotes algal blooms, depresses dissolved oxygen, and can shift mangrove soils from carbon-accumulating to carbon-emitting.

**Phosphate** drives primary productivity in phosphorus-limited tropical coastal soils and plays a critical role in mangrove root growth, seed germination, and the establishment of regeneration. At Keta, phosphate values in moist mangrove-proximate soils ranged from 52 to 103 mg/kg — substantially higher than the 1 to 32 mg/kg recorded in upland stations — reflecting the phosphorus enrichment of waterlogged soils near active leaf litter fall and decomposition. At Obane in the Songor system, phosphate reached an exceptional 705 mg/kg, a level that the December 2025 assessment attributed to cattle grazing and the concentration of organic waste at that location. While elevated phosphate supports plant growth in the short term, phosphate loading from non-natural sources indicates that human pressures are altering the nutrient chemistry of the soils at these stations and may be masking the underlying degradation of organic soil structure.

**Potassium** is essential for the cellular regulation, osmotic adjustment, and photosynthetic efficiency of all wetland vegetation, including mangroves under saline conditions. At Keta, potassium ranged from 2 to 65 mg/kg in upland stations to 78 to 201 mg/kg in moist sites; the mean potassium value across the sampling area reached 56.25 mg/kg, with the highest concentrations at Agbastivi and Galotse — precisely the zones where the most developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands were observed. At Songor, potassium reached 1,280 mg/kg at Obane and contrasted with only 7 mg/kg at Pute lagoon, a difference that likely reflects both the cattle-mediated nutrient enrichment at Obane and the nutrient-depleted condition of the Pute lagoon's heavily stressed soils. Tracking potassium trends over time provides indirect evidence of changes in soil organic matter cycling, since potassium is released during decomposition of leaf litter and woody material — the same process that contributes to soil organic carbon (SOC) accumulation.

**Soil pH** governs the chemical availability of all soil nutrients and directly determines whether decomposition pathways are dominated by aerobic bacterial activity (higher pH, aerobic conditions) or anaerobic microbial processes (lower pH, waterlogged conditions). It is the single most important determinant of SOC stability in mangrove soils: acidic, waterlogged, anaerobic soils suppress microbial decomposition and are the dominant mechanism by which blue carbon ecosystems sequester carbon at rates up to ten times those of terrestrial forests. At Keta, soil pH ranged from 5.55 to 7.0, with the highest values recorded at the well-developed mangrove stations in the Salo and Galotse corridor. A pH range of 6.2 to 7.0 is considered ideal for phosphate availability and the activity of the soil microbial communities that drive nutrient cycling. At Songor, pH ranged from 5.57 to 6.84, with an exceptional low value of 4.78 recorded at Pute lagoon — indicating the presence of potential acid sulphate conditions similar to those flagged at the Anyanui-Tunu road water station at Keta, and consistent with a degraded, drainage-exposed soil environment.

**Moisture content** is the foundational parameter linking soil chemistry to ecosystem function: waterlogged soils with high moisture are anaerobic, producing the conditions that preserve organic carbon; dry, aerated soils are aerobic and decompose organic matter rapidly, releasing CO₂. At Keta, moisture levels were higher at stations closer to water bodies — Aborlorvi at 25.4%, Afiadenyigba bridge at 26.4%, Genui at 38.7%, and Galotse at 40.4% — and lower at upland stations. At Songor, moisture ranged from 21.5 to 29.45%, with the highest values at Pute lagoon intake (30.6%) and Obane (25.1%), consistent with those stations' position within active lagoon and mangrove habitat. Tracking moisture content quarterly captures the seasonal drying and rewetting of mangrove soils, which governs whether the carbon stored in those soils is stable across the year or is exposed to episodic oxidation during the dry season — a risk that is heightened if drainage or hydrological disruption increases the depth of the dry-season water table drawdown.

**Humus content** quantifies the stable, decomposed organic fraction of the soil — the primary reservoir of soil organic carbon in mangrove systems. High humus content reflects the long-term accumulation of leaf litter, root turnover, and decomposed woody material under anaerobic conditions; it is the material substance of the blue carbon stock that this MRV system is designed to protect. At Keta, humus content ranged from 14.6 to 26.2%, with the highest values at Tunu (44.3%), Galotse (40.6%), and Agortoe (37.5%) — stations where dead mangroves, leaf fall, and decomposed organic matter were directly observed during the December 2025 field assessment. At Songor, the Obane restored mangrove site recorded a humus level of 26.0%, the highest at that site, consistent with active organic matter accumulation in a recovering mangrove stand. The exceptionally low humus of 4.78% at Pute lagoon indicates severely depleted soil organic matter, likely the combined result of historic drainage, salt pan encroachment, and the absence of mangrove canopy cover to supply the litter inputs that build humus over time. Declining humus trends at any station across successive monitoring rounds would constitute a direct signal of SOC loss and must trigger a field investigation.

**Soil temperature** integrates moisture, solar radiation, and organic matter content and is a master variable governing the rate of microbial decomposition. Higher soil temperatures accelerate decomposition and the mineralisation of organic carbon; lower temperatures, combined with high moisture, slow decomposition and favour SOC preservation. At Songor, soil temperature reached 38.4°C at Pute walkway — a hot, exposed surface under low vegetation cover — and 34.2°C at the Obane restored mangrove site, where canopy shade provided measurable cooling. This temperature gradient across a relatively small area illustrates the micro-climatic effect of mangrove canopy cover on soil carbon stability: restored mangrove stands begin generating the cooler, more stable soil thermal environment within which SOC accumulates, even at an early stage of recovery.

## 7.5 Integration with the MRV Layers

The monitoring station network at Keta and Ada is not a standalone environmental assessment programme. It is operationally integrated into the three functional layers of the national MRV system, each of which draws on the station data for a distinct analytical purpose.

Layer I — the Ecological and Carbon Integrity Layer — uses water and soil data from the 80 stations as continuous ecosystem health proxies that sit alongside and support the biomass and soil carbon measurements taken at Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs). PSP re-measurement provides the direct carbon accounting data on a three-to-five year cycle; the station network provides the quarterly ecological context within which changes in carbon stocks are interpreted. A declining trend in dissolved oxygen and rising salinity at the Salo-Galotse stations, combined with falling humus values at adjacent soil stations, provides early evidence of mangrove stress before die-back becomes visible in satellite imagery or PSP re-measurement. This early warning function is particularly important at Keta, where the historical deforestation rate of 2.15% per year — driven in part by hydrological changes traceable to Akosombo Dam operations — means that delays in detecting ecological deterioration translate directly into carbon stock losses that cannot be recovered on management timescales.

Layer II — the Environmental Pressure Surveillance layer — uses the station network to detect pollution events, salinity anomalies, and evidence of physical disturbance that are the direct signatures of the pressures identified in the baseline assessment. The Keta Lagoon Complex is subject to chronic fish-smoking fuelwood demand, which drives selective harvesting of *Rhizophora racemosa* — but it is also subject to acute events: domestic waste discharges, chemical spills, and the episodic blockage of inter-connecting channels. An acute pollution event at a station near Anloga, Woe, or Havedzi would appear in the monitoring data as a sudden depression of DO, a spike in conductivity or TDS, and potentially an anomalous shift in pH — a multi-parameter signal that is distinguishable from normal seasonal variation. At Songor, the contested land-use at Obane, where cattle grazing is elevating nitrate and phosphate values to levels ten to twenty times those recorded at comparable stations, is already visible in the December 2025 baseline data. Quarterly monitoring will establish whether those values are stable, declining, or increasing — directly informing whether the land-use pressure at Obane requires escalation to the Ada East District Assembly or the Greater Accra Regional Wildlife Division.

Layer III — the Governance and Safeguard Assurance layer — draws on the station network in a less direct but structurally important way: the functioning of the monitoring network is itself a governance indicator. Whether CCAs maintain access to their designated stations, whether data is submitted on schedule, and whether communities are actively engaged in field sampling are all observable outcomes that reflect the operational condition of the community-level governance structure. A monitoring station that becomes inaccessible because community relations have broken down, or because a land-use conflict has cut off access to a sampling point, is a Layer III signal as much as it is an operational inconvenience. MLNR and MESTI, as joint national custodians of the MRV system, use the completeness and regularity of monitoring data submissions as one of their indicators of whether governance and community engagement at the site level are functioning as designed.

## 7.6 Reporting from the Monitoring Network

Quarterly data from the 80 stations — 57 at Keta and 23 at Ada — follows a defined pathway from field collection to institutional action. Field teams, led by trained CCAs and supervised by Wildlife Division officers at the district level, record all parameter readings with GPS coordinates, device model and calibration information, and any field observations of ecological or social anomalies. Data sheets are completed at the time of sampling; digital transcription occurs at the district validation stage and then at the national platform level, where the data is aggregated, quality-checked, and archived.

Annual analysis of the multi-quarter dataset distinguishes between seasonal patterns — which are expected and ecologically normal — and multi-year trends, which are the primary signals for adaptive management. A three-year period of quarterly data provides sufficient resolution to separate the Keta lagoon's predictable dry-season salinity increase from a structural trend of increasing annual salinity that would indicate progressive hydrological deterioration. Trend analysis reports are prepared annually and submitted to MLNR, MESTI, and the Wildlife Division, as well as to the district Municipal and Metropolitan District Assemblies (MMDAs) with jurisdiction over the monitoring areas.

Any parameter reading exceeding the defined alert thresholds at any station triggers an escalation protocol that operates independently of the annual reporting cycle. Thresholds are set for each parameter based on the ecological ranges established in the December 2025 baseline assessment: a pH below 4.0 or above 9.0, a DO reading below 3.0 mg/l, a salinity spike exceeding 50 ppt, or a nitrate reading above 500 mg/kg at a mangrove-proximate soil station are each examples of alert-level readings that require immediate follow-up. An alert-level reading triggers first a field verification visit within 14 days to confirm whether the anomaly is an instrument error, a one-off event, or a sustained change. If the follow-up confirms a sustained anomaly, a formal escalation report is submitted to the district Wildlife Division within 30 days. If the Wildlife Division does not resolve the issue within 60 days of the escalation report — either by identifying the cause and initiating a management response, or by determining that the anomaly is not of management concern — the issue escalates to MLNR and MESTI. This structured escalation ensures that the monitoring data produces management decisions, not simply an archive of readings.

The overall goal of the monitoring network is not data collection for its own sake. It is to provide the continuous, spatially explicit, multi-parameter evidence base that allows Ghana's principal blue carbon landscapes to be managed adaptively — detecting ecological change early, attributing it to its causes, and routing that information to the institutions with the authority and capacity to respond.
