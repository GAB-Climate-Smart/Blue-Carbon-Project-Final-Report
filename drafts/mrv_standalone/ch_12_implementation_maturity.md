# Chapter 12: Implementation Maturity Assessment

---

## 12.1 Purpose of the Maturity Assessment

A monitoring system that presents itself as more advanced than it is loses credibility with the audiences whose confidence it most needs to retain. Government ministries allocating recurrent budget to a monitoring programme, development partners calibrating the next phase of WACA investment, and carbon market verifiers assessing whether the Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) architecture can support a credible issuance cycle all require an honest account of what the system can currently demonstrate versus what it has been designed to achieve. A system that over-claims operational status — asserting that platforms are live when they are in development, that Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) governance structures are functioning when they are nascent, that quarterly field monitoring is routine when it has been conducted once under an assessment-period contract — will be exposed under due diligence, and the credibility cost of that exposure will be disproportionate to the maturity gap being concealed.

The opposite failure is equally damaging. A system that under-represents what has genuinely been achieved — dismissing a complete, 319-reference-point geospatial baseline as merely preliminary, describing trained Community Change Agents (CCAs) at Dzita, Pute, and Sankor as untested when they have demonstrably conducted field monitoring under the December 2025 assessment programme, or treating the documented national MRV architecture as aspirational when it represents a formally commissioned and delivered government output — fails to make the case for the continued investment that the system's next phase of maturation requires.

This chapter provides the honest baseline that neither inflates nor deflates. It assesses implementation maturity across the four institutional tiers and three functional layers of the National Blue Carbon MRV Framework as of the conclusion of the WACA baseline assessment phase, drawing directly on the evidence produced during that phase. It distinguishes between what the assessment phase has placed firmly in the ground, what is actively progressing toward operational status, and what remains beyond the current investment horizon and will require deliberate action to build. The purpose of this assessment is not to audit shortcomings but to provide the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR) and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI) with an accurate foundation for planning the MRV system's next operational phase.

## 12.2 Maturity Assessment — By Institutional Tier

### Community Tier

The community tier of Ghana's National Blue Carbon MRV Framework is defined by two interdependent institutional elements: the CREMAs — established as the formal governance bodies through which communities manage their coastal resources and enter into carbon market arrangements — and the CCAs, who serve as the operational field arm of community-level monitoring. The baseline assessment phase has advanced both elements substantively, while leaving the formalisation work that would make them independently operable without continued external facilitation still incomplete.

What is in place is more substantial than a zero-baseline assessment would suggest. At all three sites — the Keta Lagoon Complex, the Songor Ramsar Site, and the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site — CCAs have been identified through community nomination processes, trained in field data collection protocols during the December 2025 assessment programme, and demonstrated in practice their capacity to mobilise community groups, navigate local governance dynamics, and collect standardised environmental data. At Dzita on the Keta Lagoon, CCAs successfully facilitated a dialogue between landowners and youth to resolve a dispute over a potential restoration site, demonstrating the social as well as technical dimensions of the CCA role. At Sankor in Muni-Pomadze, CCAs leveraged existing social networks — church groups, football clubs — to disseminate project information and mobilise participation in a way that institutional channels could not have achieved at equivalent speed. At Pute in Songor, CCAs navigated a community landscape shaped by long-standing land tenure conflicts and deep social fractures to maintain project engagement in the most operationally demanding social environment of the three sites. The training investment made during the assessment phase has created a cadre of field-competent local monitors at each early action hub.

CREMA structures exist at all three sites in nascent form. At Muni-Pomadze, the communities of Sankor and Bewadze demonstrate the highest social readiness — strong community cohesion, active and organised women's groups, and a history of constructive engagement with the Wildlife Division — that the governance assessment describes as sufficient for a fast-track CREMA establishment within a twelve- to twenty-four-month timeframe. Emerging CREMA clusters at the Keta Lagoon, including the Angor and Dusornu clusters and the Dzita-Agbatsivi grouping, have begun boundary mapping and constitutional drafting. At Songor, where long-standing land tenure disputes at Pute and Elavanyo create governance conditions that the assessment characterises as "high potential marred by land-use conflict," CREMA formation is oriented toward communities in the Ada East District where the direct pressures of commercial salt mining operations are less immediate.

What is actively maturing is the formalisation of these structures into legally recognised and independently functional governance entities. Formal CREMA constitutions and by-laws are in preparation at multiple sites but have not been submitted for the gazettement process that would give them statutory force under the Wildlife Resources Management Act, 2023 (Act 1115). The CCA data collection capacity established during the assessment period is real and demonstrated, but it has not yet transitioned from assessment-period activity — conducted within a structured programme with external coordination support — to routine quarterly practice operated by communities themselves. The critical prerequisite that no amount of community capacity can resolve without national action is the finalisation of the CREMA Legislative Instrument (LI), currently under development but not yet passed by Parliament, whose absence creates legal uncertainty around the CREMA establishment, governance, and benefit-sharing processes that all three sites are attempting to advance.

What requires investment is the completion of CREMA formation and legal recognition at all three sites; the formalisation of CCA roles within CREMA structures, providing them with the terms of reference, performance-based support, and tools they need to sustain monitoring quality beyond the assessment period; and the transition from CCAs as project participants to CCAs as institutionally embedded community monitors whose function within the national MRV system does not depend on the continuation of any specific project contract.

### District Tier

The district tier — the intermediate validation and coordination layer connecting community field monitoring to national technical oversight — has been engaged through the baseline assessment phase in a way that has advanced awareness without yet consolidating the operational workflows that routine MRV requires.

What is in place is demonstrable. Wildlife Division officers participated in field validation activities during the December 2025 assessment, gaining direct familiarity with the monitoring protocols, the PSP establishment process at Salo, and the wetland station network design logic that guides the Keta monitoring system. This participation means that the technical staff responsible for district-level data validation have first-hand understanding of the data collection methodology whose outputs they will be required to review and consolidate. Metropolitan, Municipal, and District Assemblies (MMDAs) in the Keta, Songor, and Muni-Pomadze districts are the designated consolidation and escalation authorities within the institutional tier architecture, and their roles within the MRV system are formally documented and understood.

What is maturing is the translation of that documented understanding into functioning data submission and validation workflows. The pathway from community CCA field return, through Wildlife Division consistency checking and cross-validation against satellite analysis, to national platform archiving — validated under the Keta pilot monitoring experience described in Chapter 11 — has not yet become a routine operational cadence at any site. Wildlife Division field officers cover vast territories with inadequate staffing and limited logistical support, and the "capacity trap" documented in the governance assessment — where statutory bodies are assigned clear mandates that their operational resources cannot support — is an honest description of the current reality at district level across all three sites. Data management responsibilities related to the MRV system have not yet been assigned to dedicated district-level personnel; they sit alongside the Wildlife Division's full existing brief without the additional capacity needed to execute them reliably.

What requires investment is dedicated institutional capacity within MMDAs and the Wildlife Division for ongoing MRV data management, field verification, and conflict escalation functions — not new knowledge, but the logistical and staffing resources that translate existing knowledge into sustained practice. The Wildlife Division offices serving the Keta, Ada East, and Winneba districts each require identified MRV focal persons, with the operational budget and access to transport that quarterly data validation cycles demand. Without this investment, the district tier will function as a bottleneck in the data flow architecture rather than as the validation and quality control layer the MRV system design depends on.

### National Technical Tier

The national technical tier represents the most advanced state of maturity in the current MRV system, reflecting the fact that the baseline assessment phase was primarily an investment in national-level institutional knowledge and methodology.

What is firmly in place is the complete national baseline package. MLNR and MESTI have commissioned and received the full set of Year 0 reference documents: the geospatial mangrove extent baseline covering 30,025 hectares of coastal mangrove resource across 35 districts and 550 kilometres of coastline, classified using Harmonised Sentinel-2 imagery at 10–20 metre resolution and validated with 319 participatory ground-truth reference points; the carbon inventory assessment including the Permanent Sample Plot (PSP) established at Salo in the Keta Lagoon Complex; the ecosystem health, environmental pressure, governance readiness, and socio-economic baseline reports covering 37 communities; and the National Blue Carbon MRV Framework design documented in this report. The national monitoring methodology is fully documented. The institutional roles and responsibilities are formally assigned. The carbon accounting protocol is published and aligned with the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Wetlands Supplement. The spatial data architecture is defined and the platform technical specifications are established. Ghana enters the operational phase of MRV implementation with a more complete national baseline package than most countries at an equivalent stage of blue carbon programme development.

What is maturing is the digital infrastructure through which that baseline is operationalised for continuous monitoring. The web-based spatial monitoring platform — developed by GAB Climate Smart Investment Ltd on a Next.js/Supabase architecture, with EOSDA satellite monitoring services — is in active development. The platform architecture is defined, the Supabase database reference is established, and the GitHub repository is active. The continuous satellite monitoring integration through EOSDA, designed to provide weekly imagery updates and automated change detection alerts to the national platform, is configured but has not yet completed its first full operational monitoring cycle delivering routine outputs to MLNR. The annual satellite-based mangrove extent update cycle, which is the primary ecological monitoring mechanism between the five-year PSP re-measurement cycles, needs to be formally operationalised as a standing institutional function with named ownership within MLNR or the Forestry Commission rather than a service dependent on continued external contract.

What requires investment is the full operationalisation of the digital platform — completing the build, establishing data ingest pipelines from CCA field submissions and district consolidation, and standing up the user access and reporting functions for national, district, and community users; institutionalising the annual MRV report production cycle as a scheduled government output owned by MLNR and MESTI; and establishing a dedicated national MRV team with the GIS, data management, and carbon accounting capacity to sustain the technical oversight function after the WACA programme investment cycle is complete.

### Independent Review Tier

The independent review tier occupies a distinct position in the maturity assessment: it is the only tier that is not yet relevant to the current operational phase but whose architecture must be established now if carbon market participation is to become a realistic near-term objective.

What is in place is the framework definition. The MRV system design specifies when independent review applies — at the point of carbon credit issuance cycles, significant scale restoration expansion, or formal carbon market participation — and what it entails: validation of carbon calculations, assessment of QA/QC documentation, review of environmental pressure integration, examination of safeguard documentation, and confirmation of methodological consistency with declared protocols. The MRV system design makes explicit that independent verification does not replace national institutional ownership but complements it when required, preserving Ghana's institutional authority over its own blue carbon data while creating a pathway to third-party assurance for market actors.

What requires investment for this tier to be functional at the point it is needed — which is not yet, but could realistically be within a three- to five-year horizon if CREMA formation proceeds on schedule and restoration activities reach the scale required for credit issuance — is the identification of accredited verifiers with experience in West African coastal blue carbon systems, the negotiation and agreement of verification protocols with MLNR and MESTI that are aligned with the Verra/VCS, Gold Standard, or Plan Vivo standards under which Ghana would most likely seek to issue credits, and the assembly of the audit trail documentation that independent review will require: georeferenced restoration site registrations, time-stamped PSP measurement archives, governance composition records, and the grievance and benefit-sharing documentation that safeguard review demands. This work does not begin with the verifier engagement; it begins now, with the systematic archiving of the baseline-phase documentation that will form the historical record an independent review will need to examine.

## 12.3 Maturity Assessment — By Functional Layer

### Layer I: Ecological and Carbon Integrity

Layer I is the most mature of the three functional layers as measured by baseline completeness, which is the appropriate reference point for an assessment conducted at the close of the Year 0 phase. The ecological and carbon integrity baseline is not a design aspiration; it is a completed field programme.

The 2025 Geospatial Mangrove Extent Baseline is complete. The national mangrove estate of 30,025 hectares has been mapped, classified, and validated, providing the Year 0 spatial reference against which all future annual extent measurements will be benchmarked. The stratification schema established during the baseline — by species composition, canopy density class, hydrological zone, and degradation intensity category — is the framework that will be maintained across future re-measurement cycles to ensure statistical comparability over time. The historical deforestation rate of 2.15% per year at the Keta Lagoon Complex, and the spatial gradient of degradation that concentrates the most severe fragmentation near the highest-density fish-smoking settlements of Bomigo, Galo, and Sota, are documented as the ecological context within which the monitoring system operates.

The carbon inventory is complete. The PSP at Salo has been established with a full Year 0 measurement set: species composition record, diameter at breast height distribution for all stems meeting the inclusion threshold, height sampling, canopy cover assessment, and soil organic carbon (SOC) cores to the IPCC Wetlands Supplement-required depth of 100 centimetres. The above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (BGB), and SOC pool baselines from the 2025 inventory provide the carbon stock anchors from which any future change detection and credit calculation will be derived.

The annual satellite monitoring cycle — the operational mechanism through which Layer I transitions from a baseline event to a continuous monitoring function — represents the principal maturity gap within this layer. The cycle design is specified: Sentinel-2 imagery analysis on Google Earth Engine (GEE), consistent with baseline methodology, producing annual net mangrove area change, fragmentation trends, and encroachment hotspot mapping. But the first operational cycle, producing outputs delivered to MLNR and MESTI as a standing institutional deliverable rather than a project activity, has not yet been completed. The PSP re-measurement at Salo is appropriately scheduled for three to five years hence under the standard biomass re-measurement cycle — this is not a gap but the correct operational cadence for a freshly established PSP. The maturity investment required in Layer I is the operationalisation of the annual satellite cycle as a routine national function rather than an ad hoc assessment activity.

### Layer II: Environmental Pressure Surveillance

Layer II has achieved substantive completion at the design and baseline documentation levels, with the field surveillance transition representing the next operational stage.

Pressure profiles are documented for all three sites with the site-specificity that the complexity of each landscape demands. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, the five-core-indicator surveillance framework is calibrated to detect the fuelwood harvesting pressure from fish-smoking kilns, the preference for *Rhizophora racemosa* that concentrates cutting on the highest-biomass mangrove species, the Akosombo Dam-driven sediment starvation that operates as a landscape-level structural pressure no community governance mechanism can address, and the coastal retreat dynamics that create geomorphological instability for the entire monitoring estate. At the Songor Ramsar Site, the pressure profile documents the salt pan expansion dynamic and land tenure conflicts at communities including Elavanyo and Obane as the primary drivers of the landscape's degraded condition. At Muni-Pomadze, the pressure assessment documents agricultural encroachment, charcoal production, and the low mangrove density that limits the ecological response to any reduction in pressure. These profiles are not generic; they are site-specific, community-specific, and analytically connected to the carbon risk and governance implications they generate.

The five core indicators — land conversion monitoring, mangrove cutting signal detection, fuel source shift monitoring, charcoal and extraction hotspot tracking, and the conflict and access pressure index — were validated in community and field settings during the 2025 assessment. CCAs understand what they are measuring and why. Wildlife Division officers understand the escalation protocols that translate field detection into institutional response. The indicator logic has been tested against the actual pressure dynamics of the three sites.

What has not yet occurred is the transition to routine quarterly surveillance through CCAs and the Wildlife Division as a standing operational cadence. The five core indicators were documented and validated during a bounded assessment period; they have not yet been collected as part of an independently operating monitoring routine. The quarterly cycle — the cadence that gives the surveillance system its early-warning function by detecting emerging pressure trends before they produce irreversible ecological damage — requires CCAs who are equipped, compensated, and supported for routine collection, and district officers who are positioned to receive, validate, and transmit the resulting data outside of a project-funded activity framework. Establishing this cadence is the principal Layer II maturity investment of the next twelve months.

### Layer III: Governance and Safeguard Assurance

Layer III reflects the most complex maturity picture, because the governance and safeguard indicators depend on institutions — the CREMAs, the GRMs, the inclusive governance bodies — that are themselves in formation. Layer III monitoring cannot outrun the governance reality it is designed to measure.

The baseline is documented with precision. The finding that more than 80% of the 37 communities profiled maintain informal grievance systems rather than formal Grievance Redress Mechanisms (GRMs) is not an abstraction; it is the specific governance deficit that Layer III monitoring is designed to close by tracking the establishment and functioning of formal GRMs at each landscape over time. The CREMA governance composition requirements — including the minimum 40% female representation in executive leadership that the national MRV standard mandates — have been established as monitoring targets against which annual governance composition statistics will be benchmarked. Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) processes were conducted for the baseline assessment at all three sites, establishing the procedural baseline and the documentation standard for the participation records that Layer III requires going forward.

The site-differentiated governance picture is documented with honesty about the asymmetry between sites. At Muni-Pomadze, where Sankor's high community cohesion, active women's groups, and history of engagement with the Wildlife Division's Winneba office create conditions for rapid governance formalisation, Layer III monitoring indicators are likely to show progress earlier and more linearly than at other sites. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, where CREMA formation is fragmented across the landscape and the Dzita early action hub represents a leading edge rather than a typical baseline, Layer III monitoring must be designed to capture the uneven pace of governance formation across a geographically and institutionally heterogeneous landscape. At Songor, where the land tenure conflicts affecting Pute and Elavanyo have "created deep social fractures and paralysed local leadership," Layer III monitoring will initially reflect governance fragility rather than governance progress — a finding that should be reported honestly rather than suppressed, because it is precisely the signal that the early-warning function of the MRV system must be designed to generate.

What requires investment in Layer III is the formal establishment of GRMs at all three sites as functional institutions with documented submission pathways, designated focal persons, and escalation procedures — not design specifications, but operational systems that can receive and process a grievance when one is submitted. This formalisation cannot happen without CREMA formation preceding it, which reinforces the critical path logic that connects governance investment (CREMA LI finalisation, CREMA legal recognition) to the operationalisation of Layer III monitoring capacity.

## 12.4 Priorities for the Next 12 Months

The maturity assessment identifies a small number of actions that are not simply desirable but constitute the critical path to operational MRV. Without them, the monitoring system designed and baselined during the WACA assessment phase cannot transition to the routine, institutionally self-sustaining operation that its design intends. With them, the transition from assessment phase to operational phase becomes achievable within the 2026–2027 annual cycle.

The single most consequential enabling action is the finalisation and Parliamentary passage of the CREMA Legislative Instrument. Every community-tier maturity investment — CREMA constitution completion, bylaw gazettement, CCA formalisation within CREMA governance structures, FPIC process institutionalisation, and GRM establishment — is legally uncertain until the LI provides the regulatory framework that makes CREMA formation, governance, and benefit-sharing arrangements legally unambiguous. MLNR is the responsible ministry. The WACA project should treat LI finalisation as a programme-level milestone, not a background legal process, because its absence is the single most significant systemic risk to community-tier and Layer III operationalisation across all three sites.

The second priority is the formal operationalisation of the annual satellite monitoring cycle as a standing government function. This requires that MLNR or the Forestry Commission designate named staff with the GIS and remote sensing competence to conduct annual Sentinel-2 mangrove extent analysis consistent with the Year 0 baseline methodology, produce the annual net change outputs and fragmentation trend summaries that the national MRV reporting framework requires, and archive the outputs in the national platform under version-controlled records. The technical methodology is established; what is required is the institutional ownership and recurrent budget allocation that transforms a project activity into a national monitoring function.

The third priority is the transition of CCA monitoring from assessment-period engagement to routine quarterly practice. This requires, at minimum, the provision of adequate field equipment — calibrated multi-parameter water quality instruments and soil analysis devices of the type used during the December 2025 training programme — to CCAs at Dzita, Pute, and Sankor; the establishment of a compensation mechanism, aligned with the CREMA governance formalisation process, that provides CCAs with consistent and performance-based support rather than the voluntary engagement model that creates high burnout and attrition risk; and the activation of district Wildlife Division data reception procedures that give CCAs a functional institutional counterpart for their field submissions. The Wildlife Division offices at the relevant districts — Keta Municipal, Ada East, and Efutu Municipal — are the responsible entities, and each requires the logistical budget for regular field validation visits that the current operational funding does not consistently provide.

The fourth priority is the formal establishment of at least one functioning GRM at each of the three sites, anchored in whichever community cluster demonstrates sufficient governance readiness to host it. At Muni-Pomadze, Sankor's governance readiness profile makes it the natural host for a pilot GRM that can be established and documented during the first quarterly governance reporting cycle. At the Keta Lagoon, the Dzita-Agbatsivi cluster, where the CRMA formation process is most advanced, is the appropriate starting point. At Songor, GRM establishment should follow, not precede, the conflict mediation investment that the governance assessment identifies as the necessary precondition for any formal governance structure in the Pute area.

The fifth priority is the completion of the digital platform build to a level that supports the basic data flow functions the MRV system requires: CCA field data submission, district validation logging, and national archive access. The GAB Climate Smart platform under development provides the architecture for an investment-grade system with advanced analytics, spatial visualisation, and change detection alert functions. In the near term, the minimum viable platform is one that securely receives, timestamps, validates, and archives the quarterly field submissions from all three sites and makes them accessible to national-level users. This functionality is achievable within the current development trajectory and should be the platform milestone against which the first operational monitoring cycle is measured.

## 12.5 The MRV System's Long-Term Trajectory

A mature national blue carbon MRV system for Ghana looks different from what exists today, but the distance between those two points is not as great as it might appear from a single measurement of what remains to be built. The architecture has been designed. The methodology has been established. The Year 0 baseline has been laid. The communities who will be the system's operational frontline have been engaged, trained, and demonstrated their capacity. The national institutions that will own the system have received the complete baseline package and made a formal government commitment to the monitoring framework it describes. These are not small things; they are the foundations that cannot be constructed in a single phase of investment and whose absence is the reason most countries at comparable levels of blue carbon potential have not reached even this starting point.

When fully mature — which, on the basis of the implementation trajectory described in this chapter, is achievable within a five- to eight-year horizon from the close of the assessment phase — the system will operate as follows. At each of the three Ramsar sites, a legally recognised and functioning CREMA — at Dzita for the Keta Lagoon Complex, at Pute or an Ada East cluster for Songor, and at Sankor for Muni-Pomadze — will submit quarterly monitoring returns, covering Layer I restoration survival data, Layer II cutting and pressure signals, and Layer III governance composition and grievance records, to their respective district Wildlife Division offices. Annual Sentinel-2 extent updates, processed by a national GIS team within MLNR, will be published on the web-based spatial monitoring platform as a standing national reporting product. The PSP at Salo will have completed its first re-measurement cycle, and PSP installations at Songor and Muni-Pomadze will have extended the carbon monitoring network to the full three-site scope that the national framework defines. An Annual National Blue Carbon MRV Report, consolidating field, satellite, and governance monitoring data, will be issued by MLNR and MESTI as a formal government document, providing the transparency that government, development partners, and — at the point of carbon market participation — carbon credit buyers require.

The investment in the WACA baseline assessment phase has created something of genuine institutional durability: a documented national methodology, a trained community workforce, a completed ecological and carbon baseline, and a governance architecture designed to outlast the project cycle that funded its design. The MRV system is not a project mechanism; it is a national framework whose value accumulates with each operational year added to the monitoring record. A five-year monitoring series is worth more than a Year 0 baseline alone; a ten-year series, anchored by a first PSP re-measurement cycle and capable of demonstrating verifiable carbon stock trends, is worth more still. Ghana's position in the blue carbon landscape — as a country with the methodological rigour, institutional commitment, and community engagement depth to generate credible blue carbon accounting — will be determined not by the ambition of the framework that has been designed but by the consistency of the monitoring practice that the next phase of implementation builds. The baseline phase has earned the right to that next phase; the maturity assessment in this chapter is, above all, the map for how to use it.
