# Chapter 10: Monitoring Framework and Operational Action Plan

---

## 10.1 From Baseline to Monitoring Programme: The Strategic Shift

The December 2025 survey did what a baseline is supposed to do: it documented the condition of the ecosystem at a defined moment in time, with sufficient spatial resolution and parameter coverage to establish the ecological benchmarks against which future change will be measured. It recorded water pH of 2.7 at Anyanui-Tunu road, salinity of 40 ppt at Dzita, ORP readings of 832–1,547 mV in the Ada-Songor interior lagoon, and humus concentrations of 40.6% in the benchmark Galotse mangrove soils. These are not simply data points. They are the starting coordinates of a system that is, in several of its sub-zones, already under acute ecological stress and moving in a measurable direction.

A baseline without a monitoring programme is a photograph with no film in the camera. It records a moment — accurately, thoroughly — but cannot tell you whether the next moment is better or worse. Ghana's most important blue carbon landscape contains an acid sulphate emergency at Anyanui, a hypersaline stress zone at Dzita, a geochemical anomaly at the Ada interior lagoon, and a deforestation trajectory of 2.15 percent per year across the site that contains 69 percent of the nation's coastal mangroves. Understanding whether any of those conditions is improving, stable, or worsening — and doing so in time for management action to be meaningful — requires a system that generates comparable data from the same locations across successive time periods. The December 2025 baseline established the reference coordinates. This chapter describes how the detection system that reads against those coordinates operates.

Three things make that detection system possible. First, the 65 georeferenced stations established during the baseline campaign — all permanently marked and GPS-recorded — are the physical infrastructure of the monitoring network. The same locations can be returned to, season after season, and measured against their own history. Second, the Community Change Agent (CCA) network trained during the December 2025 survey provides the institutional capacity to operate those stations continuously, without requiring the full technical team that conducted the baseline to be in the field for every quarterly round. Third, the instruments used in December 2025 — the Apera SX751 for water quality and the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD for soil chemistry — are retained as the standard monitoring platform, ensuring that every subsequent reading is directly comparable to the baseline value at the same station.

The monitoring programme described in this chapter translates these three assets — georeferenced infrastructure, trained community capacity, and calibrated instrumentation — into an operational system: 80 stations across two Ramsar sites, measured quarterly, against defined alert thresholds, with an escalation protocol that routes ecological signals to the institutions with the authority to act on them.

## 10.2 The 80-Station Quarterly Monitoring Network

### 10.2.1 Network Structure and Expansion Logic

The quarterly monitoring network expands from the 65 stations established in the December 2025 baseline to 80 permanent stations, distributed across both sites in proportion to their ecological complexity and management priority. The Keta Lagoon Complex, with its 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves across a 530 km² landscape and its 9 ecological assessment clusters, accounts for 57 stations. The Ada-Songor Lagoon, with 465.76 hectares of fragmented mangroves across 4 ecological clusters, accounts for 23 stations. The combined network of 80 stations represents the minimum spatial coverage necessary to characterise the ecological gradient at each site and to detect localised anomalies — the pH 2.7 reading at Anyanui-Tunu road was identified precisely because the December 2025 network was sufficiently dense to resolve sub-cluster variation within the Anyanui-Dzita zone.

All 80 stations are permanently georeferenced using GPS coordinates recorded with a Garmin Inreach Explorer or equivalent device. Station positions are recorded in the national MRV platform's spatial database and plotted on the supervised GIS map prepared for each site. No station relocation is permissible without formal notification to the district Wildlife Division and documentation of the original and replacement coordinates in the platform. The permanent nature of the station network is not an administrative formality: it is the scientific foundation of the trend analysis that, after three years of quarterly data, will reveal whether the ecological conditions at each cluster are stable, improving, or deteriorating.

All 80 stations measure the same 8 water quality parameters (pH, temperature, electrical conductivity, salinity, total dissolved solids, dissolved oxygen, ORP, resistivity) and 7 soil chemical and biological parameters (nitrate, phosphate, potassium, pH, moisture, humus, temperature) established in the December 2025 baseline. The parameter list is fixed. Substituting or dropping parameters mid-programme destroys comparability with the baseline record; adding new parameters should be done as supplementary measurements that do not replace the core 15.

### 10.2.2 Cluster Network and Priority Classification Table

The 13 monitoring clusters — 9 at Keta and 4 at Ada — are classified into three monitoring priority levels. Standard priority clusters operate on the quarterly schedule as described in Section 10.3. Elevated priority clusters receive enhanced monitoring attention: field teams are expected to record additional qualitative observations at these stations (photographs of water condition, vegetation canopy, and visible anthropogenic activity) alongside the standard parameter readings, and any anomalous reading triggers a follow-up inspection within 21 days. Alert priority clusters receive monthly monitoring until the cause of the anomaly is confirmed and a formal management response is in place, as described in Section 10.4.

The priority classification is based on the condition assessment findings of Chapter 8 and is subject to revision following each quarterly analysis. A cluster whose parameter trend deteriorates across two consecutive quarters may be elevated from Standard to Elevated, or from Elevated to Alert, without waiting for the annual review.

## 10.3 Quarterly Schedule and Seasonal Logic

### 10.3.1 Why Quarterly: Not Monthly, Not Annual

The quarterly monitoring cadence is not arbitrary — it is calibrated to the ecological dynamics of the two systems and to the operational capacity of the CCA network that will conduct the surveys.

Monthly monitoring would generate twelve datasets per year per station. The analytical capacity required to process, validate, and interpret 80 × 15 parameter readings across twelve time points annually would exceed the current institutional capacity of both the district Wildlife Division and the national MRV platform. More importantly, ecological variation in these systems at the monthly timescale is dominated by weather noise — a single unseasonal rainfall event can temporarily depress salinity by several parts per thousand at a given station; a calm, cloudless week can elevate surface water temperature by 2–3°C. These short-cycle fluctuations are real but are not the signals the monitoring system is designed to detect. They would require constant interpretation and, without the analytical infrastructure to contextualise them, would generate false escalation alerts that erode institutional confidence in the system.

Annual monitoring — a single survey per year — would cost almost nothing in operational terms but would provide almost no ecological intelligence. The December 2025 baseline itself demonstrated why: it was conducted during the dry season and yet recorded unseasonal rainfall effects at several stations, demonstrating that a single annual snapshot cannot characterise even the seasonal behaviour of these systems, let alone detect the sub-seasonal anomalies — pollution spikes, acute hypersalinity events, dissolved oxygen crashes during the hottest dry-season weeks — that represent the most ecologically consequential signals.

Quarterly monitoring captures the four ecologically distinct phases of the annual cycle that govern mangrove function and carbon dynamics in coastal Ghana. The dry season brings maximum thermal stress, maximum evaporative concentration of dissolved solids, minimum freshwater input from the Volta, and the conditions under which acid sulphate chemistry advances most aggressively when tidal exchange is disrupted. The late-dry-season transition sees salinity begin to decline as the first rains arrive, but water temperatures remain high and dissolved oxygen can be depressed by the warm, stratified conditions before mixing events restore the DO profile. The wet season brings freshwater dilution that can drop salinity by 10–15 ppt at the most Volta-proximate stations, reduces temperatures, and triggers the highest biological productivity across the mangrove stands — this is the season in which the *Rhizophora racemosa* canopy is at its most photosynthetically active and in which soil organic matter inputs from leaf fall are beginning to accumulate. The wet-to-dry transition is the season of maximum hydrological instability: some years the rains end abruptly; in others, a second rainfall pulse modifies what would otherwise be the expected salinity and conductivity trajectory. Quarterly monitoring resolves all four phases and builds the temporal pattern against which anomaly detection operates.

Fieldwork for each round should be completed within a 14-day window to minimise within-round temporal variability. At Keta, fieldwork typically requires 5–7 field days given the 57-station coverage across the 530 km² complex; at Ada, 2–3 days for 23 stations. The order of station visits within each round must be consistent across rounds — visiting stations in the same sequence prevents the systematic time-of-day bias that can otherwise inflate apparent within-round variability at stations visited in the afternoon, when water temperatures are elevated, versus early morning.

## 10.4 Alert Thresholds and Escalation Protocol

### 10.4.1 Parameter Alert Thresholds

The monitoring system operates against a defined set of alert thresholds for both water quality and soil parameters. These thresholds are set at values that represent ecologically critical boundaries — levels at or beyond which the ecosystem's capacity to sustain carbon sequestration or mangrove productivity is directly and materially compromised. They are not aspirational targets. They are the lines that, when crossed, require an institutional response to occur within a defined timeframe.

**Water quality alert thresholds:**

- **pH below 4.0 or above 9.0** — Immediate escalation. A pH below 4.0 indicates active acid sulphate chemistry in contact with the water column; a pH above 9.0 indicates extreme alkalisation incompatible with mangrove physiology. The pH 2.7 reading at Anyanui-Tunu road during the December 2025 baseline already exceeds this threshold and is under active Alert protocol (see Section 10.4.3).

- **Dissolved oxygen (DO) below 3.0 mg/L** — Immediate escalation. At DO below 3 mg/L, most aquatic fauna are in hypoxic stress; below 2 mg/L, the water column is effectively anoxic. Sustained low DO in the water column adjacent to mangrove stands indicates organic pollution, algal bloom, or the collapse of the aerobic decomposition cycle that supports nutrient availability for mangrove roots.

- **Salinity above 50 ppt** — Immediate escalation. At 50 ppt, both dominant Keta mangrove species (*Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans*) are outside their physiological tolerance range. Even *Avicennia*, the more salt-tolerant species, shows growth suppression above 45 ppt. A reading above 50 ppt signals a hydrological crisis — either the complete isolation of a lagoon compartment from freshwater input or an extreme evaporative concentration event incompatible with mangrove survival.

- **ORP sustained above +600 mV in water column** — Escalation required. ORP above +600 mV indicates a strongly oxidising geochemical environment hostile to the reducing conditions that preserve soil organic carbon. The Ada-Songor interior lagoon readings of 832–1,547 mV already exceed this threshold. ORP values above 600 mV in water overlying mangrove or wetland sediments mean that the geochemical barrier that suppresses aerobic decomposition of the carbon pool is absent, and the organic matter is being actively oxidised.

**Soil parameter alert thresholds:**

- **Nitrate above 500 mg/kg at mangrove-proximate stations** — Escalation required. This represents the MRV system's eutrophication alert level, established from the Ada-Songor baseline data where 984 mg/kg was recorded at Wasakuse. At nitrate concentrations above 500 mg/kg, nitrogen-stimulated microbial decomposition of soil organic matter becomes a meaningful risk — the nutrient enrichment that promotes short-term plant growth also accelerates the breakdown of the humus pool that constitutes the site's blue carbon asset.

- **Soil pH below 4.5** — Immediate escalation. This threshold indicates the onset of confirmed acid sulphate soil conditions in the soil profile itself, not merely in the adjacent water column. It signals that the acid front has penetrated from the water surface into the organic matter layer, placing the humus pool under direct chemical attack.

**Cross-parameter trend alert:**

- **Any parameter exceeding the December 2025 baseline value by more than 30% in two consecutive quarterly rounds** — Trend alert requiring escalation review. This threshold is designed to catch progressive deterioration that does not yet trigger individual-parameter alerts but represents a directional ecological change that requires investigation before it crosses a critical threshold. A 30% increase in salinity over two consecutive rounds at a station where the December 2025 value was 25 ppt, for example, would bring that station to 32.5 ppt — not yet at the critical threshold, but on a trajectory that, if continued for another round, would approach it.

### 10.4.2 Standard Escalation Protocol

When a field team records a reading at or beyond any alert threshold, the following protocol applies regardless of the monitoring round in progress:

**Step 1 — Alert reading recorded.** CCA or field team records the anomalous value in the MRV Mobile App, flags it using the app's alert function, and photographs the station and water or soil condition at the time of measurement. This is completed on the day of the field reading.

**Step 2 — 14-day field verification visit.** Within 14 days of the alert reading, the monitoring team returns to the flagged station for an independent verification measurement. The purpose of this visit is to distinguish between an instrument calibration error, a one-off acute event that has already resolved, and a confirmed sustained anomaly. If the verification reading confirms the anomaly — either at or beyond the original alert threshold — the station moves to the confirmed-alert category. If the verification reading is within normal range, the original reading is documented as a potential instrument error or one-off event and the station returns to standard monitoring.

**Step 3 — Confirmed anomaly report.** If the verification visit confirms a sustained anomaly, the CCA or district field officer prepares a formal confirmed-anomaly report and submits it to the district Wildlife Division within 30 days of the original alert reading. The report must include: the station ID and GPS coordinates, the original alert reading with date and time, the verification reading with date and time, photographs from both visits, the field officer's assessment of the likely cause, and a recommendation for management response.

**Step 4 — Wildlife Division response window.** The district Wildlife Division has 60 days from receipt of the confirmed anomaly report to: (a) identify the probable cause of the anomaly and initiate or commission a management response, or (b) determine that the anomaly does not require a management intervention at this time and document the reasoning for that determination. Either response must be formally recorded in the MRV platform.

**Step 5 — National escalation.** If the district Wildlife Division has not formally responded within 60 days of receiving the confirmed anomaly report, or if the identified cause requires a management response beyond the authority or resource capacity of the district tier, the issue escalates to MLNR and MESTI. National-level escalation is also required automatically for any anomaly involving acid sulphate formation, active vegetation die-back combined with anomalous chemistry, or any combination of parameters suggesting an acute pollution event of off-site origin.

### 10.4.3 Special Protocols for Existing Alert-Level Stations

As of the December 2025 baseline, three station zones are already at or beyond alert-level thresholds and require specific monitoring arrangements that differ from the standard quarterly schedule.

**Anyanui-Tunu Road (Keta Cluster 1, pH anomaly):** The pH 2.7 recorded at the Tunu Road stations during the December 2025 baseline represents confirmed, ongoing acid sulphate soil formation. This condition does not require a 14-day verification visit — it was confirmed during the baseline assessment and independently interpreted in Chapter 8 of this report. The management response required is a field hydrological investigation and engineering intervention to restore tidal exchange at the road crossing. Until that engineering recommendation has been issued by a qualified hydrologist and the tidal restoration works have been completed or formally commissioned, the Anyanui-Tunu Road stations are elevated to **monthly monitoring**. Monthly measurements track whether the acid chemistry is expanding spatially (measured by whether pH continues to fall at adjacent Agbledorne stations), whether seasonal variation in tidal conditions produces any temporary improvement, and whether the soil pH at the Tunu Road dead mangrove stations is declining below 5.5 — the threshold at which the acid front has penetrated the organic matter layer and the 44.3% humus is under direct chemical attack.

**Patukope-Pute (Ada Cluster 12, near-acid-sulphate soil conditions):** The soil pH of 4.78 recorded at Pute lagoon during the December 2025 assessment places this station in the confirmed-stressed category with an imminent risk of crossing the soil-alert threshold (pH below 4.5). Pute lagoon stations are elevated to **monthly monitoring** until a full soil characterisation at those stations — including laboratory analysis of acid sulphate indicators — confirms whether the conditions represent a localised anomaly or an expanding acid front comparable to the Anyanui situation. The key diagnostic question is whether the low pH is driven by organic acid accumulation under waterlogged conditions (a recoverable, manageable state) or by active pyrite oxidation following hydrological disruption (the irreversible acid sulphate process that has produced the pH 2.7 emergency at Keta).

**Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane (Ada Cluster 13, ORP anomaly and extreme nutrient loading):** The ORP values of 832–1,547 mV in the Ada interior lagoon and the nitrate reading of 984 mg/kg at Wasakuse both exceed their respective alert thresholds (ORP >600 mV; nitrate >500 mg/kg). These stations are also elevated to **monthly monitoring** until: the cause of the ORP anomaly has been formally attributed (whether to long-term hydrological alteration from reduced Volta freshwater discharge or to a more proximate, reversible driver), and cattle access to the Obane wetland margin has been formally restricted under a documented land-use agreement. Monthly monitoring at Wasakuse-Obane tracks whether the ORP values are stable, improving, or worsening across the seasonal cycle, and whether the extreme nutrient loading at the wetland margin is being reduced as cattle management interventions are implemented.

## 10.5 Community Change Agent (CCA) Operational Role

### 10.5.1 CCA Mandate and Training Foundation

Community Change Agents are the operational backbone of the quarterly monitoring network. Their training occurred in the field, alongside the scientific team, during the December 2025 baseline campaign — this was not a classroom induction but a hands-on demonstration of sampling methodology, instrument operation, and data recording at the actual monitoring stations. CCAs handled the Apera SX751 and Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD instruments during the baseline survey and are familiar with the station locations, the parameter ranges expected at their designated clusters, and the physical landscape and access routes they will navigate in subsequent rounds.

Each of the 13 monitoring clusters has one or more designated CCAs drawn from the adjacent community or communities. This geographic assignment is intentional: CCAs with local knowledge of a specific cluster are better placed to distinguish normal ecological conditions from genuine anomalies, to recognise changes in vegetation condition, and to identify anthropogenic activities — fishing, cattle grazing, salt pan expansion, fuelwood harvesting — that may be the proximate drivers of parameter change. They are not simply data collectors; they are locally-embedded ecological observers whose contextual knowledge amplifies the signal value of the instrument readings they record.

If instruments different from those used during the December 2025 baseline are introduced for future monitoring rounds, CCAs must receive updated training specifically on those instruments before the round in which they are deployed. The quality of the instrument data depends entirely on correct calibration and operation; introducing a new device without training undermines the comparability of the entire dataset.

### 10.5.2 Quarterly Field Procedures

CCAs operating under the quarterly monitoring schedule are responsible for the following activities at each round:

**Water quality sampling:** Using the Apera SX751, CCAs measure pH, temperature, electrical conductivity, salinity, total dissolved solids, dissolved oxygen, ORP, and resistivity at each of their designated water quality stations. Measurements are taken at the same sub-location within each station (defined by the GPS coordinates and field description established in December 2025), at a consistent depth (surface to 30 cm, or mid-water column in shallow lagoon settings), and recorded with the date, time, station ID, GPS coordinates, and the device model and last calibration date.

**Soil sampling:** Using the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD, CCAs collect nitrate, phosphate, potassium, pH, moisture, humus, and temperature readings at designated soil sampling stations. Soil samples are taken from the established sampling depth (0–20 cm surface horizon) at the GPS-recorded locations used in December 2025. Where the instrument requires insertion into undisturbed soil, CCAs must clear surface debris before insertion to prevent contamination of the reading by decomposed litter.

**Data upload:** All field readings are entered into the MRV Mobile App — the smartphone interface connected to the Blue Carbon MRV platform at `blue-carbon-mrv-green.vercel.app` — after each field session. Data should be uploaded before the CCA leaves the field area or, at the latest, on the evening of the sampling day. Delayed upload increases the risk of data loss and deprives the district validation tier of timely access to field records.

**Alert notification:** If any reading meets or approaches an alert threshold during the field session, the CCA activates the app's alert function, which generates an immediate notification to the district Wildlife Division officer. This alert function bypasses the standard quarterly reporting cycle and ensures that anomalous readings trigger a rapid institutional response without waiting for the end-of-round data compilation. Concurrent with the digital alert, the CCA photographs the sampling location — water surface, soil condition, vegetation condition, and any visible anthropogenic disturbance — and uploads the photographs alongside the parameter reading.

**Anomaly documentation:** Where a CCA observes conditions that are not captured by instrument measurements — a dead mangrove stand previously not present, new infrastructure encroachment, visible oil sheen or discolouration on water, cattle damage to wetland margins — they record a qualitative field observation note in the app. These qualitative observations are as ecologically significant as the quantitative readings in many cases: the dead mangrove canopy at the Anyanui-Tunu road site was the visible indicator that led the December 2025 team to investigate the pH chemistry that revealed the acid sulphate emergency.

### 10.5.3 CCA Station Access and Governance Implications

Access to monitoring stations is not only a logistical matter; it is a governance indicator. Whether CCAs can consistently reach their designated stations — whether paths are maintained, whether community relationships support free movement through the landscape, whether no new land use or physical barrier has compromised access — directly reflects the operational health of the community governance structure underpinning the monitoring programme.

A station that becomes inaccessible because a land-use conflict has escalated, because a road construction project has blocked a wetland access route, or because community relations with the monitoring programme have broken down is not merely an operational inconvenience. It is a Layer III MRV signal — a governance and safeguard indicator that the institutional conditions required to sustain monitoring at that location have deteriorated. The Wildlife Division, when reviewing quarterly data submission records, should track not only the parameter values recorded but also the completeness of station coverage. Any station that is not visited in a given quarterly round requires an explanation logged in the platform — whether the gap resulted from equipment failure, field team absence, or access restriction. The distinction matters: equipment failure is a logistical problem solvable by maintenance and spare inventory; access restriction is a governance problem requiring institutional attention from the district Wildlife Division, the MMDA, and potentially MLNR.

The formalization of CCA roles — including compensation arrangements, defined institutional relationships with the Wildlife Division, and documented recognition within CREMA governance structures — is a prerequisite for maintaining the 80-station network beyond the initial few rounds of the programme. CCAs who conducted the December 2025 baseline were motivated by training, participation, and the novelty of the programme; sustaining that motivation across multi-year quarterly cycles requires that their role carries formal institutional recognition, predictable operational support, and a tangible connection to the outcomes the programme is designed to achieve.

## 10.6 Integration with EOSDA Drone Monitoring

### 10.6.1 The Two-Layer Detection System

The ground-level 80-station monitoring network measures what is happening inside the ecosystem — at the water surface, in the soil profile, and within the biochemical environment that governs carbon storage. It detects chemistry. The EOSDA satellite monitoring platform, operating at the four defined Areas of Interest (AoIs), measures what is happening on top of the ecosystem — changes in canopy cover, vegetation water stress, and land-surface reflectance patterns detectable from satellite-derived indices. Together, these two monitoring layers create a mutually reinforcing detection architecture: EOSDA detects surface vegetation signals; the ground monitoring network diagnoses the underlying cause.

The four EOSDA AoIs and their hectarage, as defined in the spatial boundary files already prepared and deposited in the project repository, are:

| AoI Name | Area (Ha) | Boundary File |
|---|---|---|
| Aborlove Nolopi | 52 | `aborlove_protection_area_ndmi_26feb2026_boundary.geojson` |
| Agatsivi-Agortoe | 762 | *(overlapping Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster zone)* |
| Salo-Agortoe Stretch | 525 | `salo_restoration_area_ndmi_26feb2026_boundary.geojson` |
| Anyanui Combine | 128 | *(overlapping Anyanui-Dzita Alert cluster)* |
| **Total EOSDA coverage** | **1,467 Ha** | |

These four AoIs are not randomly selected. They correspond precisely to the zones where the ground monitoring network has identified the most significant ecological management questions and where early detection of vegetation change carries the highest management dividend.

### 10.6.2 Spatial Overlap Between EOSDA AoIs and Ground Monitoring Clusters

**Anyanui Combine (128 Ha) overlaps with Monitoring Cluster 1 (Anyanui-Tunu Road — ALERT).** This is the most important EOSDA-to-ground integration in the network. The 128-hectare Anyanui Combine AoI encompasses the zone of active acid sulphate formation and dead mangrove canopy identified at pH 2.7 during the December 2025 baseline. EOSDA satellite monitoring of this AoI will detect further vegetation loss — additional mangrove die-back — before it is confirmed by the quarterly ground measurement. When the NDMI (Normalised Difference Moisture Index) output for the Anyanui Combine AoI shows a vegetation stress signal, the ground team at the Anyanui-Tunu Road stations should visit within 14 days to assess whether the signal corresponds to a further advance of the acid front, a new hydrological disruption event, or another causal mechanism requiring investigation.

**Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha) and Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha) overlap with Monitoring Clusters 3 and 4 (Saviatula-Galotse and Agortoe-Agbastivi — GOOD/Standard).** These AoIs cover the most ecologically productive and carbon-dense zone in the entire Keta monitoring network — the benchmark mangrove core where humus concentrations reach 40.6% and soil moisture exceeds 40%. EOSDA monitoring of this zone provides the earliest possible warning of fuelwood harvesting encroachment into the benchmark carbon area. If NDVI or NDMI values decline by more than 15% within either AoI between consecutive satellite observations, the adjacent ground monitoring stations at Galotse and Agortoe-Agbastivi should be visited within 14 days. The ground visit will assess whether the satellite signal reflects a legitimate vegetation stress response (rising salinity, falling DO, changing water chemistry) or a harvesting event, and will produce the evidence needed for a Wildlife Division enforcement or CREMA mediation response.

**Aborlove Nolopi (52 Ha) overlaps with Monitoring Cluster 5 (Aborlorvi-Norlopi — ELEVATED, restoration candidate).** This 52-hectare AoI is the smallest and most precisely defined EOSDA zone, corresponding to the proposed mangrove restoration site at Aborlorvi. Satellite monitoring here serves a different purpose from the other three AoIs: rather than detecting degradation, it will track the trajectory of the restoration site — whether early planting survives and generates a measurable vegetation signal in subsequent NDVI readings. When EOSDA detects a change in this zone, the Aborlorvi-Norlopi ground stations provide the soil moisture and chemistry context that explains whether improved conditions at the site are supporting or constraining establishment.

### 10.6.3 EOSDA Integration Protocol

The intended integration between EOSDA and the ground monitoring network operates as follows. When EOSDA satellite monitoring detects a vegetation change signal — specifically, an NDVI change greater than 15% or an NDMI reading outside the expected seasonal range at any of the four AoIs — the following actions are initiated:

The EOSDA platform flags the relevant AoI in the national MRV platform interface. The district Wildlife Division is notified through the platform. Within 14 days, the CCAs or a supervised field team visits the ground monitoring stations nearest to the flagged zone within the corresponding cluster. The field visit assesses: whether the satellite signal is confirmed by visible change in canopy or vegetation condition; what the current water chemistry and soil parameter values are at the adjacent monitoring stations; and whether any anthropogenic activity (harvesting, construction, cattle grazing, salt pan expansion) is visibly contributing to the change. The field team submits a satellite-response field report through the MRV platform within 7 days of the visit.

This two-layer architecture means that the monitoring programme's detection capability is not limited to what occurs during the four designated quarterly rounds per year. EOSDA provides continuous remote observation; the ground network provides the quarterly physiochemical context and the rapid-response capability when satellite signals require diagnosis. Neither layer is sufficient alone: satellite imagery cannot measure soil pH, dissolved oxygen, or nitrate concentration; ground stations cannot observe the full spatial extent of a vegetation change event that may cover tens or hundreds of hectares across the AoI.

Once EOSDA account configuration, AoI polygon uploads, and recurring monitoring workflow activation have been completed by MLNR's designated technical officer — the next institutional action step for the satellite monitoring layer — the combined detection system described here will be fully operational.

## 10.7 Data Management and MRV Platform Integration

### 10.7.1 Field-to-Platform Data Flow

Quarterly monitoring data from the 80 stations follows a defined institutional pathway from field collection to national archive. Understanding this pathway — and the quality-assurance functions performed at each tier — is essential for the site managers and CCAs who are the primary data producers.

At the community tier, CCAs enter field readings into the MRV Mobile App at the time of or immediately following sampling. The app captures the station ID, GPS coordinates, date and time, device model and calibration date, parameter readings, and any qualitative field observations or alert flags. Submissions that are incomplete — missing a GPS coordinate, omitting a device calibration reference, or recording implausible values without explanation — are automatically queued for clarification rather than passing directly into the validated dataset.

At the district tier, Wildlife Division officers review CCA-submitted records within the platform's validation queue. Their role is not simply to approve all records but to exercise professional judgement: do the values recorded fall within the ecologically plausible range for that station and season? Do the qualitative observations align with what field staff know of conditions at that location? Are there any systematic patterns — all readings from a particular CCA's sessions being consistently slightly high or low across parameters — that might indicate instrument calibration drift? District validation is the quality-assurance gate that protects the integrity of the national dataset.

At the national tier, MLNR and MESTI access validated quarterly records through the platform's national dashboard. Their interface presents aggregated spatial summaries of parameter trends by cluster and site, alert status by station, and data submission completeness metrics. Annual analysis at the national tier distinguishes between seasonal patterns — which are expected and ecologically normal, matching the four-round structure described in Section 10.3 — and multi-year trends, which are the primary signals for adaptive management.

### 10.7.2 Trend Analysis and the Three-Year Milestone

A single quarterly dataset can confirm the baseline and detect acute anomalies. Two quarterly datasets can suggest a directional trend. After three years of quarterly monitoring — twelve rounds of data at each of the 80 stations — the dataset will have sufficient temporal depth to formally separate seasonal oscillation from structural ecological change. The three-year trend analysis will answer the questions that the December 2025 baseline cannot answer alone: Is salinity at the Anyanui corridor declining, stable, or rising? Is the ORP anomaly in the Ada interior lagoon varying seasonally or is it a fixed, structural feature of the system? Is humus accumulation continuing at the Galotse benchmark stations, or is there evidence of the first declines in soil organic matter?

These trend assessments are not academic exercises. They feed directly into three operational outputs: the annual national GHG inventory submitted by Ghana under its Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitments, which uses the ecological condition data to contextualise carbon stock status; carbon verification site visits by Verra, Gold Standard, or Plan Vivo auditors, who will review the quarterly dataset as evidence of continued ecological integrity at the monitored sites; and adaptive management decisions at the district and national tier, including when to escalate interventions from monitoring and CCA engagement to active management responses such as hydrological engineering at Anyanui, cattle exclusion at Obane, or restoration planting at Aborlorvi.

### 10.7.3 Backups and Data Integrity

The Supabase Pro database hosting the MRV platform provides automated point-in-time recovery for all deposited data, ensuring that the quarterly monitoring archive can be restored to any earlier state in the event of accidental modification, corruption, or system failure. However, digital backup alone is not sufficient governance for a dataset that underpins national GHG accounting and international carbon market obligations. CCAs and district officers should maintain physical data sheet backups — completed at the time of measurement in the field — which are archived at the district Wildlife Division office after each quarterly round. These physical records are the last-resort verification document if digital records are ever challenged during a carbon audit.

All field data sheets must be completed with the standard information set: site name, GPS coordinates, descriptive notes on site condition, date, time, recorder's name, and equipment model and calibration status. Any deliberate variation from the standard sampling protocol — a changed sample location, a substitute instrument, a skipped station — must be recorded and communicated to the district Wildlife Division. Silent variations destroy comparability with the baseline record and with other quarterly rounds.

## 10.8 Equipment Standardisation and Maintenance

### 10.8.1 Instrument Continuity as a Scientific Requirement

The scientific value of the quarterly monitoring dataset depends entirely on the comparability of measurements across rounds. A dissolved oxygen reading of 5.2 mg/L recorded by the Apera SX751 in December 2025 is directly comparable to a reading of 5.4 mg/L recorded by the same model in June 2026, if both instruments were calibrated against the same reference solutions before the survey. That same June 2026 reading recorded by a different instrument model — even one marketed as equivalent — is not directly comparable without a validated cross-calibration exercise that documents the offset between instruments and applies a correction factor. Cross-calibrations are technically feasible but operationally burdensome and introduce their own sources of uncertainty.

The default position of the monitoring programme is therefore instrument continuity: the Apera SX751 multiparameter water testing device for water quality, and the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD Ver 1.0 for soil chemistry, are the designated instruments for all 80 stations across all quarterly rounds, maintained in serviceable condition and replaced like-for-like if they reach end of operational life. Any departure from this standard requires a formal protocol variation notice submitted to the district Wildlife Division and recorded in the MRV platform, with documentation of the cross-calibration exercise conducted before the variant instrument is used in the field.

### 10.8.2 Calibration Protocol

Before each quarterly survey round — meaning before fieldwork begins, not during or after — both instruments must be calibrated against certified reference solutions. For the Apera SX751, calibration must cover at minimum the pH, conductivity/salinity, and DO channels, using reference solutions within their certified validity window. Calibration drift in any channel should be documented and, if it exceeds the instrument's stated accuracy specification, reported as a data quality flag on all readings taken after the last confirmed calibration point.

For the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD, the calibration protocol specified in the instrument manual must be followed for each soil parameter channel. The soil instrument's quick-test platform relies on insert probes that are sensitive to soil contamination; probes must be cleaned between station insertions according to the manufacturer's specification to prevent carryover error between sites.

Calibration records — the date, time, reference solution batch number, pre-calibration reading, post-calibration reading, and the name of the technician performing the calibration — must be recorded in the pre-survey calibration log and uploaded to the MRV platform as metadata associated with the corresponding quarterly dataset. These records are audit requirements for carbon market verification and must be maintained throughout the programme.

### 10.8.3 Equipment Inventory and Spare Instruments

The monitoring programme must budget for the full lifecycle cost of instrument maintenance, not only the cost of instrument procurement. Sensor replacement for the Apera SX751 — particularly the DO membrane, pH electrode, and conductivity cell — is required on the manufacturer's recommended service interval, which varies by use intensity and storage conditions. In a tropical coastal environment, electrode degradation is faster than in laboratory settings; annual sensor replacement for heavily used instruments is a realistic planning assumption.

It is strongly recommended that one complete spare set of monitoring instruments — one Apera SX751 and one Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD — be maintained at each of the two sites (Keta and Ada), held under the custody of the district Wildlife Division office. These spare sets serve two functions: they enable immediate instrument substitution if a field instrument fails mid-survey, preventing the loss of an entire quarterly round's data; and they provide the reference instruments against which field instruments can be cross-checked if calibration anomalies are suspected. The spare instrument held at the Wildlife Division office must itself be maintained in calibrated condition — a spare that has never been calibrated and has been sitting on a shelf for twelve months is not a reliable backup.

Calibration consumables — reference solutions, electrode storage solutions, cleaning materials — must be included in the annual programme budget as recurring items, not as one-off procurement costs. Underfunding of consumables is one of the most common failure modes of community-based monitoring programmes in analogous regional contexts: instruments are procured, CCAs are trained, surveys begin — and then field calibration becomes approximate or ceases entirely because no budget provision was made for the reference solutions required to verify instrument accuracy before each round.
