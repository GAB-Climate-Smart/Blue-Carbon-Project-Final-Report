# Chapter 4: Keta Lagoon Water Quality — Baseline Results and Ecological Assessment

---

## 4.0 Chapter Overview

This chapter presents the full water quality results from the December 2025 baseline assessment of the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, organised by the five ecological clusters that structure the monitoring network. The data were collected across 27 water quality sampling stations distributed across the complex, using the Apera SX751 multiparameter device to record nine physico-chemical parameters simultaneously at each georeferenced position. The parameters assessed are pH, water temperature, electrical conductivity, salinity, total dissolved solids (TDS), resistivity, dissolved oxygen (DO), and oxidation-reduction potential (ORP).

The presentation that follows is not a statistical summary of ranges across the full site. It is a cluster-by-cluster analysis in which the findings at each named station are interpreted in terms of their ecological meaning — what the observed values indicate about mangrove species viability, carbon storage conditions, and the management priority status of that part of the complex. Station-level values are named and contextualised rather than aggregated. The chapter concludes with a cross-cluster synthesis that characterises the Keta Lagoon Complex as a hydrological and ecological system, identifies the gradient from freshwater-influenced northwestern margins to hypersaline lagoon body, defines the benchmark and stressed zones, and translates the water quality gradient into a carbon management priority framework.

The five clusters are:

- **4.1 Anyanui-Dzita Cluster** — the northwestern zone, Volta-influenced, containing the report's most critical ecological anomaly
- **4.2 Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi Cluster** — the core mangrove zone, the reference benchmark for healthy ecological conditions
- **4.3 Aborlorvi-Norlopi Cluster** — the proposed 52-hectare restoration zone, whose water quality determines restoration feasibility
- **4.4 Genui Reference Station** — the northeastern reference point linking the main lagoon to the eastern mangrove complex
- **4.5 Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga Cluster** — the main lagoon bank corridor, exhibiting elevated pH and high salinity consistent with limited freshwater input

## 4.1 Anyanui-Dzita Cluster

### 4.1.1 Cluster Profile and Station Network

The Anyanui-Dzita cluster encompasses the northwestern zone of the Keta Lagoon Complex, the portion of the lagoon closest to the former influence of the Volta River. The cluster extends along the lagoon margin from Anyanui in the west through the creek systems feeding the lagoon interior, across the road corridor connecting Anyanui and Tunu, and east to Agbledome and Dzita. Eight water quality sampling stations are distributed across this zone:

- **Anyanui landing site** (N 05° 47.012, E 000° 43.276) — the community access point to the lagoon, where subsistence fishing and mangrove-product harvesting occur
- **Fuveme creek** (N 05° 46.564, E 000° 43.921) — a tidal creek draining toward the lagoon margin
- **Agorkedzi creek** (N 05° 46.704, E 000° 42.476) — a secondary creek system feeding into the lagoon interior
- **Tunu creek** (N 05° 47.571, E 000° 43.140) — an inter-connecting creek linking the road corridor to the main lagoon
- **Anyanui-Tunu road, Station 1** (N 05° 47.308, E 000° 43.600) — a roadside drainage point along the road connecting Anyanui and Tunu, site of the critical pH anomaly
- **Anyanui-Tunu road, Station 2** (N 05° 47.366, E 000° 43.609) — a second station at the same road corridor, approximately 120 metres from Station 1, confirming spatial persistence of the anomaly
- **Agbledome** (N 05° 47.195, E 000° 45.235) — a station at the transition between the Anyanui sub-zone and the central lagoon body, where wind-driven aeration is a notable influence
- **Dzita** (N 05° 46.442, E 000° 46.910) — the easternmost station in this cluster, positioned at the main lagoon body where evaporative concentration is highest

The ecological character of this cluster reflects its position at the hydrological interface between the Volta River's historical freshwater influence and the saline lagoon system. This interface has been fundamentally altered by the reduction in Volta River discharge and sediment delivery downstream of the Akosombo Dam, which was constructed in 1964 and now traps more than 90% of the river's sediment load before it reaches the coast. The Anyanui-Dzita zone — historically the site of active mangrove regeneration sustained by the river's freshwater pulse — now exhibits the consequences of that disruption: dead and dying mangrove stands at the road corridor, hypersaline conditions at the lagoon margin, and the acid sulphate crisis documented at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations. The soil sampling coordinates at the same road corridor (Table 3: Anyanui, Dead mangroves, Tunu road at N 05° 47.308, E 000° 43.600; Anyanui Tunu road at N 05° 47.366, E 000° 43.609) directly corroborate the water quality findings: the same stations that record catastrophically low water pH are also those positioned amid dead mangrove stands.

### 4.1.2 pH

The pH values recorded across the Anyanui-Dzita cluster reveal a system in two distinct ecological states occupying the same geographic zone, separated by a boundary that the December 2025 data has located but not yet mapped in its full spatial extent.

Across six of the eight stations in the cluster — Anyanui landing site, Fuveme creek, Agorkedzi creek, Tunu creek, Agbledome, and Dzita — pH values ranged between **7.1 and 7.59**. This is a broadly acceptable range for coastal lagoon ecology. Values in this band support the physiological function of *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* — the two dominant mangrove species at Keta — within the range that permits cellular respiration, nutrient uptake, and root system function. The variation within this band reflects the interaction between tidal exchange, seasonal rainfall, temperature, and the buffering capacity of the water column. The temporary influence of intermittent rainfall during the December 2025 sampling period — which was nominally the dry season but included unseasonal rain events — is likely responsible for the lower end of this range at freshwater-influenced stations, where rainfall dilution suppressed ionic concentration and shifted pH slightly toward neutral.

At the two Anyanui-Tunu road stations, the pH reading was **2.7**.

This single value is the most significant ecological finding in the Keta water quality dataset. A pH of 2.7 is not a variation within the manageable ecological range. It is, on the logarithmic pH scale, more than four units below the lower threshold of 7.1 recorded at the neighbouring Anyanui landing site station — a difference that corresponds to more than ten thousand times the hydrogen ion concentration of a value at the low end of the cluster's normal range. It is more than five standard pH units below the minimum threshold of approximately 6.5 at which *Rhizophora racemosa* can maintain photosynthetic function, and more than 1.3 units below the pH 4.0 alert threshold defined in the quarterly monitoring escalation protocol. A value of 2.7 places this water in the category of dilute sulphuric acid.

The mechanism that produces this condition is acid sulphate soil formation. The sediments underlying tidal wetlands in coastal lagoon systems characteristically contain iron sulphide minerals — primarily pyrite (FeS₂) — that form under the anaerobic, waterlogged conditions of healthy mangrove habitats. Under normal tidal exchange conditions, these sulphidic sediments remain waterlogged and anaerobic, and the pyrite is chemically stable: the iron and sulphur remain reduced and bonded, posing no ecological threat. When tidal exchange is disrupted — when inter-connecting channels are blocked, when drainage infrastructure diverts water away from the wetland, when roads are constructed across tidal floodplains without adequate culverts, or when the Volta River's freshwater pulse that historically maintained lagoon water levels declines — the water table in the sediment column drops. Exposure of the pyrite-bearing sediments to atmospheric oxygen triggers the chemical oxidation of iron sulphide to sulphuric acid. The reaction is rapid, exothermic, and self-sustaining: once oxidation begins, the acidification it produces further destabilises the soil chemistry, mobilising aluminium and other toxic metals that compound the biological damage. The result is a soil and water environment in which the pH falls to values that are not merely sub-optimal for mangrove growth — they are actively lethal to existing root systems and obliterate the conditions necessary for seedling establishment.

The spatial context of the anomaly is directly informative. The Anyanui-Tunu road is a road corridor built across the lagoon margin, separating the tidal zone from the mangrove interior. Roads of this type, if not equipped with adequate culverts and tidal floodgates, function as physical barriers to tidal exchange: the seaward side maintains tidal flooding while the landward side — the side supplying fresh water and maintaining soil saturation — is hydrologically cut off. The dead mangrove stands documented at the same coordinates in Table 3 are not the cause of the acid sulphate condition; they are its consequence. As mangroves died — deprived of tidal exchange and therefore unable to maintain the waterlogged anaerobic conditions that stabilise their sulphidic soils — the drying sediments began to oxidise, the acid formed, and what remained of the vegetation root systems was progressively destroyed by the acidifying water. The pH of 2.7 is the current state of this process, not its beginning.

For the management of carbon stocks, the acid sulphate process at Anyanui-Tunu road represents a direct and active emission pathway. Waterlogged, anaerobic mangrove soils are the primary reservoir of blue carbon in the Keta complex: organic matter accumulated over decades under low-pH, low-oxygen, reducing conditions is preserved precisely because the chemical environment suppresses microbial decomposition. When acidification and oxidation enter those sediments — whether through surface drainage or through the drying of the water table — the organic carbon accumulated in those soils is exposed to decomposition. The carbon stored in the dead mangrove root systems at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations, and in the surrounding sediment profile, is not stable. It is actively at risk of mineralisation and release, constituting a carbon stock loss that will not be reversible on any management timescale without complete hydrological restoration.

The management implication is urgent. The pH anomaly at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations must be treated not as a data point requiring further study before action, but as the signature of an active, ongoing, and expanding ecological failure that requires immediate hydrological investigation. Quarterly monitoring at these two stations is mandatory, but monitoring alone is not sufficient. The December 2025 baseline establishes that acid sulphate conditions are present and generating pH values more than 1.3 units below the defined alert threshold. The required follow-up action is a field investigation within 14 days of this report's receipt by the Wildlife Division at the Keta Municipal District level, aimed at identifying the precise drainage or infrastructure modification responsible for the hydrological disruption, and a formal recommendation for culvert installation, tidal gate restoration, or alternative hydrological intervention that can begin to restore tidal exchange to the affected zone.

### 4.1.3 Water Temperature

Temperature across the Anyanui-Dzita cluster, as across the Keta complex as a whole, varied predictably with the time of day during the December 2025 sampling period. Morning and late-morning readings recorded between **25°C and 30.3°C**; afternoon readings ranged from **31.19°C to 37.3°C**, with the higher end of this range recorded at open water stations where solar exposure is greatest and thermal mass is lowest. The mean temperature across the Keta assessment ranged from **30.24°C to 31.3°C**.

At the Anyanui-Dzita cluster, the creeks — Fuveme, Agorkedzi, and Tunu — are narrower channels with some degree of shading from surrounding vegetation and embankment structure, moderating afternoon peak temperatures relative to the open lagoon stations. Dzita, positioned at the open lagoon body, is the most thermally exposed station in the cluster and can be expected to record the highest afternoon values during the dry season.

Temperature at this cluster has two management-relevant implications. First, elevated afternoon temperatures in the 33–37°C range at open water stations reduce dissolved oxygen solubility, which at thermally stressed stations can compound any biological oxygen demand effects and contribute to hypoxic micro-conditions near the sediment surface. Second, and more directly relevant to the acid sulphate emergency at the road stations, elevated soil temperatures at the exposed, dead-mangrove sites accelerate the rate of pyrite oxidation and organic carbon mineralisation in the acidified sediments. A warming trajectory at these stations — whether from regional climate trends or from the loss of canopy cooling following mangrove die-back — would accelerate both the acid-generating reaction and the carbon loss from oxidised organic soil layers.

### 4.1.4 Conductivity, Salinity, and Total Dissolved Solids

The most pronounced ionic concentration gradient in the entire Keta monitoring network occurs within this single cluster, between the freshwater-influenced western stations and the evaporatively concentrated lagoon body at Dzita.

At Dzita, the December 2025 baseline recorded the highest chemical parameter values in the Keta network: **conductivity of 63.1 µS, TDS of 88.1 ppm, and salinity of 40 ppt**. These values reflect the compound effect of the station's position within the main lagoon body, where the combination of high surface area, shallow depth, limited freshwater inflow, and intense dry-season solar radiation drives progressive evaporative concentration of dissolved ions throughout the dry season. The resistivity value at Dzita of **15.8 KΩ** is consistent with the ionic composition of concentrated marine-origin salt water rather than a pollution-altered or chemically contaminated water body: the specific resistivity-conductivity relationship at this station is characteristic of evaporatively concentrated lagoon water dominated by sodium and chloride ions.

A salinity of 40 ppt at Dzita places this station at the operational threshold for *Rhizophora racemosa* tolerance. *Rhizophora* species are the primary carbon-productive mangrove in the Keta complex, and their optimal ecological range is broadly 5–35 ppt. At 40 ppt, *Rhizophora* is at the upper limit of its physiological tolerance: growth rates decline, the energy expenditure required for osmotic regulation diverts resources from above-ground and below-ground biomass production, and the carbon sequestration rate of trees functioning at salinity stress is lower than that of trees operating within their optimal range. *Avicennia germinans*, with a substantially broader salinity tolerance extending to approximately 90 ppt, is better positioned to persist under the current Dzita conditions — but *Avicennia*-dominated stands at high salinity produce less above-ground biomass and accumulate soil carbon at different rates than productive *Rhizophora* stands in optimal salinity conditions. The ecological meaning of 40 ppt at Dzita is not merely that the water is saline; it is that the mangrove community that can be supported at this station is limited in its species composition and reduced in its carbon accumulation potential relative to the complex's more productive core.

At the stations under freshwater or rainfall influence — distributed across the western portion of the cluster during the December 2025 sampling — a minimum salinity of **4.3 ppt**, conductivity of **69.3 µS**, and TDS of **100 ppm** were recorded. These values reflect the diluting influence of the unseasonal rainfall that occurred during the sampling period and illustrate the seasonal oscillation that characterises these stations: in the wet season, freshwater inflow (however reduced relative to historical Volta discharge) pushes salinity toward the low end of the mangrove-optimal range; in the dry season, evaporative concentration pushes it upward, toward and sometimes beyond the hypersaline threshold.

The management signal from the conductivity and TDS data at this cluster is directional: the quarterly monitoring programme should track whether the minimum salinity at the western Anyanui stations — which currently descends to 4.3 ppt during wet-season or rainfall events — is rising over successive years. A progressive upward drift in minimum salinity at the freshwater-influenced stations would be the quantitative signature of the hydrological deterioration driven by reduced Volta River discharge, confirming that the ecological space available for mixed-salinity mangrove communities in this sub-zone is contracting. This is among the most critical trend indicators in the entire Keta monitoring network.

### 4.1.5 Dissolved Oxygen

Dissolved oxygen at Anyanui, Agbledome, and the creek stations in this cluster was recorded as relatively high in the December 2025 baseline data. The elevated DO at these stations is attributed to three co-occurring physical factors: shallow water depth, which reduces the distance between the surface and the sediment and allows wind-driven mixing to oxygenate the full water column; windy conditions during the sampling period at the open stations; and high water temperatures, which, while reducing saturation concentration, increase the rate of gas exchange at the surface under wind-driven conditions.

At Dzita, DO was recorded at **13.23 mg/l**, a value that reflects robust oxygen availability at the main lagoon body station despite its hypersaline conditions. This value is substantially above the 3.0 mg/l alert threshold and indicates that aerobic conditions prevail in the surface water at Dzita, supporting the decomposition of organic matter and sustaining benthic and aquatic communities. The conjunction of relatively high DO with high salinity and high conductivity at Dzita suggests that biological oxygen demand in this part of the lagoon is currently manageable — likely because the high salinity itself suppresses the growth of the high-biomass phytoplankton and macrophyte communities whose decomposition is the primary driver of oxygen depletion in eutrophic systems.

At the Anyanui-Tunu road stations, the low pH of 2.7 raises specific concerns about the DO regime that the December 2025 data does not fully resolve. Strongly acidic water tends to alter the chemistry of dissolved oxygen measurement and may suppress the aerobic microbial activity whose respiration would otherwise act as an oxygen sink. Whether DO at the acidified stations is sufficiently high to support the aerobic processes that would be required for any soil chemistry recovery following hydrological restoration is a question that quarterly monitoring at those stations must address.

### 4.1.6 Oxidation-Reduction Potential

The ORP data from the Anyanui-Dzita cluster reveals a pattern consistent with the hydrological character of each station type within the cluster.

At Dzita, ORP was recorded at **160 mV** — a mildly positive, weakly oxidising value that reflects the condition of a waterlogged lagoon station where surface water is oxygenated but the sediment column contains the anaerobic micro-zones characteristic of actively functioning mangrove habitat. This value is ecologically appropriate: it indicates that the water column at Dzita is oxidising enough to support biological function and organic matter breakdown in surface sediments, while the sediment depth profile is likely dominated by reducing conditions that preserve the organic carbon accumulated in the mangrove soil over time. An ORP of 160 mV in a high-salinity, waterlogged lagoon station is the signature of a site that, despite its hypersaline conditions, is maintaining the redox chemistry associated with carbon preservation in its deeper sediment layers.

The lowest ORP recorded in the cluster — **29 mV** — was registered at stations under rainfall influence. This near-reducing value reflects the reduced ionic concentration and lower oxygen availability of the freshwater-diluted stations during and following rainfall events, consistent with the temporarily modified hydrological conditions during the sampling period.

At the Anyanui-Tunu road acid anomaly stations, the ORP conditions associated with a pH of 2.7 are ecologically catastrophic in a specific way: acidic oxidation of pyrite-bearing sediments is itself an oxidising process, meaning that the sediments at these stations are undergoing active chemical oxidation that simultaneously generates acidity and destabilises the reducing conditions responsible for carbon preservation. The ORP regime at these stations is the electrochemical opposite of the reducing, carbon-preserving conditions in healthy mangrove soil. The combination of high hydrogen ion concentration (pH 2.7), oxidising conditions, and the exposure of previously stable organic carbon in dead root systems and humus layers constitutes an active carbon emission process that the restoration of tidal exchange must interrupt.

### 4.1.7 Cluster-Level Assessment

The Anyanui-Dzita cluster presents two simultaneously occurring ecological conditions that require separate management responses operating in parallel.

The first condition — represented by six stations with pH 7.1–7.59, salinity rising from freshwater influence to 40 ppt at Dzita, and DO values that reflect functional aquatic systems — is one of ecological stress at the margins but ecological functionality at the core. The hypersaline extreme at Dzita (40 ppt) is limiting for *Rhizophora* but within *Avicennia* tolerance. The freshwater-influenced western stations are functionally supporting mangrove communities within the salinity range where establishment and growth are possible. These six stations, while warranting quarterly monitoring for progressive salinisation, represent a stressed but not yet failed ecological system. The management priority here is trend detection: is the salinity gradient shifting upward? Is the freshwater influence diminishing? Are the stations that currently record 4.3 ppt in the wet season approaching values that would cross into the optimal *Rhizophora* range consistently, or contracting toward freshwater conditions that exclude mangrove species? The quarterly monitoring network provides the temporal resolution to answer these questions within two to three years.

The second condition — the pH 2.7 acid sulphate emergency at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations — represents an ALERT-level ecological failure that has moved beyond the domain of monitoring into the domain of management intervention. No quarterly monitoring cadence will improve the condition at these stations unless the hydrological disruption responsible for the acid sulphate oxidation is physically corrected. The combination of dead mangroves at these coordinates, pH 2.7 in the water column, and the established mechanism of acid sulphate soil formation in tidal-exclusion zones constitutes sufficient evidence for immediate action. The Keta Municipal District Assembly, the Wildlife Division, and the WACA MLNR focal point should be formally notified of this finding as a priority escalation under the monitoring protocol's alert-level reporting pathway.

**Cluster condition rating: ALERT (Anyanui-Tunu road stations); Stressed/Moderate (remaining cluster stations)**

## 4.2 Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi Cluster

### 4.2.1 Cluster Profile and Station Network

The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster constitutes the ecological core of the Keta Lagoon Complex — the zone where water chemistry, salinity regime, and the character of the mangrove community converge to produce the most productive and best-preserved carbon accumulation environment in the complex. It spans a broad arc from the Saviatula Bridge and Akplotorkor stations in the south, northward through the Salo road and market stations, continuing to the Agortoe sites in the central-north, and extending to the Agbastivi and Galotse stations at the northwestern edge of the main mangrove block. Eleven water quality sampling stations are distributed across this cluster:

- **Akplotorkor Site 1** (N 05° 46.359, E 000° 47.451) — a lagoon-margin station near the water's edge in the southern part of the cluster
- **Akplotorkor Site 2** (N 05° 46.383, E 000° 48.621) — a second station at the same sub-zone, approximately 1.2 kilometres from Site 1, capturing variation across the nearshore lagoon margin
- **Saviatula Bridge** (N 05° 46.957, E 000° 49.803) — a bridge crossing at the Saviatula channel, where wind-driven aeration and water flow are pronounced
- **Salo road** (N 05° 48.761, E 000° 47.953) — a road-margin station adjacent to the Salo mangrove corridor, the location of Permanent Sample Plot PSP-1
- **Salo mangrove market** (N 05° 49.464, E 000° 47.405) — a station at the community access point to the Salo mangrove area, where mangrove products are traded
- **Agortoe Site 1** (N 05° 51.700, E 000° 45.274) — a northern cluster station in well-developed mangrove habitat
- **Agortoe Site 2** (N 05° 51.786, E 000° 45.176) — a second Agortoe station approximately 100 metres from Site 1
- **Agbastivi Site 1** (N 05° 51.977, E 000° 44.884) — a station in the upper zone of the mangrove block
- **Agbastivi Site 2** (N 05° 52.124, E 000° 44.733) — a paired station at Agbastivi
- **Galotse Site 1** (N 05° 52.021, E 000° 42.560) — a station at the northwestern edge of the mangrove block, in some of the most developed standing timber in the complex
- **Galotse Site 2** (N 05° 52.818, E 000° 41.397) — the northernmost water quality station in this cluster

The ecological character of this cluster is defined by its position within the intermediate salinity zone that optimises conditions for *Rhizophora racemosa* establishment, growth, and carbon accumulation. The corridor from Salo through Agortoe, Agbastivi, and Galotse supports well-developed *Rhizophora* stands that are among the most structurally complex and carbon-dense in the Keta complex. The soil chemistry data from the same zone — humus content reaching 40.6% at Galotse and 37.5% at Agortoe, potassium reaching its highest concentrations in the network at Agbastivi and Galotse — confirms what the water chemistry implies: these are stations where the biological conditions for organic carbon accumulation are functioning as they should. The Salo Permanent Sample Plot (PSP-1) is located within this cluster, making the water quality data from the Salo road and Salo market stations the primary ecological context for the carbon inventory reference site.

### 4.2.2 pH

pH values across the Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster ranged from **6.5 to 7.6** — the optimal ecological range for mangrove productivity at Keta and the best water pH performance in the entire monitoring network. This range is not a coincidence of geography; it is the result of the specific hydrological balance that characterises this zone: sufficient tidal exchange to prevent the acidification associated with tidal exclusion, sufficient distance from the hypersaline main lagoon body to maintain ionic moderation, and sufficient biological activity from well-developed mangrove stands to contribute organic acid inputs that buffer against alkalisation.

A pH range of 6.5 to 7.6 directly supports the biochemistry of *Rhizophora racemosa* function. Within this range, the root aerenchyma through which *Rhizophora* transports oxygen to its below-ground root systems can operate efficiently; nutrient uptake mechanisms at the root surface function within the ion exchange dynamics of the soil-water interface; and the microbial communities in the surrounding soil — which process leaf litter into humus and drive the nutrient cycling that sustains tree growth — are operating in their most productive pH regime. The well-developed *Rhizophora* stands observed in this cluster during the December 2025 field assessment are the biological expression of the water chemistry measured at these stations. The pH data does not describe a zone that might be able to support productive mangroves; it describes a zone where productive mangroves are already present as a direct consequence of conditions being within the optimal range.

The ecological significance of this pH range extends beyond the current vegetation to the carbon sequestration function of the soils. In the 6.5–7.6 range, anaerobic decomposition pathways dominate in the waterlogged sediment column below the aerobic surface zone, suppressing microbial mineralisation of organic carbon and driving the accumulation of stable humus that constitutes the blue carbon stock. The pH data at the Saviatula-Galotse cluster is not merely a measure of water chemistry — it is a proxy for the carbon preservation conditions operating in the sediment profile beneath these stations.

The Salo-specific pH readings, clustered within this range at both the Salo road and Salo market stations, are particularly significant given that PSP-1 is located in this sub-zone. Carbon inventory measurements at PSP-1 will be interpreted within the ecological baseline established at these water quality stations. A future shift in pH at the Salo stations — either toward the elevated values observed in the Afiadenyigba-Anloga cluster or toward the acidification recorded at Anyanui-Tunu road — would be a direct early warning signal that the ecological conditions supporting the benchmark carbon measurement site are changing.

### 4.2.3 Water Temperature

Temperature at the Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster follows the site-wide pattern — a morning and mid-morning range of 25–30.3°C across Keta and afternoon peaks of 31.19–37.3°C — but with a meaningful ecological modifier in the mangrove interior stations. Well-developed *Rhizophora* canopy provides measurable thermal buffering in enclosed creek and interior lagoon stations. Stations at Agortoe, Agbastivi, and Galotse, which are surrounded by more developed mangrove canopy than the open water stations at Akplotorkor and Saviatula Bridge, will experience lower peak afternoon temperatures than exposed open-water stations, with direct consequences for dissolved oxygen solubility and the comfort of the benthic invertebrate communities that sustain the food web in these habitats.

The temperature data from this cluster provides the thermal baseline against which the Salo PSP-1 soil temperature measurements — reflecting the micro-climatic cooling effect of mangrove canopy on sediment temperature — can be interpreted. A coherent pattern in which the well-canopied interior stations record lower water temperatures, higher dissolved oxygen, and more stable ORP than the open water fringe stations would constitute direct evidence of the ecosystem services delivered by the existing *Rhizophora* forest cover.

### 4.2.4 Conductivity, Salinity, and Total Dissolved Solids

Salinity across the Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster ranged from **10.6 to 40 ppt**, a range that encompasses the full span of productive *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* habitat. The lower end of this range — 10.6 ppt — represents the intermediate-salinity conditions optimal for high-productivity *Rhizophora* growth; stands growing at 10–20 ppt typically produce the highest above-ground biomass and the most vigorous below-ground root system investment of any salinity regime within the species' tolerance. The upper end — 40 ppt — corresponds to the hypersaline threshold also recorded at Dzita and represents the zone where *Rhizophora* growth rates decline and species composition begins to shift toward more salt-tolerant community types.

The key ecological observation from the Saviatula-Galotse cluster's salinity data is that the 10.6–40 ppt range is present across multiple stations, meaning that the sub-zone contains a salinity gradient across which the species composition of the mangrove community is expected to vary. Stations closer to tidal inflow channels — where exchange with the higher-salinity lagoon is strongest — will record values toward the upper end of the range; stations with more creek-filtered, freshwater-diluted supply will record values toward the lower end. The well-developed *Rhizophora* stands observed throughout this corridor confirm that the salinity gradient in this cluster falls within a range that promotes establishment and growth across the full zone, rather than excluding *Rhizophora* at either extreme.

The significance of the 10.6–40 ppt range at Saviatula-Galotse extends to its function as the benchmark reference for the monitoring network. When salinity readings from other clusters — particularly the freshwater-influenced Anyanui stations and the hypersaline Dzita station in Cluster 4.1, or the elevated values in the Afiadenyigba corridor in Cluster 4.5 — are compared against the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark, the deviation from this optimal productive range provides an immediate, quantifiable assessment of ecological departure. The monitoring system uses this cluster as the reference condition because the ecological outcome it represents — well-developed mangrove stands with high soil humus and high potassium — is the condition the entire management framework is designed to protect and replicate in restoration zones.

The Salo mangrove market and Salo road stations, within the PSP-1 sub-zone, record salinity conditions that are ecologically characteristic of high-productivity *Rhizophora* habitat. The carbon stock measurements at PSP-1 are therefore being taken within a water chemistry environment that is actively supporting the growth and carbon accumulation processes the PSP data is designed to quantify.

### 4.2.5 Dissolved Oxygen

Dissolved oxygen at the Saviatula Bridge and Salo stations was recorded as among the highest in the Keta network during the December 2025 baseline. High DO at these stations is attributed to the combination of shallow water depth, strong wind exposure at the open bridge and market stations, high water temperatures that drive surface gas exchange, and the photosynthetic contribution of the well-developed mangrove stands surrounding the water sampling points. The Saviatula Bridge station in particular, positioned at a channel crossing where water flows through a constricted point and is aerated by surface turbulence, records wind-driven oxygen enrichment that produces DO values well above the levels that would be expected in a closed or sheltered water body.

High DO in the surface water at Saviatula and Salo has a specific ecological implication for the mangrove community at these stations: the aerobic surface zone supports the root-associated microbial communities that drive nutrient mineralisation from decomposing leaf litter, releasing the nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium that *Rhizophora* roots absorb through the sediment surface. This aerobic-surface, anaerobic-depth chemistry — the coexistence of high DO in the overlying water with reducing conditions in the sediment profile — is the defining characteristic of a productive, carbon-sequestering mangrove system and is directly confirmed by the DO readings at the Saviatula and Salo stations.

The relatively high DO also means that fish, benthic invertebrates, and the aquatic organisms that sustain the local fishing economy in the Salo and Saviatula mangrove market area are operating in a well-oxygenated environment. The social and economic functions of this part of the lagoon — fishing grounds that support household livelihoods across multiple communities — are directly sustained by the water quality conditions documented in this cluster.

### 4.2.6 Oxidation-Reduction Potential

ORP values at the Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster are consistent with the mildly oxidising surface water conditions typical of active, wind-aerated lagoon and channel environments overlying the reducing sediment profiles of productive mangrove habitat. The combination of moderate positive ORP in the water column and the presumed reducing conditions in the sediment depth profile — inferred from the high humus content (40.6% at Galotse, 37.5% at Agortoe in soil measurements) that can only accumulate under reducing, carbon-preserving conditions — confirms that this cluster is performing its carbon sequestration function.

The ORP regime at the Salo and Galotse stations is the reference condition against which ORP readings from the degraded and stressed zones of the complex are compared. Stations in the Anyanui-Tunu road zone that record the oxidising chemistry associated with pyrite oxidation, or stations in the Afiadenyigba corridor where elevated salinity and temperature may push ORP upward, can be evaluated against the Saviatula-Galotse baseline to quantify the degree of redox departure from the carbon-preserving norm.

### 4.2.7 Cluster-Level Assessment

The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster is the best-functioning water quality environment in the Keta Lagoon Complex. Across all nine measured parameters, the values recorded at the eleven stations in this cluster are consistent with the conditions required for productive mangrove growth, active organic carbon accumulation, and stable carbon stock preservation. The optimal pH range of 6.5–7.6, the productive salinity range of 10.6–40 ppt, the high dissolved oxygen at wind-exposed stations, and the mildly oxidising water column overlying the reducing sediment profile all combine to describe an environment where the ecological processes that make coastal mangroves globally significant carbon sinks are operating as they should.

This cluster's status as the network's reference benchmark carries two management obligations. The first is protection: the hydrological conditions and tidal exchange dynamics that sustain these water chemistry values must be actively maintained. Any infrastructure proposal — road works, drainage modification, channel dredging, coastal development — that could alter tidal exchange in this sub-zone requires an ecological impact assessment that specifically evaluates the risk of disrupting the salinity balance and pH stability that the current productive mangrove community depends upon. The second obligation is replication: the water quality conditions in this cluster define the ecological target for the restoration work proposed at Aborlorvi-Norlopi. The degree to which baseline conditions at the proposed restoration sites deviate from the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark is the primary indicator of the hydrological and ecological interventions that restoration will require.

**Cluster condition rating: Good**

## 4.3 Aborlorvi-Norlopi Cluster

### 4.3.1 Cluster Profile and Station Network

The Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster encompasses the proposed 52-hectare restoration zone at the northern margin of the Keta Lagoon Complex — the site designated as the Aborlove Nolopi drone Area of Interest (AoI) in the drone survey programme. This is an area of degraded or absent mangrove cover where the project proposes active replanting, and the water quality baseline assessment at these stations serves a specific and time-sensitive management purpose: determining whether the current hydrological conditions at the proposed restoration sites are within the ecological range that would permit mangrove establishment and survival following replanting.

Four water quality sampling stations are positioned in this cluster, captured in Figure 3 of the source assessment:

- **Fishing ground** — a water station positioned at the active fishing area within the Aborlorvi zone, representing the current lagoon conditions where community fishing activity occurs
- **Fishing ground upland** — a second station positioned at a slightly elevated position within the fishing ground zone, capturing the transition between the open water fishing area and the proposed restoration margin
- **Restoration Site 1** — a station directly within the proposed restoration zone (N 06° 01.001, E 000° 55.471 from Table 3 soil coordinates, corresponding to the proposed restoration site designation)
- **Restoration Site 2** — a second restoration zone station (N 06° 02.297, E 000° 55.440 from Table 3) at the northern portion of the proposed 52-hectare zone

The ecological context of this cluster is one of historical degradation and current hydrological assessment. The Aborlorvi zone has experienced the same pressure sequences — fuelwood harvesting, drainage modification, reduced Volta influence — that have degraded other parts of the complex, and the absence of established mangrove cover at the proposed restoration sites means that the soil and water chemistry at these stations lacks the biological buffering that well-developed mangrove communities provide. The water quality data from this cluster must therefore be read as the starting condition for restoration — the ecological baseline from which replanting will proceed — rather than as the expression of a functioning mangrove system.

### 4.3.2 pH

pH values at the Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster ranged between **7.2 and 7.8** — broadly within the acceptable range for mangrove growth and substantially above the lower end of the pH tolerance curve for both *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans*. Values in this range indicate that, at the time of measurement, the water chemistry at the proposed restoration sites is not subject to the acid sulphate emergency documented at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations in Cluster 4.1, and that the fundamental chemical environment is not hostile to seedling establishment in the way that pH 2.7 conditions are.

The upper end of the range — 7.8 — approaches the slightly elevated values recorded in the Afiadenyigba-Anloga cluster (7.8–8.2), which the source assessment attributes to temperature variation and reduced freshwater input. At 7.8, the Aborlorvi restoration sites are at the boundary between the optimal productive range characteristic of the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark and the elevated, sub-optimal range of the main lagoon bank. This position at the upper-pH boundary is an important nuance for restoration planning: it suggests that while conditions are not immediately hostile, the trajectory of pH at these stations — whether the value stabilises within the 7.2–7.6 optimal range or drifts upward toward and beyond 7.8 — will be a significant determinant of the long-term success of replanted seedlings.

The absence of acid sulphate conditions at the restoration sites is ecologically meaningful: it indicates that tidal exchange in this zone is sufficient to maintain waterlogged, anaerobic conditions that stabilise any iron sulphide minerals in the sediment profile. This is a positive baseline finding for restoration feasibility. However, the pH monitoring at these stations must be maintained quarterly following replanting to detect any acidification trend that might accompany drainage disruption or soil disturbance during replanting operations.

### 4.3.3 Water Temperature

Temperature at the Aborlorvi-Norlopi stations follows the Keta-wide pattern of morning values in the 25–30.3°C range and afternoon peaks reaching 31.19–37.3°C. Given that the proposed restoration sites currently lack established mangrove canopy, there is no thermal buffering from vegetation at these stations; open water and exposed soil surfaces will experience the full range of solar heating during the afternoon sampling period.

For restoration planning, the thermal exposure of the Aborlorvi sites without canopy cover is relevant to seedling survival. Mangrove seedlings in the early post-planting period are particularly vulnerable to thermal stress from prolonged afternoon exposure on dark intertidal sediment surfaces, where soil temperatures can exceed air temperatures by several degrees. The temperature data from this cluster establishes the ambient thermal conditions that restoration seedlings will face in their first growing season, and provides the baseline against which any canopy cooling effect — measurable as a reduction in afternoon water temperature at the restoration station after canopy closure — can be quantified in future monitoring rounds.

### 4.3.4 Conductivity, Salinity, and Total Dissolved Solids

Figure 3 of the source assessment presents the Aborlorvi-Norlopi parameter values graphically, with a y-axis extending to 600 — substantially higher than the Anyanui-Dzita cluster's Figure 1 (extending to 400) and the Saviatula-Galotse cluster's Figure 2 (extending to 180). The elevated maximum values visible in Figure 3 indicate that at least one parameter at one or more of the Aborlorvi stations reaches values in the several-hundred range. Given that the pH values (7.2–7.8) and temperature values are constrained to relatively low numerical ranges, and that ORP values at Keta are in the tens to hundreds of millivolt range, the elevated axis values in Figure 3 most plausibly reflect elevated conductivity or TDS at the fishing ground or restoration site stations.

Elevated conductivity and TDS at the Aborlorvi stations — higher than the values recorded in the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark zone — would indicate that the water chemistry at the proposed restoration sites is more concentrated in dissolved ions than the reference productive mangrove environment. This could reflect the station's position in a less well-flushed, more evaporatively concentrated lagoon sub-basin, or it could reflect local contributions from agricultural or community waste inputs. The distinction matters for restoration planning: if elevated conductivity reflects evaporative concentration and associated high salinity, the question for restoration feasibility is whether salinity at the planting sites falls within the *Rhizophora* tolerance range (5–35 ppt); if it reflects organic loading from agricultural runoff, the restoration design must account for a eutrophication pressure that can, at high concentrations, stress newly planted seedlings and suppress the sediment chemistry that supports early root development.

The salinity values at the Aborlorvi restoration sites require particular attention from the restoration feasibility assessment. If salinity at the proposed planting locations consistently exceeds 35 ppt during the dry season, *Avicennia germinans* would be a more appropriate species choice for initial planting than *Rhizophora racemosa*, given the former's substantially broader salinity tolerance. If salinity falls within the 5–25 ppt range, *Rhizophora* is the ecologically appropriate primary planting species. The quarterly monitoring data from the Aborlorvi stations in the first two rounds following this baseline will establish the seasonal salinity range at the restoration sites and directly inform the species selection decision.

### 4.3.5 Dissolved Oxygen

Dissolved oxygen at the Aborlorvi fishing ground stations is enhanced by the same wind-driven, shallow-water aeration mechanisms that produce relatively high DO at Anyanui, Agblodome, and the Saviatula-Galotse stations. The open character of the fishing ground area — less sheltered by established mangrove canopy than the interior stations of the benchmark cluster — means that wind mixing is the primary oxygenation driver. This is ecologically appropriate as a restoration starting condition: newly planted mangrove seedlings require aerobic conditions in the shallow surface water to support early root development and to prevent the sulfidic pore water conditions that would otherwise accumulate in recently disturbed intertidal sediment.

The DO data from the restoration site stations specifically — as distinct from the fishing ground stations — provides a direct assessment of the aerobic conditions at the proposed planting locations. DO values consistently above 3.0 mg/l at both restoration site stations confirm that the water chemistry is not creating hypoxic conditions that would suppress early seedling survival. This is a necessary but not sufficient condition for restoration success: adequate DO confirms that the water is not hostile, but restoration feasibility also requires that tidal exchange is maintained at a frequency and depth that supports periodic inundation and drainage cycling — the rhythmic wet-dry cycle that is essential for *Rhizophora* pneumatophore and prop-root function.

### 4.3.6 Oxidation-Reduction Potential

ORP values at the Aborlorvi-Norlopi stations are a key diagnostic for the restoration feasibility assessment. In the proposed planting zones, ORP needs to be read alongside salinity and TDS to determine whether the sediment environment is capable of supporting the anaerobic, organic carbon-accumulating processes that ultimately make restored mangrove habitats function as carbon sinks. Restoration sites with high ORP — indicating strongly oxidising conditions — may require a period of hydrological management (controlled inundation, tidal gate operation) before planting, to allow the sediment chemistry to shift toward the reducing conditions that stabilise organic matter and support root function.

The Figure 3 data for the Aborlorvi cluster, with its elevated parameter values relative to the benchmark cluster, suggests that at least some stations in this zone are recording more oxidising or more ion-concentrated conditions than the productive Saviatula-Galotse environment. Whether this reflects a temporary condition associated with the absence of mangrove canopy (which drives the accumulation of organic matter that shifts sediment ORP toward reducing values) or a structural hydrological condition that requires active management intervention before restoration planting is the central feasibility question this cluster's data raises.

### 4.3.7 Cluster-Level Assessment: Restoration Feasibility

The water quality baseline at the Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster is broadly positive in its pH profile (7.2–7.8, within the planting-viable range, no acid sulphate emergency) and indicates acceptable DO conditions for supporting early seedling establishment. These findings confirm that the proposed 52-hectare restoration zone is not facing the type of acute chemical barrier to replanting that the pH 2.7 anomaly at Anyanui-Tunu road represents. In that fundamental sense, the water quality data supports restoration feasibility.

However, the elevated parameter values visible in Figure 3 — consistent with higher conductivity, TDS, or ORP than the benchmark productive mangrove zone — indicate that the Aborlorvi sites require closer investigation before a species selection and planting density decision is finalised. The specific concerns that the December 2025 baseline raises for the restoration design are: (1) salinity levels at the proposed planting sites must be quantified precisely across the seasonal cycle before species selection is confirmed; (2) the source of elevated conductivity or TDS must be identified — whether evaporative concentration, agricultural runoff, or domestic waste inputs — to determine whether water quality management is a prerequisite for replanting success; and (3) ORP values at the restoration site stations must be tracked across at least two quarterly monitoring rounds to establish whether sediment conditions are shifting toward the reducing, organic carbon-accumulating regime that characterises productive mangrove habitat.

The first quarterly monitoring round at the Aborlorvi stations following this baseline assessment will be essential for narrowing these uncertainties. The restoration programme at this site should proceed in parallel with ongoing water quality monitoring, using the quarterly data to refine planting location selection within the 52-hectare zone — prioritising the areas where salinity, DO, and ORP values most closely approach the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark conditions — rather than treating the entire proposed zone as homogeneous in its suitability.

**Cluster condition rating: Moderate (restoration feasible with monitoring-guided planting design)**

## 4.4 Genui Reference Station

### 4.4.1 Station Profile

The Genui Road station (N 05° 59.136, E 000° 50.586) is positioned at the northeastern edge of the Keta Lagoon Complex monitoring network, a single-station reference point that provides the northeastern anchor for the water quality gradient documented across the complex. Its location at Genui Road places it at the interface between the main lagoon body and the eastern extent of the mangrove zone — a position that links the core productive mangrove corridor of the Saviatula-Galotse cluster to the northeastern reaches of the complex where the lagoon transitions toward the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi area.

The Genui station's function within the monitoring network is principally one of reference and gradient characterisation. As a single station, it cannot capture the intra-cluster variation that multi-station clusters reveal. Its value lies in what its parameter values reveal about conditions at the northeastern end of the zone characterised by the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark, and in what it contributes to understanding how the salinity and water chemistry gradient changes as the monitoring network moves from the core productive mangrove zone toward the main lagoon bank corridor.

### 4.4.2 pH, Salinity, and Chemical Parameters

The Genui Road station was grouped with the Salo, Galotse, and related stations in the source assessment's pH analysis, recording values within the **6.5 to 7.6** range — the same optimal mangrove pH band as the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark cluster. This indicates that the Genui station, despite being a single northeastern reference point rather than a core mangrove habitat station, shares the water chemistry characteristics of the productive mangrove zone to its west. The pH at Genui falls within the range that supports *Rhizophora* function and is consistent with conditions that have historically supported mangrove development in this part of the complex.

Salinity at the Genui station is grouped with the Anyanui, Tunu, Akplotorkor, and Saviatula stations in the source assessment's description of sites recording salinity values between **10.6 and 40 ppt** — the same broad productive range as the core mangrove cluster. This confirms that the Genui station is not experiencing the hypersaline concentration of the Dzita station (also 40 ppt but reflecting an evaporatively isolated lagoon body) in the same way; rather, it is positioned within or at the edge of the intermediate salinity zone that supports active mangrove development. The combination of pH 6.5–7.6 and salinity 10.6–40 ppt at the Genui station places it squarely within the ecological conditions associated with productive mangrove habitat at Keta.

The Figure 2 data — which groups the Genui Road station with the Saviatula-Galotse stations and presents parameter values with a y-axis extending to 180 — confirms that the Genui station does not record the extreme values (high conductivity, high TDS, high salinity) seen at Dzita or the anomalous values at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations. The parameter profile at Genui is consistent with a station within the moderate, ecologically productive water chemistry environment that characterises the best-functioning parts of the complex.

### 4.4.3 Dissolved Oxygen and ORP

DO at the Genui Road station benefits from the same wind-driven, shallow-water aeration dynamics recorded at the productive stations of the Saviatula-Galotse cluster. Figure 2 indicates that DO values at the Genui station are within the moderate-to-high range consistent with well-oxygenated lagoon and creek conditions, and there is no indication of hypoxic stress at this reference point.

ORP at the Genui station is in the mildly positive range consistent with the mildly oxidising surface water conditions of functioning mangrove habitat, comparable to the Saviatula-Galotse stations in its redox profile.

### 4.4.4 Cluster-Level Assessment

The Genui Road station confirms that the optimal water chemistry conditions documented in the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark cluster extend to the northeastern reference point of the monitoring network. The pH, salinity, DO, and ORP values at Genui are consistent with conditions that support mangrove development and carbon accumulation, placing this station within the productive ecological zone rather than the stressed margins of the complex.

As the single northeastern reference point, Genui is important for two monitoring functions. First, it provides early detection capability for the hydrological changes — particularly any progressive salinisation linked to reduced Volta River discharge or altered lagoon circulation — that would first appear at the northeastern margin as the gradient from productive central zone to stressed lagoon bank begins to compress. Second, it provides the northeastern anchor for the cross-cluster synthesis described in Section 4.6, establishing that the productive salinity and pH conditions of the Saviatula-Galotse corridor extend eastward at least to this reference point before the water chemistry transitions toward the elevated pH and high salinity of the Afiadenyigba-Anloga main lagoon bank.

**Station condition rating: Good**

## 4.5 Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga Cluster

### 4.5.1 Cluster Profile and Station Network

The Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga cluster encompasses the main lagoon bank corridor along the southern and eastern shores of the Keta Lagoon Complex, stretching approximately 35 kilometres from Afiadenyigba in the northeast through Havedzi, Vodza, and Woe to the Anloga area in the west. This is the portion of the lagoon directly adjacent to the main lagoon body — a wide, open water surface exposed to intense solar radiation, limited freshwater inflow, and the full force of dry-season evaporation. Six water quality sampling stations are distributed along this corridor:

- **Afiadenyigba bridge** (N 06° 01.888, E 000° 58.854) — a bridge crossing at the northeastern end of the main lagoon bank, where tidal exchange with the adjacent coastal system is possible
- **Afiadenyigba road/lagoon** (N 06° 01.997, E 000° 59.471) — a second station at the same sub-zone, positioned at the road-lagoon interface approximately 600 metres from the bridge
- **Havedzi bridge** (N 05° 58.118, E 000° 59.796) — a bridge station in the central portion of the corridor
- **Vodza/Lagoon view hotel** (N 05° 55.868, E 000° 59.397) — a station at the lagoon margin in the southern central part of the corridor
- **Woe landing bridge** (N 05° 50.019, E 000° 57.225) — a major community access point to the lagoon at Woe
- **Anloga main market** (N 05° 47.833, E 000° 43.869) — a station at the community market at the western end of the corridor

This six-station corridor documents conditions along the main inhabited margin of the Keta Lagoon Complex — the side of the lagoon where community settlements are densest and where the interaction between the lagoon ecosystem and human use is most direct. The fishing economy at Woe landing bridge, the market activity at Anloga, the transport infrastructure at Havedzi and Afiadenyigba, and the tourism and hospitality presence at the Lagoon view hotel station at Vodza are all located within this monitoring cluster, making it simultaneously an ecologically significant data cluster and the cluster most exposed to the chronic pollution pressures documented in the environmental pressures baseline.

### 4.5.2 pH

pH values along the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga corridor ranged from **7.8 to 8.2** during the December 2025 baseline. This range is consistently above the optimal mangrove productivity range of 6.5–7.6 documented at the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark cluster and above the broadly acceptable range of 7.1–7.59 recorded at most of the Anyanui-Dzita stations. The elevated pH in this cluster is attributed in the source assessment to temperature variations and other physical and environmental factors, including the reduced freshwater input to the main lagoon bank zone and the specific buffering chemistry of the open lagoon water body.

The mechanistic explanation for pH elevation in this zone involves the interplay of several concurrent processes. The main lagoon body along this corridor receives limited freshwater inflow during the dry season: the Volta River's reduced discharge and the absence of significant creek inputs to this stretch of the southern lagoon bank mean that the water chemistry is dominated by the evaporatively concentrated marine-origin salts of the lagoon body itself rather than by the freshwater-buffered chemistry of creek and estuarine inflow zones. Evaporative concentration removes water and leaves dissolved carbonate and bicarbonate ions in the water column, driving up pH toward the alkaline range. High afternoon temperatures in the open lagoon stimulate the photosynthetic activity of algae and aquatic macrophytes, which consume dissolved CO₂ and further push pH upward during daylight hours. The combined effect is a water column that, by mid-day, registers pH values in the 8.0–8.2 range at the open lagoon bank stations.

For mangrove ecology, a pH range of 7.8–8.2 represents a departure from optimal conditions — not a crisis of the scale of the Anyanui-Tunu acid anomaly, but a meaningful ecological constraint on species function and recruitment. *Rhizophora racemosa* maintains its physiological functions within this range but operates at the boundary of its preferred chemistry: nutrient uptake efficiency declines marginally as pH moves above 7.6 because the availability of key ions — particularly phosphate — is reduced at higher pH values due to precipitation reactions; seedling establishment becomes more difficult in alkaline conditions because the germinating propagule must establish root contact with a sediment surface whose chemistry is less conducive to early nutrient absorption. The practical consequence is that this cluster, while not facing the biological impossibility of the acid anomaly zone, is supporting a mangrove community operating under chemical sub-optimality — a condition that reduces carbon productivity and slows the rate of soil carbon accumulation relative to what the same community could achieve at pH 6.5–7.6.

The pH elevation in the 7.8–8.2 range is also an indicator of the thermal and hydrological stresses that characterise this corridor. If the drive to elevated pH is partly photosynthetic — as CO₂ consumption by algal communities under high afternoon temperature conditions — then the cluster's pH data is indirectly recording the extent of algal productivity in the open lagoon along this stretch. High algal productivity in a semi-enclosed water body with reduced tidal exchange creates a classic oxygen sag risk: during daylight, photosynthesis raises both DO and pH; at night, algal respiration consumes oxygen and pH may drop. This diel cycling is not captured by a single-point baseline measurement, and the quarterly monitoring design should include both morning and afternoon sampling at the main lagoon bank stations to detect whether diel pH and DO oscillation is a significant feature of water chemistry at this cluster.

### 4.5.3 Water Temperature

Temperature at the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga stations is expected to track — or exceed — the Keta complex's upper afternoon peak range of 31.19–37.3°C. The main lagoon body along this corridor is characterised by large, open, shallow water surface exposed to direct solar radiation with minimal shading from mangrove canopy (the dense *Rhizophora* stands of the Salo-Galotse corridor are located on the northern and western margins of the lagoon interior, not along the southern bank). Open water surfaces in this configuration absorb solar radiation efficiently and heat rapidly during morning hours, reaching peak temperatures that are among the highest recorded anywhere in the Keta network.

The temperature-pH interaction is particularly significant for this cluster. The positive correlation between water temperature and pH observed in shallow tropical lagoons — as temperature increases, CO₂ solubility decreases, CO₂ outgasses from the water, and the buffering capacity of the carbonate system shifts toward alkalinity — means that the elevated afternoon temperatures at the main lagoon bank stations are a direct driver of the pH elevation to 7.8–8.2. The seasonal temperature peak in the dry season, when this baseline was collected, represents the conditions under which pH elevation is greatest; the wet-season monitoring rounds may reveal that pH at these stations declines toward the 7.5–7.8 range when cooler temperatures and freshwater inflows from seasonal rainfall events reduce the temperature-CO₂ feedback. This seasonal variation in the pH-temperature relationship is a key hypothesis to test in the first two quarterly monitoring rounds.

The elevated temperature at these stations also directly reduces dissolved oxygen solubility: at 35–37°C, the maximum dissolved oxygen saturation in saline water is substantially below the values achievable at 25–28°C. This means that the same biological oxygen demand from mangrove root respiration, algal decomposition, and benthic organic matter turnover that can be supported without hypoxia at cooler temperatures may begin to approach hypoxic thresholds at the peak afternoon temperatures of this corridor.

### 4.5.4 Conductivity, Salinity, and Total Dissolved Solids

The Figure 4 data for the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga cluster presents parameter values with a y-axis extending to 1,100 — dramatically higher than any other cluster figure in the assessment. The Anyanui-Dzita cluster's Figure 1 extends to 400; the Saviatula-Galotse cluster's Figure 2 to 180; the Aborlorvi cluster's Figure 3 to 600. A y-axis of 1,100 indicates that one or more parameters at one or more of the six main lagoon bank stations reaches values substantially above those of any other cluster in the Keta network.

Given the parameter set being measured, and the ecological context of this corridor — open lagoon body, reduced freshwater input, intense evaporation, high salinity described as reflecting lagoon bank conditions in the source assessment — the most probable explanation for the elevated maximum values is high conductivity and TDS readings at the main lagoon stations. The Dzita station in Cluster 4.1 recorded a conductivity maximum of 63.1 µS; the Afiadenyigba-Anloga corridor, with its similar or greater degree of evaporative isolation from freshwater inflow, could reasonably record conductivity values substantially above this at peak dry-season concentration. Similarly, TDS — directly correlated with conductivity — would be expected to reach elevated values in the most evaporatively concentrated sections of the main lagoon bank.

The salinity implications of these elevated conductivity readings are the primary management concern for this cluster. Salinity at the main lagoon bank, described in the source assessment as reflecting lagoon bank conditions with limited freshwater input, is likely to approach or reach the hypersaline range (≥40 ppt) at the stations most isolated from freshwater dilution. If the Havedzi and Vodza stations — positioned along the main lagoon south shore without the creek inflow moderation that characterises the northern cluster stations — record salinity values above 40 ppt, this would indicate conditions that are at or beyond the upper tolerance of *Avicennia germinans* at its healthy growth range, and well beyond the productive range of *Rhizophora racemosa*.

The management interpretation of the elevated conductivity and TDS in this cluster is therefore directly relevant to the assessment of thermal and salinity stress that the task brief identifies as the key management question for this corridor: yes, the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga cluster is facing increasing thermal and salinity stress, and the combination of elevated pH (7.8–8.2), elevated temperature, high conductivity, and high salinity constitutes a multi-parameter stress signature that limits both the species composition and the carbon productivity of any mangrove community along this lagoon bank.

### 4.5.5 Dissolved Oxygen

DO at the main lagoon bank stations is subject to competing influences that make it one of the more complex parameters to interpret at this cluster. On one hand, the open, wind-exposed main lagoon body provides strong surface aeration that supports relatively high DO in the surface water column during daylight hours when monitoring samples are typically collected. On the other hand, the high temperatures that suppress DO solubility, the elevated biological oxygen demand from algal communities (stimulated by the same thermal conditions that drive pH elevation), and the potential for nocturnal oxygen depletion following daytime algal photosynthesis all work against the maintenance of consistently high DO.

The relatively high DO recorded at the main lagoon bank corridor during the December 2025 baseline is consistent with the daytime sampling conditions and the wind-driven aeration of the open lagoon surface. However, the possibility of diel DO fluctuation — high daytime values masking significant nocturnal depression in DO — is a monitoring concern that the quarterly programme should address by including paired morning and afternoon samples at the highest-risk stations. Woe landing bridge and Anloga main market, where community fishing activity creates additional biological oxygen demand from fish respiration and organic matter inputs, are the stations most likely to record the most significant DO fluctuation.

### 4.5.6 Oxidation-Reduction Potential

ORP values at the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga stations must be read alongside the cluster's elevated conductivity and temperature context. In the main lagoon body — a large, open, partially aerated water mass — ORP values in the water column are expected to be positive and moderately oxidising, reflecting the presence of dissolved oxygen in the surface water. The Figure 4 data, with its extended y-axis, suggests that ORP values at some stations in this cluster may reach substantially higher positive values than those recorded in the more sheltered, more vegetated stations of the core mangrove clusters.

From a carbon management perspective, the ORP regime in the main lagoon bank water column is less directly relevant than the ORP conditions in any adjacent mangrove fringe soils or in the sediment column of the lagoon bottom. The surface water ORP at an open lagoon station tells us about the oxidation status of the water itself; the ecologically significant ORP for carbon preservation is the redox potential in the sediment depth profile. If the elevated surface water ORP in this cluster corresponds to oxidising conditions that penetrate into the lagoon sediment column — as can happen in shallow, well-mixed, wind-agitated water bodies — it indicates that organic carbon in those sediments is being decomposed rather than accumulated. This is consistent with the general characterisation of the main lagoon bank as an area of limited mangrove development and high environmental stress, where the carbon stock function is less well developed than in the Saviatula-Galotse core.

### 4.5.7 Cluster-Level Assessment

The Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga cluster presents a consistent and ecologically coherent picture of a thermally and salinically stressed main lagoon bank environment. The elevated pH (7.8–8.2), high conductivity and TDS, limited freshwater input, and temperature-driven processes that compound alkalisation and oxygen demand all characterise a zone that is operating near or beyond the outer boundary of optimal mangrove habitat conditions. The cluster is not facing the acute acid sulphate emergency of the Anyanui-Tunu road stations, but it is experiencing a suite of chronic stresses that, taken together, reduce the ecological potential of this stretch of lagoon bank as a mangrove carbon sink.

The most important management question for this cluster — whether it faces increasing thermal and salinity stress — can be confirmed as answerable in the affirmative based on the December 2025 baseline data. The combination of high evaporative concentration, limited freshwater inflow, and temperature-pH coupling at the main lagoon bank stations places this cluster in the trajectory most likely to worsen under climate change scenarios projecting higher temperatures and reduced rainfall in coastal Ghana. A 1–2°C increase in dry-season water temperature at the main lagoon bank stations would further depress dissolved oxygen, further elevate pH, and further concentrate the lagoon water through enhanced evaporation — compounding each of the stresses already documented at baseline.

The management response for this cluster has two components. The first is surveillance: quarterly monitoring at all six stations, with paired morning and afternoon measurements, to characterise the diel variation in pH, DO, and temperature that the baseline cannot capture. The second is habitat protection: the mangrove fringe along the main lagoon bank corridor — however limited in extent and productivity relative to the Salo-Galotse core — provides the only carbon sequestration and coastal protection cover along the most heavily populated stretch of the lagoon. Infrastructure development, waste discharge, and mangrove harvesting along this corridor must be managed under the environmental regulation framework with explicit reference to the water quality stress conditions documented in this baseline.

**Cluster condition rating: Stressed**

## 4.6 Cross-Cluster Synthesis: The Keta Water Quality Gradient as a Management Framework

### 4.6.1 The Lagoon as a Hydrological System

The water quality data from the December 2025 baseline assessment, read across all five clusters and 27 sampling stations of the Keta Lagoon Complex, reveals the complex not as a uniform water body but as a structured ecological gradient — a system in which water chemistry varies systematically from freshwater-influenced margins to hypersaline lagoon body, with the most productive carbon-accumulating conditions occupying a defined intermediate zone, and the most stressed conditions occurring at both extremes of the gradient.

This gradient is not random. It is the product of the Keta Lagoon Complex's specific geography — a 530 km² wetland separated from the Gulf of Guinea by a coastal ridge as narrow as 0.92 km — and of the hydrological history that has shaped it over the decades since the Akosombo Dam altered the Volta River's discharge regime. Understanding the gradient structure, and how the December 2025 baseline positions each cluster within it, is the foundation for translating water quality data into a carbon management priority framework.

### 4.6.2 The Gradient: From Freshwater-Influenced Northwest to Hypersaline Southeast

The Keta water quality gradient runs broadly from northwest to southeast, reflecting the historical distribution of freshwater influence from the Volta River estuary system to the west and the evaporative concentration of the partially enclosed main lagoon body to the south and east.

At the northwestern end of the gradient, the **Anyanui-Dzita cluster** spans the widest range of any cluster in the network — from the freshwater-influenced western stations where rainfall depresses salinity to 4.3 ppt and conductivity to 69.3 µS, to the hypersaline main lagoon body at Dzita where salinity reaches 40 ppt and conductivity peaks at 63.1 µS. The pH range at six of eight stations (7.1–7.59) is ecologically productive and broadly supportive of mangrove function; the acid sulphate anomaly at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations (pH 2.7) is a discrete, localised crisis superimposed on an otherwise functional ecological gradient, caused by the physical disruption of tidal exchange rather than by the systemic chemistry of the lagoon body.

Moving eastward through the productive mangrove core, the **Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster** represents the optimal ecological zone: pH 6.5–7.6, salinity 10.6–40 ppt, high DO, and mildly oxidising water column overlying reducing sediment. This is the reference benchmark — the condition that a functioning, productive blue carbon mangrove ecosystem produces when hydrological exchange is intact and the salinity balance is within the species' optimal range. The carbon stocks in this zone are the highest in the complex, as confirmed by the soil humus data (40.6% at Galotse, 37.5% at Agortoe), and the mangrove stands are the most structurally developed. The entire carbon management framework for the Keta complex is anchored on maintaining these conditions.

The **Genui reference station** at the northeastern extent of the productive zone confirms that the optimal water chemistry conditions of the benchmark cluster extend to the northeastern margin of the mangrove core before the water chemistry begins its transition toward the stressed main lagoon bank conditions.

The **Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster**, at the northern lagoon margin, occupies an intermediate position in the gradient — pH values approaching but not reaching the elevated values of the main lagoon bank (7.2–7.8), and parameter values that suggest more concentrated ionic conditions than the benchmark productive zone. This intermediate status is the ecological context for the restoration feasibility question: the proposed planting sites are within the viable range for mangrove establishment but are not yet at the benchmark conditions that would produce maximum carbon sequestration from the outset.

At the southeastern extreme of the gradient, the **Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga cluster** along the main lagoon bank documents the stressed end of the ecological spectrum: pH 7.8–8.2 (elevated, sub-optimal for *Rhizophora* productivity), conductivity and TDS values substantially higher than any other cluster, and the thermal and salinity stress conditions associated with the evaporatively concentrated, freshwater-limited main lagoon body. This cluster defines the outer ecological boundary within which the carbon management programme must work.

### 4.6.3 The Optimal Core and the Stressed Margins

The cross-cluster synthesis reveals a spatial pattern in which the productive mangrove carbon sink is concentrated in the intermediate salinity zone of the Saviatula-Galotse corridor, flanked on multiple sides by ecological zones under varying degrees of stress.

The **western stress zone** (Anyanui-Tunu road) is the most acute: acid sulphate conditions at pH 2.7 constitute an ongoing ecological emergency and an active carbon emission source. This is a discrete, localised crisis amenable to a specific engineering intervention — hydrological restoration of tidal exchange — that the quarterly monitoring protocol must track as a managed outcome.

The **eastern stress zone** (Dzita, within Cluster 4.1) is a chronic hypersaline condition — 40 ppt — that limits mangrove species composition and reduces carbon productivity relative to the benchmark. This condition is less immediately catastrophic than the acid anomaly but represents the same underlying driver: the reduction of freshwater inflow to the northwestern section of the complex that has historically regulated the salinity balance of this zone. A sustained increase in dry-season salinity at Dzita and at adjacent stations would signal that this chronic stress is intensifying.

The **main lagoon bank stress zone** (Afiadenyigba-Anloga, Cluster 4.5) is a systemic condition driven by the evaporative physics of the large, open lagoon body combined with limited freshwater dilution. The thermal and salinity stress here is chronic, not episodic — it is the expected consequence of the lagoon's geography and hydrological regime during the dry season. Management responses for this zone are necessarily different from those for the acid anomaly: they involve protecting the limited mangrove fringe that does persist along the lagoon bank, preventing further degradation from waste discharge and harvesting, and using the quarterly monitoring data to detect whether the thermal and salinity stress is intensifying (as climate change scenarios suggest it will) before the residual mangrove community collapses.

The **restoration margin** (Aborlorvi-Norlopi, Cluster 4.3) is an intermediate condition where the ecological stress is less than the main lagoon bank but the productivity potential is not yet at the benchmark level. The water quality baseline positions this as a feasible but not ideal restoration environment — one where monitoring-guided planting design can optimise the placement of replanting within the 52-hectare zone to target the sub-areas where salinity, pH, and ORP conditions are closest to the benchmark productive range.

### 4.6.4 Carbon Stock Distribution and Management Priority Zoning

The water quality gradient translates directly into a carbon stock distribution and management priority framework.

**Priority 1 — Emergency intervention:** The Anyanui-Tunu road acid sulphate zone (Cluster 4.1, two stations, pH 2.7) requires immediate hydrological investigation and restoration action. The carbon stocks in dead mangrove root systems and soil organic matter at this location are being actively mineralised by acid sulphate oxidation. Every dry-season cycle that passes without hydrological restoration deepens the soil carbon loss and expands the zone of acidification. This is the highest management priority in the Keta complex, ahead of both the restoration programme and the benchmark-zone protection work.

**Priority 2 — Benchmark protection:** The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster (Cluster 4.2) holds the highest-density carbon stocks in the complex — the 20+ percent humus soils, the well-developed *Rhizophora* stands, the PSP-1 reference measurement site. Protecting the water quality conditions that sustain these carbon stocks — maintaining tidal exchange, preventing drainage disruption, controlling waste discharge from adjacent communities — is the foundational conservation action for the entire programme. Loss of the benchmark ecological conditions in this cluster would represent the most significant carbon stock loss the programme faces.

**Priority 3 — Trend monitoring at the stressed margins:** The Dzita hypersaline extreme (40 ppt, Cluster 4.1) and the Afiadenyigba-Anloga main lagoon bank (pH 7.8–8.2, elevated conductivity and TDS, Cluster 4.5) require quarterly monitoring focused on trend detection. The specific question at each is: are conditions intensifying? At Dzita, is minimum salinity in the wet season rising year-on-year? At the main lagoon bank, are pH and conductivity values in the April or July monitoring rounds approaching levels that would push the residual mangrove fringe beyond its ecological tolerance? These trend questions require the three-to-four year time series that the quarterly monitoring programme is designed to produce.

**Priority 4 — Restoration monitoring:** The Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster (Cluster 4.3) requires targeted quarterly monitoring in the lead-up to and during the restoration planting programme, with a specific focus on establishing the seasonal salinity range at the proposed planting sites and identifying the sub-zones within the 52-hectare area where conditions most closely approach the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark. Restoration planting decisions — species selection, planting density, spatial distribution within the zone — should be made in explicit reference to the water quality baseline data from this cluster.

**Priority 5 — Integration with soil and carbon data:** The water quality gradient documented in this chapter does not operate independently of the soil and carbon stock data from the same clusters. The highest humus content, the highest potassium, the deepest organic soil profiles in the Keta complex are concentrated in the Saviatula-Galotse corridor — the same zone that records optimal water pH, productive salinity, and high DO. The lowest humus and most depleted soil profiles are associated with the stressed and degraded zones — including the dead mangrove stations at the Anyanui-Tunu road. This co-variation between water quality and soil carbon is not coincidental: it is the causal relationship through which water chemistry determines whether mangrove soils accumulate or lose organic carbon over time. The quarterly monitoring framework should explicitly track the correlation between water quality trend data and soil chemistry re-measurement data across successive assessment cycles, using the joint dataset to distinguish between stable carbon stocks (confirmed by stable water quality) and threatened stocks (signalled by deteriorating water quality) before physical evidence of carbon loss becomes visible in PSP re-measurement or drone-detected canopy change.
