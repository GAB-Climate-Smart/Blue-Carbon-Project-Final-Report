# Chapter 7: Ada-Songor Soil Quality Results

## 7.1 Ada-Songor Soil Context

The soil chemistry of the Ada-Songor system does not present a single, uniform story of degradation. It presents three stories operating simultaneously in different parts of the same landscape — stories that are spatially concentrated, analytically distinct, and with different management implications. Understanding which story applies to which location is the central interpretive challenge of this chapter.

The first story is one of extreme degradation. At the Pute lagoon stations in the south of the Songor system, soil conditions are the most compromised in the entire monitoring network. Near-acid-sulphate pH, severely depleted organic matter, nutrient values at or close to zero, and high surface temperatures from the absence of canopy shade combine to describe a soil environment that is not merely stressed — it is losing its capacity to function as a carbon-storing wetland substrate. This is not background noise; it is the signal of a system in active decline.

The second story is one of extreme anthropogenic nutrient enrichment. At the Wasakuse-ngwa station and at the Obane creek station, soil nutrient values — particularly nitrate at Wasakuse and phosphate and potassium at Obane — are elevated far beyond the natural range for mangrove soils. The driver at both locations is cattle grazing on wetland margins. Where the Pute story is about nutrient absence, the Wasakuse-Obane story is about nutrient excess. Both conditions are damaging, but they are damaging in different ways and require different management responses.

The third story, embedded within the Obane data, is the most important for management purposes: it is a story of recovery in progress. The Obane restored mangrove site records measurably higher humus, lower surface temperature, and more stable pH than the Pute lagoon just three kilometres to the south. These are not subtle differences; they are analytically significant contrasts that demonstrate, in direct empirical terms, what active mangrove restoration can achieve in the Ada soil environment. The recovered canopy at Obane is already changing the soil conditions beneath it.

The distinction between the Keta and Ada soil pictures is important contextual framing. At the Keta Lagoon Complex, the primary soil pressures are driven by hydrology: hydrological blockage at the Anyanui end creates waterlogged, near-stagnant soil conditions, while hypersalinity at the Dzita-Salo corridor limits biological productivity despite relatively high organic matter. At Ada-Songor, the hydrological context matters too — the reduced Volta freshwater discharge and salt pan operations documented in Chapter 6 bear on soil conditions at the Pute end of the system — but the dominant soil stories here are not hydrological. They are about degradation and exposure at Pute, about livestock-mediated nutrient loading at Obane-Wasakuse, and about the measurable benefit of restoration. These are not ecosystem-wide problems. They are spatially concentrated hotspots that require targeted, site-specific management responses.

The analysis that follows works through each of the four Ada soil sampling stations in sequence, before drawing the Pute-Obane comparison that is the analytical core of this chapter's management argument.

## 7.2 Patukope-Pute Cluster — The Degradation Baseline

**Soil stations:** Pute walkway and lagoon intake, black mangroves (N 05°47.082, E 000°32.585); Pute lagoon, white mangroves (N 05°47.070, E 000°33.027).

### Pute as the Network's Lowest Point

The Patukope-Pute cluster records the most degraded soil conditions in the entire Ghana Blue Carbon monitoring network. The two Pute stations — positioned approximately 400 metres apart within the same degraded mangrove fringe — document a soil environment that scores at or near the bottom of every parameter the monitoring protocol measures, from pH through organic matter to individual nutrients. No other combination of conditions in the network presents as complete a picture of soil system failure.

### Soil pH — Critical Threshold Proximity

The most urgent finding at Pute is pH. A mean value of 4.78 recorded at the Pute lagoon (white mangrove) station represents the lowest soil pH in the Ada monitoring network and places this station within proximity of confirmed acid sulphate soil conditions, the threshold for which is pH 4.5. The overall sampling-site mean for Ada ranges from 5.57 to 6.84; at 4.78, Pute lagoon is both an outlier and a warning.

The mechanism that produces pH values in this range in coastal wetlands is well understood. Mangrove sediments accumulate iron sulphides — principally pyrite (FeS₂) — under the anaerobic, waterlogged conditions that are natural to intertidal soils. As long as those soils remain waterlogged and oxygen-excluded, the pyrite is geochemically stable and ecologically inert. The problem begins when the soils are exposed to oxygen — through drainage, physical disturbance, tidal exclusion following hydrological disruption, or the progressive lowering of the water table during prolonged dry periods. When pyrite oxidises in the presence of oxygen and water, it generates sulfuric acid. This process is not self-limiting; it is self-reinforcing. The acidification produced by pyrite oxidation accelerates the decomposition of organic matter, releasing further acidic byproducts. Lowered pH further inhibits the biological communities that would otherwise buffer the system. Each incremental decline in pH makes recovery more difficult.

The water quality data reported in Chapter 6 provides the hydrological context within which this pH reading must be interpreted. The Patukope-Pute cluster records the highest salinity values in the Ada network — 28.2 to 28.6 ppt at the lagoon intake stations — consistent with a system where reduced Volta freshwater inflow and salt pan operations are concentrating dissolved solids and where the periodic cycling of saline water over potentially acid sulphate soils creates conditions that can mobilise the acid-generating reactions described above. The 4.78 pH at Pute lagoon is not merely a soil measurement. It is a soil-water system measurement, and the water quality chapter already flagged it as a compound ecological vulnerability that must be tracked through quarterly monitoring.

At a pH of 4.78, aluminium and iron become soluble and can reach concentrations toxic to plant roots. Phosphate availability collapses — becoming bound in aluminium and iron complexes that mangrove roots cannot access regardless of how much phosphate is present in the soil. The chemical environment in which a mangrove seedling would need to establish itself at Pute lagoon is hostile at a fundamental biochemical level.

### Humus and Organic Matter — Severe Depletion

The humus data at the Pute stations confirms what the pH suggests: these are soils that have lost, and are continuing to lose, their organic foundation.

The Pute lagoon intake (black mangrove) station records a humus content of 4.78% — the lowest in the entire Ada soil dataset. The Pute lagoon (white mangrove) station, 400 metres away, records 9.2%. Both values are severely depleted against the benchmark established by the best-condition mangrove soils in the Keta network: Galotse at 40.6%, Tunu at 44.3%, and Agortoe at 37.5%. By comparison, the Obane restored mangrove site at Ada records 26.0%. The Pute lagoon intake's humus of 4.78% is approximately 12% of the Galotse benchmark — a fraction that reflects not a slightly depleted soil but one that has been largely stripped of its organic matter.

The consequences of low humus in a mangrove soil environment are multiple and mutually reinforcing. Humus is the primary reservoir of soil organic carbon — the material substance of the blue carbon stock the monitoring system is designed to protect. The loss of humus at Pute represents a direct, quantified reduction in carbon stock at this location. But humus also performs structural and hydrological functions: it retains moisture, improves soil aggregation, and provides the nutrient reservoir that sustains plant establishment. A soil with 4.78% humus cannot retain water, cannot provide the nitrogen and phosphorus buffering capacity that seedlings need, and has little structural resistance to compaction or erosion. The physical and chemical environment at Pute lagoon intake is not just carbon-depleted — it is functionally impaired as a substrate for ecological recovery.

The cause of this depletion is inferrable from the context. Humus accumulates under the specific conditions of mangrove systems: anaerobic decomposition of leaf litter and root turnover in waterlogged soils, where microbial activity is suppressed and organic matter builds up over decades. Those conditions require an intact mangrove canopy producing leaf litter, adequate waterlogging to maintain anaerobic conditions, and an absence of major disturbance. At Pute, the canopy is largely absent — directly evidenced by the high surface temperature data reported below — and the hydrological conditions are stressed by the saline lagoon dynamics of the Pute intake zone. With no canopy contributing leaf fall, no stable waterlogging protecting organic matter from oxidation, and an acidic soil environment accelerating whatever decomposition does occur, the humus balance at Pute is negative: more is being lost than is accumulating.

### Soil Nutrients — System-Wide Depletion

All three nutrient parameters at Pute lagoon are severely depleted:

**Nitrate (N):** 3 mg/kg. This is at the floor of what the monitoring instrument can reliably distinguish from zero, and it is among the lowest nitrate values recorded in the entire Keta and Ada soil dataset. For comparison, the monitoring network records a mean nitrate range of 48 to 984 mg/kg across Ada stations, with the 984 mg/kg at Wasakuse representing the extreme enrichment end. At 3 mg/kg, Pute lagoon has essentially no nitrogen available for plant uptake. Without nitrate, there is no protein synthesis, no chlorophyll production, and no meaningful mangrove establishment.

**Phosphate (P):** 3 mg/kg. Consistent with the nitrate picture, phosphate at Pute lagoon is at near-zero. The Obane station records 705 mg/kg; the Keta moist mangrove stations record 52 to 103 mg/kg; even Keta's upland stations record 1 to 32 mg/kg. At 3 mg/kg, the Pute lagoon phosphate reading reflects both the depletion of organic phosphorus from humus loss and the chemical phosphate fixation that occurs at low pH, as noted above. Even if mangrove seedlings could tolerate the near-acid-sulphate conditions at this station, they would have no phosphate to draw on for root development.

**Potassium (K):** 7 mg/kg. The Obane station, three kilometres away, records 1,280 mg/kg. The Keta moist mangrove stations record 78 to 201 mg/kg. At 7 mg/kg, Pute lagoon's potassium is at the threshold of physiological significance for plant life. Potassium governs osmotic regulation in saline-exposed plants — including the adjustment that allows mangroves to exclude salt at their roots — making its absence in a saline-exposed soil environment particularly damaging.

The combination of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium at trace levels in a soil that is also near-acid-sulphate in pH and stripped of organic matter is not a coincidence. These parameters are interconnected: low pH reduces the bioavailability of all three nutrients; loss of humus removes the organic reservoir that supplies and retains them; and the absence of mangrove vegetation means there is no root exudate activity or leaf litter decomposition to return nutrients to the soil. The system at Pute lagoon is in a low-nutrient trap from which it cannot self-recover.

### Moisture Content

Moisture at the Pute stations — 28.3% at Pute lagoon and 30.6% at Pute lagoon intake — falls within the broader Ada range of 21.5 to 29.45%. These values are not anomalous in isolation; the timing of the December 2025 baseline collection, following recent rainfall, likely elevated soil moisture across the site. However, the moisture data at Pute should not be interpreted as evidence of stable hydrological conditions. The combination of high salinity in the water column (documented in Chapter 6), the absence of canopy shade documented by the temperature data below, and the strongly positive ORP values in the Ada water body (ranging from 832 to 1,236 mV in the interior lagoon, indicating highly oxidising conditions) means that the Pute soil environment is exposed to drying and oxidation between the sampling periods — a cycle that progressively depletes organic matter and drives pyrite oxidation toward acid sulphate conditions.

### Soil Temperature — The Canopy-Absence Signal

The soil surface temperature of 38.4°C recorded at the Pute walkway station is the highest in the Ada monitoring network and is a direct marker of canopy absence. Bare or sparsely vegetated soil in tropical coastal conditions absorbs solar radiation without the shading effect that a closed mangrove canopy would provide. The Obane restored mangrove site — discussed in Section 7.4 — records a soil temperature of 34.2°C. The 4.2°C differential between these two stations within the same landscape is not merely a microclimate observation; it is a carbon management signal.

Soil temperature governs the rate of microbial decomposition. Even within the temperature range recorded at Ada, the relationship between temperature and decomposition is non-linear: a 10°C increase in soil temperature approximately doubles the rate of organic matter mineralisation under aerobic conditions. The elevated temperature at Pute walkway directly accelerates the decomposition of whatever residual organic matter the soil retains, converting what little humus remains into CO₂ rather than allowing it to accumulate. At 38.4°C, organic matter at Pute is decomposing faster than it is accumulating — even if there were a canopy providing litter inputs, which there is not.

### Management Interpretation

Pute is the most degraded soil environment in the entire Ghana Blue Carbon monitoring network. The simultaneous presence of near-acid-sulphate pH, nutrient depletion across all three measured macronutrients, severely depleted humus at both stations, and a surface temperature that actively accelerates whatever organic matter loss remains possible creates conditions where passive ecological recovery — simply protecting the site and waiting — is not a viable management strategy. The chemical and physical conditions are not merely suboptimal for mangrove establishment; they are, in combination, actively hostile to it.

Active soil preparation would be required before any restoration planting at Pute could succeed. This would need to address at minimum: (1) liming or pH amendment to bring soils above pH 5.5 before establishment; (2) organic matter addition to begin rebuilding the humus layer that gives soil its structural and nutritional capacity; and (3) nutrient supplementation, particularly nitrogen, to bring soils within the range that supports seedling growth. The spatial comparison to Obane, reported fully in Section 7.5, provides the management lesson: what Pute could become with investment is already visible three kilometres away.

## 7.3 Wasakuse-ngwa Station — Extreme Nutrient Enrichment

**Soil station:** Wasakuse ngwa water near ponds (N 05°50.338, E 000°33.322).

### Inversion of the Pute Condition

The Wasakuse-ngwa station presents the structural opposite of the Pute condition. Where Pute records nutrient depletion across all measured parameters, Wasakuse records the most extreme nitrogen enrichment in the entire monitoring network. Where Pute's story is about what soils lose when mangroves are absent and conditions are degraded, Wasakuse's story is about what happens when an external nutrient source — in this case, cattle — deposits concentrated waste in direct contact with a wetland margin.

### Nitrate — Critical Alert Threshold Exceeded

The nitrate value of 984 mg/kg at Wasakuse-ngwa is the highest nitrate reading in the entire Ada and Keta monitoring network. The MRV alert threshold for eutrophication risk at mangrove-proximate soil stations is 500 mg/kg. At 984 mg/kg, the Wasakuse reading is nearly double the alert threshold. This is not a value that represents a minor exceedance of a precautionary limit — it is a value that is approaching the level at which nitrogen toxicity to root systems becomes a documented risk in tropical wetland soils, and at which the microbial community dynamics that govern carbon cycling are fundamentally altered.

The source of this enrichment is unambiguous: cattle grazing at Wasakuse brings large concentrations of animal waste into direct and repeated contact with the wetland soil and water system at this location. Cattle are not diffuse contributors of nitrogen to the landscape; they are mobile, spatially concentrated sources of fresh organic nitrogen that deposit in the wetland margin every time herds graze on its fringe. At the volumes implied by the 984 mg/kg reading — more than four hundred times the nitrate recorded at Pute lagoon — this is a sustained and intense nutrient loading event that has altered the soil chemistry at this station beyond what natural wetland processes could buffer.

### Eutrophication as a Carbon Sink Risk

The management concern at Wasakuse is not that the soil has too much nitrate to support mangrove growth — mangroves are not nitrate-sensitive in the way that some upland plants are — but that extreme nitrogen enrichment in a wetland soil has documented secondary effects that compound over time and ultimately compromise the site's function as a carbon sink.

At elevated nitrogen concentrations, the microbial communities in soil shift their metabolic balance toward decomposition. High nitrogen availability stimulates the activity of heterotrophic bacteria that break down organic matter, converting soil carbon into CO₂ and methane. This process — nitrogen-stimulated organic matter mineralisation — is the primary mechanism by which eutrophication converts a wetland from a carbon accumulator to a carbon emitter. The irony of the Wasakuse condition is that the same nitrogen that would, in small quantities, assist mangrove growth is, at 984 mg/kg, actively undermining the soil's capacity to hold the carbon it currently contains.

At concentrations this high, nitrogen-stimulated decomposition also alters the redox chemistry of the soil, creating conditions that favour the reduction of iron and sulphate, and in doing so can mobilise phosphate from iron-phosphate complexes — a process that in turn shifts the soil chemistry toward the kind of eutrophication-driven nutrient cycling that, at the water column level, promotes algal dominance over macrophyte communities. The consequence of algal bloom development in the water adjacent to Wasakuse would be a depression of dissolved oxygen through the decomposition of algal biomass, feeding back into the anaerobic-aerobic cycling that drives further carbon loss.

### Moisture and Contextual Parameters

Soil moisture at Wasakuse-ngwa was 25.7%, consistent with the broader Ada moisture range. The moisture value confirms that this station sits within the wetland fringe rather than on upland margin, and that the nitrogen at 984 mg/kg is being deposited directly into a soil that is hydrologically connected to the lagoon and creek system — meaning that soluble nitrogen will migrate from soil to water, contributing to water column nutrient loading that the water quality chapter did not yet detect in the December 2025 baseline but that quarterly monitoring will track going forward.

### Management Interpretation

The Wasakuse-ngwa station requires quarterly monitoring and a spatially specific management response. The current cattle grazing pattern at Wasakuse is depositing nitrogen into the wetland margin at a rate that has already exceeded the MRV eutrophication alert threshold by a factor of nearly two. The management response is not to eliminate cattle grazing — which is a livelihood activity for communities in the area — but to implement spatial separation: physical barriers or management agreements that prevent cattle from accessing the wetland margin within a defined buffer distance of the water's edge. The MRV system's Layer III governance function (described in the monitoring chapter) specifically identifies contested land-use at wetland margins as requiring escalation to the Ada East District Assembly if field monitoring does not show improvement over successive rounds. The 984 mg/kg nitrate reading constitutes grounds for immediate inclusion of Wasakuse-ngwa in the formal escalation pathway.

## 7.4 Obane Creek — Two Stories in One Station

**Soil station:** Obane restored mangrove (N 05°48.338, E 000°33.322).

The Obane station is the most analytically complex location in the Ada soil monitoring network, because it presents two apparently contradictory conditions within a single sampling point: evidence of anthropogenic nutrient enrichment that is comparable in scale to the Wasakuse signal, and evidence of active ecological recovery that directly contradicts the degraded condition at Pute. Understanding both dimensions simultaneously is essential to correctly interpreting the Obane data and making sound management decisions for this location.

### Story One: Cattle Enrichment at Obane

**Phosphate (P): 705 mg/kg.** This value is the highest phosphate reading in the entire monitoring network and is approximately 7 to 14 times the phosphate recorded at the best-condition moist mangrove soil stations in the Keta network (52–103 mg/kg). It represents extreme phosphorus loading that is directly attributable to the cattle grazing and the concentrated cow dung deposits observed during the December 2025 field assessment at this location. While the Wasakuse story is dominated by nitrogen, the Obane story is dominated by phosphorus — though both reflect the same underlying driver: concentrated animal waste at the wetland margin.

**Potassium (K): 1,280 mg/kg.** This is the highest potassium value in the entire Ada and Keta monitoring network — more than six times the highest potassium recorded in the best-developed Keta mangrove stations (Agbastivi and Galotse, approximately 200 mg/kg), and nearly 183 times the 7 mg/kg recorded at Pute lagoon three kilometres away. Potassium at this level is not a sign of natural soil fertility; it is a marker of organic waste concentration. Animal manure is high in exchangeable potassium, and the accumulation visible in the Obane soil reflects repeated, sustained deposition in a location where cattle return to graze regularly.

**Nitrate (N):** Elevated, consistent with the cattle enrichment pressure shared with the Wasakuse system. While the specific Obane nitrate value is represented within the site-wide range of 48 to 984 mg/kg for the Ada stations (see Figure 18, source report), the combined pattern of extreme P and K enrichment confirms that Obane, like Wasakuse, is experiencing multi-nutrient loading from the same source.

The long-term risk of this enrichment pattern at Obane is analogous to the Wasakuse risk but with a different nutrient emphasis. Extreme phosphate loading at 705 mg/kg creates the conditions for algal proliferation in adjacent water bodies — particularly in combination with elevated nitrogen — and can shift the competitive balance within the soil microbial community away from the fungi and bacteria that stabilise organic matter toward the decomposers that mineralise it. The fact that Obane currently records high humus (discussed below) means there is organic matter at this station that this enrichment regime is actively threatening. The trajectory of the Obane enrichment signal must be tracked quarterly.

### Story Two: Recovery in Progress

Despite the cattle enrichment pressure, the Obane restored mangrove station records soil conditions that are categorically better than Pute across every restoration-relevant parameter.

**Humus: 26.0%.** This is the highest humus value recorded in the Ada soil network — more than six times the 4.78% at Pute lagoon intake and nearly three times the 9.2% at Pute lagoon. It is substantially below the best-condition Keta benchmarks (40.6% at Galotse), but it represents a meaningfully functional level of organic matter — a soil that can retain moisture, supply nutrients, and sustain the microbial communities that drive healthy decomposition cycling. The Obane humus value reflects what restored mangrove canopy does to a wetland soil over time: it supplies leaf litter and root turnover material, it creates the shade and moisture conditions that slow aerobic decomposition, and it sustains the anaerobic micro-environments that allow organic matter to accumulate rather than mineralise.

**Soil pH: 6.2–6.84.** The Obane pH range is near-neutral, within the optimum window for phosphate availability and microbial activity in tropical wetland soils. Against Pute lagoon's 4.78, this near-neutral pH signals a soil environment that has not experienced the pyrite oxidation and acid sulphate progression visible at Pute. Whether this is because the Obane station has never experienced the same degree of drainage and hydrological disruption, or because the restored canopy has helped stabilise the hydrology above the Obane soils, the contrast is stark and management-relevant.

**Soil Temperature: 34.2°C.** The Obane temperature is 4.2°C lower than the 38.4°C recorded at the Pute walkway station. This difference is attributable to canopy shading: the restored mangrove stand at Obane intercepts solar radiation before it reaches the soil surface, reducing the thermal loading that accelerates organic matter decomposition. A 4.2°C cooling effect is not a marginal difference; in terms of decomposition rates, it represents a measurable reduction in carbon mineralisation speed. Every mangrove tree restored at a site like Pute does not merely provide biomass carbon — it also begins moderating the soil thermal environment in a way that slows the loss of existing soil carbon. Canopy restoration is simultaneously above-ground carbon investment and below-ground carbon preservation.

**Moisture: 25.1%.** Within the normal Ada range, confirming that the Obane station sits within a functioning wetland environment with adequate soil moisture for anaerobic condition maintenance.

### Management Interpretation

The Obane station demands a dual management response. For the cattle enrichment dimension, the same spatial management logic that applies at Wasakuse applies here: the extreme P and K loading visible in the December 2025 data requires monitoring escalation and a livestock management buffer at the wetland margin. Unlike Wasakuse, where the soil story is primarily one of damage, Obane contains soil conditions worth protecting — the 26.0% humus and near-neutral pH are assets, not artefacts, and they are at risk from sustained nutrient enrichment. Quarterly monitoring at Obane must specifically track whether P and K values are increasing, stable, or declining as a direct indicator of whether the cattle management pressure is changing.

For the recovery dimension, the Obane data should be read as a management argument for restoration investment at Pute. If the Obane restored mangrove stand can generate soil conditions — higher humus, lower temperature, stable pH — that are categorically better than the degraded conditions at Pute, then the pathway from Pute's condition to Obane's condition is empirically demonstrated. It requires canopy restoration, which requires initial planting, active soil preparation, and protection from hydrological disruption. The data does not tell us how long it took Obane to reach its current soil condition. But it tells us that a comparable Ada site, under restoration, can exceed 26% humus and 34.2°C soil temperature. That is the target.

## 7.5 The Degradation-Recovery Continuum: Pute vs. Obane

The Patukope-Pute cluster and the Obane restored mangrove site are three kilometres apart. They share the same Ada-Songor landscape, the same broadly comparable coastal climate, the same Volta-mediated hydrological context, and — as the coordinates confirm — similar positions within the lagoon system. What separates their soil conditions is not geography. It is the presence or absence of mangrove canopy, and the management history that produced or prevented it.

Presenting both stations side by side in comparable terms is the most direct way to make the management case that the Ada soil data supports.

The table encapsulates what the preceding sections have established in detail. Across every parameter where comparison is possible, Obane outperforms Pute. The pH differential alone — 4.78 versus 6.2–6.84 — places the two stations in fundamentally different geochemical regimes: one at risk of acid sulphate activation, the other within the neutral-to-slightly-acidic range that maximises nutrient bioavailability. The humus differential — 4.78% to 9.2% at Pute against 26.0% at Obane — represents a carbon stock difference that is not theoretical; it is directly measurable in the ground right now. The temperature differential of 4.2°C, driven entirely by canopy presence, is a continuous, daily moderating effect that slows organic matter loss at Obane every hour of every day.

What would it take to move a site from Pute-condition to Obane-condition? The honest answer is that there is no single intervention and no short timeline. The combination of near-acid-sulphate pH, depleted nutrients, extremely low humus, and high surface temperature at Pute does not yield to a single-season restoration planting. A realistic progression requires several distinct management stages.

The first stage is soil stabilisation and pH amendment. Before any planting succeeds at Pute lagoon, the soil chemistry must be adjusted toward a range where seedlings can survive. This requires liming — the application of calcium carbonate or calcium magnesium carbonate to neutralise the acidity — combined with whatever organic matter input can be managed to begin rebuilding the humus layer. This stage likely requires a minimum of one to two growing seasons before conditions are compatible with establishment of even the most acid-tolerant mangrove species. The investment at this stage produces no measurable above-ground carbon benefit; it is entirely preparatory.

The second stage is canopy establishment and protection. Once soil pH has been brought above 5.5 and some humus has been rebuilt through organic matter application, targeted planting of *Rhizophora* or *Avicennia* propagules — the species native to the Ada system — can begin. Early establishment success at Pute will depend heavily on whether the hydrological conditions that drove the original degradation (likely a combination of saline lagoon dynamics, reduced freshwater dilution, and periodic drainage exposure) can be managed or buffered during the vulnerable early growth period. This requires engagement with the upstream hydrological governance — specifically, with the management of the Pute lagoon intake zone, where salt pan operations and reduced Volta discharge interact to produce the high-salinity conditions documented in Chapter 6.

The third stage is soil development under canopy. Once canopy is established — even a sparse, early-successional canopy — the soil transformation documented at Obane begins to operate. Leaf litter falls, root turnover adds organic matter, canopy shade reduces surface temperature, and moisture retention improves. Humus begins to build. The transition from 9.2% to 26% humus, as the Obane baseline demonstrates is achievable, is a multi-year process. A realistic estimate, based on what is known about mangrove soil development rates in West African coastal systems, is that measurable humus improvement — sufficient to show a statistically meaningful trend in quarterly monitoring — would appear after three to five years of successful canopy establishment. Carbon storage benefits accrue throughout this period: even a sparse canopy begins accumulating above-ground biomass carbon from year one, while the soil below it is simultaneously beginning to accumulate below-ground organic matter. The two carbon pools — above-ground and below-ground — develop together, and the soil carbon pool ultimately represents the larger fraction of the total blue carbon benefit.

The fourth stage is ecological maturity and monitoring validation. A fully recovered mangrove stand at a former Pute-condition site would, if the Obane trajectory holds, eventually approach the humus levels of the Keta benchmark stations — 30% to 40% over a decade or more of development. At that point, the site enters the maintenance phase of carbon management: protected, monitored quarterly, and contributing to the verified carbon stock that underpins the national blue carbon programme.

The carbon benefits that accrue at each stage are cumulative and reinforcing. Stage one generates no measurable above-ground carbon but prevents further soil carbon loss — a carbon-conservation benefit that is real even if it is difficult to quantify under current MRV accounting approaches. Stage two generates initial above-ground biomass carbon from the first year of canopy establishment. Stage three generates the accelerating below-ground carbon benefit as humus builds and soil temperature declines. Stage four delivers the full carbon co-benefit of a mature mangrove stand: above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, soil organic carbon, and the ongoing annual sequestration that comes from a functioning, productive wetland.

The Pute-to-Obane transition is not guaranteed. It requires sustained investment, community engagement, hydrological management, and protection from the pressures — salt pan expansion, cattle encroachment, drainage disturbance — that produced the Pute condition in the first place. But it is demonstrated as ecologically achievable. The Obane data exists. The recovery trajectory is visible. What is required now is the management commitment to pursue it.

## 7.6 Carbon Storage Implications of Ada Soil Findings

The Ada-Songor soil data cannot be read in isolation from the water quality conditions documented in Chapter 6. The two datasets describe a system under simultaneous stress at multiple ecological levels, and the interaction between water column conditions and soil conditions is where the most significant carbon storage risks are concentrated.

### The Pute System: Active Carbon Loss

At Pute, the combined water-soil picture is one of active carbon loss. The water column data from Chapter 6 established that the Patukope-Pute cluster sits at the high-salinity, high-TDS end of the Ada gradient: salinities of 28.2 to 28.6 ppt, TDS of 57.3 ppm, and a water column chemistry consistent with concentrated lagoon water and possible salt pan seepage. The soil data from this chapter establishes that the soils beneath that water column are near-acid-sulphate in pH, severely depleted in organic matter, and without the nutrient base needed to support vegetation recovery.

The ORP data from Chapter 6 adds a further critical layer. ORP values in the Ada interior lagoon and creek systems ranged from 832 to 1,236 mV — strongly positive, indicating highly oxidising water column conditions. Strongly oxidising water in contact with potentially acid sulphate soils creates the conditions under which pyrite oxidation is not merely possible but actively driven: the oxidising water supplies the electron acceptors needed for iron sulphide oxidation, and the periodic exposure of the soil surface during tidal cycles or seasonal water level drawdown provides the oxygen needed to complete the reaction. This is not a speculative risk at Pute. The 4.78 pH at Pute lagoon is the result of that reaction having already occurred, to some degree, at this station.

High ORP + near-acid-sulphate pH + depleted humus + high surface temperature is a combination that describes a site where carbon storage is not merely static but actively compromised. The existing soil carbon at Pute lagoon — already reduced to 4.78% to 9.2% humus — is being mineralised at an accelerated rate by the elevated temperature and by whatever microbial activity the residual organic matter can sustain. This site is not retaining carbon; it is losing it.

### The Wasakuse-Obane System: Enrichment-Driven Carbon Risk

At the Wasakuse and Obane stations, the risk mechanism is different but the carbon storage concern is equally real. Extreme nitrogen at Wasakuse (984 mg/kg) and extreme phosphate and potassium at Obane (705 mg/kg and 1,280 mg/kg respectively) represent nutrient loading that, at the sustained levels documented, stimulates microbial decomposition of existing organic matter. At Obane, where humus stands at 26.0%, there is more organic matter at risk than at Pute — which means more carbon at stake. If the enrichment signal at Obane is intensifying rather than stable — a determination that quarterly monitoring will make possible — the existing soil carbon stock there could decline significantly within monitoring-relevant timescales (two to three years), even while above-ground biomass continues to accumulate.

The Chapter 6 water data did not yet show elevated nutrient concentrations in the water column adjacent to the Wasakuse-Obane area at December 2025 baseline levels. This is consistent with the known dynamics of nutrient migration from soil to water: the soil enrichment signal appears first, and the water column signal follows as soluble nutrients are mobilised by rainfall, tidal exchange, and groundwater movement. The December 2025 baseline captures the soil enrichment in its early-to-intermediate stage. Quarterly monitoring of both soil and water at Wasakuse and Obane in 2026 will establish whether the water column nutrient signal is beginning to emerge — which, if it does, would constitute the confirmation of an active eutrophication trajectory.

### Implications for the Carbon Inventory Report

The Ada-Songor Carbon Inventory Report cannot assume stable baseline soil conditions at the Pute or Wasakuse-Obane stations. Stable baseline conditions are a technical requirement of the verification frameworks used in blue carbon accounting (e.g., Verra VM0033 and the IPCC Wetlands Supplement), which assume that the baseline represents a stable or predictably varying state from which project activities represent a measurable improvement. At Pute, the baseline is not stable: it is actively deteriorating. At Obane-Wasakuse, the baseline includes enrichment signals that represent an additional carbon risk not captured by standard baseline methodologies.

The Carbon Inventory Report should account for at least three non-standard conditions in the Ada soil data:

**Active carbon loss at Pute.** The combination of near-acid-sulphate pH, depleted humus, high surface temperature, and strongly oxidising water column conditions indicates that Pute lagoon is an active carbon-loss location within the monitoring baseline period. Any carbon accounting that assigns a stable or historical average humus value to the Pute stations will overstate the carbon stock retained at this location. A conservative accounting approach would treat Pute's current humus values as an upper bound on residual carbon stock and model expected further loss under continued degradation conditions.

**Potential carbon loss acceleration from nutrient enrichment.** The extreme N at Wasakuse (984 mg/kg) and extreme P and K at Obane (705 and 1,280 mg/kg respectively) represent enrichment conditions that the standard soil carbon accounting framework does not directly incorporate as a loss risk. The Carbon Inventory Report should flag these stations as subject to nutrient-stimulated decomposition risk and should apply a conservative treatment of the Obane humus value (26.0%) pending two to three quarters of monitoring data that can establish whether it is stable or declining.

**Recovery trajectory at Obane.** Conversely, the Obane restored mangrove data provides a defensible recovery reference for the Ada system. The 26.0% humus, near-neutral pH, and 34.2°C soil temperature at Obane represent conditions that are consistent with active carbon accumulation under a functioning restored mangrove canopy. The Carbon Inventory Report can use the Obane baseline as the reference condition for what a successfully restored Ada mangrove site achieves at an intermediate stage of recovery — a reference point that anchors the modelled carbon benefit of any planned restoration at Pute or adjacent degraded sites.

Taken together, the Ada soil data establishes that the Songor system is not a uniform landscape with a single carbon storage trajectory. It is a mosaic of active carbon loss at Pute, potential carbon loss acceleration at Wasakuse-Obane from nutrient enrichment, and demonstrable carbon recovery at the Obane restored site. Each zone requires a distinct treatment within the Carbon Inventory framework, and each zone requires the quarterly monitoring cadence established under the MRV system to track whether conditions are improving, stable, or worsening — because at Ada-Songor, the answer is different in every corner of the landscape.
