# Chapter 9: Pressures, Threats, and Drivers of Ecosystem Change

---

## 9.1 Overview: A Multi-Pressure System

The water and soil quality data assembled in Chapters 4 through 7 record a set of ecological conditions that are, in aggregate, more alarming than any individual reading in isolation. A pH of 2.7 at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations. Hypersalinity of 40 ppt at Dzita. A nitrate concentration of 984 mg/kg at Wasakuse — nearly double the MRV eutrophication alert threshold. A deforestation rate of 2.15 percent per year across the Keta Lagoon Complex. Oxidation-reduction potential (ORP) values of 832 to 1,547 mV in the interior lagoon at Ada-Songor, where the geochemical conditions should be suppressing decomposition and preserving carbon. Peak water temperatures of 37.3°C in open Keta lagoon stations during the December 2025 dry-season baseline. These are not random ecological fluctuations. They are the measurable signatures of specific human decisions and structural changes operating across both systems.

The pH 2.7 anomaly at Anyanui is connected to road infrastructure decisions — specifically, the construction of the Tunu road without adequate culverts, which blocked tidal exchange, drained formerly waterlogged intertidal sediments, and triggered the pyrite oxidation that generates sulphuric acid in the exposed soil layer. The 40 ppt hypersalinity at Dzita is connected to commercial salt production and, more fundamentally, to the decades-long reduction in freshwater dilution from the Volta River caused by the Akosombo Dam. The 984 mg/kg nitrate at Wasakuse is connected to cattle grazing economics — the dry-season use of wetland margins as grazing land by herders from adjacent communities whose animals deposit concentrated waste directly into lagoon-fringe soils. The 2.15 percent per year deforestation at Keta is connected to the fish-smoking economy: thousands of women fish-processors operating kilns that run on *Rhizophora racemosa* wood, each individually making a rational livelihood decision that collectively drives the most important deforestation trend in Ghana's national blue carbon estate. The ORP anomaly at Ada-Songor is connected to the Akosombo Dam's reduction of the Volta's freshwater discharge, which has diminished the dilution and the reducing geochemical conditions that previously maintained the interior lagoon as a carbon-preserving environment.

None of these pressures can be addressed in isolation, and none will resolve themselves through ecological intervention alone. Managing the ecosystems at Keta and Ada-Songor means managing the drivers — the infrastructural, economic, agricultural, and industrial decisions that are producing the field measurements that this report has documented. This chapter is a diagnostic examination of those drivers: who and what is causing them, through what mechanisms they operate, what the trajectory is if nothing changes, and where multiple pressures converge to create the zones of highest ecological risk.

Six primary pressure categories are active across the two systems. They are hydrological alteration from the Akosombo Dam legacy; fuelwood harvesting driving the 2.15 percent per year deforestation trajectory; salt pan expansion at Dzita and Pute; cattle grazing and associated nutrient loading; plastics and diffuse pollution; and climate change as a system-wide pressure multiplier. Each is examined in turn.

## 9.2 Hydrological Alteration — The Akosombo Dam Legacy

The Akosombo Dam, completed in 1964 on the Volta River approximately sixty kilometres upstream of the coast, is the single most significant structural driver of long-term ecological change in both the Keta and Ada-Songor systems. Its effects are not proximate — the dam is not located within the project landscape — but they have been operating continuously for sixty years and have fundamentally reshaped the water chemistry, sediment dynamics, and hydrological regime of both lagoon systems in ways that no community-level governance intervention can reverse.

The dam traps more than 90 percent of the Volta River's natural sediment load before it reaches the coast. Before 1964, the Volta's annual flood pulse delivered massive quantities of sand and silt to the delta, sustaining the morphology of the coastal sandspit that separates the Keta Lagoon from the Atlantic Ocean and maintaining the delta as a prograding, river-dominated system that built seaward. With more than nine-tenths of that sediment now permanently trapped behind the dam structure, the delta was transformed into a wave-dominated, eroding system. The narrow sandspit barrier protecting the Keta Lagoon — the landform on which the largest concentration of mangroves in Ghana sits — has been in progressive coastal retreat ever since. Communities in Dzita and along the lagoon edge have documented this retreat across generations. Elders describe mangroves that were once "thick like a wall"; the contrast with the scattered, fragmented stands visible in the 2025 satellite baseline is not a metaphor but a measured ecological loss. The GIS landcover analysis confirms that the most accessible mangrove stands closest to major settlements show the greatest fragmentation and edge degradation — a spatial pattern consistent with the combined effect of harvesting pressure and coastline instability.

At Ada-Songor, the Akosombo Dam operates through a different but equally consequential mechanism. The freshwater discharge that once reached the Songor estuary in sufficient volume to dilute marine salinity intrusion and maintain the brackish chemistry historically tolerated by the system's mangroves has been reduced. The Volta still flows, and its freshwater influence is still measurable at the Azizanya-Ayigbo stations — where ultra-low conductivity of 2.64 µS and near-zero TDS confirm an active freshwater pulse from the river margin — but it is a diminished version of its historical self. The practical consequence is a progressive, long-term shift toward higher average salinity in the Ada lagoon interior. This shift is directly visible in the water quality gradient documented in Chapter 6: from the near-freshwater conditions at Azizanya-Ayigbo in the north, through the transitional Futuenya-Big Ada corridor, to the near-marine salinities of 28.2 to 28.6 ppt at the Patukope-Pute intake zone and the strongly oxidising interior lagoon at Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane. That gradient — from reducing, freshwater-influenced, carbon-preserving chemistry in the north to strongly oxidising, high-salinity, carbon-hostile chemistry in the south — is in substantial part the geochemical fingerprint of the Akosombo Dam's operation over sixty years.

The ORP anomaly at the Ada interior lagoon — values of 832 to 1,547 mV where a functioning blue carbon system would show readings in the 0 to 200 mV range — is the most direct expression of what reduced freshwater inflow means for carbon storage function. High ORP indicates strongly oxidising conditions: the geochemical environment favours the breakdown of organic matter rather than its preservation. The water overlying the Ada-Songor interior sediments is not suppressing decomposition; it is actively promoting it. This is not a consequence of a single storm event or a short-term drought. It is the cumulative product of six decades during which the freshwater dilution that once maintained reducing conditions in the lagoon interior has been progressively reduced by upstream impoundment. Whether that trajectory is worsening, stable, or subject to seasonal recovery is a central question that the quarterly monitoring network established in Chapter 3 is designed to answer.

At Keta, the Dam's secondary effects compound the direct fuelwood pressure documented in Section 9.3. Sediment starvation of the delta has changed the tidal exchange dynamics of the lagoon: without the sandbar maintenance that river sediment delivery previously provided, tidal blockage events become more frequent, and hydrological connectivity between lagoon compartments becomes less reliable. The Anyanui-Tunu road pH anomaly sits within this context: the road was constructed across a zone that was already experiencing altered hydrology from upstream dam effects, and its inadequate culvert infrastructure delivered the tidal disconnection that triggered the acid sulphate chemistry now measured at pH 2.7. The road decision and the dam are both contributors to the same acid sulphate emergency.

Climate change will worsen both the Keta and Ada-Songor dam effects. Reduced rainfall under projected West African climate scenarios will further reduce the Volta's natural freshwater discharge reaching the coast, intensifying saline intrusion at Ada and reducing the residual tidal and seasonal freshwater buffering at Keta. Higher evaporation will accelerate the concentration of dissolved solids in semi-enclosed lagoon sections. Sea level rise will increase the frequency and severity of tidal incursion events over the deteriorating sandspit at Keta, exposing mangrove soils to saltwater stress and wave energy from which they currently receive partial protection from the barrier landform. The Akosombo Dam established the trajectory; climate change is the accelerant.

## 9.3 Fuelwood Harvesting — The 2.15 Percent Per Year Deforestation Driver

The baseline deforestation rate of 2.15 percent per year at the Keta Lagoon Complex is the most consequential single metric in the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment. Applied to the 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves mapped in the 2025 geospatial baseline — approximately 69 percent of Ghana's entire coastal mangrove resource — this rate represents approximately 445 hectares of mangrove loss per year in the business-as-usual scenario. Over a ten-year crediting period without intervention, the trajectory eliminates more than 4,400 hectares of the nation's largest blue carbon asset. Under exponential decay at 2.15 percent per year, the half-life of the Keta mangrove estate is approximately 32 years — meaning that by the mid-2050s, without a structural change in the driving pressure, the estate would be reduced to approximately half its current extent. The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster, which this report establishes as the reference benchmark for healthy ecological conditions and the baseline against which the entire carbon accounting framework is calibrated, would be progressively undermined as fuelwood pressure moves inward from the accessible perimeter stands into the core. If the current spatial gradient — in which the most accessible stands near Bomigo, Galo, and Sota show the greatest fragmentation — continues to propagate inward, the benchmark core itself faces effective depletion within decades, not centuries. The consequence for the project's additionality case would be severe: the reference benchmark against which conservation benefit is measured would be eroding from within.

The primary driver of this deforestation is the demand for *Rhizophora racemosa* — Red Mangrove — as fuel for fish-smoking kilns operating across communities including Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, Salo, and Dzita. This demand is not incidental or casual. *Rhizophora racemosa* wood is preferred by fish-smokers for specific, commercially rational reasons: it burns at high temperature, generating the sustained heat that fish-smoking requires, and its smoke imparts a characteristic colour and flavour to smoked fish that commands a price premium in local and regional markets. Fish processors who switch to alternative fuels — and some report having experimented with agricultural residues or imported firewood — describe losing that quality characteristic and experiencing a corresponding reduction in market price. The preference for *Rhizophora* wood is therefore not simply a habit that could be changed by awareness-raising. It is embedded in a commercial quality standard that determines household income for the women fish-processors who dominate this economy.

Those women are the primary actors in the fuelwood harvesting chain, and understanding their role is essential to understanding why the deforestation rate has proven so resistant to intervention. Over 70 percent of households in the project communities depend on mangrove-linked livelihoods, and within the fish-processing sector — which is the dominant post-harvest industry in the Keta region — fuelwood collection is not a marginal activity but a core livelihood strategy embedded in family income and food security. Women fish-processors are not primarily mangrove harvesters who happen to smoke fish; they are commercial food producers whose processing method has an indissoluble energy input, and who face a market signal that specifically rewards the use of mangrove fuel. Changing the deforestation trajectory requires changing this energy economics equation — not by prohibiting fuelwood harvesting, which would directly threaten household incomes without providing an alternative, but by making alternative fuel sources commercially viable and technically superior through a combination of improved cookstove adoption, sustainable woodlot establishment, and possibly market development for alternative-fuel fish products. The importance of the improved cookstove component of the WACA intervention cannot be overstated in this context: it is not an ancillary benefit but an enabling condition for the conservation of the nation's largest carbon asset.

The feedback loop created by this livelihood pressure — what the baseline analysis characterises as a social-ecological trap — compounds the urgency. Fish stocks in the Keta Lagoon depend on the mangrove fringe as nursery habitat: the root systems of *Rhizophora* provide the shelter, organic matter, and invertebrate productivity that support juvenile fish populations. As mangrove cover is reduced by fuelwood harvesting, nursery habitat is diminished, fish stocks decline, fishing effort must increase to maintain household incomes, and the demand for fuel to process a declining catch does not fall proportionately because fixed costs of operating a smoking kiln are maintained. The loop tightens. The 2.15 percent per year deforestation rate is both the cause and the consequence of this dynamic — a feedback system that does not resolve itself through ecological recovery and whose business-as-usual trajectory continues regardless of what the monitoring network detects, unless the underlying energy and livelihood economics are structurally transformed.

At the Keta end of the fuelwood pressure, the spatial clustering of damage is also important for project design. The 2025 environmental pressures assessment found that approximately 70 percent of natural mangrove cover at Keta is already degraded or fragmented. The most severely affected stands are those nearest to the largest fishing settlements — Bomigo, Galo, and Sota — where physical access to mangroves is easiest and the time cost of fuelwood collection is lowest. As these perimeter stands are exhausted, harvesters travel further into the lagoon system, extending the cutting front inward. The spatial gradient of fragmentation that the GIS analysis documents is, in management terms, a pressure map: it shows the direction in which the cutting front will advance if the demand is not reduced, and it identifies the Saviatula-Galotse benchmark core as the ultimate destination of a pressure wave that has already consumed the lagoon's accessible margins.

## 9.4 Salt Pan Expansion — Dzita and Pute

Commercial salt production is a significant and growing land-use activity at both Keta (principally in the Dzita cluster) and Ada-Songor (principally at Pute and the broader Elavanyo-Obane zone). It is simultaneously an economic reality for the communities that depend on it and the primary mechanism by which the Songor Ramsar Site has been reduced from a functioning mangrove ecosystem to a landscape of isolated fragments. The GIS landcover analysis is unambiguous: the Songor Ramsar Site now contains only 465.76 hectares of mangroves — 2 percent of the national total — and the landcover map shows a landscape dominated by salt pans and grasslands where mangroves once stood. This is not early-stage degradation with an ecological recovery pathway. It is the near-complete replacement of one ecosystem type by another through decades of systematic land conversion for industrial and artisanal salt production, with communities at Elavanyo and Obane at the centre of the most intensive phase of this conversion.

The mechanism of salt pan impact on wetland ecology is multidimensional and the effects are not confined to the area of the pan itself. Salt pans are physically bunded — enclosed by earthen walls that block the tidal exchange that mangroves require for their fundamental physiological functions. As the bunded area is converted to evaporative brine ponds, the tidal flows that previously delivered sediment, diluted salinity, and refreshed the nutrient cycle in the adjacent intertidal zone are disrupted. The ecological boundary of the salt pan's impact extends into the surrounding wetland through three pathways. First, brine seepage from evaporation ponds moves through the soil and shallow groundwater, introducing hypersaline water into adjacent lagoon areas and soil profiles — a process that is directly implicated in both the high salinity readings at the Patukope-Pute cluster (28.2 to 28.6 ppt at the lagoon intake) and the near-acid-sulphate soil conditions at Pute lagoon (pH 4.78). Second, the bunding infrastructure itself changes local drainage patterns, concentrating salt in adjacent water bodies and preventing the seasonal flushing events that would otherwise dilute accumulated salts and rehydrate the organic soil layer. Third, the presence of commercial salt infrastructure attracts secondary development — access roads, processing facilities, and accommodation — that further encroaches on mangrove fringe habitat.

At Dzita in the Keta system, the 40 ppt hypersalinity recorded at the main lagoon body stations is the co-product of two pressures: the Akosombo Dam's reduction in freshwater dilution from the Volta, and the concentration of brine from salt production operations in the eastern Keta lagoon zone. The 40 ppt reading is at the upper tolerance boundary for *Rhizophora racemosa*, beyond which mangrove productivity and carbon accumulation capacity are substantially reduced and species composition shifts toward halophyte communities that sequester less carbon per unit area. While the Dzita cluster retains some mangrove function — its soil chemistry data shows evidence of ongoing organic matter accumulation — the hypersaline water column constraint means that the carbon productivity of vegetation in this zone is systematically lower than what would be expected under brackish conditions, and that further intensification of salt production in the eastern lagoon zone would push conditions beyond even the most salt-tolerant mangrove's tolerance threshold.

At Pute in the Ada-Songor system, the salt pan legacy is most clearly expressed in the soil chemistry data detailed in Chapter 7. The Pute lagoon stations record the most degraded soil conditions in the entire monitoring network: pH of 4.78, humus of 4.78 to 9.2 percent (against benchmarks of 26 to 40.6 percent at better-condition sites), and nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium all at or near trace levels. This combination of near-acid-sulphate pH and nutrient depletion is not an independent ecological phenomenon. It is the soil signature of a system from which the periodic tidal flushing and freshwater dilution that maintained aerobic conditions have been disrupted — exactly the conditions created by the presence of bunded salt pan infrastructure in the adjacent zone, combined with the reduced freshwater input from the Akosombo Dam. The water quality data reinforces this picture: the Patukope-Pute cluster records the highest salinities in the Ada network, consistent with concentrated lagoon water supplemented by brine seepage from neighbouring salt operations.

The governance dimension of the salt pan pressure cannot be separated from its ecological dimension. Land tenure disputes between private salt companies, the Ada Traditional Council, and fishing communities have created a political economy in which formal resource protection is contested rather than reinforced. Communities at Pute — the designated early action hub for blue carbon piloting at Ada-Songor — are caught between the loss of traditional resource access from expanding salt concessions and the economic pressures that simultaneously drive unsustainable alternative practices. Any restoration strategy at Pute that addresses the soil chemistry conditions documented in Chapter 7 without resolving the hydrological disruption caused by adjacent salt pan operations will face a perpetual uphill struggle: restored mangroves cannot establish their tidal flushing conditions if the bunded infrastructure blocking that flushing remains in place. High-level land-use dialogue involving the Ada Traditional Council, the Ada East District Assembly, private salt producers, and MLNR is a prerequisite for ecological restoration success at Pute, not an optional complement to it.

## 9.5 Cattle Grazing — Ada-Songor Nutrient Loading

The extreme soil nutrient values recorded at the Wasakuse-ngwa and Obane stations in the Ada-Songor monitoring network are directly attributable to the grazing of cattle on wetland margins and the concentrated deposition of animal waste that this practice produces in direct contact with lagoon-fringe soils.

The mechanism is straightforward but its consequences are ecologically significant and compound over time. Wetland margins at Ada-Songor — particularly along the interior lagoon at Wasakuse and the creek system at Obane — are used as dry-season grazing areas by cattle herders from adjacent communities. During the dry season, when upland pastures are desiccated, the perennially moist wetland fringe remains productive and accessible, drawing cattle herds back repeatedly to the same marginal zones. The physical impacts of repeated grazing are multiple: hoof compaction disrupts the natural drainage micro-topography of the wetland margin, alters soil porosity, and can directly damage the root systems of established or establishing mangrove vegetation. But the most consequential impact is chemical. Cattle are not diffuse contributors of nutrients to the landscape; they are mobile, spatially concentrated sources of fresh organic nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium that deposit in large quantities wherever herds graze and rest.

The soil data at Wasakuse-ngwa documents the cumulative result of this deposition at the most extreme recorded level in the entire Keta and Ada monitoring network. The nitrate value of 984 mg/kg at this station is nearly double the MRV eutrophication alert threshold of 500 mg/kg. To contextualise this reading: the mean nitrate range across Ada soil stations spans 48 to 984 mg/kg, placing Wasakuse at the absolute maximum of the dataset; the Pute lagoon station at the other end of the spectrum records 3 mg/kg — effectively zero. The 984 mg/kg reading at Wasakuse is not simply elevated background enrichment. It represents a sustained and intense nutrient loading event that has altered the soil chemistry at this station beyond what natural wetland buffering processes can absorb.

The management concern is not primarily that excess nitrate inhibits mangrove growth — mangroves are relatively tolerant of high nitrogen in soils — but that extreme nitrogen enrichment in a waterlogged soil environment has documented secondary effects that progressively undermine the site's function as a carbon sink. At elevated nitrogen concentrations, the microbial communities in wetland soils shift their metabolic balance toward decomposition. High nitrogen availability stimulates heterotrophic bacteria that break down soil organic matter, converting the accumulated soil organic carbon (SOC) pool into CO₂ and methane. The process — nitrogen-stimulated organic matter mineralisation — is the mechanism by which eutrophication converts a wetland from a carbon accumulator to a carbon emitter. The Wasakuse reading, at 984 mg/kg, is already at the level at which this conversion is actively occurring. Quarterly monitoring of the water column adjacent to Wasakuse is designed to detect the downstream signature of this loading — elevated dissolved nitrogen in the lagoon water — but the soil data makes clear that the source-level enrichment has already reached a magnitude that constitutes a carbon permanence risk.

At Obane, the phosphorus and potassium signals provide the complementary picture. The phosphate reading of 705 mg/kg at the Obane restored mangrove station is the highest in the entire monitoring network — 7 to 14 times the phosphate values at best-condition Keta moist mangrove stations. Potassium at 1,280 mg/kg is similarly the highest value in the network, nearly six times the highest Keta benchmark values and 183 times the 7 mg/kg recorded at Pute lagoon three kilometres to the south. These extreme readings reflect repeated, concentrated dung deposition from cattle returning to the same wetland margin over multiple dry seasons. The long-term risk at Obane is analogous to Wasakuse — phosphorus loading at 705 mg/kg creates the conditions for algal proliferation in adjacent water bodies, which in turn drives oxygen depletion, altered redox chemistry, and the kind of nutrient cycling dynamics that shift the soil microbial community from carbon preservation toward carbon mineralisation.

Crucially, the governance challenge at Wasakuse and Obane is intersectoral. The communities herding cattle to the wetland margins may not be the same communities engaged in wetland management, fishing, or the CREMA governance processes being established for blue carbon management. Cattle herding and artisanal fishing are distinct livelihood systems, often involving different households and different patterns of seasonal resource use. The MRV system's Layer III governance function specifically identifies contested land-use at wetland margins as requiring escalation to the Ada East District Assembly if field monitoring does not show improvement: the 984 mg/kg nitrate at Wasakuse already constitutes grounds for inclusion in the formal escalation pathway. But an effective management response will require inter-community dialogue, negotiated spatial management agreements that establish buffer distances between the wetland edge and the grazing zone, and potentially alternative dry-season water access or supplementary feed provisions that reduce the incentive to use the wetland margin as dry-season pasture. These are institutional solutions to what the water and soil chemistry data has identified as a direct threat to the carbon storage function of the Ada-Songor interior lagoon zone.

## 9.6 Plastics, Pollution, and Wastewater

Solid and plastic waste represents a chronic, cross-cutting pollution pressure on both the Keta and Ada-Songor systems. The socio-economic and environmental baselines conducted across the 37 project communities identify plastic pollution as a primary community concern across all three Ramsar sites, and direct field observations during the 2025 assessment confirmed significant accumulations of plastic waste at multiple monitoring locations. Communities including Dzogborve, Galotse, Klonu, and Dzita in the Keta system, and the broader Songor-area communities at Ada, are identified as pollution hotspots where plastic waste has accumulated at levels that directly affect water quality, fishing operations, and community health.

Ghana generates an estimated 0.84 million tonnes of municipal plastic waste annually, with per capita plastic consumption growing at 3.4 percent per year. Only 10 percent of this waste is properly disposed of; the remainder enters open dumpsites, waterways, or accumulates as environmental litter. The near-total absence of formal waste management infrastructure in the project communities means that this national failure translates directly into lagoon-edge pollution. Projections based on current trends estimate that the volume of plastic entering Ghana's water bodies will increase by 190 percent by 2040, rising from 78,000 tonnes per year in 2020 to a potential 228,000 tonnes annually. For lagoon ecosystems that are already under stress from hydrological alteration, salinisation, and eutrophication, this trajectory represents a compounding burden.

The primary waste types entering the lagoon systems are single-use packaging materials — polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP) bags and bottles dominate the waste stream — together with discarded fishing gear including abandoned nets, line, and floats. The pathways of entry are multiple. Surface runoff from settlements on the lagoon edge carries loose waste directly into the water during rainfall events. Direct disposal — the dumping of household and market waste into lagoon margins — is documented across the project communities, where the absence of collection systems and the proximity of the lagoon to settlement creates the path of least resistance for solid waste disposal. Fishing operations contribute through the accumulation of lost or discarded gear, which entangles in mangrove root systems and, as it breaks down into microplastics, enters the food chain through the invertebrate communities that occupy the root zone.

The interaction between plastic pollution and the water quality findings of Chapters 4 and 6 operates through several pathways. Accumulations of plastic waste on the lagoon surface and at the sediment interface create physical barriers to gas exchange between the water column and the atmosphere, potentially altering the oxygen dynamics in enclosed and semi-enclosed sections of both lagoons — particularly relevant at the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga corridor in Keta, where the monitoring data already documents chronic thermal and oxygen stress. Decomposing organic material trapped within plastic accumulations contributes to localised chemical oxygen demand, creating hypoxic micro-environments near the sediment surface that can alter the microbial communities responsible for organic matter cycling. In sections of the lagoon where conductivity and TDS are already elevated by evaporative concentration and reduced freshwater input — the conditions documented at the Dzita end of the Keta system and at the Pute intake zone at Ada — additional dissolved chemical inputs from waste decomposition and runoff contribute to the cumulative dissolved solids load that the monitoring parameters are recording.

Beyond the direct water chemistry interactions, plastic pollution degrades the visual and market quality of the fisheries on which community livelihoods depend. Fishers across both systems report entanglement of nets in accumulated plastic waste, reduced catch, and contamination of fish processing areas by plastic debris — an issue that is particularly acute for women fish-processors operating smoking kilns in areas where plastic waste accumulates. This is both a livelihood issue and a health issue: the combustion of mixed plastic waste near fish-processing operations generates toxic combustion products with documented public health consequences for processors and their families. The WACA Blue Carbon initiative's proposal to integrate community-based plastic waste collection into the CREMA governance framework — treating plastic waste management as a blue carbon co-benefit verifiable under international standards — directly addresses this compound threat, creating the institutional infrastructure for waste management while simultaneously generating community income through emerging plastic credit markets.

## 9.7 Climate Change Projections — Amplifying All Pressures

All five of the pressure categories described above are already operating at measurable and, in several cases, critical levels within the current climate regime. Climate change does not introduce new pressure categories; it amplifies the magnitude and frequency of every existing pressure, reducing the ecosystem's capacity to buffer each stress event and compressing the time available for natural recovery between disturbances.

Sea level rise is the most direct climate amplifier for both systems. As sea levels rise, the frequency and reach of marine tidal incursion over the Keta sandspit increase, exposing interior mangrove soils to saltwater stress and wave energy that the barrier currently moderates. In a system where the Akosombo Dam has already eliminated the sediment supply needed to maintain that barrier's elevation, even modest sea level rise advances the timeline of barrier breach events that could inundate large sections of the lagoon's mangrove fringe. At Ada-Songor, sea level rise increases the rate of saline intrusion through the lagoon intake at Pute, further reducing the freshwater dilution that the system already receives insufficiently from the Volta. The salinity gradient documented in Chapter 6 — from near-freshwater at Azizanya-Ayigbo to near-marine at the Pute intake — would compress northward under sustained sea level rise, pushing higher salinity conditions into areas currently within the brackish range that supports mangrove function.

Reduced rainfall under West African climate projections directly intensifies the Akosombo Dam effect at Ada-Songor. The dam's impact on freshwater delivery to the estuary is already the primary structural driver of the interior lagoon's oxidising chemistry and elevated salinity. Every reduction in seasonal rainfall reduces the Volta's natural discharge at the coast and diminishes the residual freshwater buffer that currently maintains the lowest-salinity conditions at the Azizanya-Ayigbo fringe. If the canary stations at Azizanya — whose ultra-low conductivity and near-zero TDS are the current evidence that some freshwater pulse still reaches the system — begin to show rising conductivity across successive quarterly monitoring rounds, that is the climate-change signal for the entire Ada water chemistry gradient, indicating that the system is losing its final residual freshwater buffer.

Increased temperature operates as a pressure multiplier in the most literal geochemical sense. At the Keta Lagoon, peak afternoon water temperatures already reach 37.3°C at open lagoon stations during the December 2025 dry-season baseline. Even a 1 to 2°C increase in dry-season temperatures — within the range of projections for coastal Ghana — would drive peak temperatures into the 38 to 39°C range, compressing dissolved oxygen solubility in the warm, shallow lagoon water and intensifying the thermal loading that accelerates organic matter decomposition in exposed intertidal soils. At the Anyanui-Tunu road dead mangrove site, where the highest humus stock in the Keta network (44.3 percent) is already exposed to the aerating, acid-generating conditions of active acid sulphate soil formation, warmer soil temperatures directly accelerate the rate at which that carbon is mineralised. Every degree of warming is a multiplier on the decomposition rate of an already threatened carbon stock. At Ada-Songor, elevated temperatures increase evaporative concentration in the semi-enclosed interior lagoon, further raising salinity and dissolved solid concentrations in a system that is already at the upper end of its ecological tolerance range.

Increased frequency of extreme weather events — intensified rainfall and flooding — creates a paradoxical additional pressure. Episodic heavy rainfall events contribute to surface runoff that carries plastic waste, cattle waste nitrogen from the Wasakuse-Obane margin, and road construction pollutants into the lagoon system. In the Anyanui zone specifically, intense rainfall events can mobilise the acid sulphate chemistry from the Tunu road drainage into the wider lagoon water, spreading the pH 2.7 anomaly from its current localised expression into a broader acidification event that could affect the adjacent Agbledorne sub-zone — which currently retains some tidal connectivity and ecological function.

The strategic implication of climate change as a pressure multiplier is that every year of delay in addressing the structural drivers — hydrological alteration, fuelwood demand, salt pan expansion, nutrient loading — is a year in which the climate envelope for intervention narrows. The pressures already documented at baseline are manageable with the tools the WACA project has designed. Under 1.5 to 2°C of additional warming, the same pressures become progressively harder to manage: the restoration windows are shorter, the degraded soils more resistant to recovery, the oxidising conditions at Ada more entrenched, and the coastal erosion at Keta more advanced. Acting on the pressure drivers now, while the monitoring network still has reference benchmarks to defend and restoration sites that can succeed, is not merely an ecological preference. It is the difference between a programme that can demonstrate additionality against a recoverable baseline and one that is documenting the terminal phase of an ecosystem's carbon storage function.

**Table 9.1 notes:**
*Rating definitions — Low: pressure present but manageable within normal ecosystem buffering capacity; Medium: pressure at levels requiring monitoring and preventive management response; High: pressure at levels causing or imminently threatening measurable ecological damage; Critical: two or more High-rated pressures converging, with compound effects that exceed individual pressure severity.*

Three clusters — Anyanui-Dzita, Patukope-Pute, and Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane — carry a CRITICAL overall risk designation, reflecting the convergence of multiple High-rated pressures that are already producing the most extreme ecological measurements in the monitoring network: the pH 2.7 acid event at Anyanui, the compound vulnerability of near-acid-sulphate soil and near-marine salinity at Pute, and the extraordinary ORP anomaly of 832 to 1,547 mV combined with livestock-driven eutrophication at Wasakuse-Obane. These are not zones where ecological deterioration is a future risk to be monitored. They are zones where it is already measured and active. The management response at each must be proportionate to the severity of the finding: immediate, coordinated, and addressing the structural pressure drivers rather than treating the ecological symptoms.

The Saviatula-Galotse benchmark core and the main lagoon bank corridor at Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga carry HIGH designations — serious but not yet in the acute crisis condition of the Critical zones. The conservation priority at the benchmark core is to maintain its current condition against an advancing cutting front; without the reference benchmark, the entire carbon accounting framework loses its calibration point. The management priority at the main lagoon bank is to prevent the chronic thermal and salinity stress already documented from intensifying toward the threshold conditions that would foreclose mangrove recovery along this corridor.

The Aborlorvi-Norlopi restoration zone and the Azizanya-Ayigbo Volta inflow cluster carry MEDIUM designations — important monitoring attention is required, but the conditions are currently within the range where management intervention can succeed without requiring the emergency response that the Critical zones demand.
