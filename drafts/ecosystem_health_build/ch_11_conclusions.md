# Chapter 11: Conclusions and Management Recommendations

---

## 11.0 Chapter Overview

This chapter closes the Standalone Ecosystem Health Monitoring Report by translating the findings of Chapters 4 through 10 into a structured, institutionally grounded management programme. It is organised in four sections. Section 11.1 states the five core findings that the December 2025 baseline has permanently established — the knowledge claims that define this report's legacy and that must shape all subsequent management decisions at both sites. Section 11.2 organises all management recommendations into a three-tier priority framework: immediate actions required within six months to prevent irreversible ecological and carbon losses; short-term interventions within eighteen months to arrest further degradation; and medium-term structural investments within five years to build the governance and programme infrastructure for sustained ecosystem recovery. Section 11.3 presents the institutional responsibility matrix, naming the lead and supporting institutions, budget source, and timeline for each of the ten recommendations generated by this assessment. Section 11.4 provides the carbon nexus synthesis — the argument for why the findings of this report strengthen, rather than undermine, the investment case for Ghana's blue carbon programme at Keta and Ada-Songor.

The analysis in this chapter draws directly on the parameter-level data established in Chapters 4 through 7, the condition scores assigned in Chapter 8, the pressure diagnosis delivered in Chapter 9, and the monitoring framework set out in Chapter 10. Recommendations are grounded in the field evidence, not in generic ecological management principles. Every recommendation names a responsible institution, specifies an action, identifies a timeline, and states the ecological or carbon outcome it is designed to achieve.

## 11.1 Summary Assessment: What the December 2025 Baseline Reveals

The December 2025 baseline assessment represents the first systematic, multi-parameter ecological characterisation of both the Keta Lagoon Complex and the Ada-Songor Lagoon conducted to the depth and spatial resolution required for a carbon market crediting programme. It produced 80 monitoring stations across the two sites, recording water quality and soil chemistry data that, in combination with the condition assessment framework of Chapter 8, generates the most comprehensive picture of blue carbon ecosystem health in Ghana's coastal Ramsar network to date. What follows are the five findings that stand as the report's knowledge legacy — the conclusions that will remain foundational to site management regardless of what subsequent quarterly rounds reveal.

**Finding 1: The Keta Lagoon Complex retains a high-quality blue carbon core.** The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi benchmark cluster has been confirmed, through direct measurement, to maintain the ecological conditions for active, ongoing blue carbon accumulation. Humus values of 40.6 percent at Galotse and 37.5 percent at Agortoe represent some of the highest documented soil organic carbon concentrations in West Africa's coastal mangrove estate. Water pH of 6.5–7.6, moisture at 40.4 percent, and intact *Rhizophora racemosa* canopy across the cluster confirm that the conditions sustaining this accumulation remain operational. The Salo Permanent Sample Plot is anchored within this benchmark zone, ensuring that Ghana's primary carbon stock reference is located where the ecological case for carbon crediting is strongest. This finding provides the evidential foundation for the entire national blue carbon investment programme: the asset is real, it is measurable, and the conditions producing it are intact. That foundation must now be protected with the same institutional energy that this report has invested in documenting it.

**Finding 2: The Anyanui-Tunu road pH anomaly is the single most urgent management finding in the network — and it must be acted upon before the next dry season.** A water pH of 2.7 at the Tunu Road stations, co-occurring with dead *Rhizophora* canopy and a soil humus concentration of 44.3 percent at the same location, is the combination of measurements that defines a category-one ecological emergency in the Ghana Blue Carbon context. The acid sulphate formation process is confirmed: road infrastructure has blocked tidal exchange, pyrite-bearing sediments have been exposed to atmospheric oxygen, and the sulphuric acid produced by that oxidation is now present in the water column at concentrations more than ten thousand times greater than the productive mangrove pH threshold. The 44.3 percent humus underneath the dead canopy is the largest legacy SOC value in the Keta monitoring network. It is not a stable carbon stock: it is a carbon stock under active acidification threat. Every dry-season cycle without hydrological restoration advances the acid front toward the organic layer. The science is unambiguous. The action required is physical: restore tidal exchange at the Anyanui corridor. This report cannot specify that outcome more precisely than it has been specified in the data. The management system must deliver it.

**Finding 3: The Ada-Songor interior ORP signal is the most significant carbon storage risk in the monitoring network.** Oxidation-reduction potential values of 832 to 1,547 mV in the interior lagoon at Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane represent strongly to extremely oxidising geochemical conditions — conditions in which the chemical environment is actively hostile to the preservation of organic carbon in sediment profiles. A functioning blue carbon system produces ORP values in the range of negative 200 to positive 300 mV in the sediment zone, indicating the reducing, anaerobic conditions under which organic matter is protected from microbial decomposition rather than consumed by it. The Ada interior lagoon is at the opposite end of this scale. The existing carbon stocks at Ada cannot be assumed stable under these geochemical conditions, and the cause — principally the Akosombo Dam's sixty-year reduction of freshwater dilution in the Volta estuary, compounding with seasonal variability and potentially with cattle-related nitrogen loading at the wetland margin — must be investigated to determine whether the ORP anomaly is a structural, permanent state or a manageable, seasonally influenced pressure. The Ada-Songor system's carbon accounting position depends on the answer to this question.

**Finding 4: Pute and Obane together demonstrate that degradation and recovery are both real and simultaneous outcomes at Ada-Songor — and that the difference between them is the presence or absence of investment in management.** The Pute lagoon stations returned the most degraded soil conditions in the monitoring network: pH of 4.78, humus of 4.78 to 9.2 percent, and nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium all at or near trace levels — the soil signature of a system from which decades of tidal disruption by salt pan infrastructure have stripped both organic matter and nutrient cycling function. Three kilometres to the north, the Obane restored mangrove stations tell a contrasting story: humus of 26 percent, lower surface temperature consistent with canopy shading, and stabilised soil pH, all indicating that the restoration investment made at Obane has produced a measurable and ongoing ecological recovery trajectory. The co-existence of these two states within the same lagoon system is one of the most important management messages this report can deliver. Degradation at Ada-Songor is not uniform, inexorable, or ecologically predetermined. Where tidal conditions and organic matter inputs have been partially restored, the ecosystem has responded. Pute can be what Obane is — but only with equivalent investment in hydrological restoration and active management.

**Finding 5: Cattle enrichment at Wasakuse-Obane represents a specific, addressable anthropogenic pressure that existing governance structures have the authority to manage — if given the mandate and the resources to do so.** Soil nitrate of 984 mg/kg at the Wasakuse-ngwa station — nearly double the MRV eutrophication alert threshold — and phosphate of 705 mg/kg and potassium of 1,280 mg/kg at Obane are not random ecological fluctuations. They are the direct chemical signature of concentrated cattle waste deposition at the wetland margin, from herders using the moist lagoon fringe as dry-season grazing land. This pressure is intersectoral — the herding communities and the fishing communities engaging in wetland governance are often distinct — but the pressure itself is spatially bounded, seasonally predictable, and mechanistically understood. It is amenable to the kind of community-level spatial management agreement that CREMA governance structures exist precisely to negotiate and enforce. The data has identified the problem with specificity. The governance infrastructure exists in prototype form. The missing element is the institutional will to deploy that governance infrastructure in service of this specific carbon management outcome.

## 11.2 Priority Recommendations — Tiered by Urgency and Institutional Responsibility

### Tier 1 — Immediate (0–6 months): Actions to prevent irreversible ecological and carbon losses

---

**Recommendation 1.1: Hydrological Investigation at Anyanui Estuary**

*Who:* MLNR / MESTI, commissioning the CSIR-Water Research Institute as the lead technical agency, with Wildlife Division (Keta District) providing field access and site knowledge.

*What:* Commission a hydrological assessment of the Anyanui-Tunu road causeway to diagnose the infrastructure failure responsible for the pH 2.7 acid sulphate conditions. The scope of this assessment must include: (a) a physical survey of all tidal culverts or drainage structures associated with the road crossing, documenting their design, current condition, obstruction status, and hydraulic capacity; (b) installation of temporary water level loggers upstream and downstream of the road crossing to characterise the tidal exchange deficit quantitatively; (c) soil sampling at minimum six locations along a transect from the dead mangrove canopy toward the adjacent intact mangrove zone, testing for acid sulphate soil presence, extent, and depth; (d) preparation of at minimum two design options for restoring tidal exchange — a culvert upgrade option, a sluice gate or adjustable tidal regulator option, and, if neither is structurally viable, identification of an alternative tidal corridor route — with indicative costings and implementation timeline for each. The assessment report must explicitly address the 44.3 percent humus stock at the Tunu Road site and its vulnerability to continued acidification in the absence of intervention.

*When:* Commission issued within 3 months of report receipt; field assessment completed and report delivered within 6 months.

*Carbon outcome:* The Anyanui Combine drone Area of Interest (128 Ha) and the adjacent mangrove fringe remain at confirmed carbon risk until tidal exchange is restored. Without intervention, the acid sulphate chemistry in the water column will penetrate the soil profile across successive dry-season cycles, converting the 44.3 percent humus legacy SOC stock — currently the highest value in the Keta monitoring network — from an endangered but potentially recoverable carbon asset into a confirmed irreversible carbon emission source. This is not a theoretical risk trajectory: it is the established chemical process, already in progress.

---

**Recommendation 1.2: Elevated Monitoring Frequency at Alert-Level Stations**

*Who:* Community Change Agents (CCAs), under direct supervision of Wildlife Division district officers (Keta District for the Anyanui, Dzita, and Wasakuse stations; Ada East District for Pute stations).

*What:* Elevate monitoring cadence from quarterly to monthly at the following confirmed alert-level or rapidly evolving stations: Anyanui-Tunu Road stations (primary parameters: water pH, ORP); Dzita main lagoon stations (primary parameter: salinity, specifically monitoring whether the 40 ppt threshold is being breached or retreating); Pute lagoon stations (primary parameters: soil pH and humus, tracking acid sulphate advance); Wasakuse-ngwa (primary parameter: nitrate, tracking eutrophication trajectory). The standard quarterly monitoring cycle is appropriate for stable or slowly evolving conditions, but it is insufficient for sites where irreversible damage can advance significantly within a three-month interval. Monthly monitoring at these four station zones provides the temporal resolution needed to detect whether conditions are worsening, stabilising, or improving in response to any management interventions initiated under Recommendations 1.1 and 1.3, and generates the early-warning signal that the escalation protocol of Chapter 10 requires.

*When:* Effective from the next scheduled CCA deployment following report receipt. No capital investment is required; the action requires only a reallocation of CCA field time and the issuance of revised monitoring frequency instructions by the Wildlife Division.

*Carbon outcome:* Monthly data from alert stations enables the MRV reporting system to accurately characterise carbon stock stability at sites currently classified as uncertain or at-risk. It also generates the evidence base required to demonstrate intervention effectiveness once Tier 1 management actions are implemented — without monthly monitoring, the management response and the ecological trajectory cannot be linked causally in the carbon accounting record.

---

**Recommendation 1.3: Cattle Exclusion Planning at Wasakuse-Obane**

*Who:* Ada East District Assembly (lead, for spatial planning authority and by-law enforcement mandate); Wildlife Division Ada District (supporting, for wetland governance authority and CREMA integration); community governance structures at Wasakuse and Obane communities, including existing CREMA leadership where constituted.

*What:* Initiate a three-phase process to reduce direct cattle-wetland contact at the Wasakuse-Obane zone: Phase 1 — spatial mapping of the active cattle grazing area and identification of the specific wetland margin sections receiving the highest deposition loads, using GPS records from CCA monitoring visits and community knowledge of seasonal grazing patterns; Phase 2 — formal engagement with herder community leadership in co-management dialogue, presenting the soil chemistry data as evidence of the ecological damage being caused and exploring negotiated spatial management arrangements, including buffer zones between the wetland edge and permitted grazing areas, and seasonal restrictions on grazing near the most sensitive mangrove margins; Phase 3 — design and costing of cattle exclusion fencing for the highest-priority sections of the mangrove margin, integrating the fencing design with any existing or proposed CREMA zone boundary to ensure governance alignment.

*When:* Community mapping and initial herder engagement within 3 months; fencing design and cost estimate completed within 6 months; fencing procurement and installation as a Tier 2 follow-on action.

*Carbon outcome:* Reducing the nitrate loading at Wasakuse from its current 984 mg/kg toward the site-wide background range of 48–73 mg/kg at non-affected stations is the primary carbon management outcome. The secondary outcome is the protection of the Obane recovery trajectory — the 26 percent humus recorded at Obane restored mangrove stations is the result of restoration investment that will be progressively undermined if the nutrient loading from grazing activity continues to accumulate in adjacent soils.

### Tier 2 — Short-Term (6–18 months): Targeted interventions to arrest further degradation

---

**Recommendation 2.1: Tidal Restoration Works — Anyanui Estuary**

*Who:* MLNR as implementing authority; CSIR-Water Research Institute as design lead; Wildlife Division Keta District as site supervisor; contractor procurement through standard MLNR procurement framework.

*What:* Implement the preferred tidal restoration option identified in the Recommendation 1.1 hydrological assessment. This is the single highest-priority capital investment available to the project in terms of the carbon stock value it protects. The preferred option — whether culvert upgrade, sluice gate installation, or alternative tidal corridor — must be designed to restore tidal exchange to the Anyanui creek at a frequency and volume sufficient to submerge the affected intertidal zone for the daily duration required to re-establish the reducing, anaerobic conditions in the soil profile that will halt acid sulphate formation and begin to buffer the pH at the Tunu Road stations back toward the productive range. Post-works monitoring under the Recommendation 1.2 monthly schedule will track whether tidal restoration is producing the expected water chemistry recovery. Minimum monitoring endpoints: pH at the Tunu Road stations rising above 4.0 within six months of restoration works completion; pH approaching 5.5 within twelve months; dead mangrove standing structure survey to determine salvage replanting feasibility once pH has stabilised.

*When:* Works design completed within 6 months of Recommendation 1.1 assessment delivery; procurement and construction within 12–18 months total.

*Carbon outcome:* Tidal restoration at Anyanui is the only intervention that can halt the conversion of the 44.3 percent humus legacy carbon stock from an at-risk asset to a confirmed emission source. No monitoring, no governance action, and no community engagement can substitute for the physical restoration of tidal exchange at this site. The investment required is a direct and measurable protection of the single highest-SOC-value location in the Keta monitoring network.

---

**Recommendation 2.2: Pute Lagoon Restoration Planning and Feasibility**

*Who:* MESTI and MLNR (joint commissioning); Ecological Nest Ltd. or equivalent with specific blue carbon restoration expertise (technical delivery); Ada East District Assembly and Pute community leadership (co-design partners).

*What:* Commission a site-specific restoration feasibility study for the Pute lagoon zone, addressing: (a) soil amelioration options to address the pH 4.78 near-acid-sulphate conditions, including applicability of agricultural lime or calcium carbonate to neutralise acidity in the organic layer without triggering secondary nutrient imbalances; (b) hydrological assessment of tidal inundation potential at Pute, identifying whether bunded salt pan infrastructure is the proximate cause of tidal disruption and whether removal, modification, or bypass of that infrastructure is technically and socially feasible; (c) propagule source mapping from the adjacent Obane restored mangrove stands, which provide the ecologically closest reference community for species composition and planting stock; (d) replanting design for priority areas confirmed as hydroloigcally suitable once soil and tidal conditions have been ameliorated; (e) a community stewardship arrangement framework compatible with the CREMA governance pathway, specifying CCA roles in post-planting monitoring and the governance body responsible for protecting the restored zone from re-encroachment.

*When:* Feasibility study commissioned within 6 months; completed within 12 months; restoration implementation initiated in months 12–18 where conditions allow.

*Carbon outcome:* Successful restoration at Pute — even partial hydrological recovery toward the Obane reference standard of 26 percent humus — represents a measurable carbon stock increase in the most degraded zone of the Ada-Songor monitoring network. It also demonstrates that the Ada-Songor investment can generate demonstrable ecological and carbon outcomes, strengthening the programme's credibility with national and international stakeholders.

---

**Recommendation 2.3: Dzita Community Engagement — Salt Pan Buffer Zone**

*Who:* Wildlife Division Keta District (lead); Dzita CREMA leadership once constituted (co-implementer); Keta Municipal Assembly (spatial planning and land-use authority).

*What:* Engage formally with salt pan operators and the Dzita community governance structures to: (a) quantify the land area at active risk from salt pan encroachment into the adjacent mangrove fringe, using the monitoring network's spatial records and the 40 ppt hypersalinity data as the evidential basis for the engagement; (b) explore the development of a voluntary buffer zone between active salt pan operations and the mangrove fringe, with spatial dimensions sufficient to reduce brine seepage impact on lagoon water chemistry; (c) assess alternative land-use options available to salt pan operators in the buffer zone, including any livelihoods diversification opportunities that the WACA project can facilitate; (d) where community agreement is achievable, formalise the buffer zone within CREMA bylaw development, providing it with governance enforcement authority beyond voluntary compliance.

*When:* Formal community engagement initiated within 6 months; buffer zone design and voluntary agreement documented within 12 months; integration into CREMA bylaws during the standard CREMA formation process.

*Carbon outcome:* Reducing saline intrusion into the Dzita mangrove fringe from 40 ppt toward the 25–30 ppt range associated with higher *Rhizophora racemosa* productivity and carbon accumulation rates is the primary carbon benefit. The secondary benefit is the protection of the long-term viability of the Dzita early action hub designation within the MRV pilot programme.

---

**Recommendation 2.4: Ada ORP Root Cause Investigation**

*Who:* MESTI (commissioning authority); CSIR-Water Research Institute (technical lead); Ghana Water Research Institute as additional technical resource where applicable.

*What:* Commission a targeted investigation into the root cause of the 832–1,547 mV ORP values recorded in the Ada-Songor interior lagoon. The investigation must address three alternative explanatory frameworks: (a) structural geochemical change attributable to the Akosombo Dam's sixty-year reduction of freshwater dilution — if this is the primary driver, the ORP anomaly is a permanent condition requiring the MRV programme to adjust its carbon accounting approach for the Ada interior lagoon zone; (b) seasonal variability — if the ORP anomaly is driven by the dry-season water volume decline in the interior lagoon, the quarterly monitoring programme should detect its attenuation during wet-season rounds, and carbon stock risk may be lower than the baseline data alone suggests; (c) compound effects of cattle nutrient loading on microbial activity and oxygen demand in the overlying water column. The investigation deliverable must conclude with a determination of whether the Ada-Songor ORP anomaly represents a manageable environmental pressure or a structural geochemical condition, and specify the management or carbon accounting implications of each conclusion.

*When:* Investigation commissioned within 6 months; completed and reported within 12–18 months.

*Carbon outcome:* The carbon accounting integrity of the entire Ada-Songor monitoring network depends on resolving this question. If the ORP anomaly is structural and permanent, the MRV system must apply explicit uncertainty adjustments to reported carbon stocks in the Ada interior lagoon zone. If it is seasonal and recoverable, the monitoring data from completed quarterly rounds will provide the evidence base for a more stable accounting position. Either outcome is preferable to the current state of confirmed anomaly without diagnostic explanation.

### Tier 3 — Medium-Term (18 months–5 years): Structural and programme investments

---

**Recommendation 3.1: Fuelwood Reduction Programme — Sustained Investment in Livelihood Alternatives**

*Who:* MLNR and MESTI (policy mandate and programme authority); WACA Project Implementation Unit (implementation coordination and donor reporting); CCAs and CREMAs (community delivery); women's fish-processor associations at Agbatsivi, Galotse, Bomigo, Salo, Dzita, and Sota (primary beneficiaries and co-designers).

*What:* Develop and resource a sustained fuelwood reduction programme targeting the 2.15 percent per year deforestation trajectory at the Keta Lagoon Complex. The programme must address the underlying livelihood economics that make *Rhizophora racemosa* fuelwood the rational choice for fish-processing operations: the economic case for the alternative must be commercially credible to the women fish-processors who drive the demand, not merely technically available. Programme components must include: (a) scaled deployment of improved cookstove technology, with demonstration of equivalent or superior fish quality outcomes to remove the market-price barrier to adoption; (b) sustainable woodlot development at appropriate distances from the lagoon fringe, providing an alternative managed wood supply that reduces pressure on the natural mangrove estate; (c) community co-management agreements within the CREMA framework that establish and enforce sustainable harvesting limits within designated harvest zones of the lagoon fringe; (d) an annual deforestation rate monitoring protocol that links the drone-derived or satellite-derived extent change record to the cookstove adoption and community management data, enabling the programme to demonstrate and report the deforestation reduction achieved.

*When:* Programme design and initial community co-design consultation within 12 months; first-phase implementation of improved cookstove deployment within 18 months; full programme operational within 36 months; annual deforestation rate assessment integrated into MRV Year 2 reporting.

*Carbon outcome:* The 2.15 percent per year deforestation rate is both the primary project additionality lever and the primary credibility risk in the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment. Every percentage point reduction in the annual deforestation rate directly translates into protected carbon stocks — approximately 445 hectares per year at the current rate. A reduction to 1.0 percent per year through programme intervention would protect approximately 230 additional hectares of mangrove per year and generate the demonstrable, measurable avoided-deforestation credit volume that the programme's carbon market position requires.

---

**Recommendation 3.2: Full Four-Quarter Monitoring Cycle and Year 1 Trend Analysis**

*Who:* CCAs under Wildlife Division district supervision (field data collection); MLNR/MESTI national MRV platform team (data aggregation and trend analysis); Ecological Nest Ltd. or equivalent technical partner (annual assessment report).

*What:* Complete four full quarterly monitoring rounds across all 80 stations at Keta and Ada-Songor to establish site-wide seasonal parameter ranges. The December 2025 baseline is a single-point snapshot: it establishes the parameter values at one point in the annual cycle (dry season, December), but it cannot distinguish between stable baseline conditions, cyclically variable conditions, and unidirectional deterioration trends without the comparison data that subsequent quarterly rounds provide. After the completion of Year 1 (four complete rounds), initiate an annual trend analysis that: (a) calculates the mean, minimum, and maximum for each parameter at each station across the four rounds, establishing the seasonal envelope within which each station operates; (b) identifies stations where parameter values are consistently outside the optimal range across all four rounds — confirming structural rather than seasonal stress conditions; (c) assesses whether the alert-level conditions at the Anyanui, Pute, and Wasakuse stations are improving, stable, or worsening; (d) produces an updated condition assessment score for each cluster, reflecting the first full annual data cycle rather than the single-round baseline. The Year 1 trend analysis becomes the primary input for the second-year management priority review, allowing the institutional responsibility matrix in Section 11.3 to be updated with evidence of whether interventions commissioned in Tier 1 and Tier 2 are producing the intended ecological outcomes.

*When:* Quarterly monitoring rounds on schedule through 2026; Year 1 trend analysis completed and reported by Q1 2027; updated condition assessment scores published in the Year 1 Ecosystem Health Annual Report.

*Carbon outcome:* The Year 1 trend analysis establishes whether the carbon stocks at Keta and Ada-Songor are on a stable, improving, or deteriorating trajectory. This determination is the evidence base for the annual MRV report's carbon stock stability declaration and for any adjustment to the uncertainty factors applied in the national carbon inventory's net accounting calculation.

---

**Recommendation 3.3: Integration of Ecosystem Health Condition Scores with the National Carbon Inventory**

*Who:* MLNR (lead, as the national carbon inventory authority); MESTI (co-authority, as the national climate reporting agency); Ecological Nest Ltd. (technical link between ecosystem health assessment and carbon accounting methodology).

*What:* Formally establish the methodological linkage between the ecosystem health condition scores produced by this monitoring programme and the carbon accounting protocol of the National MRV System. The integration must operate through three specific linkages: (a) GOOD-condition clusters at Keta (Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi, Genui) are designated as primary carbon stock reference zones, with their parameter values anchoring the allometric and soil carbon models applied in the national inventory; (b) STRESSED and ALERT clusters at both sites are designated as adjusted-accounting zones, where explicit uncertainty factors reflecting the documented ecological risk are applied to reported carbon stocks — consistent with IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance on reporting stock changes under conditions of active disturbance or chemical stress; (c) the ecosystem health condition score for each cluster, updated annually following each Year-end trend analysis, is reported as a transparency annex to the National MRV annual report submitted to MLNR and MESTI, creating a fully traceable linkage between field-measured ecological condition and the carbon stock claims made in national climate reporting. This integration protects the credibility of Ghana's national carbon accounting position: it demonstrates to international verifiers that the country is monitoring, reporting, and adjusting its carbon inventory in response to field evidence of ecological change, rather than reporting against a static, unreviewed baseline.

*When:* Integration methodology documented within 18 months; first integrated reporting cycle covering ecosystem health and carbon accounting in the Year 1 MRV Annual Report (target: Q2 2027); ongoing annually thereafter.

*Carbon outcome:* A fully integrated ecosystem health and carbon accounting reporting framework is a prerequisite for the long-term credibility of any carbon credit claims generated by the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment under international verification standards. The findings of this report — particularly the ALERT conditions at Anyanui and the ORP anomaly at Ada — make that integration not a theoretical aspiration but an immediate accounting necessity.

## 11.4 The Carbon Nexus: Why Ecosystem Health Determines Carbon Project Viability

There is a version of this report that its findings could be used to tell — a story in which pH 2.7 at the Anyanui road, ORP values above 1,500 mV at the Ada interior, and a 2.15 percent annual deforestation rate are reasons for scepticism about the Ghana blue carbon programme's prospects. That interpretation is available, and some readers will reach for it. It is the wrong reading.

The December 2025 baseline confirms that Ghana's blue carbon programme at Keta and Ada-Songor is operating on ecosystems that are productive and significant — but under measurable, specific, and, in the most important respects, manageable stress. The findings of this report do not undermine the case for carbon investment. They sharpen it. They tell investors, verifiers, and government agencies exactly where the risks are, what management is required, and what the return on intervention will be. A carbon programme that cannot locate its own ecological risks is not a credible programme. A carbon programme that has identified its risks, quantified them, named the institutions responsible for addressing them, and set a timeline for doing so is precisely what the Verified Carbon Standard and the international voluntary carbon market have always required as the minimum condition for permanence crediting. This report makes Ghana's blue carbon programme more credible, not less, because it takes the monitoring obligation seriously enough to report what monitoring actually found.

The five findings of Section 11.1 and the ten recommendations of Section 11.2 are not qualifications on the value of the Keta and Ada-Songor ecosystems. They are the management programme that transforms those ecosystems from a natural asset with unquantified risk into a verified, institutionally governed, and actively protected carbon stock. The Saviatula-Galotse benchmark at 40.6 percent humus is one of the most significant blue carbon soil conditions on the West African coast. The Obane recovery trajectory is direct evidence that investment in restoration produces measurable ecological outcomes. The monitoring network of 80 stations, generating quarterly data from Community Change Agents trained and deployed in December 2025, is the operational infrastructure that makes the asset legible to the national and international institutions that must validate it.

A well-managed, monitored, and restored Keta–Ada ecosystem is not just an ecological asset. It is a credible, verifiable carbon stock that can generate sustainable climate finance for Ghana's coastal communities, deliver on the country's nationally determined contributions under the Paris Agreement, and demonstrate — at a moment when the global blue carbon investment community is looking for evidence that community-based carbon programmes in West Africa can work — that they can. The management recommendations in this chapter are the pathway to that outcome. The institutions named in the responsibility matrix of Section 11.3 are the agents who must walk it.
