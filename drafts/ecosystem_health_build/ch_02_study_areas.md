## 2.1 Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site

The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site (KLRS) is situated in the southern Volta Region, bounded by latitudes 5° 45' N and 6° 05' N and longitudes 0° 50' E and 1° 08' E, bordered to the west by the Volta River, to the south and east by the Gulf of Guinea, and to the north by the Accra-Lomé highway. Designated under the Ramsar Convention in 1992, the site covers 530 km² and stretches approximately 40 kilometres along the coast, encompassing open lagoon, brackish floodplain, marshland, inter-connecting creek systems, and the most extensive contiguous mangrove forest in Ghana.

The defining ecological statistic of the KLRS is unambiguous: the site contains an estimated 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves — approximately 69% of Ghana's entire coastal mangrove resource. This concentration is not a gradual ecological transition from surrounding landscapes but a discrete, immense expanse of mangrove forest extending in large continuous stretches between the main lagoon body and the Volta River estuary. The 2024 geospatial baseline, using Sentinel-2 imagery and a Random Forest classification validated through 319 ground-truthed reference points, confirmed this figure as the most methodologically robust mangrove extent measurement Ghana has produced.

The mangrove community at KLRS is structured by three dominant species occupying distinct hydrological niches: *Rhizophora mangle* on the intertidal fringe, *Avicennia germinans* in elevated back-mangrove zones with higher soil salinity, and *Rhizophora racemosa* in active creek systems where tidal exchange and nutrient flux support the tallest, structurally most complex stands — particularly in the Salo, Galotse, and Agortoe sub-zones confirmed by the December 2025 assessment as the best-developed remaining in the complex.

Despite the scale of this resource, KLRS is under active deforestation pressure at an estimated 2.15% per year. The primary driver is *Rhizophora* harvesting for fish-smoking fuelwood — a practice deeply embedded in the coastal food economy. Women operating fish-smoking kilns in communities such as Agbastivi, Galotse, Bomigo, and Salo depend on mangrove wood for its burning characteristics, creating relentless demand across the most accessible stands. Approximately 70% of natural mangrove cover is degraded or fragmented, with the most severe losses near major settlements documented since 2000. The livelihood system that depends on mangrove-supported fish stocks is simultaneously the mechanism by which those stocks are undermined.

The coastal morphology of KLRS amplifies these pressures. The lagoon is separated from the Gulf of Guinea by a narrow coastal ridge — only 2.5 kilometres at its widest point and 0.92 kilometres at its narrowest — whose structural integrity is essential to the salinity regime and tidal dynamics that support mangrove productivity. Since the Akosombo Dam's construction in 1964, over 90% of the Volta's natural sediment load has been trapped upstream, starving the coastline of the material needed to resist Atlantic erosion. The result is progressive coastal recession, increasing marine salinity intrusion, blockage of inter-connecting channels linking the main lagoon to the Avu lagoon to the north, and disruption of the drainage system that historically regulated lagoon water levels — landscape-level risks operating beyond community control.

### Ecological Sub-Zones and the Monitoring Network

The December 2025 baseline assessment deployed sampling stations across nine ecological sub-zones spanning the full hydrological and pressure gradient of the complex.

**Anyanui** (northwestern estuary) is the Volta-Atlantic interface and the zone of maximum freshwater influence on the KLRS. The baseline recorded an ALERT-level water pH of 2.7 at the Anyanui-Tunu road station alongside observations of dead mangroves, indicating potential acid sulphate soil conditions that represent an active threat to *Rhizophora* root systems. This sub-zone is covered by the Anyanui Combine drone AoI (128 Ha).

**Dzita-Akplotorkor** (western lagoon bank) records the complex's highest salinity (40 ppt) and conductivity (63.1 μS at Dzita), reflecting evaporative concentration in the partially enclosed lagoon. It marks the high-salinity extreme of the KLRS gradient and provides the reference baseline for detecting hydrological deterioration.

**Saviatula** (mid-lagoon transition) connects the Volta-influenced northwest with the more saline interior, resolving the critical midpoint on the KLRS salinity gradient.

**Salo** (northern mangrove corridor) is the northern gateway to the central mangrove core, within the Salo-Agortoe Stretch drone AoI (525 Ha). The Salo mangrove market sampling site reflects this sub-zone's direct interface between community resource extraction and mangrove habitat.

**Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi** is the central mangrove core — the zone where the best-developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands are concentrated, soil humus values are highest (Galotse 40.6%, Agortoe 37.5%), and active organic matter cycling is most evident. This sub-zone sits within the Agatsivi-Agortoe drone AoI (762 Ha) and serves as the primary ecological benchmark against which degraded sub-zones elsewhere in the complex are rated.

**Aborlorvi-Norlopi** (northeastern restoration zone) is the designated priority restoration area, covered by the 52-Ha Aborlorvi-Norlopi drone AoI. Monitoring at this sub-zone is specifically designed to determine whether hydrological conditions — salinity, soil moisture, nutrient availability — are suitable for mangrove re-establishment. It is the primary site where ecosystem health data directly informs restoration investment decisions.

**Genui** (northeast reference zone) provides the ecological baseline for the lagoon's northeastern conditions, positioned away from the dominant fuelwood pressure of the central lagoon communities.

**Afiadenyigba-Havedzi** (southeastern lagoon bank) characterises the southeastern lagoon margin under moderate salinity and periodic inundation, with soil moisture of 26.4% at Afiadenyigba bridge.

**Vodza-Woe-Anloga** (southeastern extent) spans the most densely populated stretch of the KLRS coastal fringe from Vodza through Woe to Anloga, where pH values of 7.8–8.2 reflect increasing marine influence at the lagoon's most intensively used margin.

## 2.2 Ada-Songor Lagoon System

The Ada-Songor Lagoon System, located approximately 100 kilometres west of Keta at the mouth of the Volta River in the Greater Accra Region, comprises the Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. The Songor Lagoon covers 51,133 hectares across low-lying terrain (highest point under 10 metres above sea level), encompassing three structurally distinct habitat types: a brackish lagoon system including the main Songor Lagoon and associated lagoons at Kadza, Kunye, and Truku; a riverine ecosystem comprising the lower Volta River, inter-connected creeks, and floodplains; and a marine coastal fringe. The open water extends approximately 20 kilometres along the coast and 8 kilometres inland behind a narrow sand dune on which communities including Pute and Totokpoe are situated.

In ecological terms, Ada-Songor presents a stark contrast to Keta. The Songor system retains only 465.76 hectares of mangroves — approximately 2% of Ghana's national total — distributed as fragmented, stressed patches across a landscape where salt pan expansion has progressively displaced wetland vegetation. Small mangrove remnants persist in the intermediate salinity zones between hyper-saline ponds and the near-freshwater Volta estuary influence; the near-complete displacement of mangrove forest by industrial and artisanal salt mining is visible in GIS landcover data and confirmed by community testimony from Totope and Obane.

The system's hydrology has been fundamentally altered by the Akosombo Dam, constructed in 1964 approximately 60 kilometres upstream. The dam traps over 90% of the Volta's natural sediment load, dramatically reduces dry-season freshwater discharge to Ada-Songor, and allows marine salinity intrusion to penetrate further inland for longer periods than the pre-dam baseline. Communities in Azizanya and Totope report coastal erosion, lagoon siltation, and declining fish catches as direct consequences — conditions confirmed by the December 2025 monitoring data. The dam's legacy is not a local management problem; it is a national infrastructure constraint whose ecological consequences at Ada-Songor must be tracked systematically and factored into any restoration investment.

The Ada-Songor system also sustains significant cattle grazing pressure on wetland margins, with grazing-associated organic inputs — particularly concentrated at Obane — producing soil nitrate values of 984 mg/kg and phosphate of 705 mg/kg, ten to twenty times higher than comparable stations elsewhere. Commercial salt production, artisanal fishing, and cattle grazing intersect in a complex livelihood economy whose pressures the monitoring network must track and quantify.

### Ecological Sub-Zones and the Monitoring Network

Twenty-three monitoring stations at Ada-Songor are distributed across four ecological sub-zones that capture the full hydrological gradient from near-freshwater Volta influence to hyper-saline interior lagoon.

**Azizanya-Ayigbo** (Volta estuary interface, low salinity baseline) is the freshwater end of the system's ecological gradient — salinity 0.2 ppt, conductivity 2.64–6.23 μS — providing the reference baseline against which salinisation trends in the interior lagoon are detected and quantified through quarterly monitoring at Azizanya-Kewunor bridge and Ayigbo bridge.

**Patukope-Pute** (lagoon intake and critical acid sulphate risk zone) is the hydrologically critical zone where the Songor lagoon receives its primary water inputs from the estuary system. Salinity at Pute lagoon intake reached 28.6 ppt — the highest at Ada-Songor — while soil pH of 4.78 at Pute lagoon indicates acid sulphate conditions comparable in severity to the ALERT-level finding at Keta's Anyanui-Tunu road cluster. Degraded black mangroves persist here under combined pressure from high salinity, acid sulphate soils, and salt pan encroachment — conditions the quarterly monitoring network is designed to track before they become irreversible.

**Futuenya-Big Ada creeks** (transitional hydrological zone) occupies the intermediate zone between Volta freshwater influence and the hyper-saline interior. Dissolved oxygen at Futuenya and Luhue creek bridges (6.09–6.91 mg/l) is among the highest in the Ada-Songor network. Intermediate brackish salinity across this sub-zone represents the ecological space where mangrove regeneration potential is highest if hydrological conditions can be stabilised.

**Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane** (interior lagoon, cattle enrichment hotspot, restoration evidence) is the most complex Ada-Songor sub-zone. At Obane, restoration is underway and measurable: humus content of 26.0% and soil temperature of 34.2°C — lower than 38.4°C at the exposed Pute walkway — indicate canopy re-establishment is already generating the cooler, organic-accumulating soils in which blue carbon sequestration operates. Extreme nutrient enrichment from cattle grazing requires monitoring to determine whether eutrophication is developing that could undermine these restoration outcomes.

## 2.3 Why These Two Sites Together Define Ghana's Blue Carbon Programme

The Keta Lagoon Complex and the Ada-Songor Lagoon System are not two parallel projects operating independently. They are the two sites that together constitute the near-totality of Ghana's functional coastal blue carbon sink.

Keta provides the conservation case: 20,717.95 hectares of existing mangrove stock — 69% of the national resource — whose ecological integrity is the primary determinant of whether Ghana can claim credible, verifiable blue carbon outcomes at national scale. If Keta's ecosystem health deteriorates through acid sulphate expansion, progressive salinisation, fuelwood-driven deforestation, or coastal destabilisation, the credibility of Ghana's carbon accounting weakens and the livelihoods of communities dependent on lagoon fish stocks and mangrove ecosystem services erode irreversibly. The ALERT condition documented at Anyanui-Tunu road in December 2025 is the first quantitative evidence that specific zones within this irreplaceable asset are approaching those thresholds.

Ada-Songor provides the restoration case: a degraded, internationally recognised system where 465.76 hectares of mangrove remnants persist, where restoration outcomes are already measurable at Obane, and where the drivers of degradation — dam-altered hydrology, salt pan encroachment, cattle grazing — are documented with sufficient precision to inform targeted intervention. Ada-Songor is where Ghana demonstrates that blue carbon restoration, not only conservation, is achievable and verifiable at programme scale.

Together, these two sites are where Ghana must demonstrate, monitor, and verify blue carbon outcomes to fulfil NDC commitments, meet Ramsar reporting obligations, and attract international climate finance. The ecosystem health condition of both sites — assessed through 387 water samples, 168 soil samples, and 65 georeferenced station positions collected in December 2025 — is the ecological foundation on which the credibility of every carbon market claim, governance arrangement, and community livelihood co-benefit in this programme rests.
