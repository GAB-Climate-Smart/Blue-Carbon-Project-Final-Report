# Chapter 3: Field Methods and Monitoring Design

---

## 3.1 Survey Design Rationale

The December 2025 baseline assessment of the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site and the Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve was not designed as a one-off environmental snapshot. It was designed as the founding data layer of a multi-year monitoring network — a permanent, georeferenced, replicable dataset whose value compounds with each subsequent sampling round. Every methodological choice made in December 2025, from the spatial distribution of stations to the selection of instruments to the training of Community Change Agents, was made with that long-term function in mind. The design philosophy is therefore not simply what was done and how, but why the choices were made in the way they were, and how those choices ensure that data collected in the third year of the programme will be directly comparable, ecologically meaningful, and management-actionable when placed alongside data collected in the first.

The foundational design choice was the adoption of a supervised, GIS-based stratified random sampling framework. A supervised GIS map of both study areas was prepared in advance of the field survey, classifying the landscape into ecologically distinct units — open lagoon, brackish creek systems, active mangrove forest, degraded mangrove and fringe habitat, proposed restoration zones, and upland terrestrial margins. Sampling stations were then assigned within each of these units using a stratified approach, meaning that the spatial distribution of stations was constrained to ensure representative coverage of each ecological stratum rather than allowing purely random placement, which would statistically under-sample some strata and over-sample others.

The ecological rationale for stratification is specific to the character of these sites. The Keta Lagoon Complex covers 530 km² and encompasses ecological conditions that vary dramatically across short distances. The northwestern section near Anyanui, where the Volta River's historical influence is strongest, exhibits near-freshwater salinity values, distinct pH anomalies, and stands of dead and dying mangroves at the Volta-Atlantic interface. Sixty kilometres east, the main lagoon body near Dzita and Akplotorkor reaches salinities of 40 parts per thousand under intense evaporative concentration during the dry season — conditions that are essentially the ecological opposite. The central mangrove core at Salo, Galotse, Agortoe, and Agbastivi represents a third distinct condition: well-developed, actively functioning *Rhizophora racemosa* stands with high soil humus content and the deepest organic carbon stocks in the complex. The proposed restoration zones at Aborlorvi and Norlopi constitute a fourth stratum — ecologically depleted areas whose current water and soil chemistry determines whether mangrove re-establishment is feasible. A simple random design applied to the 530 km² complex would assign stations in rough proportion to area; because the open lagoon surface is vast and ecologically uniform relative to the narrow creek systems and fringe habitats where the most critical ecological processes occur, a random design would vastly over-represent open water and under-represent the ecologically sensitive habitat types where monitoring information matters most for management.

The stratified design ensures that each of the nine ecological clusters at Keta and each of the four clusters at Ada-Songor are sampled with the spatial density required to characterise conditions within that cluster reliably. Within each cluster, station placement was randomised to avoid systematic spatial bias while ensuring that the full range of micro-habitats — shaded canopy interior, canopy edge, open water, sediment surface, terrestrial fringe — was represented in the data.

Station positioning was also conducted in direct consultation with the Community Change Agents (CCAs) stationed in each community cluster. CCAs provided practical knowledge of access routes, tidal conditions, seasonal inundation patterns, and local ecological history that GIS analysis alone cannot capture. The stations mostly correspond to labelled sites on the pre-prepared GIS map, with field adjustments made where CCA knowledge indicated that a nominally assigned position was inaccessible, ecologically unrepresentative, or duplicated another station. This integration of community knowledge into station positioning is not merely operationally pragmatic — it is the mechanism by which the station network reflects the actual ecological geography of the landscape as understood by the people who work within it daily.

The final station register comprises 65 permanent georeferenced positions — 57 covering the nine clusters of the Keta Lagoon Complex and 23 covering the four clusters of the Ada-Songor system, with stations distributed across both water and soil sampling locations within each cluster. These 65 stations constitute the permanent baseline network. At each station, water quality measurements, soil measurements, or both were collected depending on the habitat type at that position: aquatic and intertidal stations received water quality measurement using the Apera SX751 multiparameter device; terrestrial and fringe stations received soil measurement using the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD; and stations located at the aquatic-terrestrial interface received measurements from both instruments. The result was 387 individual water quality measurements — 270 at Keta and 117 at Ada — and 168 individual soil measurements — 140 at Keta and 28 at Ada. These are not 387 and 168 unique locations; some stations contributed multiple measurements across repeat visits or from paired instruments. The 65 georeferenced positions are the spatial anchors; the individual measurement totals reflect the number of valid instrument readings recorded.

The permanent registration of these 65 positions is what transforms a field survey into a monitoring network. In subsequent quarterly survey rounds and annual re-assessment cycles, field teams will return to these exact coordinates and repeat the same measurements under the same protocol. The ecological trends that emerge from successive rounds of data at the same positions — rising or falling salinity, shifting soil pH, changes in humus content, decline or recovery of dissolved oxygen — are the information that adaptive management requires. Without positional permanence, each survey round produces a new snapshot with no baseline comparison. With it, each round produces a data point in a time series whose ecological meaning accumulates with each addition.

## 3.2 Water Quality Instrumentation — Apera SX751

Water quality data was collected at all aquatic and intertidal stations using the Apera SX751 multiparameter water testing device, a professional-grade handheld instrument designed for in-situ measurement of physico-chemical water properties. The device reads all parameters simultaneously from a single deployment, providing a real-time, integrated characterisation of water conditions at the moment of measurement without requiring sample extraction, transport, or laboratory processing. This in-situ methodology is methodologically appropriate for the wetland habitats of the Keta and Ada-Songor systems: many of the parameters being measured — particularly dissolved oxygen and oxidation-reduction potential — are sensitive to sample handling and storage and are most accurately recorded at the point of collection. The Apera SX751 was used across all 387 measurement events at both sites during December 2025.

The instrument records eight parameters that, taken together, provide a comprehensive description of the physico-chemical state of the water column at each station. Each parameter has a defined accuracy specification and a specific ecological interpretation within the context of a coastal mangrove wetland subject to the pressures documented at Keta and Ada-Songor. The parameters and their management significance are described below.

**pH (accuracy ±0.01)** measures the hydrogen ion concentration of the water, expressed on a logarithmic scale from 0 (strongly acidic) to 14 (strongly alkaline), with 7.0 as chemically neutral. In coastal wetland systems, pH governs the bioavailability of all major nutrients — nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium — by controlling the chemical speciation of dissolved compounds at the soil-water interface. pH also determines the biochemistry of organic matter decomposition: aerobic bacterial decomposition proceeds most efficiently at pH 6.5–8.5, while strongly acidic conditions suppress microbial activity and can shift decomposition pathways in ways that affect carbon cycling and nutrient export. For mangrove systems specifically, water pH is a critical indicator of acid sulphate risk: soils containing iron sulphide minerals (pyrite), which are common in tidal wetland sediments, can generate highly acidic conditions when exposed through drainage or erosion. The pH anomaly of 2.7 recorded at the Anyanui-Tunu road station during the December 2025 baseline — alongside observations of dead and dying mangroves at that location — constitutes an active monitoring alert requiring quarterly follow-up to determine whether acid sulphate conditions are spatially contained or expanding. Routine pH monitoring across all stations distinguishes stations within the optimal mangrove growth range of 6.5–8.0 from those approaching or exceeding acid or alkaline thresholds that signal deteriorating conditions for mangrove regeneration and carbon sequestration.

**Temperature (accuracy ±0.1°C)** records the in-situ water column temperature at the time of measurement. Water temperature directly determines the solubility of dissolved gases — particularly dissolved oxygen, whose saturation concentration decreases as temperature rises — and governs the metabolic rates of all aquatic organisms. In shallow coastal lagoons such as those at Keta and Ada-Songor, water temperature responds rapidly to solar radiation and air temperature, particularly in the open lagoon sections where depth is limited and thermal mass is low. Temperature variation also determines the rate of organic matter decomposition in surface sediments: warmer water temperatures accelerate microbial activity, increasing biological oxygen demand and potentially contributing to hypoxic conditions in sheltered sub-zones. The canopy cooling effect of well-developed mangrove stands — measurable as a systematic temperature differential between shaded intra-canopy stations and adjacent open water stations — is directly observable in the temperature data from the December 2025 baseline and provides evidence of the micro-climatic ecosystem service that mangrove cover provides. Afternoon peak temperatures of 31.19°C to 37.3°C recorded at open water stations at Keta during the December 2025 survey, against lower values in shaded creek environments, illustrate this gradient and its implications for aquatic habitat quality. Temperature data provides the seasonal context within which all other parameters are interpreted: elevated salinity or depressed dissolved oxygen readings are ecologically manageable if associated with dry-season temperature peaks but may signal structural deterioration if they persist under cooler wet-season conditions.

**Electrical conductivity (µS/cm)** measures the water's capacity to conduct electrical current, which is directly proportional to the total concentration of dissolved ions — primarily sodium, chloride, calcium, magnesium, sulphate, and bicarbonate — in the water column. Conductivity is one of the most sensitive indicators of hydrological change in a coastal lagoon system, because the ionic concentration of lagoon water reflects the balance between freshwater inflow, which dilutes the ion pool, and evaporation and marine intrusion, which concentrate it. At Keta, the highest conductivity of 63.1 µS was recorded at Dzita, a station at the main lagoon body where the combination of high salinity, reduced freshwater inflow, and intense dry-season evaporation concentrates dissolved ions. The lowest values were recorded at stations under freshwater influence during seasonal rainfall events. At Ada-Songor, the dramatic conductivity contrast between the near-Volta stations at Azizanya (6.23 µS) and Ayigbo (2.64 µS) and the interior lagoon stations at Pute (high) and Togbloku directly traces the freshwater-saline gradient that defines the ecological structure of the Songor system. Because conductivity integrates the contributions of all dissolved ion sources, sustained upward trends in conductivity at stations previously under freshwater influence — particularly at the Anyanui stations at Keta — constitute one of the earliest quantitative signals of progressive hydrological deterioration linked to reduced Volta River discharge. This makes conductivity one of the highest-priority trend indicators in the quarterly monitoring programme.

**Salinity (ppt)** is derived from conductivity measurements and represents the total concentration of dissolved salts in the water, expressed as parts per thousand. Salinity is the most ecologically informative single parameter in the entire monitoring network, because it is the primary determinant of mangrove species composition, individual tree growth rates, and the carrying capacity of the coastal wetland for blue carbon accumulation. Each mangrove species has a defined salinity tolerance range: *Avicennia germinans* tolerates conditions up to approximately 90 ppt but grows optimally at intermediate salinities; *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Rhizophora mangle* are characteristic of intermediate salinities (5–35 ppt) and are the species that dominate the most carbon-productive stands in the Keta complex. At Keta, the baseline salinity gradient ranges from 4.3 ppt at stations under freshwater influence to 40 ppt at Dzita — a range that encompasses both the freshwater-influenced conditions suitable for mixed mangrove communities and the hypersaline conditions that progressively exclude all tree species and support only salt-tolerant herbaceous vegetation. At Ada-Songor, the contrast between near-freshwater Volta influence (0.2 ppt at Futue creek) and hypersaline interior lagoon conditions (28.6 ppt at Pute lagoon) maps the ecological corridor within which mangrove remnants persist — the intermediate salinity zone between 3 and 20 ppt where *Rhizophora* and *Avicennia* can establish. The fundamental management question at both sites — whether the ecological space available for mangroves is expanding, contracting, or stable — is answered directly by tracking salinity trends across the monitoring network. A sustained increase in minimum salinity at formerly freshwater-influenced stations signals hydrological deterioration; a narrowing of the intermediate-salinity zone at Ada signals salt pan encroachment or reduced Volta River freshwater delivery; and localised salinity spikes at specific stations may indicate tidal channel changes, drainage blockages, or the consequences of infrastructure modification. Salinity monitoring is not a background parameter — it is the primary ecological indicator of whether the conditions for blue carbon conservation and restoration are improving or worsening.

**Total Dissolved Solids (TDS, ppm)** quantifies the aggregate mass concentration of all dissolved material in the water column, including both inorganic ions and dissolved organic compounds. TDS is closely correlated with conductivity but incorporates contributions from dissolved organic matter — including humic substances from mangrove leaf litter decomposition, algal exudates, and suspended fine particles that pass through filter membranes — making it sensitive to changes in biological productivity and organic loading that conductivity alone does not capture. At Keta, TDS peaked at 88.1 ppm at Dzita and declined to approximately 100 ppm at stations under rainfall influence. At Ada-Songor, TDS reached 57.3 ppm at Pute lagoon and fell to 1.95 ppm at Ayigbo bridge, tracking the salinity gradient closely but with station-specific variation that reflects local organic loading. TDS provides a cross-check on conductivity measurements and, where the two diverge significantly, indicates either instrument calibration issues or significant organic matter loading that warrants investigation. Elevated TDS values in creek and canal stations that are not matched by equivalent conductivity increases may indicate eutrophication events in which dissolved organic matter from agricultural runoff or domestic waste is the dominant contributor rather than marine salt intrusion.

**Dissolved oxygen (DO, mg/L, optical sensor)** is the single parameter most directly indicative of aquatic ecosystem health and biological vitality. Dissolved oxygen sustains the aerobic respiration of all fish, benthic invertebrate, and macrophyte communities; supports the aerobic decomposition pathways that regulate nutrient cycling in surface sediments; and maintains the oxidative conditions that govern the redox chemistry of the soil-water interface. The Apera SX751 measures dissolved oxygen using an optical (luminescent) sensor rather than the polarographic electrodes used in older instruments, providing a faster response time, lower maintenance requirement, and greater accuracy in the low-oxygen conditions characteristic of shallow tropical lagoons with high biological oxygen demand. The alert threshold for ecological concern in wetland management is 3.0 mg/L; sustained values below this threshold indicate hypoxic conditions that cause fish mortality, stress mangrove roots, and may trigger anaerobic decomposition processes that release methane and dissolved sulphides. During the December 2025 baseline at Keta, dissolved oxygen values at stations including Anyanui, Agbodome, Akplotorkor, Saviatula, and Salo were relatively high, consistent with shallow water depth, wind-driven mixing, and the photosynthetic activity of surrounding mangrove and aquatic plant communities. The value of 13.23 mg/l recorded at Dzita alongside that station's high salinity and conductivity reflects the complex relationship between thermal stratification, biological oxygen demand, and mixing in the partially enclosed main lagoon body. At Ada-Songor, DO values of 5.51 to 7.13 mg/l across most stations, with peak values of 6.09 and 6.91 mg/l at the Futue and Luhue creek bridges, reflected the benefit of open lagoon conditions, wind exposure, and the absence of significant algal mat growth. Monitoring dissolved oxygen quarterly captures the seasonal variation in oxygen availability that determines whether shallow lagoon habitats support productive aquatic communities year-round or experience seasonal hypoxic episodes that reduce ecological carrying capacity and carbon storage potential.

**Oxidation-Reduction Potential (ORP, mV)** quantifies the electrochemical potential of the water to act as either an oxidising or a reducing agent, directly reflecting the presence and relative concentration of dissolved oxygen and reduced chemical species (hydrogen sulphide, ferrous iron, methane precursors, and others). ORP is the parameter most directly related to the carbon preservation function of mangrove wetlands: strongly reducing conditions (negative or low positive ORP) indicate the dominance of anaerobic processes, which suppress microbial decomposition of organic matter and are the proximate mechanism by which mangrove soils accumulate blue carbon at rates that exceed those of most other ecosystem types. Higher positive ORP values indicate greater oxygen availability, aerobic decomposition, and progressively greater carbon mineralisation risk. At Keta, the relatively moderate ORP of 160 mV recorded at Dzita alongside its high salinity reflects the waterlogged conditions typical of mangrove habitats, where anoxic micro-zones within the sediment column maintain reducing chemistry even when surface water contains dissolved oxygen. At Ada-Songor, the dramatic ORP gradient — from 11 to 76.6 mV at the near-freshwater Azizanya and Ayigbo stations to 832 to 1,236 mV in the interior lagoon and creek stations — signals a landscape where oxidation conditions vary by over two orders of magnitude across short distances, creating a complex mosaic of carbon-preserving anaerobic zones and carbon-releasing aerobic zones whose spatial distribution shifts with the seasons. The management interpretation of ORP data is direct: stations consistently recording low or negative ORP in waterlogged mangrove habitats are functioning as carbon sinks; stations recording high positive ORP in nominally mangrove-eligible habitats indicate that drainage, desiccation, or hydrological disruption has exposed organic soils to oxidation and active carbon loss. ORP trend analysis across the quarterly monitoring network is therefore one of the most sensitive direct indicators of whether the carbon storage function of these wetlands is being maintained or eroded.

**Resistivity (KΩ·cm)** is the inverse of electrical conductivity and provides a complementary characterisation of water chemistry that, when interpreted alongside conductivity, allows the source of dissolved ions to be distinguished. In high-salinity, marine-influenced water, both conductivity and resistivity values follow predictable relationships determined by the ionic composition of sea water. Where resistivity diverges from what conductivity values would predict — either higher or lower than expected — it indicates that the dissolved ion pool is being altered by contributions from sources other than marine salt intrusion: industrial effluents, domestic waste discharges, acid drainage from exposed acid sulphate soils, or agricultural runoff. The high resistivity value of 15.8 KΩ recorded at Dzita, despite that station's elevated conductivity and salinity, reflects the specific ionic composition of the Keta lagoon's hypersaline water body, which is dominated by evaporatively concentrated marine salts rather than pollution inputs. Tracking resistivity alongside conductivity over successive quarterly monitoring rounds provides the analytical tool for distinguishing between ecologically expected salinity-driven changes in water chemistry and anthropogenic pollution-driven changes — a distinction with direct management consequences, since the former may require hydrological intervention at the watershed scale while the latter requires community engagement and enforcement action at the point source.

## 3.3 Soil Quality Instrumentation — Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD

Soil quality data was collected at all terrestrial and ecotone stations using the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD Ver 1.0, a professional handheld quick-test platform designed for rapid in-situ measurement of soil chemical and biological indicators. Like the Apera SX751 for water, the 4002-BXSZD delivers results in the field without requiring sample extraction and laboratory processing, making it operationally appropriate for the remote, access-constrained conditions of mangrove fringes and wetland interiors that characterise many of the higher-priority monitoring stations at both sites. The instrument records seven parameters that, together, provide a characterisation of soil chemistry, organic carbon status, and biological activity at each station. Soil measurements were conducted at a total of 168 stations during December 2025 — 140 at Keta across the nine cluster areas and 28 at Ada-Songor across the four cluster areas. These totals reflect the terrestrial and ecotone portion of the full station network; stations positioned exclusively over open water received water quality measurement only.

**Nitrate / N (mg/kg)** is the primary bioavailable form of nitrogen in most soil systems and the dominant inorganic nitrogen source for mangrove growth and the microbial communities that drive organic matter decomposition in wetland soils. The nitrogen cycle in mangrove ecosystems is tightly coupled to soil oxygen conditions: under anaerobic, waterlogged conditions, denitrification by anaerobic bacteria converts nitrate to nitrogen gas, reducing the pool of plant-available nitrogen; under aerobic conditions, nitrification converts ammonium to nitrate, making nitrogen more mobile and potentially more subject to leaching. At Keta, nitrate values ranged from 1 to 23 mg/kg in upland stations with drier, more aerobic conditions to 37 to 73 mg/kg in the moist, waterlogged stations typical of active mangrove habitat, where the combination of leaf litter decomposition, root turnover, and microbial nitrogen cycling maintains a larger plant-available nitrogen pool. At Ada-Songor, the extreme value of 984 mg/kg recorded at Wasakusengwa, driven by cattle grazing and concentrated organic waste deposition, represents a nitrogen loading level that constitutes a eutrophication risk and a signal of direct anthropogenic pressure at that station. Contrast this with only 3 mg/kg at Pute lagoon, a degraded station where the absence of mangrove cover and continuous litter inputs has depleted the soil nitrogen pool. Monitoring nitrate quarterly provides direct evidence of whether agricultural runoff, domestic waste discharges, or cattle grazing is increasing nutrient loading at stations near community settlements or land-use pressure zones — an eutrophication trajectory that at elevated concentrations promotes algal bloom development, oxygen depletion, and the shift from carbon-accumulating to carbon-emitting soil conditions.

**Phosphate / P (mg/kg)** drives primary productivity in phosphorus-limited tropical coastal soils and plays a critical role in mangrove root growth, propagule germination, and seedling establishment. In mangrove wetlands, phosphorus cycling is strongly controlled by redox conditions: under reducing, anaerobic conditions, phosphate is released from iron-phosphate complexes in the sediment and becomes more bioavailable; under oxidising conditions, phosphate is adsorbed onto iron oxides and becomes less accessible to plant roots. This redox-phosphorus coupling means that phosphate levels reflect both the nutrient status of the soil and its hydrology. At Keta, phosphate values in moist mangrove-proximate soils ranged from 52 to 103 mg/kg, substantially higher than the 1 to 32 mg/kg recorded in upland stations, reflecting the phosphorus enrichment of waterlogged soils near active leaf litter fall and root zone decomposition. At Ada-Songor, the exceptional phosphate value of 705 mg/kg at Obane — an active restoration site also subject to heavy cattle grazing — represents phosphorus enrichment at a level well above what mangrove biological processes alone can produce, indicating that human and livestock pressure is fundamentally altering the soil chemistry at that station. Phosphate trend monitoring distinguishes between the natural phosphorus dynamics of recovering mangrove soils — where values are expected to increase as litter input and root biomass build over successive seasons — and non-natural phosphorus loading from anthropogenic sources, which indicates that management intervention is required to prevent eutrophication from undermining the restoration investment.

**Potassium / K (mg/kg)** is essential for osmotic regulation, stomatal function, photosynthetic efficiency, and cellular enzyme activation in all wetland vegetation, with particular importance for mangroves operating under saline conditions where osmotic stress places elevated demands on potassium-mediated ion transport. In the soil, potassium is released during the decomposition of organic matter — leaf litter, roots, and woody material — making soil potassium content an indirect indicator of the rate of organic matter cycling and, by extension, of soil organic carbon dynamics. At Keta, potassium ranged from 2 to 65 mg/kg in upland stations to 78 to 201 mg/kg in moist mangrove-associated soils, with a mean of 56.25 mg/kg across the Keta sampling area and the highest concentrations recorded at Agbastivi and Galotse — precisely the sub-zones identified as supporting the best-developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands in the complex, where active litter fall and root turnover maintain elevated soil potassium through continuous organic matter decomposition. At Ada-Songor, the extreme contrast between 1,280 mg/kg at Obane — enriched by concentrated cattle dung — and 7 mg/kg at Pute lagoon — a nutrient-depleted, highly degraded station — reflects two extreme positions on a spectrum from nutrient enrichment to nutrient exhaustion that characterises the range of ecological conditions across the Ada network. Tracking potassium trends over successive monitoring rounds provides indirect evidence of whether soil organic matter cycling is accelerating — consistent with vegetation recovery and increasing carbon inputs — or declining — consistent with continued habitat degradation and reduced organic carbon accumulation.

**Soil pH** is the master chemical control on nutrient availability, decomposition rate, and microbial community structure in wetland soils, and is the most direct soil-based indicator of whether the conditions for blue carbon preservation are being maintained. Acidic, anaerobic soils with pH between approximately 5.5 and 7.0 suppress the aerobic microbial decomposition pathways that mineralise organic carbon, creating the chemical environment in which organic matter accumulates over decades and centuries to form the deep, carbon-dense soils characteristic of mature mangrove systems. The critical threshold is pH 4.5: below this value, acid sulphate conditions become chemically active, releasing aluminium ions toxic to mangrove roots, generating dissolved sulphides that stress aquatic communities, and triggering iron chemistry changes that can dramatically alter phosphate availability. At Keta, soil pH ranged from 5.55 to 7.0 across the sampling area, with the highest values at the well-developed mangrove stations in the Salo and Galotse corridor — within the range considered optimal for the nutrient cycling processes that sustain mangrove productivity. At Ada-Songor, the range of 5.57 to 6.84 at most stations and an exceptional low of 4.78 at Pute lagoon aligns with the acid sulphate risk already flagged from the pH 2.7 water measurement at Anyanui-Tunu road in Keta, and indicates that drainage-exposed, degraded soils at Pute are approaching the threshold below which chemical conditions become actively hostile to mangrove re-establishment. Quarterly soil pH monitoring provides the trend data to determine whether these acid-trending stations are continuing to acidify — requiring management intervention — or whether natural processes of waterlogging and organic matter accumulation are buffering pH towards conditions more conducive to recovery.

**Moisture content (%)** is the foundational physical parameter linking soil chemistry to ecosystem function, and the most direct indicator of whether the soil carbon preservation mechanism of anaerobic suppression is operational. Waterlogged soils with high moisture are anaerobic, creating the reducing conditions in which microbial decomposers cannot access the oxygen required for aerobic respiration, causing organic matter to accumulate rather than mineralise. Drained, aerobic soils with low moisture allow oxygen to penetrate the pore space, activating aerobic decomposition that converts stored organic carbon to carbon dioxide and progressively depletes the soil carbon stock. This moisture-carbon relationship is the mechanistic basis of blue carbon: mangrove wetlands sequester carbon at high rates because their waterlogged soils are continuously anaerobic, and they lose carbon when drainage or dewatering events expose those soils to aerobic conditions. At Keta, moisture levels were systematically higher at stations physically proximate to water bodies — Aborlorvi at 25.4%, Afiadenyigba bridge at 26.4%, Genui at 38.7%, and Galotse at 40.4% — and lower at upland and ridge stations where soil drainage is greater. At Ada-Songor, moisture ranged from 21.5 to 29.45% across most stations, with peak values at Pute lagoon intake (30.6%) and Obane (25.1%), consistent with those stations' position within active lagoon and mangrove habitat. Tracking moisture content quarterly captures the critical seasonal variation in soil hydrological state: during the wet season, soil moisture is uniformly high and carbon preservation conditions are maximised; during the dry season, some stations will experience significant moisture drawdown that temporarily exposes soil organic matter to aerobic oxidation. The amplitude of this seasonal fluctuation — how far moisture drops during the dry season and how quickly it recovers with wet season onset — is a direct indicator of hydrological stability and resilience at each station. Stations where dry-season moisture loss is increasing across successive annual monitoring cycles may indicate drainage deterioration, vegetation thinning, or altered groundwater hydrology requiring management response.

**Humus content (%)** quantifies the stable, slowly decomposable fraction of soil organic matter — the dark, chemically complex material produced by the long-term microbial breakdown and humification of plant residues under anaerobic conditions. Humus is the primary repository of soil organic carbon in mangrove systems and the material substance of the blue carbon stock that the monitoring programme is designed to protect and, in degraded areas, to restore. High humus content is the product of decades or centuries of organic matter accumulation under continuously waterlogged, anaerobic, biologically productive conditions; it cannot be rapidly rebuilt once lost. At Keta, humus content ranged from 14.6 to 26.2% across most sampling stations, with the highest values recorded at Tunu (44.3%), Galotse (40.6%), and Agortoe (37.5%) — stations where active leaf fall, dead mangrove material, and long-term organic matter cycling under anaerobic conditions have built up substantial organic soil horizons. These stations represent the most carbon-dense soils in the Keta network and are the primary conservation priority: maintaining the hydrological and ecological conditions that sustain these high humus values is the proximate management objective at those stations. At Ada-Songor, the Obane restored mangrove site recorded humus of 26.0% — the highest at that site — consistent with active organic matter accumulation in a recovering stand where mangrove litter inputs are beginning to rebuild the depleted soil organic matter. The exceptionally low humus of 4.78% at Pute lagoon reflects severely depleted soil organic matter, the combined result of historic drainage, salt pan encroachment, and the prolonged absence of mangrove canopy litter inputs that are the primary source of humified organic matter in these systems. Declining humus trends at any station across successive monitoring rounds constitute the most direct observable signal of ongoing soil carbon loss and must trigger an immediate field investigation to identify the cause and initiate a management response before the depletion becomes irreversible.

**Soil temperature (°C)** is the master variable governing the rate of microbial decomposition and is therefore, alongside moisture content, the most direct soil-based indicator of whether organic carbon is stable or being actively mineralised. The Arrhenius relationship between temperature and microbial metabolic rate means that small increases in soil temperature produce disproportionate increases in decomposition rate: a 10°C increase approximately doubles the rate of aerobic microbial respiration. In mangrove wetlands, soil temperature is controlled by a combination of solar radiation input, soil moisture (evaporative cooling), and most significantly by the shading and microclimate modulation provided by the mangrove canopy. The management implication of this relationship is important: mangrove canopy cover is not only a habitat and carbon stock in its own right but is an active contributor to the carbon preservation conditions of the soil beneath it. Removal of canopy cover — through fuelwood harvesting, storm damage, or die-back — exposes previously shaded soils to direct solar heating, accelerating decomposition and initiating a positive feedback loop in which carbon loss from exposed soils produces conditions less favourable to mangrove regeneration. At Ada-Songor, soil temperature reached 38.4°C at the exposed Pute walkway station under minimal vegetation cover, compared to 34.2°C at the Obane restored mangrove site where canopy establishment is already generating measurable thermal buffering. This 4.2°C temperature differential across a relatively small landscape area illustrates the canopy cooling effect in quantitative terms and provides evidence that the restoration at Obane is generating the microclimate conditions within which carbon accumulation in restored soils will proceed. Tracking soil temperature quarterly at both sites provides a sensitive indicator of vegetation condition changes and an early warning of canopy loss before those losses become visible in remotely sensed imagery.

## 3.4 Positioning and Station Registration

All 65 sampling station positions were georeferenced using a Garmin Inreach Explorer satellite communicator and GPS unit, a device selected for its combination of high-accuracy positional recording, satellite messaging capability, and durability under field conditions in remote, water-accessible terrain. The Garmin Inreach Explorer records positions using the GPS constellation and, where signal conditions allow, GLONASS augmentation, providing positional accuracy sufficient for repeat-visit identification of the exact measurement point at each station. All coordinates were recorded at the time of measurement and are documented in the station registers presented in Tables 1 through 4 of the source assessment report.

The permanent registration of these positions is the single most important feature of the monitoring network for ensuring long-term data comparability. In quarterly and annual subsequent surveys, field teams will use the registered coordinates — loaded as waypoints on the same model of GPS device — to navigate to within centimetres of the original measurement point. This spatial precision ensures that measurements taken in 2026, 2027, and subsequent years are genuinely comparable to the December 2025 baseline: they reflect conditions at the same point in the landscape under the same seasonal conditions, not conditions at a nearby but ecologically distinct location. Without this positional permanence, trend analysis is methodologically invalid, because observed changes in parameter values cannot be distinguished from the natural spatial variability of the landscape.

The station coordinates also define the spatial architecture of the monitoring network in terms that are directly compatible with the GIS infrastructure of the wider WACA Blue Carbon Assessment programme. Station positions are expressed in decimal degrees to five significant figures, compatible with the coordinate reference system used for the satellite imagery analysis, drone survey Area of Interest boundaries, and Permanent Sample Plot positions documented elsewhere in the programme. This multi-layer spatial consistency allows ecosystem health monitoring data to be integrated directly with remotely sensed landcover change data, drone-derived vegetation structure measurements, and plot-level carbon stock assessments — enabling the multi-evidence synthesis required to distinguish between instrument-detected changes in water and soil chemistry and camera-detected changes in vegetation structure, and to determine whether they are consistent with a common underlying ecological cause.

## 3.5 Seasonal Context

The December 2025 baseline survey was conducted during what is nominally designated as the dry season in Ghana's coastal ecological calendar — the period from approximately November through February when the intertropical convergence zone has retreated northward, the harmattan influence is strongest, freshwater inflow from the Volta catchment is at its annual minimum, and evaporation rates from the open lagoon surface are at their seasonal peak. This seasonal timing was deliberate and methodologically significant: the dry season represents the period of greatest ecological stress for the Keta and Ada-Songor systems, the conditions under which freshwater-dependent vegetation and aquatic communities face maximum osmotic and thermal pressure, and the seasonal extreme against which all future monitoring data will be compared.

Establishing the monitoring baseline under dry-season conditions is the methodologically correct choice for a programme whose primary management function is early warning of ecological deterioration. The dry season records maximum salinity, minimum dissolved oxygen (due to elevated temperatures reducing gas solubility and maximum biological oxygen demand from decomposing organic matter), and minimum soil moisture — the parameter conditions most likely to approach or exceed ecological thresholds. A baseline established under these conditions captures the system at its most stressed, providing the reference values against which future deterioration signals are most clearly distinguished. A baseline established under wet-season conditions — when freshwater inflow dilutes salinity, lower temperatures support higher dissolved oxygen, and high soil moisture maintains anaerobic carbon preservation conditions — would represent the ecological best case for the system, against which almost any future measurement would appear to be deteriorating simply because the comparison point is unusually favourable.

It should be noted that while December 2025 was nominally the dry season, unseasonal rainfall occurred during the sampling period and likely influenced some physico-chemical parameter values, particularly at stations where rain-induced freshwater inputs may have temporarily diluted salinity and conductivity readings. This qualification is noted in the source assessment and is important context for interpreting specific parameter values: the baseline conditions recorded in December 2025 are representative of the dry season broadly but may understate peak dry-season salinity and conductivity levels at stations where rainfall dilution occurred during sampling. Future quarterly surveys will progressively resolve this uncertainty by providing a multi-year dataset of seasonal variation against which December measurements can be calibrated.

The management value of the seasonal context is greatest in relation to two specific indicators. The first is salinity: the dry-season baseline records peak annual salinity at most stations, establishing the maximum threshold that mangrove communities at each cluster must tolerate each year. Future dry-season surveys that record salinity values systematically higher than the December 2025 baseline — without a corresponding shift in rainfall patterns — indicate structural salinisation of the system, not seasonal variation. The second is soil moisture: the dry-season baseline records the minimum annual soil moisture at terrestrial stations, establishing how deeply the soil profile desiccates under worst-case dry-season conditions. Monitoring whether this minimum is becoming more extreme across successive annual cycles provides direct evidence of whether hydrological deterioration is increasing the duration and intensity of carbon exposure periods in the dry season.

Future monitoring cycles planned under the quarterly programme will add wet-season data rounds — typically April-May (early wet season) and September-October (late wet season) — to the annual record. The combination of dry-season baseline and wet-season measurements will yield the seasonal amplitude of each parameter at each station: the difference between minimum and maximum values across the year. Seasonal amplitude is itself a management indicator, because it characterises the ecological resilience range of each cluster. Clusters with narrow seasonal amplitude — where conditions are relatively stable across the year — are less vulnerable to episodic stress events than clusters where the amplitude is large. A narrowing of seasonal amplitude over successive years (e.g., high dry-season salinity approaching wet-season values) indicates that the system's seasonal self-regulatory capacity is diminishing, which is an early warning of approaching threshold conditions.

## 3.6 Community Change Agent Training

The Community Change Agents who participated in the December 2025 baseline assessment — individuals recruited from within the communities of Anyanui, Agbledome, Salo, Galotse, Aborlorvi, Havedzi, Woe, Pute, and Obane, among others — received hands-on instrument training throughout the field data collection period. Training was embedded within the survey process rather than delivered as a separate classroom exercise: CCAs learned to operate both the Apera SX751 and the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD in real field conditions, collecting genuine baseline data under the guidance of the lead researchers and directly observing the ecological conditions at each station as measurements were taken.

This training approach reflects a deliberate programme design choice with structural consequences for the monitoring system's operational sustainability. The December 2025 baseline survey was not only a scientific data collection exercise; it was simultaneously the first training iteration of Ghana's community-based wetland monitoring capacity at Keta and Ada-Songor. By the time the baseline data collection was complete, the CCAs had direct operational familiarity with both instruments, an understanding of the ecological significance of each parameter being measured, and direct experiential knowledge of the spatial geography of the station network — where each station is located, what it looks like, what conditions to expect at it in the dry season, and what anomalous conditions look like in contrast to the baseline.

This experiential knowledge base is the essential operational foundation for the quarterly monitoring programme described in Chapter 10. The quarterly programme requires CCAs to visit each station in their community cluster four times per year, collect water and soil measurements, complete the standardised data sheet, and submit data through the designated reporting pathway to the district-level Wildlife Division. This is a technically demanding protocol for community-level monitors operating without laboratory infrastructure or specialist scientific support during each sampling round. The December 2025 training gave CCAs the practical competence to execute this protocol reliably and consistently — a competence that cannot be acquired from a manual or a classroom session alone, but only from hands-on measurement under real field conditions with direct feedback from experienced field researchers.

The Monitoring Plan for Keta and Ada Wetlands Ecosystem, developed following the December 2025 baseline, formalises the CCA training investment with specific operational provisions. It specifies that instrument model and any defects or calibration variations should be documented at each sampling round; that any variations in sampling strategy or decisions to modify designated station positions should be formally recorded and reported; and that any drastic or unusual changes in physical or chemical water quality should be reported immediately to the management authority or District Assembly, outside of the regular quarterly reporting cycle. These provisions are designed to preserve the integrity of the time-series data across rounds and to ensure that the early warning function of the monitoring network operates in real time, not only in annual review cycles.

The Monitoring Plan also provides that if devices acquired for subsequent rounds are different from those used for the December 2025 training, CCAs should receive additional training on the new instruments before those rounds begin. This provision recognises that instrument consistency across the time series is important for data comparability: a change in instrument type may introduce systematic differences in measured values that could be misinterpreted as real ecological change if the instrument transition is not documented and the data adjusted accordingly. The investment in CCA training made during the December 2025 baseline should be understood not as a one-time event but as the first iteration of an ongoing capacity-building programme whose continuity is essential to the scientific integrity of the multi-year monitoring record.
