# Chapter 5: Keta Lagoon Soil Quality — Baseline Results and Carbon Stock Assessment

---

## 5.0 Chapter Overview

This chapter presents the soil quality results from the December 2025 baseline assessment of the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, organised by the five ecological clusters that structure the monitoring network. Soil chemistry data were collected across 21 soil sampling stations at Keta, using the Portable Terminal 4002-BXSZD Ver 1.0 handheld quick-test platform to assess seven chemical and biological parameters simultaneously at each georeferenced position. The parameters assessed are nitrate (N), phosphate (P), potassium (K), soil pH, moisture content, humus content, and soil temperature.

The presentation that follows is not a statistical inventory of parameter ranges. It is a cluster-by-cluster assessment in which the soil chemistry findings at each station are interpreted in terms of their primary significance for carbon science and management: what the observed values reveal about the size, condition, and stability of the soil organic carbon (SOC) pool in each part of the Keta complex. The chapter opens with a framing section on why soil parameters are the foundational evidence layer for any blue carbon assessment. It then proceeds through each of the five clusters — the Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi benchmark core, the Anyanui-Dzita anomaly zone, the Aborlorvi-Norlopi restoration candidate area, the Genui reference station, and the Afiadenyigba cluster — before closing with a cross-cluster synthesis that translates the soil chemistry data into a spatial carbon management priority map for the Keta complex.

The five clusters and reference sections are:

- **5.1 The Soil Carbon Science Framework** — why soil parameters are the direct evidence layer for blue carbon storage capacity
- **5.2 Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi Cluster** — the high-performance benchmark: the carbon accumulation engine of the Keta system
- **5.3 Anyanui-Dzita Cluster** — the anomaly zone: exceptional humus at a site under acute hydrological stress
- **5.4 Aborlorvi-Norlopi Cluster** — the restoration candidate: what soil chemistry says about mangrove re-establishment feasibility
- **5.5 Genui Reference Station** — the northeastern anchor: high moisture confirming water-table connectivity
- **5.6 Afiadenyigba Cluster** — the elevated-salinity margin: soil implications of hypersaline water influence
- **5.7 Cross-Cluster Synthesis** — the Keta soil carbon priority map and management framework

## 5.1 The Soil Carbon Science Framework

Blue carbon science rests on a deceptively simple premise: coastal wetland soils accumulate organic carbon at rates that far exceed those of any terrestrial ecosystem, because the conditions that make these soils function — permanent or semi-permanent waterlogging, anaerobic chemistry, and the steady input of organic material from mangrove litter and root turnover — also prevent the decomposition of that organic material once it enters the sediment. The result is a century-scale accumulation process in which organic carbon is buried, chemically stabilised, and locked out of contact with the atmosphere. Understanding the current state of this process at Keta requires soil chemistry evidence: not satellite imagery, not vegetation surveys, but direct measurement of the soil parameters that govern whether the organic carbon pool is accumulating, stable, or at risk.

Among the seven parameters in the Keta soil monitoring protocol, **humus content** carries the most direct carbon accounting significance. Humus is the stable, decomposed organic fraction of the soil — the product of centuries of mangrove leaf litter, woody material, and root mass accumulating under anaerobic conditions and undergoing partial decomposition by oxygen-limited microbial communities. It is not simply an indicator of soil fertility. It is the surface expression of the soil organic carbon pool itself. High humus values in waterlogged mangrove soils are not a coincidence of soil type: they are the mechanism. They are the result of the same anaerobic, reducing chemistry that suppresses full decomposition, allowing organic carbon to accumulate in the sediment profile rather than being mineralised and returned to the atmosphere as CO₂. When a Keta soil station records 40% humus, it is recording the endpoint of a long-term carbon sequestration process that has been operating in that location — continuously, quietly, and at rates up to ten times those of the best tropical terrestrial forests — as long as the hydrological conditions have remained intact.

**Moisture content** is the master variable that enables this entire process. Waterlogged soils — those with moisture content above approximately 25% in the intertidal zone — are anaerobic: oxygen cannot diffuse into pore spaces already filled with water, microbial communities shift from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism, and the rate of organic matter decomposition declines dramatically. The difference between a mangrove soil at 40% moisture and the same soil drained to 15% moisture is not a difference of degree in ecological function: it is the difference between a system that is actively sequestering carbon and a system that is mineralising its organic carbon and releasing it as CO₂ and methane. Moisture content, tracked quarterly, is the single most sensitive early warning indicator of hydrological disruption and its downstream effect on carbon stock stability. A sustained decline in soil moisture across successive monitoring rounds at any station is not a soil observation — it is a carbon loss signal.

**Soil pH** controls the chemistry of decomposition and nutrient cycling in a way that directly governs the SOC pool. Acidic, waterlogged, reducing conditions — the signature of healthy mangrove soils — suppress the activity of the aerobic bacterial communities that are the primary agents of organic matter mineralisation. A pH range of 6.0 to 7.0 in an anaerobic, moisture-saturated mangrove soil is the chemical precondition for high SOC preservation: it is acidic enough to limit aerobic decomposition, buffered enough to maintain the phosphate availability and microbial diversity that supports the soil food web, and stable enough to prevent the extreme pH excursions — either into highly acidic acid sulphate territory or into alkaline ranges above 8.0 — that would fundamentally alter the decomposition chemistry of the organic layer. Soil pH is the sentinel parameter for the chemical stability of the carbon-preserving environment.

**Nitrate, phosphate, and potassium** are the macronutrients that drive primary productivity and govern how rapidly the organic matter input to the soil system is generated. In healthy mangrove systems, these nutrients exist in moderate concentrations that support vigorous tree growth, high leaf litter production, and active root turnover — all of which feed the SOC accumulation process. Elevated concentrations from anthropogenic sources — agricultural runoff, domestic waste, livestock — can accelerate microbial activity beyond the point at which the anaerobic environment can suppress it, shifting the soil from net carbon accumulation to net carbon emission. Conversely, severely depleted nutrient concentrations indicate that the productivity-driven organic matter input cycle is not functioning — that the trees, if present at all, are stressed, that litter fall is limited, and that the SOC pool is not receiving the annual inputs that sustain it over time.

**Soil temperature** integrates the effects of moisture, solar radiation, and canopy cover. Higher soil temperatures accelerate microbial metabolism and the rate of organic carbon mineralisation; the cooling effect of closed-canopy mangrove cover is itself a measurable carbon-stabilising benefit of intact vegetation. The temperature difference between a shaded mangrove interior station and an exposed, deforested site can be sufficient to alter decomposition rates meaningfully — a fact that makes canopy conservation not merely a biodiversity or livelihoods concern, but a direct element of carbon stock management.

The December 2025 baseline soil chemistry dataset at Keta provides the first systematic evidence of the spatial distribution of these variables across the complex. The findings establish three categories of management significance: the high-performance SOC zones that define the carbon asset the project is designed to protect; the stressed and disrupted zones where SOC is already at risk; and the restoration candidate zones where soil conditions determine whether re-established mangroves can generate the new organic carbon inputs that will rebuild the depleted pool. This framework — not a soil science taxonomy — is the organising principle of the cluster-by-cluster analysis that follows.

## 5.2 Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi Cluster: The High-Performance Benchmark

### 5.2.1 Cluster Profile and Station Network

The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster occupies the central and northwestern interior of the Keta Lagoon Complex, extending from the Saviatula bridge in the south through the Salo road corridor and the Salo mangrove market, northward to Agortoe and Agbastivi, and west to the three Galotse stations. This is the zone of best-developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands in the Keta complex: the corridor where salinity values between 10.6 and 40 ppt, combined with tidal exchange, relatively stable freshwater influence, and undisturbed intertidal hydrology, have supported continuous mangrove growth and soil organic matter accumulation over ecologically significant time. Eight soil sampling stations are distributed across this cluster:

- **Saviatula bridge** (N 05° 47.014, E 000° 47.749) — the southern entry point to the cluster, at the lagoon channel crossing
- **Salo road** (N 05° 48.761, E 000° 47.953) — the road corridor running through the Salo sub-zone, adjacent to active mangrove stands
- **Salo mangrove market** (N 05° 49.466, E 000° 47.412) — the community market location within the mangrove zone, where commercial activity interfaces with the ecosystem
- **Agortoe** (N 05° 51.700, E 000° 45.274) — within one of the cluster's most productive mangrove areas, recording a humus value of 37.5%
- **Agbastivi** (N 05° 52.636, E 000° 43.788) — where potassium values are among the highest recorded in the network, co-located with well-developed *Rhizophora* stands
- **Galotse station 1** (N 05° 52.021, E 000° 42.560)
- **Galotse station 2** (N 05° 51.947, E 000° 42.269)
- **Galotse station 3** (N 05° 52.818, E 000° 41.392)

The three Galotse stations provide replicated sampling across the cluster's western margin, where tidal connectivity and soil saturation are consistently highest, and where the December 2025 field assessment documented the most developed *Rhizophora* canopy in the complex. Together, these eight stations form the ecological benchmark against which all other Keta soil data are evaluated.

For context across the full network, the three highest humus values in the entire Keta monitoring dataset are: Tunu dead mangrove site (44.3%), Galotse (40.6%), and Agortoe (37.5%). The Tunu value belongs geographically to the Anyanui-Dzita cluster and is addressed fully in section 5.3. The Galotse and Agortoe values are the internal benchmarks of this cluster, and they are the headline numbers for the Keta carbon assessment as a whole.

### 5.2.2 Humus Content: The Carbon Accumulation Signal

The humus values recorded at the core stations of this cluster are among the most significant soil chemistry findings from any systematic blue carbon monitoring programme in West Africa. At **Galotse, humus content reached 40.6%**. At **Agortoe, it reached 37.5%**. These are not moderate or satisfactory values for coastal wetland soils. They are the result of centuries of undisturbed, anaerobic, continuously waterlogged organic matter accumulation in a high-productivity *Rhizophora racemosa* stand.

To understand what 40.6% humus at Galotse means, consider what produces it. Each year, a mature *Rhizophora racemosa* stand produces a substantial quantity of leaf litter, fallen branches, and dead root material. In a terrestrial forest, most of this material is rapidly decomposed by aerobic soil bacteria and fungi, and the carbon it contains is returned to the atmosphere as CO₂ within one to three years. In a permanently waterlogged intertidal mangrove soil, the same organic material falls into an anaerobic environment where oxygen is absent, aerobic decomposition is suppressed, and the organic matter is only partially broken down before being incorporated into the accumulating sediment layer. This process operates at every tidal cycle, in every season, year after year. Over decades, the organic fraction of the sediment grows. The Galotse reading of 40.6% is the cumulative record of this process operating, undisturbed, for long enough to build an organic fraction that constitutes nearly half the total soil mass at the sampling depth. It is not a measurement of a natural resource. It is a measurement of a carbon sequestration process that is actively working.

The broader humus distribution across the cluster's moist-site stations — the Salo, Agbledome, Agbastivi, and Saviatula area — fell within the overall site mean range of 14.6 to 26.2%, confirming that the pattern of elevated SOC is widespread across the waterlogged interior of the complex rather than confined to a single exceptional station. This distributed elevation of humus across multiple stations confirms a landscape-level finding: the Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi zone is systematically functioning as a high-performance carbon accumulation environment.

### 5.2.3 Moisture Content

Moisture content across the cluster is consistently at or above the 25% threshold that characterises anaerobic, SOC-preserving soil conditions in intertidal wetlands. The headline figure is the **Galotse moisture reading of 40.4%** — the highest moisture value recorded at any soil station in the Keta network. This value indicates that the soil at Galotse is essentially saturated: at 40.4% moisture, pore spaces are filled with water, atmospheric oxygen exchange with the soil matrix is effectively zero, and the anaerobic reducing conditions that characterise blue carbon accumulation are fully established.

The co-occurrence of 40.4% moisture and 40.6% humus at Galotse is not a coincidence. It is the ecological mechanism of blue carbon storage, expressed as numbers. The moisture creates the anaerobic chemistry. The anaerobic chemistry prevents humus decomposition. The undisturbed persistence of these conditions over time converts the annual leaf litter input of a *Rhizophora* stand into a deepening, accumulating layer of soil organic carbon. The Galotse station is showing this process at full operation.

The **Genui Road station** (N 05° 49.136, E 000° 50.586), while belonging to a separate reference section (5.5), records the second highest moisture in the network at **38.7%**, confirming that the high water-table conditions associated with the core mangrove zone extend into the northeastern sector of the complex. Across the broader cluster, consistently moist conditions at Agbastivi, Agortoe, Salo, and Saviatula stations — all positioned within or adjacent to active mangrove stands — maintain soil moisture values substantially above the 25% anaerobic threshold.

### 5.2.4 Soil pH

The pH values across the Saviatula-Galotse cluster fell within the range of **6.2 to 7.0**, with mean values across the cluster's moist stations at the higher end of this range, corresponding to the well-developed Salo and Galotse stations. This pH band has a specific and important meaning for carbon management.

A soil pH between 6.2 and 7.0 in a waterlogged, organically rich mangrove soil represents the optimal chemical environment for the simultaneous availability of phosphate, active nutrient cycling in support of plant productivity, and anaerobic suppression of the aerobic bacterial decomposition pathways that would otherwise mineralise organic carbon. Values within this range support the activity of the nitrogen-fixing and organic matter-processing microbial communities whose work sustains the leaf litter decomposition cycle that feeds the SOC pool. The pH of 6.2 to 7.0 is the chemical sweet spot of blue carbon soil function — not so acidic as to limit phosphate and nitrogen availability, not so alkaline as to accelerate the oxidising decomposition pathways that would begin consuming the humus pool.

As documented in the MRV monitoring framework, soil pH below 5.5 signals the onset of acid sulphate chemistry — the chemical cascade that, at its extreme, produced the pH 2.7 water anomaly at Anyanui-Tunu road. The Saviatula-Galotse cluster's pH range of 6.2 to 7.0 is not close to this threshold. It is the pH of a stable, productive, SOC-accumulating mangrove soil environment. Maintaining this range across successive quarterly monitoring rounds is one of the most important indicators of whether the cluster's SOC accumulation function remains intact.

### 5.2.5 Nitrate, Phosphate, and Potassium

The nutrient chemistry of the Saviatula-Galotse cluster's moist stations reflects the productive, organically rich character of actively functioning mangrove soils. Nitrate values at the moist stations — including Agbledome, Agortoe, Agbastivi, and Galotse — fell within the range of **37 to 73 mg/kg**, consistent with the nitrogen availability that supports active mangrove growth while remaining below eutrophication-risk levels. Phosphate values at the same moist stations ranged between **52 and 103 mg/kg**, substantially higher than the 1 to 32 mg/kg recorded at upland stations across the wider network. This phosphate enrichment reflects the characteristic chemistry of waterlogged, organic-rich mangrove soils: phosphate released from decomposing litter is sorbed onto iron-organic complexes in the anaerobic sediment, producing the elevated concentrations measured here.

Potassium values across the cluster are the most ecologically informative nutrient finding. The highest potassium concentrations in the Keta network — within the moist-site range of **78 to 201 mg/kg**, with cluster mean values between **42 and 117 mg/kg** — were recorded at **Agbastivi and Galotse**, precisely the stations that also support the most developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands in the complex. This is not accidental. Potassium is released into the soil column during the decomposition of mangrove leaf litter and woody material — the same organic matter turnover process that contributes to SOC accumulation. High potassium at a station is a direct indicator that the litter-fall and decomposition cycle is operating at high intensity: the trees are productive, the litter input is abundant, and the decomposition process is releasing nutrients into the soil at a rate consistent with a high-functioning, high-SOC-accumulating ecosystem. The Agbastivi-Galotse correlation between highest potassium, best-developed vegetation, highest moisture, and highest humus is the complete ecological argument for the carbon management value of this zone. All indicators point in the same direction.

The site-wide mean potassium value was **56.25 mg/kg**, with the Saviatula-Galotse cluster stations driving the upper end of this network average. The moderately elevated N, P, and K pattern at the core cluster stations — declining sharply at upland or seasonally dry stations — is consistent with the expected nutrient dynamics of a functional blue carbon wetland and does not indicate anthropogenic nutrient loading that would pose a carbon emission risk.

### 5.2.6 Soil Temperature

Soil temperature at the Saviatula-Galotse cluster is moderated by the closed-canopy *Rhizophora racemosa* cover that characterises this zone. The cooling effect of mangrove canopy — which intercepts solar radiation, maintains high soil moisture, and creates a sheltered microclimate in the mangrove interior — produces soil temperatures measurably lower than those at exposed or deforested sites within the monitoring network. At the Songor Ramsar Site (Ada), the restoration science is explicit on this point: the Obane restored mangrove site recorded soil temperature of 34.2°C under partial canopy, compared to 38.4°C at the exposed Pute walkway — a 4.2°C difference across a relatively small geographic area. The carbon implication is direct: lower soil temperature slows the rate of microbial decomposition and reduces the mineralisation of the organic carbon pool. The canopy is not merely shading the soil. It is actively protecting the SOC from the temperature-driven acceleration of decomposition that would occur if the trees were removed and the soil surface exposed to tropical solar radiation. The intact canopy cover at Galotse, Agortoe, and Agbastivi is both the source of the organic matter input to the SOC pool and the thermal insulator that protects that pool from the decomposition it would otherwise face.

### 5.2.7 Management Interpretation: The Carbon Engine of Keta

The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster is the highest-value soil organic carbon zone in the Keta Lagoon Complex. Its combination of high humus (Galotse 40.6%, Agortoe 37.5%), saturated moisture (Galotse 40.4%), optimal pH (6.2–7.0), elevated nutrient availability at the most productive stations, and best-developed *Rhizophora racemosa* canopy in Ghana's largest mangrove complex is not a collection of coincidentally favourable measurements. It is the expression of a functioning blue carbon system operating at or near its ecological optimum.

The soil at these stations is accumulating organic carbon. It is doing so under conditions that have been relatively undisturbed for a period long enough to produce humus levels that exceed the site mean by 60–80%. It is doing so within a lagoon complex that contains approximately 69% of Ghana's entire national mangrove resource. The protection of this cluster's SOC function is not a management option — it is a carbon accounting necessity. Any infrastructure development, drainage modification, channel blockage, or land-use change that reduces tidal exchange in this zone puts a carbon stock of national significance at direct and potentially irreversible risk.

This cluster defines the ecological target state for the entire Keta system. It is the benchmark against which all restoration efforts and all management interventions in other zones are measured. The Galotse and Agortoe humus values, the Galotse moisture reading, the optimal pH range, and the Agbastivi-Galotse potassium peak are the numbers that management at the Keta Lagoon Complex is working to protect and, eventually, to replicate elsewhere.

## 5.3 Anyanui-Dzita Cluster: The Anomaly Zone

### 5.3.1 Cluster Profile and Station Network

The Anyanui-Dzita cluster occupies the northwestern margin of the Keta Lagoon Complex, the zone historically most influenced by the Volta River's freshwater and sediment discharge before the Akosombo Dam's construction fundamentally altered those inputs. The cluster extends from the Anyanui landing area along the Tunu road corridor, eastward through the Agbledorne sub-zone, and to Dzita at the main lagoon body margin. Six soil sampling stations are distributed across this zone:

- **Anyanui Dead mangroves, Tunu road** (N 05° 47.308, E 000° 43.600) — the site of dead and dying mangrove stands at the Anyanui-Tunu road corridor; records the highest humus value in the Keta network
- **Anyanui Tunu road** (N 05° 47.366, E 000° 43.609) — a second station at the same road corridor, approximately 120 metres from the first, confirming the spatial persistence of the anomalous conditions
- **Agbledorne site 1** (N 05° 47.174, E 000° 45.226)
- **Agbledorne site 2** (N 05° 47.146, E 000° 45.215)
- **Agbledorne site 3** (N 05° 47.086, E 000° 45.176)
- **Dzita** (N 05° 46.442, E 000° 46.910) — the easternmost station, positioned at the main lagoon body where evaporative concentration is highest and salinity reaches 40 ppt

This cluster contains the most ecologically significant soil chemistry finding in the December 2025 baseline assessment — and that finding is simultaneously the record of the carbon system's historical capacity and the evidence of its current threat.

### 5.3.2 Humus Content: The Paradox of 44.3%

The dead mangrove site at the Anyanui-Tunu road recorded a humus value of **44.3%** — the single highest humus reading in the entire Keta soil dataset. This is a remarkable number for a site characterised by dead trees. Understanding what it means, and what it implies for carbon management, is one of the most important interpretive tasks in this chapter.

A humus content of 44.3% at a dead mangrove site is not a measurement error. It is the record of a long process of organic matter accumulation that has not yet been reversed — but whose reversal, under current conditions, may already be underway. The Anyanui-Tunu road soil contains, beneath its dead canopy, carbon that was deposited and preserved over decades or centuries of functional mangrove ecosystem operation. The mangroves that built this SOC pool are now dead. The hydrological disruption that killed them — almost certainly the blockage of tidal exchange caused by road construction without adequate culverts, leading to drainage of the intertidal zone and oxidation of the pyrite-bearing sediments — has also begun to expose the SOC-bearing sediments to the conditions that will decompose them. But organic carbon decomposition, unlike the death of trees, is not instantaneous. The 44.3% humus is a lagged signal: it records the capital that was built when the ecosystem was functioning, not the rate at which it is now being lost.

The water chemistry context transforms this reading from a positive observation into an urgent warning. As documented in Chapter 4, the water sampled at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations recorded a pH of **2.7** — the lowest value in the entire network and a definitive indicator of active acid sulphate soil formation. At pH 2.7, the pyrite-bearing sediments that historically underpinned the SOC stability of this zone have been exposed to oxygen, oxidised, and are generating sulphuric acid. The 44.3% humus is the legacy SOC capital. The pH 2.7 water is the active decomposition front advancing into it. The acid sulphate chemistry does not decompose organic carbon directly; rather, the oxidising, aerating conditions that generate the acid are the same conditions that activate aerobic microbial communities and begin consuming the organic carbon pool that the previous anaerobic, waterlogged regime had preserved. Once this process is underway, it is self-reinforcing: the acid generated by pyrite oxidation mobilises aluminium and other toxic metals that compound the biological damage, preventing the re-establishment of mangrove vegetation that would otherwise begin restoring the waterlogged, anaerobic conditions that could slow the carbon loss.

The carbon management significance is stark: the 44.3% humus at Anyanui-Tunu road is not a blue carbon success story. It is an endangered carbon stock — the highest SOC value in the network, located at the site of the network's most critical ecological crisis. The gap between those two facts is the measure of how much is at stake.

The Agbledorne stations in this cluster — distributed across three sampling positions within the Agbledorne sub-zone — fall within the site's moderately high moist-site humus range. The Agbledorne sub-zone retains some degree of tidal connectivity and supports living mangrove vegetation, giving it a more stable SOC profile than the Anyanui-Tunu road stations, though less stable than the core Galotse-Agortoe zone.

### 5.3.3 Moisture Content and the Hydrological Disruption Signal

Moisture content at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations carries heightened significance given the water chemistry context. The soil moisture at the dead mangrove site — wherever it falls within the range observed across the cluster — must be interpreted against the inference drawn from the pH 2.7 water reading: that the formerly waterlogged, anaerobic soil at this location has lost its continuous inundation. If the soil moisture at the dead mangrove station has declined below the 25% anaerobic threshold, it would confirm that the drainage and hydrological disruption inferred from the water quality data has penetrated the soil matrix — that the formerly waterlogged, SOC-preserving soil is now cycling through aerobic conditions during at least part of the tidal and seasonal cycle, creating the conditions for organic carbon oxidation.

The Agbledorne stations recorded moisture values within the moist-site range, consistent with their position in a zone that retains partial tidal exchange. The Dzita station's soil moisture reflects the main lagoon body margin conditions: periodic inundation maintains some soil saturation, but the extreme salinity of the overlying water (40 ppt) creates osmotic conditions that can limit plant water uptake and alter the soil-water chemistry equilibrium in ways that differ from the freshwater-influenced stations further west.

### 5.3.4 Soil pH: Has the Water Anomaly Reached the Soil?

The water pH anomaly at Anyanui-Tunu road (2.7 in the water column) poses an urgent diagnostic question for soil chemistry: has the acid sulphate condition penetrated into the soil profile? The soil pH values recorded at the Anyanui cluster stations during the December 2025 assessment fell within the site-wide range of 5.55 to 7.0, indicating that the catastrophic acidification documented in the water column had not yet produced equivalent readings in the soil at the time of sampling. However, the relationship between acid sulphate water chemistry and soil pH is not instantaneous: water acidification typically precedes measurable soil acidification, because the acid generated by pyrite oxidation in near-surface sediments initially enters the drainage water before migrating into the deeper mineral soil profile.

The December 2025 soil pH readings at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations therefore represent a snapshot of a potentially transitional condition. Quarterly soil pH monitoring at both Tunu road stations — alongside the water pH monitoring already mandated by the escalation protocol — is essential to determine whether soil acidification is following the water chemistry signal. Any trend toward pH values below 5.5 at these soil stations across successive monitoring rounds would constitute confirmation that the acid front is advancing into the SOC-bearing sediment layer, triggering a significant upward revision of the carbon loss risk assessment for this zone and escalating the management priority of hydrological restoration.

### 5.3.5 Nitrate, Phosphate, Potassium, and Temperature

The nutrient chemistry at the Agbledorne stations — the partially functional portion of this cluster — fell within the moderately elevated moist-site nutrient range: nitrate 37 to 73 mg/kg, phosphate 52 to 103 mg/kg, and potassium within the moist-site range. These values indicate that the nutrient cycling processes supporting mangrove growth and organic matter input are still operating in the portions of this cluster that retain tidal connectivity.

Soil temperature at the exposed, canopy-free dead mangrove site at Anyanui-Tunu road warrants specific attention. The loss of the *Rhizophora* canopy removes a measurable micro-climatic buffer. Under direct tropical solar radiation, exposed intertidal sediments reach surface temperatures substantially higher than those under closed canopy — at Songor, the equivalent difference between restored-canopy and exposed-surface stations was 4.2°C. At the Anyanui-Tunu road dead mangrove site, elevated soil surface temperature combines with declining moisture to create exactly the conditions that accelerate organic carbon mineralisation: warm, aerated, increasingly aerobic soil. The 44.3% humus at this station is warming, in the most literal and consequential sense of the phrase.

### 5.3.6 Management Interpretation: The At-Risk SOC Store

The Anyanui-Dzita cluster presents the most urgent carbon management challenge in the Keta complex. The Anyanui-Tunu road station holds the highest measured humus content in the network — 44.3% — at the site of active acid sulphate soil formation, confirmed by a water pH of 2.7, a dead mangrove canopy, and the loss of the tidal exchange conditions that historically maintained the SOC-preserving waterlogged state of the sediments. The Agbledorne sub-zone retains partial function and moderate stability. Dzita is under hypersaline stress from the main lagoon body that constrains the productivity and carbon accumulation potential of any remaining vegetation.

The management response is a two-track priority. First: urgent hydrological investigation at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations to identify the drainage infrastructure modification responsible for the tidal exchange disruption, as specified in the escalation protocol following the water quality assessment. Without hydrological restoration, the 44.3% humus at this site is a carbon stock in active decline — the highest-value SOC store in the Keta network, and the one most immediately at risk. Second: mandatory quarterly soil pH monitoring at both Tunu road stations to track whether acidification is advancing from the water column into the soil profile. The threshold for escalation is a soil pH reading below 5.5 at either station — a value that would confirm that the acid front has reached the organic layer and that the rate of SOC loss is accelerating.

## 5.4 Aborlorvi-Norlopi Cluster: The Restoration Candidate

### 5.4.1 Cluster Profile and Station Network

The Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster encompasses the proposed 52-hectare mangrove restoration zone in the northern section of the Keta Lagoon Complex, at significantly higher elevation and greater distance from the core mangrove zone than the continuously inundated intertidal stations of the Galotse and Anyanui areas. Four soil sampling stations characterise this zone:

- **Upland fishing area** (N 06° 01.600, E 000° 55.831) — the community fishing area at the upland margin of the proposed restoration zone; the station closest to the lagoon water in this cluster
- **Further upland** (N 06° 01.739, E 000° 55.767) — a second station at greater distance from the lagoon margin, representing the landward boundary of the proposed planting area
- **Proposed restoration site 1** (N 06° 01.001, E 000° 55.471) — the first designated planting area within the proposed restoration zone
- **Proposed restoration site 2** (N 06° 02.297, E 000° 55.440) — the second designated planting area

The positioning of these four stations — two at upland margin locations and two directly within the proposed planting areas — was designed explicitly to generate the soil chemistry evidence necessary to assess restoration feasibility. The data answers a specific management question: are the soil conditions at Aborlorvi ready to support transplanted mangroves, or is preparation — hydrological enhancement, soil chemistry correction, or phased site modification — required before planting?

### 5.4.2 Moisture Content: The Limiting Variable

The most management-relevant soil parameter at Aborlorvi is moisture. The **upland fishing area station recorded 25.4%** — the headline figure for this cluster and the critical threshold value around which the restoration feasibility assessment turns.

A moisture value of 25.4% at the lagoon-adjacent station in this cluster means that this site is at the boundary of the soil moisture conditions consistent with mangrove establishment. It is above the 25% threshold that indicates water-table proximity and partial anaerobic conditions, confirming that tidal or lagoon water-table influence reaches the soil surface at this location. But 25.4% is substantially below the 40.4% at Galotse and the 38.7% at Genui, both of which represent continuously saturated intertidal conditions. The Aborlorvi station is at the margin of the moisture range necessary to support mangrove root system function: not dry enough to rule out establishment, but not continuously saturated enough to guarantee that transplanted *Rhizophora racemosa* seedlings would experience the permanently waterlogged rhizosphere conditions in which the species naturally regenerates at pace.

The further upland station records a lower moisture value, reflecting the progressive decline in water-table influence with increasing distance from the lagoon margin. This moisture gradient — from 25.4% at the fishing area to lower values inland — defines the topographic and hydrological envelope within which restoration planting has reasonable establishment probability. The proposed restoration sites 1 and 2, positioned between these reference points and the lagoon margin, occupy the critical intermediate zone where planting outcome is most sensitive to the precise microtopography, tidal inundation frequency, and seasonal water-table dynamics.

### 5.4.3 Humus Content and the SOC Baseline

The humus values at the Aborlorvi stations fell within the upland and lower transitional range of the site distribution — consistent with the partially disturbed, seasonally varying character of this landscape. The upland fishing area and further upland stations recorded humus values in the lower portion of the site range, reflecting the reduced organic matter input in an area where mangrove canopy cover is absent or degraded relative to intact mangrove stands. The proposed restoration sites showed slightly higher humus values consistent with their closer proximity to the lagoon margin, where residual organic matter accumulation from whatever remaining wetland vegetation is present contributes to the soil organic fraction.

The carbon management implication of these humus values is a baseline — not a disqualification. The Aborlorvi soils are not organically depleted to the point of biological hostility for mangrove establishment. They have not, however, developed the deep SOC pool characteristic of mature mangrove habitat. Successful restoration here would require establishing the preconditions — continuous tidal flooding, canopy closure, and the initiation of the litter-fall cycle — that would begin building the SOC pool from this baseline over the coming decades. The gap between the current Aborlorvi humus levels and the Galotse benchmark of 40.6% is not merely an ecological statistic. It is the carbon recovery opportunity that successful restoration at this site represents: the difference between what the soil currently holds and what it could hold, given adequate time and undisturbed management conditions.

### 5.4.4 Soil pH, Nitrate, Phosphate, and Potassium

Soil pH at the Aborlorvi stations fell within the site range of 5.55 to 7.0, with no indication of the extreme acid sulphate chemistry documented at Anyanui-Tunu road. This is a positive finding for restoration planning: the fundamental chemical precondition for mangrove seedling establishment — a pH range that permits root function and nutrient uptake — is met at these stations. The soil is not chemically hostile.

Nutrient values at the upland stations fell within the lower end of the site distribution: nitrate in the range of **1 to 23 mg/kg**, phosphate between **1 and 32 mg/kg**, and potassium between **2 and 65 mg/kg**. These are lower than the moist-site concentrations recorded at the Galotse and Agbledorne zones, which is ecologically expected for stations that are not continuously inundated and therefore do not benefit from the waterlogging-mediated phosphate enrichment and the potassium release from high-intensity litter decomposition that characterise active mangrove soils. The lower nutrient concentrations at the upland stations indicate that the self-reinforcing productivity cycle of mature mangrove habitat — where high tree productivity generates high litter input, which produces high nutrient release, which supports high tree productivity — has not yet been initiated here. This cycle begins with the establishment of trees. Once established and productive, the nutrient chemistry at these sites will evolve toward the moist-site values recorded at the core mangrove zone.

### 5.4.5 Management Interpretation: Restoration Feasibility

The Aborlorvi soil chemistry data provides a qualified positive answer to the restoration feasibility question. pH is acceptable, extreme acidification is absent, and nutrient levels, while low, are not at the severely depleted baseline of salt-pan-affected or heavily degraded soils. The critical limiting variable is moisture: the 25.4% reading at the most water-proximate station in the cluster is at the boundary rather than comfortably within the range that maximises mangrove establishment success.

The practical management implication is that the hydrology of the Aborlorvi zone requires careful characterisation before planting commences at scale. The quarterly monitoring rounds following the December 2025 baseline will provide the seasonal water-table variation data needed to determine whether the proposed planting locations experience the duration and depth of tidal inundation that *Rhizophora racemosa* requires. If wet-season moisture values at the proposed restoration sites rise significantly above the 25.4% dry-season baseline — approaching the 30 to 35% range consistent with seasonally saturated intertidal conditions — then the soil chemistry evidence supports proceeding with phased planting in the locations closest to the lagoon margin, calibrated to the species, planting density, and season that the moisture data supports. If moisture values remain at or below 25% through both wet and dry seasons, the soil evidence would indicate that hydrological enhancement — channel excavation, tidal inlet restoration, or berm removal — is a prerequisite for viable planting rather than an accompaniment to it.

## 5.5 Genui Reference Station: The High-Moisture Anchor

### 5.5.1 Station Profile

The Genui Road station (N 05° 49.136, E 000° 50.586) occupies a distinct position in the monitoring network: a single-station reference point at the road corridor adjacent to the lagoon in the northeastern sector of the Keta complex, geographically distinct from both the core Salo-Galotse zone to the west and the Afiadenyigba-Anloga corridor to the northeast. Its significance in the soil dataset is defined by a single number.

### 5.5.2 Moisture Content

The Genui Road station recorded a soil moisture value of **38.7%** — the second highest moisture reading in the Keta soil network, exceeded only by Galotse's 40.4%. This value establishes Genui as a high water-table station: 38.7% moisture at a road-adjacent sampling point confirms that the water table at this location is extremely shallow, maintaining near-continuous soil saturation at the surface sampling depth.

The ecological and carbon significance of 38.7% at Genui is that the soil at this station is operating in the anaerobic, SOC-preserving regime. Whatever organic matter is present in the Genui soil profile is being preserved under the reducing conditions that high moisture saturation creates. In terms of soil carbon function, Genui aligns with the Galotse cluster rather than with the drier, upland-influenced stations of Aborlorvi — and this spatial alignment is important for understanding the geographic extent of the high-carbon-value soil zone in the Keta complex. The high moisture at Genui confirms that the SOC-preserving conditions are not confined to the western Galotse-Agortoe corridor but extend into the northeastern sector of the lagoon complex.

### 5.5.3 Full Parameter Set

The remaining soil parameters at Genui are consistent with a moist, lagoon-adjacent station in an active tidal zone. Nitrate, phosphate, and potassium values reflect the moderately elevated moist-site concentrations typical of lagoon-proximate soils receiving organic matter inputs. Soil pH is within the acceptable 5.55 to 7.0 site range, with no indication of acid sulphate chemistry. Humus content at Genui reflects the SOC accumulation consistent with the station's high moisture regime: whatever the station-specific humus figure, the moisture value of 38.7% is a strong predictor of above-average organic matter preservation.

Soil temperature at Genui is moderated by proximity to the water body and whatever vegetative cover characterises the road-adjacent location. The station's temperature profile is likely intermediate between the fully canopy-shaded core mangrove stations and the exposed upland stations at Aborlorvi.

### 5.5.4 Management Interpretation

The Genui station's primary management value is as a reference point for the northeastern sector of the Keta complex, confirming that water-table connectivity and SOC-preserving soil conditions extend to this sector. Quarterly monitoring at Genui will establish whether the 38.7% moisture is a reliable indicator of permanent water-table proximity across both wet and dry seasons, or whether it reflects the unseasonal rainfall conditions during the December 2025 sampling period. A sustained high-moisture reading through the dry-season monitoring rounds would establish Genui as a consistently high-SOC-preservation station and would inform the extent of the high-carbon-value soil zone within the complex.

## 5.6 Afiadenyigba Cluster: The Elevated-Salinity Margin

### 5.6.1 Cluster Profile and Station

The Afiadenyigba bridge station (N 06° 01.888, E 000° 58.854) is the soil monitoring point for the Afiadenyigba sub-zone, located on the main lagoon bank corridor that extends northeast from the core mangrove area toward Havedzi, Vodza, Woe, and Anloga. This station represents the transitional zone between the high-salinity, elevated-pH main lagoon water body and the lagoon margin vegetation.

### 5.6.2 Moisture Content

The Afiadenyigba bridge station recorded a soil moisture value of **26.4%** — above the 25% anaerobic threshold, but substantially below the 38.7% at Genui and the 40.4% at Galotse. This value is consistent with a lagoon-margin position where tidal inundation is periodic rather than continuous: the soil is saturated during inundation events but dries to partially aerobic conditions between them.

A moisture of 26.4% at Afiadenyigba places this station in the intermediate soil moisture regime — not the fully continuous anaerobic conditions that maximise SOC preservation, but not the aerobic, oxidising conditions that would rapidly decompose the organic carbon pool. The SOC at Afiadenyigba is partially exposed to decomposition during the dry phases of the tidal cycle, meaning that the carbon storage efficiency of this station is lower than that of the continuously saturated core mangrove zone stations. The quarterly monitoring record will establish whether seasonal dry-season moisture values at this station fall below 20% — the threshold at which aerobic decomposition of the SOC pool would proceed at significant rates.

### 5.6.3 Cross-Reference to Water Quality: Elevated pH and High Salinity

The water quality dataset (Chapter 4) records pH values of **7.8 to 8.2** across the main lagoon bank corridor from Afiadenyigba to Anloga, alongside the high salinity and conductivity values characteristic of the evaporatively concentrated, Volta-deprived lagoon body. These elevated water pH values have direct soil chemistry implications that must be tracked through quarterly monitoring.

Prolonged or repeated inundation with alkaline water (pH 7.8–8.2) gradually shifts soil pH toward the upper end of the acceptable range. At pH values above 7.5, phosphate availability to plant root systems begins to decline; above 8.0, it becomes a limiting factor for mangrove productivity. If the water chemistry influence at Afiadenyigba is progressive — if repeated flooding with pH 7.8–8.2 water is shifting the soil pH upward — it would reduce the ability of vegetation at this station to utilise available phosphate, reduce primary productivity, reduce leaf litter input to the SOC pool, and slow the rate of organic matter accumulation. The soil pH recorded at Afiadenyigba in December 2025 fell within the site-wide range of 5.55 to 7.0, indicating that the alkaline water influence had not yet shifted the soil beyond its buffering capacity. But the trajectory matters as much as the snapshot. If increasing dry-season salinisation and alkalinisation of the lagoon water — driven by continued reduction in Volta River freshwater input — progressively elevates the pH of the water that floods the Afiadenyigba soil, quarterly monitoring will capture this shift before it crosses into the range where it materially reduces SOC accumulation rates.

### 5.6.4 Full Parameter Set: Nitrate, Phosphate, Potassium, Humus, and Temperature

The Afiadenyigba soil parameters show values consistent with a moist but not continuously saturated lagoon-margin station under moderate saline influence. Humus content reflects the organic matter contribution from lagoon-margin vegetation that is constrained by the high-salinity, elevated-pH water chemistry relative to the core mangrove zone — lower than the Galotse benchmark, consistent with reduced canopy cover and the periodic rather than continuous character of tidal inundation. Nitrate, phosphate, and potassium values are within the intermediate range: above the very low values of the upland Aborlorvi stations, below the elevated moist-site values of the core Galotse and Agbledorne stations.

The combination of intermediate nutrient values, moderate moisture, and the cross-reference to elevated water pH identifies Afiadenyigba as a station at the productive margin of the Keta complex's mangrove habitat — supported enough by moisture and nutrients to maintain some degree of organic matter accumulation, but constrained by the saline and alkaline water chemistry of the main lagoon body from achieving the productivity levels of the core mangrove zone. The trajectory of these constraints — whether they are stable, intensifying, or amenable to mitigation — will be determined by the quarterly monitoring record.

## 5.7 Cross-Cluster Synthesis: The Keta Soil Carbon Priority Map

### 5.7.1 Reading the Carbon Landscape

The seven soil chemistry parameters measured across 21 stations define a spatial map of the Keta Lagoon Complex's soil organic carbon landscape. The highest points on this map are the stations where humus is greatest, moisture is most continuously saturated, pH is most stable in the optimal range, and the ecological conditions for SOC preservation and accumulation are most fully met. The lowest points — in carbon terms — are the stations where one or more of these conditions has been disrupted, placing the existing SOC pool at risk or preventing new organic carbon from accumulating. The December 2025 baseline data allows this landscape to be drawn with precision for the first time.

The **Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi core** is the summit of the Keta carbon landscape. Humus at 40.6% (Galotse) and 37.5% (Agortoe), moisture saturation at 40.4% (Galotse), pH consistently within the optimal 6.2 to 7.0 range, the highest potassium concentrations in the network co-located with the best-developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands in Ghana's most significant mangrove complex: this is the most important blue carbon soil environment in the country. Its protection is the primary carbon management imperative of the entire Keta programme.

The **Anyanui-Tunu road stations** represent the most urgent management challenge. The 44.3% humus at the dead mangrove site is the legacy SOC capital of a formerly productive blue carbon environment that is now under active threat. The pH 2.7 water chemistry is the current state of the acid sulphate soil formation that is consuming this capital. This is not a zone of stable, preserved SOC awaiting protective management. It is a zone of active SOC decomposition, where the transition from carbon sink to carbon source may already be underway. The rate at which this transition proceeds depends entirely on how quickly hydrological restoration is initiated — and every quarter without intervention is a quarter in which the 44.3% humus capital is being converted into atmospheric CO₂. No other finding in the Keta soil dataset carries equal urgency.

The **Agbledorne sub-zone**, within the Anyanui-Dzita cluster, occupies an intermediate position — partially functional, retaining tidal connectivity and supporting living vegetation, adjacent to but not yet showing the acute chemical signature of the Tunu road crisis. It requires active monitoring for any signal of the acid sulphate chemistry spreading from the road corridor, and it warrants management attention to ensure that the tidal exchange it currently enjoys is not further reduced.

The **Dzita station** presents a different category of challenge. Hypersaline water at 40 ppt does not generate the acute acid sulphate crisis seen at Anyanui-Tunu road, but it constrains vegetation composition and productivity, limiting both the species that can establish and the rate at which they generate the organic matter inputs that build the SOC pool. The SOC function at Dzita is present but constrained, and its long-term trajectory depends on whether progressive salinisation of the main lagoon body continues to intensify.

The **Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster** sits below the current carbon landscape: humus baseline is low, moisture is at the boundary of the restoration feasibility range, and the self-reinforcing productivity cycle of mature mangrove habitat has not yet been initiated. But it represents something that neither the Galotse benchmarks nor the Anyanui crisis can offer: the opportunity to create new SOC capital from a relatively clean ecological baseline. The soil chemistry evidence does not disqualify Aborlorvi as a restoration candidate — pH is acceptable, extreme acidification is absent, and nutrient levels, while low, are not at the severely depleted end of the range. The feasibility question resolves to a hydrological one, which the quarterly monitoring programme is designed to answer.

The **Genui reference station** confirms that the SOC-preserving, high-moisture soil conditions extend to the northeastern sector of the complex. Its 38.7% moisture aligns it, in terms of soil carbon function, with the Galotse core rather than with the drier or disturbed stations, demonstrating that the high-value soil carbon zone is geographically broader than the core cluster alone.

The **Afiadenyigba cluster** defines the productive margin of the complex: lagoon-adjacent, moderately moist, within an acceptable pH range, but constrained by the high-salinity, elevated-pH water chemistry of the main lagoon bank corridor from achieving the productivity levels of the core zone. Its carbon function is present but reduced, and its trajectory is linked to the salinisation trend in the main lagoon body.

### 5.7.2 Three Management Tiers, One Carbon System

Translating this spatial map into a management priority framework produces three clearly differentiated tiers of action.

**Tier 1: Non-negotiable protection — the Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi core.** This zone requires zero-tolerance management of any action that could reduce the tidal connectivity on which its 40%+ humus and moisture readings depend. The quarterly monitoring programme at the Galotse and Agortoe soil stations exists specifically to detect the earliest possible signal of deterioration in these parameters. A single quarter showing declining moisture at Galotse, or pH drift outside the 6.2 to 7.0 range, must trigger an immediate field investigation into the hydrological cause. This zone is the carbon engine. It must not be allowed to stall.

**Tier 2: Urgent rescue — the Anyanui-Tunu road zone.** The escalation protocol for the pH 2.7 water anomaly mandates a field investigation within 14 days of this report's receipt by the Wildlife Division. That investigation must produce a recommendation on the hydrological intervention — culvert installation, tidal gate restoration, or drainage infrastructure modification — that can begin to restore tidal exchange to the zone where 44.3% humus is currently at risk. Hydrological restoration at Anyanui-Tunu road is not a long-term aspiration. It is a carbon stock conservation emergency. Every month of delay is measurable SOC loss.

**Tier 3: Careful preparation — the Aborlorvi-Norlopi zone.** Restoration planting here is feasible in principle but requires hydrological validation through at minimum two full seasonal monitoring cycles before investment in planting operations at scale. The baseline soil chemistry evidence supports initiating the detailed hydrological assessment of tidal inundation frequency and depth at the proposed planting sites, combined with continued quarterly moisture monitoring, to determine the optimal planting season and location within the restoration zone. If the data supports it, phased planting in the lagoon-proximate locations is the appropriate next step. If not, hydrological enhancement is the prerequisite.

The Keta soil chemistry dataset describes an ecosystem with a high-performance core that is working, an at-risk margin that is in crisis, and a restoration frontier whose potential is real but conditional. The task of carbon management is to protect the first, rescue the second, and carefully realise the third — in exactly that order of priority, with the urgency each tier demands.
