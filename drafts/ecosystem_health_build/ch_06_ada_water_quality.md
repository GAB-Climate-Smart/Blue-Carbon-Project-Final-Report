# Chapter 6: Ada-Songor Lagoon — Water Quality Results

---

## 6.1 The Ada-Songor Water Quality Context

The Ada-Songor Lagoon system is not simply a smaller version of the Keta Lagoon Complex. It is a structurally different ecosystem under a different set of pressures, and understanding its water quality data requires engaging with four defining contextual features before a single measurement value is interpreted.

The first and most consequential is the Volta River. The Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve occupies the eastern margin of the Volta River estuary, and the freshwater discharge from the Volta is the single most important regulator of the Ada system's ecological character. Where Keta's water chemistry is governed principally by the dynamics of a largely enclosed lagoon — evaporation, limited tidal exchange, and the long-term concentration of dissolved solids — the Ada system exists in a continuous negotiation between the Volta's freshwater pulse and the saltwater influence of the Atlantic Ocean. This freshwater–marine gradient produces the salinity variation that defines ecological zonation at Ada: from near-freshwater conditions at the Volta estuary margin in the north and northwest, through brackish intermediate zones in the creek systems, to high-salinity lagoon water at the lagoon intake points in the south. The gradient is the system's ecological spine.

The second feature is the Akosombo Dam. Constructed in 1964 and operational since 1966, the dam now intercepts more than 90% of the Volta River's historical sediment load before it reaches the coast. The implications for the Ada system are profound and cumulative. Before the dam, the Volta's annual flood pulse delivered not only enormous volumes of sediment — sustaining delta and estuary morphology — but also the freshwater dilution that kept the lagoon's salinity within the brackish range historically tolerated by Songor's mangroves. With that sediment load and much of that freshwater now trapped behind the dam, the dilution effect is diminished. The Volta still flows, and its freshwater influence is still measurable at the Azizanya and Ayigbo stations — as the water quality data confirms — but it is a reduced version of its historical self. The practical consequence is that the Ada system is experiencing a slow, long-term shift toward higher average salinity in its interior, with less sediment input to sustain intertidal platform elevation. The water quality monitoring network established at Ada is, among other things, a tool for detecting whether this shift is continuing and at what rate.

The third feature is salt pan operations. Large-scale salt production is an established industry in the Songor area, with extensive evaporation ponds operating at Elavanyo, Obane, and surrounding locations. Salt pans do not simply sit adjacent to the lagoon; they actively alter its water chemistry through seepage, brine discharge, and the diversion of shallow groundwater flows. The effect is to introduce additional hypersaline water into the lagoon system — particularly at the Pute lagoon intake zone, where water exchange between the lagoon and adjacent areas is concentrated — and to suppress the establishment of mangroves in areas where salt pan brine has altered soil chemistry beyond the tolerance range of even highly salt-tolerant species.

The fourth feature is cattle grazing on wetland margins. While this will be treated in full in the soil quality chapter, its significance cannot be entirely deferred: grazing pressure on wetland margins introduces concentrated nutrient inputs — particularly nitrates and phosphates — through animal waste deposition. As will be shown in the soil data, this signal is already measurable and extreme at Wasakuse and Obane. It does not yet appear prominently in the water quality parameters reported here, but it is part of the cumulative pressure environment within which the water data must be understood. A system under simultaneous hydrological stress, salinisation pressure from reduced Volta discharge and salt pan operations, and nutrient loading from grazing is a system whose water quality parameters cannot be interpreted in isolation from one another.

Together, these four factors create a water chemistry regime at Ada-Songor that is under multiple simultaneous stresses, operating across a strong spatial gradient, and changing directionally — not randomly — over time. The data reported in Sections 6.2 through 6.5 below must be read within this context.

## 6.2 Azizanya-Ayigbo Cluster — The Freshwater Baseline

**Sampling stations:** Azizanya-Kewunor bridge (N 05°46.259, E 000°39.185) and Ayigbo bridge (N 05°46.371, E 000°38.447).

These two stations occupy the freshwater end of the Ada salinity gradient, positioned close to the Volta River estuary where the river's diluting influence on the lagoon system is strongest. The water quality data at Azizanya and Ayigbo is not merely low-salinity data: it is evidence of the Volta River's surviving ecological function, and it carries a specific monitoring value that goes beyond the measured values themselves.

The conductivity readings at these stations are the lowest in the entire Ada network: 6.23 µS at Azizanya-Kewunor bridge and 2.64 µS at Ayigbo bridge. These values are substantially below those recorded at any other Ada station, and the Ayigbo reading in particular sits close to the conductivity of the main Volta River itself, confirming that freshwater dilution is the dominant process governing water chemistry at this cluster. Total dissolved solids (TDS) data reinforces this picture: the lowest value in the entire Ada dataset — 1.95 ppm — was recorded at Ayigbo bridge, indicating an extraordinarily low concentration of dissolved material in a water body that is essentially riverine in character at this location. Salinity at both stations approaches 0 ppt, placing them firmly in the near-freshwater zone.

pH values at Azizanya-Ayigbo ranged between 7.6 and 8.0 — circumneutral to slightly alkaline — within the optimal range for mangrove establishment and consistent with carbonate-buffered water under moderate freshwater influence. Dissolved oxygen was relatively high, supporting aerobic water column conditions and an active aquatic community. The ORP values recorded at this cluster — 11 to 76.6 mV — were the lowest in the Ada network and stand in the sharpest possible contrast to the values recorded at the interior lagoon stations discussed in Section 6.5. The low ORP at Azizanya-Ayigbo is consistent with the reducing, organic-rich conditions of river-influenced water, and it represents the geochemical baseline of what the Ada system's carbon storage conditions would look like under stronger freshwater influence.

**Management interpretation:** The Azizanya-Ayigbo cluster should be understood as the historical reference baseline for the Ada system — the closest approximation available of what the lagoon's water chemistry looked like before the Akosombo Dam reduced the Volta's freshwater discharge and sediment delivery. The ultra-low conductivity of 2.64 µS at Ayigbo and the near-zero TDS are the fingerprint of an active freshwater pulse that is still arriving at this margin of the system. They are also, in monitoring terms, canaries. If future quarterly monitoring rounds reveal rising conductivity or rising salinity at Azizanya-Ayigbo — conditions that should by definition remain close to zero given the station's position near the Volta inflow — that is a direct indicator of one or more of three processes: a further reduction in Volta freshwater discharge reaching the estuary; increased tidal or lagoon saltwater penetration into the upper estuary zone; or reduced seasonal rainfall that would otherwise supplement freshwater inputs. Any upward conductivity trend at this cluster, sustained across two or more successive monitoring rounds, should be treated as a system-level alarm for progressive salinisation of the entire Ada water chemistry gradient.

## 6.3 Patukope-Pute Cluster — Critical Management Zone

**Sampling stations:** Patukope creek (N 05°47.111, E 000°34.209), Pute walkway lagoon intake (N 05°47.096, E 000°33.014), and Lagoon underground seepage (N 05°47.013, E 000°32.434).

If the Azizanya-Ayigbo cluster represents the freshwater end of the Ada gradient, the Patukope-Pute cluster represents its marine end. These three stations are positioned at and immediately adjacent to the lagoon intake — the zone where water exchange between the interior lagoon and the wider coastal system is concentrated, and where the influence of salt pan operations is most directly felt.

Salinity at this cluster was the highest recorded in the entire Ada dataset: 28.6 ppt at Pute walkway lagoon intake and 28.2 ppt at Patukope creek. These values confirm that the lagoon interior at this location is receiving water that is approaching marine salinity — a condition that reflects both the reduced dilution from diminished Volta freshwater input and the contribution of salt pan brine seepage. TDS at Pute lagoon reached 57.3 ppm, the highest in the Ada network, consistent with the high dissolved solid load of concentrated lagoon water at this intake zone. Conductivity at Patukope creek was 31.4 µS — the lowest of the three stations in this cluster but still substantially above the Azizanya-Ayigbo values, confirming that even the creek margin here carries a measurably higher dissolved ion load than the Volta-proximate stations. pH across the cluster ranged from 7.1 to 8.1, spanning from near-neutral to mildly alkaline, a range consistent with the mixing of slightly acidic creek water with more alkaline lagoon water at the intake zone.

**Management interpretation:** The Patukope-Pute cluster is a critical management zone for three reinforcing reasons. First, it is the lagoon intake point — the node through which water chemistry conditions propagate from the open coastal system into the interior lagoon. Any change in the salinity, TDS, or ionic composition of water entering the lagoon through this zone will eventually influence conditions across the interior system. Second, it is the zone most directly affected by both Akosombo Dam-mediated reduction in freshwater dilution and by salt pan operations at the Songor periphery; the high salinity readings here are not random but are the cumulative signature of both pressures operating simultaneously. Third — and this must be flagged explicitly for the Carbon Inventory Report — the soil chemistry chapter will reveal that Pute lagoon recorded a soil pH of 4.78, a value indicating the presence of potential acid sulphate soil conditions. The combination of high-salinity water column conditions and near-acid-sulphate soil chemistry at Pute constitutes a compound ecological vulnerability that should place this station at the highest priority tier for restoration assessment. When saline water intrudes over acid sulphate soils, the oxidation products generated can further acidify both the soil and any overlying water during drainage events. The monitoring protocol at Pute should specifically track pH and conductivity together across quarterly rounds, watching for the acidification signal in water that would indicate active acid sulphate soil exposure.

## 6.4 Futuenya-Big Ada Creek Cluster — The Transition Zone

**Sampling stations:** Futuenya creek bridge (N 05°47.212, E 000°37.253) and Big Ada creek bridge (N 05°48.425, E 000°36.491).

These two stations occupy the ecologically intermediate zone of the Ada gradient — the creek systems that connect the Volta-influenced fresher water of the upper estuary with the more saline interior lagoon. The Futuenya-Big Ada cluster is the transition zone, and its water quality data has a distinctly different character from both the freshwater baseline cluster and the saline lagoon-intake cluster.

Conductivity in this cluster shows its most notable value at Big Ada bridge: 50.4 µS, the highest recorded across the entire Ada monitoring network. This initially appears counterintuitive given that the Big Ada station is positioned in a creek that drains ultimately toward the Volta, not the high-salinity lagoon — but it reflects the reality that creek conductivity in transitional systems integrates inputs from multiple sources, including tidal exchange, seasonal rainfall variation, and upstream land-use changes that may be altering the ionic load of water draining into the creek network. The Big Ada conductivity value merits close attention in future quarterly rounds: if it is sustained or increasing, it may be an early indicator that saltwater intrusion is penetrating further into the creek system than the near-freshwater salinity values at the Azizanya stations would suggest.

Dissolved oxygen at this cluster was the highest recorded in the entire Ada monitoring network: 6.09 mg/l at Futuenya creek and 6.91 mg/l at the Luhue creek adjacent to the Big Ada system. These elevated DO values reflect the specific physical conditions of the creek environment — open channels exposed to wind agitation, shallow water with high surface-to-volume ratios that facilitate atmospheric oxygen exchange, and the absence of the dense algal mat growth that can depress DO in more enclosed water bodies. High DO in the creek system is ecologically positive: it confirms aerobic conditions that support fish populations, healthy microbial communities, and the kind of water column oxygenation that is compatible with mangrove root function in adjacent intertidal zones. Salinity at Futuenya creek bridge was 0.2 ppt — near-freshwater, confirming the continuing influence of Volta discharge at this intermediate location — while pH ranged between 8.1 and 8.6 across the cluster, the highest alkaline values recorded in the Ada network, likely reflecting carbonate buffering under wind-mixed, relatively open creek conditions.

**Management interpretation:** The Futuenya-Big Ada transition zone is currently the most ecologically stable part of the Ada water system: high DO, moderate salinity, and alkaline pH conditions are all consistent with supportive mangrove habitat. The high DO values are particularly noteworthy because they indicate that the water column in these creeks is actively oxygenated — a condition that buffers against the kind of anaerobic stress that the interior lagoon stations are showing. However, two monitoring signals warrant ongoing attention. First, the anomalously high conductivity at Big Ada bridge (50.4 µS) should be tracked to determine whether it is a stable characteristic of that station or a trend. Second, pH values of 8.1–8.6 should be watched for any downward shift: if salinisation of the creek system intensifies and the ionic balance shifts, pH buffering capacity may decline, which in turn affects mangrove physiological health.

## 6.5 Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane Cluster — The ORP Alert Zone

**Sampling stations:** Wasakusewayo restoration site (N 05°50.321, E 000°33.316), Togbloku lagoon 1 (N 05°50.322, E 000°33.237), Togbloku lagoon 2 (N 05°50.322, E 000°33.237), and Obane creek (N 05°48.291, E 000°25.396).

This cluster contains the headline finding of the Ada-Songor water quality assessment, and it must be understood not merely as an outlying data point but as a geochemical signal that changes how the entire Ada system's carbon storage function must be evaluated. The finding is this: ORP values of 832–1,547 mV were recorded across the Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane cluster, with the mean of the dataset spanning 1,216 to 1,547 mV and individual peak readings reaching as high as 1,547 mV at stations within the interior lagoon. These are not high ORP values in some relative sense. They are extraordinarily high — among the highest that would be expected in any coastal wetland environment — and they carry specific, serious implications for the carbon storage function of the Ada-Songor system.

Before interpreting what these values mean, it is necessary to explain ORP fully for non-specialist readers, because the implications are not intuitive unless the geochemical mechanism is clear.

**What ORP measures:** Oxidation-Reduction Potential (ORP, also known as redox potential) is a measurement of the tendency of a chemical environment — water, sediment, or soil — to either donate or accept electrons in chemical reactions. A high positive ORP means the environment is oxidising: it has a tendency to accept electrons, which in practical terms means it drives the breakdown of organic compounds through chemical and microbial decomposition. A low or negative ORP means the environment is reducing: it tends to donate electrons, which suppresses oxygen-dependent decomposition reactions, limits microbial activity, and creates the conditions under which organic matter can persist indefinitely without breaking down. ORP is measured in millivolts (mV). The scale ranges from approximately -400 mV (strongly reducing) to +1,200 mV and above (strongly oxidising), though in natural aquatic environments values above +800 mV are rare and ecologically significant.

**Why reducing conditions matter for blue carbon:** The fundamental mechanism by which mangrove soils store carbon is the suppression of decomposition under anaerobic, reducing conditions. When a mangrove leaf falls, a root dies, or woody material accumulates in the sediment, microbial decomposition — the process by which that organic matter would otherwise be converted to CO₂ and returned to the atmosphere — is dramatically slowed in waterlogged, anoxic sediments. The key geochemical driver of this inhibition is reducing conditions: in soils and sediments where ORP values are low (typically between -200 mV and +200 mV in healthy mangrove systems), the electron acceptors that aerobic and anaerobic bacteria require for efficient decomposition are scarce. Organic carbon accumulates because it cannot be fully degraded. Over centuries and millennia, this accumulation produces the extraordinarily carbon-dense sediments that make blue carbon ecosystems — mangroves, saltmarshes, and seagrass beds — among the most effective carbon sinks on Earth, storing up to ten times more carbon per hectare than most terrestrial forests.

This geochemical logic depends entirely on the maintenance of reducing conditions. If those conditions are disrupted — through drainage, drying, hydrological alteration, or intrinsically oxidising water chemistry — the decomposition inhibition fails. Organic carbon that was stable under reducing conditions becomes available to microbial breakdown, and the stored carbon is progressively mineralised and released as CO₂. This is not a hypothetical risk. It is the primary mechanism by which blue carbon stocks are lost when mangrove systems are degraded.

**What the Ada ORP values mean:** With this geochemical framework in place, the implications of ORP values of 832–1,547 mV at the Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane stations become clear and grave. Values in this range indicate strongly oxidising geochemical conditions in the water column and, by extension, in the near-surface sediments of the interior lagoon and creek system. This means the water overlying the Ada-Songor sediments at these stations is not suppressing decomposition — it is actively promoting it. The oxidising capacity of water at 832 mV and above means that the geochemical environment immediately above the sediment surface is thermodynamically favourable for the breakdown of organic matter. Carbon stored in Ada-Songor sediments beneath or adjacent to these stations is exposed to a geochemical regime that is actively hostile to its preservation.

Three implications follow directly, each of which must be carried forward into the Ada-Songor Carbon Inventory Report:

*First:* Carbon stored in Ada-Songor sediments in the interior lagoon zone — particularly at and near the Wasakuse, Togbloku, and Obane stations — is at active risk of mineralisation and CO₂ release. It cannot be assumed to be stable. Organic matter in sediments at these stations is being exposed to oxidising conditions that, over time, will drive aerobic and anaerobically-facilitated decomposition. The rate of carbon loss will depend on sediment depth, soil organic matter content, and whether oxidising conditions are sustained or episodic — questions that the quarterly monitoring programme, combined with the soil chemistry assessment, is designed to begin answering. But the directional finding is unambiguous: these are not conditions conducive to carbon preservation.

*Second:* The carbon sequestration function of the Ada-Songor interior lagoon zone — the ongoing capacity to remove CO₂ from the atmosphere through new organic matter accumulation in sediments — is compromised. Sequestration requires not only that mangroves grow and produce organic matter, but that the organic matter is preserved in sediments rather than decomposed. If the water column conditions above those sediments are oxidising at ORP values approaching 1,547 mV, the sequestration value of those areas is fundamentally undermined. Any carbon credit calculation that treats Ada-Songor interior lagoon sediments as equivalent in carbon stability to the reducing-condition sediments at a site like the Keta Lagoon Complex — where ORP values of approximately 160 mV at comparable lagoon stations reflect genuinely reducing conditions — will overstate the carbon storage function of the Ada system.

*Third:* This finding must be explicitly flagged in the Carbon Inventory Report for Ada-Songor. Carbon stocks at Ada-Songor cannot be assumed to be stable until ORP trends at the interior lagoon stations are monitored across multiple seasonal cycles and a multi-year trend established. At minimum, the Interior lagoon ORP anomaly should be treated as a material uncertainty in any carbon stock assessment, requiring specific disclosure in the monitoring and verification framework and targeted investigation to determine its temporal dynamics, its depth penetration into the sediment column, and its relationship to hydrological drivers including tidal exchange, seasonal rainfall, and freshwater inflow from the Volta.

**Comparison with Keta:** The contrast with Keta ORP data is stark and must be stated explicitly. The ORP value recorded at comparable interior lagoon stations at the Keta Lagoon Complex was approximately 160 mV. This value — positive but moderate — is consistent with the partially waterlogged, anoxic micro-environments characteristic of functioning mangrove soils, where some oxygen is present in the water column but reducing conditions prevail in the sediment layer. At 160 mV, Keta's interior lagoon is not under oxidising stress. The geochemical conditions at the sediment-water interface are compatible with the suppression of decomposition and the ongoing accumulation of organic carbon. The gap between 160 mV at Keta and 832–1,547 mV at the Ada interior lagoon stations is not a minor difference in degree. It is a qualitative difference in geochemical regime, with direct consequences for carbon stability. Keta's lagoon interior is reducing; Ada's interior lagoon is strongly oxidising. The implications for the comparative carbon storage function of the two systems are significant and must be reflected in how their respective carbon stocks are reported and managed.

Other water quality parameters at this cluster are broadly within the ranges recorded elsewhere in the Ada network: pH of 7.3–8.1 and salinity of 28.2–28.6 ppt at stations where lagoon water dominates. The high salinity confirms that the interior lagoon at the Wasakuse-Togbloku stations is receiving water from the high-salinity zone — consistent with the Pute intake data — and is not being significantly diluted by freshwater inputs at these locations. The combination of high salinity and strongly oxidising ORP in the interior lagoon is itself diagnostically important: it points toward a water body that is hydrologically semi-enclosed, subject to evaporative concentration, and receiving insufficient freshwater inflow to maintain the reducing chemistry that would support carbon preservation.

## 6.6 Cross-Cluster Synthesis: Ada-Songor Water Quality

The Ada-Songor water quality data, taken as a whole, reveals an ecosystem under structural and directional stress from three simultaneous pressures, operating across a pronounced spatial gradient that runs from the ecological refuge of the Volta estuary margin to the geochemically compromised interior of the lagoon.

The spatial gradient is the most important organising feature of the dataset. Moving from the Azizanya-Ayigbo stations in the north and northwest — where conductivity of 2.64–6.23 µS, TDS of 1.95 ppm, and near-zero salinity reflect an intact freshwater pulse from the Volta — through the transitional Futuenya-Big Ada creek system — where moderate salinity (0.2 ppt), high DO (6.09–6.91 mg/l), and alkaline pH (8.1–8.6) indicate a functioning intermediate zone — and into the high-salinity Patukope-Pute lagoon intake (28.2–28.6 ppt salinity, 57.3 ppm TDS) and the strongly oxidising interior of the Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane zone (ORP 832–1,547 mV), the picture that emerges is of a system whose ecological health degrades steadily from its freshwater margin to its saline interior.

At the freshwater end, the Azizanya-Ayigbo zone remains the most stable part of the Ada system and provides the ecological reference baseline against which future change will be measured. The critical monitoring function here is early detection of salinisation: any sustained upward trend in conductivity or salinity at these stations would signal that the Volta's diluting influence is further weakening, with system-wide consequences for the salinity gradient that governs mangrove distribution across Ada.

At the transitional zone, the Futuenya-Big Ada creek cluster shows conditions that are presently supportive of mangrove function — high DO, moderate pH, low salinity — but carries an anomalous conductivity signal at Big Ada bridge (50.4 µS) that warrants close tracking. This is the zone where early warning of saltwater intrusion into the creek network would appear first.

At the lagoon intake zone, the Patukope-Pute cluster is both the most saline and — when the soil data is integrated — the most compound-vulnerable. High-salinity water column conditions combined with potential acid sulphate soils make Pute a priority site for restoration assessment and a priority target for quarterly monitoring attention.

At the interior lagoon, the Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane cluster requires urgent attention at a level that goes beyond routine monitoring. ORP values of 832–1,547 mV do not represent elevated background conditions within a healthy system. They represent a geochemical departure from the conditions required for carbon storage and carbon sequestration to function. This departure has direct implications for the blue carbon credit potential of the Ada-Songor system and must be addressed explicitly in the Carbon Inventory Report.

The three simultaneous pressures driving this gradient are not independent and cannot be addressed independently. Loss of freshwater regulation from the Akosombo Dam is the system-level driver that has diminished freshwater dilution, reduced sediment input, and allowed higher-salinity water to penetrate further into the lagoon interior — setting the conditions under which the Pute salinisation and the interior ORP anomaly have developed. Salt pan operations reinforce the salinisation signal at the lagoon intake zone. Cattle grazing on wetland margins is adding nutrient loads to the system that will eventually become visible in the water column if the land-use pressure at Obane and Wasakuse is not managed. Each pressure requires its own management response, but the priority sequence should be driven by the severity and urgency of the ecological risk. On that assessment, the interior ORP anomaly — the direct threat to carbon storage function — is the highest-priority finding in the Ada-Songor water quality dataset, and it must be the first issue addressed in the monitoring programme's adaptive management cycle.
