# Chapter 1: Introduction and Strategic Context

---

## 1.1 The Strategic Case for Ecosystem Health Monitoring

Ghana's blue carbon programme rests on a single ecological reality: the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site contains an estimated 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves — approximately 69% of the entire coastal mangrove resource of Ghana. This is not a landscape that represents one of several comparable sites across the national coastline. It is, by a large margin, the dominant concentration of mangrove cover in the country, and its ecological condition directly determines whether Ghana can deliver credible, verifiable blue carbon outcomes at national scale. Alongside Keta, the Ada-Songor Lagoon System — comprising the Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, which covers 51,133 hectares and retains 465.76 hectares of mangrove habitat — represents Ghana's principal blue carbon restoration opportunity and a second internationally designated wetland with binding management obligations.

Ecosystem health monitoring at these two sites is not a scientific complement to carbon accounting. It is its foundation. The carbon sequestration rates that make blue carbon ecosystems economically and climatically significant — up to ten times greater per hectare than terrestrial forests — are not fixed properties of the landscape. They are functions of ecological condition. Healthy, productive mangrove systems with stable water chemistry, waterlogged anaerobic soils, and intact vegetation structure sequester and retain carbon at rates that justify conservation investment and support credible additionality claims in carbon markets. Degraded or stressed systems behave differently: elevated oxidation-reduction potential in waterlogged soils accelerates the mineralisation of stored organic carbon; acid sulphate conditions kill root systems and eliminate the below-ground biomass input that sustains soil carbon accumulation; hypersaline or nutrient-depleted conditions reduce canopy productivity and slow organic matter turnover. A site with declining ecosystem health is a site where carbon stocks are under active threat, where emission reductions become harder to verify, and where additionality arguments weaken.

This matters directly for the governance of Ghana's national blue carbon programme. The December 2025 field assessment at Keta identified an ALERT-level condition at the Anyanui-Tunu Road cluster, where a water pH of 2.7 — more than four pH units below the productive range for *Rhizophora racemosa* — was recorded alongside observations of dead mangroves. At Ada-Songor, oxidation-reduction potential values of 832 to 1,547 millivolts in the interior lagoon and creek systems at Patukope and Pute indicate hyperoxidising conditions that actively mineralise sediment organic carbon. These are not background findings. They are management signals — the first quantitative evidence that specific zones within Ghana's nationally significant wetlands are undergoing conditions that threaten the carbon stocks the national MRV system is designed to protect and track. Without a systematic ecosystem health monitoring programme producing the baseline against which these signals can be detected, compared, and escalated, they would remain invisible until the damage became irreversible.

The strategic case for ecosystem health monitoring is therefore inseparable from the case for Ghana's blue carbon programme itself. Decision-makers at MLNR, MESTI, and the World Bank can use this chapter to understand why field-based ecosystem condition assessment is not a Phase 2 add-on to the carbon inventory — it is the early-warning layer on which the credibility and longevity of the entire programme depends.

## 1.2 Ramsar Obligations and National Commitments

Ghana is a contracting party to the Ramsar Convention on Wetlands of International Importance. The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site, designated in 1992, and the Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve are both listed sites under that Convention, carrying formal obligations for ecological characterisation, wise-use management, and condition reporting. These are not aspirational commitments. They are legal obligations that require Ghana to maintain the ecological character of designated wetlands, to document threats and changes to that character, and to report to the Ramsar Secretariat on the status of listed sites. The December 2025 baseline field assessment — 387 water quality samples and 168 soil samples across 65 sampling stations at Keta and Ada — constitutes the first systematic physico-chemical baseline aligned simultaneously to Ramsar reporting requirements and the national blue carbon MRV framework. It gives Ghana, for the first time, quantitative evidence of the current ecological character of both listed sites in a format that serves both treaty obligations and programme management.

Ghana's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) under the Paris Agreement identifies the land sector — including blue carbon ecosystems — as the country's largest source of potential CO₂ removals. The scale of the Keta Lagoon Complex, holding nearly 70% of the national mangrove resource, means that the condition of that single site has disproportionate weight in Ghana's ability to demonstrate land-sector contributions to its NDC targets. WACA ResIP 2, financed by the World Bank under project reference P175525 and led by MESTI in collaboration with MLNR and the Wildlife Division of the Forestry Commission, provides the institutional and financial platform through which Ghana is advancing from national commitment to site-level implementation. The ecosystem health assessment mandate under Phase 2 of the consultancy — generating a scientific data baseline to guide sustainable management and long-term monitoring at the two Ramsar sites — is the direct operational expression of those national and international obligations.

Together, these commitments create an obligation structure that the ecosystem health monitoring framework in this report is designed to serve: Ramsar ecological characterisation reporting, NDC-aligned carbon outcome tracking, and WACA ResIP 2 programme monitoring and evaluation. The December 2025 baseline, and the 80-station quarterly monitoring network that flows from it, provide the evidence layer against which Ghana can demonstrate compliance with each of these obligations through data, not assertion.

## 1.3 Purpose and Scope of This Report

This report serves four distinct and operationally specific purposes.

The first is to present the December 2025 baseline findings in management-ready format. The physico-chemical data collected across both sites is organised by ecological cluster, assigned condition ratings — Good, Moderate, Stressed, or ALERT — and presented with priority actions mapped to each rating. This is not a data archive. It is a condition assessment that site managers, Wildlife Division officers, and district planning authorities can act on directly, without intermediary scientific interpretation.

The second is to establish a condition baseline for future monitoring cycle comparison. The December 2025 data constitutes the reference point — the ecological starting state — against which all subsequent quarterly monitoring results at the 80-station network will be compared. Future changes in water chemistry, soil nutrient profiles, and dissolved oxygen across the site network will only be interpretable as improvement, deterioration, or stability relative to what is documented here. This report is therefore both a standalone assessment and a permanent reference document in the national blue carbon monitoring record.

The third purpose is to identify priority management interventions. The two ALERT-level conditions at Anyanui-Tunu Road and the Ada-Songor interior lagoon require immediate action, and a set of Stressed- and Moderate-rated clusters require structured management attention within the current monitoring cycle. This report maps those priorities to specific institutional responsibilities — MLNR, MESTI, the Wildlife Division at district level, and the Ada East and Keta Municipal District Assemblies — so that each intervention has a named institutional owner and a defined escalation pathway if the initial management response is insufficient.

The fourth purpose is to provide the evidence layer for the broader Blue Carbon Assessment package, linking ecological condition findings to carbon stock credibility, additionality framing, and MRV system design. The condition ratings in this report directly inform which clusters are suitable for Permanent Sample Plot establishment, which zones require priority carbon-loss quantification, and how the national MRV system's escalation protocol should be calibrated against real-world ecological thresholds observed in the field.

Two clarifications on scope are necessary. This report is not a carbon inventory. It does not quantify above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, deadwood stocks, or soil organic carbon in tonnes per hectare — those measurements are the subject of the Carbon Inventory Standalone Report. This report documents the ecological conditions that determine whether carbon stocks are stable, accumulating, or at risk. Equally, this report is not the MRV governance framework. The institutional architecture, reporting cycles, verification protocols, and carbon accounting methodology that govern Ghana's national MRV system are set out in the MRV Framework Standalone Report. This report feeds that framework; it does not replicate it.

## 1.4 Structure of This Report

This report is organised to move from strategic context through site characterisation and field findings to management conclusions, building the ecological evidence layer in sequence before reaching its practical outputs.

Chapter 2 provides detailed profiles of both study sites — the Keta Lagoon Complex and the Ada-Songor Lagoon System — covering hydrological character, habitat types, species composition, and the community context within which the wetlands are managed. It is the primary reference for site managers and district authorities who need a complete picture of the landscape before engaging with the condition assessment.

Chapters 3 through 7 present the methodology and field findings: the supervised GIS sampling design, equipment specifications, and Community Change Agent training protocol (Chapter 3), followed by water quality results for Keta (Chapter 4) and Ada-Songor (Chapter 5), and soil health results for Keta (Chapter 6) and Ada-Songor (Chapter 7). These chapters are the technical substrate for researchers, ecological specialists, and MRV system managers who need the full parameter-level evidence base.

Chapter 8 draws the field data into the formal ecosystem health condition assessment — cluster-by-cluster ratings, health scoring criteria, and the carbon stock integrity implications of each rating. This chapter is the primary decision-support tool for site managers and Wildlife Division officers responsible for prioritising field interventions.

Chapter 9 synthesises the pressure and driver analysis: hydrological alteration from Akosombo Dam operations, fuelwood harvesting pressure, salt pan expansion, pollution, and climate variability impacts, organised by site and linked to the field signals that each pressure produces in the monitoring data.

Chapter 10 describes the 80-station quarterly monitoring framework — how the network operates, how Community Change Agents maintain the station network, how data flows from field to national platform, and how the escalation protocol functions. This chapter is the primary reference for MESTI and MLNR planners designing and resourcing the next monitoring cycle.

Chapter 11 presents the conclusions and management recommendations, organised by site and cluster, with institutional responsibilities and monitoring priorities specified. It is addressed to all decision-maker levels and provides the structured outputs from which programme management decisions can be taken immediately.

## 1.5 Relationship to the Four-Report Architecture

This report is one of four interconnected technical assessments that together constitute the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment delivered under WACA ResIP 2. Understanding the architecture prevents duplication, clarifies the complementary roles of each report, and makes explicit the data flows that link field condition findings to carbon market and policy reporting outcomes.

The four reports and their distinct functions are as follows. The National MRV Framework Report establishes the governance architecture of the national monitoring system: the four institutional tiers, the three MRV layers — Ecological and Carbon Integrity, Environmental Pressure Surveillance, and Governance and Safeguard Assurance — the reporting cycle, the carbon accounting protocol, and the Keta pilot implementation detail. The Drone and EOSDA Monitoring Report provides the remote sensing layer: drone survey Area of Interest delineation, EOSDA satellite platform integration, and the NDMI- and NDVI-based alert thresholds that trigger ground-truthing at spatially defined monitoring locations. The Carbon Inventory Standalone Report — currently in preparation — will quantify above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, deadwood, and soil organic carbon stocks in tonnes per hectare at Permanent Sample Plot locations, providing the baseline carbon stock numbers from which future emissions reductions and sequestration increments will be calculated. This report — the Ecosystem Health Monitoring Report — provides the field-based ecological condition assessment that connects and validates the other three.

The data flows between these reports are directional and sequential. Ecosystem health condition findings from this report feed the carbon inventory in two ways: the cluster condition ratings identify which zones are appropriate candidates for Permanent Sample Plot establishment (Good-condition clusters are the primary candidates), and the ALERT-level findings at Anyanui-Tunu Road and the Ada-Songor interior lagoon identify zones where active carbon stock loss quantification is a priority. Ecosystem health condition data also feeds the MRV Framework directly: the two ALERT conditions documented in December 2025 are real-world examples of the ecological thresholds that the MRV escalation protocol is designed to detect and route to institutional response, and the 80-station quarterly network described in Chapter 10 of this report is the field data layer of MRV Layers I and II. Remote sensing alerts from the Drone and EOSDA report are cross-referenced with the physico-chemical station data from this report to distinguish between satellite-detected canopy changes driven by water chemistry stress and those driven by physical disturbance or direct vegetation removal.

The architectural logic is this: a site rated Good-condition in the ecosystem health assessment supports straightforward carbon stock conservation claims. A site rated Stressed or ALERT requires a different carbon accounting treatment — one that acknowledges the risk of ongoing stock loss, adjusts additionality arguments accordingly, and builds the monitoring density necessary to detect further deterioration before it undermines verification. Ecosystem health condition, as established in this report, is the ecological input that makes the carbon inventory, the MRV framework, and the remote sensing monitoring system interpretable. It is not peripheral to the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment. It is the baseline on which the credibility of the entire programme stands.
