# Chapter 8: Integrated Ecosystem Health Condition Assessment

---

## 8.0 Chapter Overview

This chapter is the analytical centrepiece of the Standalone Ecosystem Health Monitoring Report. Its purpose is to transform the parameter-by-parameter findings of Chapters 4 through 7 into a single, coherent management decision tool: a condition score for every monitoring cluster across both sites, linked explicitly to carbon stock integrity, and translated into a prioritised action framework that site managers at MLNR, MESTI, and the Wildlife Division can use directly.

Chapters 4 and 5 established the water quality and soil quality baseline across five ecological clusters at the Keta Lagoon Complex, ranging from the acid sulphate emergency at the Anyanui-Tunu road to the high-performance benchmark carbon environment at Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi. Chapters 6 and 7 did the same across four cluster zones at the Ada-Songor Lagoon, from the Volta-stabilised freshwater baseline at Azizanya-Ayigbo to the nutrient-loaded and ORP-compromised interior at Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane. Chapter 8 synthesises those findings into a unified assessment architecture: the same classification language, the same carbon accounting implications, and the same management priority logic applied consistently across all 13 assessment zones.

The chapter is organised into six sections. Section 8.1 defines the four-tier classification system with quantitative criteria for each tier. Sections 8.2 and 8.3 deliver the cluster-by-cluster condition scores for Keta and Ada respectively — a detailed table plus explanatory narrative for each cluster. Section 8.4 presents the master integrated summary table, the single most important table in the report. Section 8.5 translates condition scores into carbon accounting language for the Carbon Inventory Report and MRV platform. Section 8.6 sets out the management priority matrix for MLNR and MESTI, organising all required interventions into a feasibility-weighted action sequence.

## 8.1 Condition Assessment Framework

### 8.1.1 The Logic of a Four-Tier System

Most ecological assessment frameworks employ a three-tier classification — Good, Moderate, and Poor — because this maps conveniently onto traffic-light communication for non-specialist audiences. This report uses four tiers: **Good**, **Moderate**, **Stressed**, and **Alert**. The reason for the additional tier is specific to the Ghana Blue Carbon context and the data in front of us.

The parameter values recorded at the Anyanui-Tunu road stations (water pH 2.7, active acid sulphate soil formation, dead mangrove canopy, endangered legacy carbon stock) and the Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane stations (ORP 832–1,547 mV, nitrate 984 mg/kg, phosphate 705 mg/kg, potassium 1,280 mg/kg — all exceeding MRV alert thresholds) cannot meaningfully be classified alongside sites that are merely showing stress signals. They represent qualitatively different ecological states — states in which core ecosystem function has not merely been impaired but where active and accelerating carbon loss is underway, and where delay in management response is itself measurable as irreversible carbon stock destruction. Compressing these into a single "Poor" tier would obscure the distinction between a site that needs increased monitoring and one that needs an emergency engineering intervention this quarter.

The four tiers are therefore defined as follows, with explicit quantitative criteria for each.

### 8.1.2 Tier Definitions and Quantitative Criteria

**GOOD** — Ecosystem function within optimal or near-optimal ranges. All monitored parameters consistent with conditions that actively support mangrove productivity and soil organic carbon accumulation. Carbon sequestration conditions fully maintained. Standard monitoring cadence appropriate.

*Water quality criteria:* pH 6.0–8.0; salinity within species tolerance (5–35 ppt for *Rhizophora*; up to 90 ppt for *Avicennia* under natural conditions); ORP between −200 and +300 mV (reducing to mildly oxidising); DO >5 mg/L; no anomalous nutrient or conductivity loading.

*Soil quality criteria:* Humus >25%; moisture >25%; soil pH 5.5–7.5; nitrate and phosphate within natural mangrove-soil range; potassium supportive of productivity; no acid sulphate indicators.

*Vegetation and carbon:* Mangrove canopy intact. SOC accumulation conditions maintained at benchmark levels. No management intervention required beyond standard protection and monitoring.

---

**MODERATE** — Ecosystem function showing measurable stress signals, but core function not yet impaired. One or more parameters outside optimal range but not exceeding critical thresholds. Carbon sequestration likely continuing but at reduced rate relative to benchmark. Management attention and monitoring intensification required; passive intervention or trend surveillance may be sufficient at this stage.

*Water quality criteria:* pH 5.5–6.0 or 8.0–8.5 (approaching but not crossing critical boundaries); salinity at upper end of optimal range or slightly exceeding it; ORP +300 to +600 mV (moderately oxidising); DO 3–5 mg/L; conductivity or TDS elevated above benchmark but not at critical levels.

*Soil quality criteria:* Humus 15–25%; moisture 20–25% (at or approaching the anaerobic threshold); soil pH approaching 5.5 or above 7.5; nutrients at sub-optimal or marginal levels.

*Vegetation and carbon:* Mangrove canopy partially degraded or vegetation under stress. Carbon sequestration likely continuing but reduced. SOC stable for now, but trend monitoring essential. Restoration feasibility assessable with additional data.

---

**STRESSED** — Core ecosystem function impaired. Multiple parameters outside acceptable ranges, or one parameter at or near a defined critical threshold. Carbon storage conditions compromised — existing stocks may be at risk of mineralisation or destabilisation. Active management intervention required; passive monitoring insufficient.

*Water quality criteria:* pH <5.5 or >8.5; salinity consistently above species optimal range (>40 ppt for *Rhizophora* or persistently hypersaline); ORP +600 to +800 mV (strongly oxidising); DO approaching 3 mg/L or below during monitoring; conductivity or TDS significantly above benchmark.

*Soil quality criteria:* Humus <15%; moisture consistently below 20%; soil pH below 5.5 or above 8.0; nutrient depletion or enrichment at levels interfering with biological function; acid sulphate indicators present.

*Vegetation and carbon:* Mangrove canopy significantly degraded or absent. Carbon storage conditions compromised — current stocks at meaningful risk. Active site management, community engagement, and hydrological assessment required.

---

**ALERT** — Immediate management action required. One or more parameters at catastrophic or irreversible-risk levels. Carbon storage at direct and current risk. Delay in intervention will result in irreversible ecological and carbon losses that cannot be recovered on any management-relevant timescale.

*Alert threshold triggers (any one of the following is sufficient for Alert classification):*
- Water pH below 4.0 (with pH 2.7 representing the extreme documented at Keta)
- Soil pH below 4.5 (approaching or at confirmed acid sulphate conditions)
- ORP sustained above +800 mV in water column (indicating strongly oxidising geochemical conditions that are actively hostile to carbon preservation)
- Nitrate at mangrove-proximate soil stations >500 mg/kg (MRV eutrophication alert threshold — exceeded at 984 mg/kg at Wasakuse)
- Active dead mangrove canopy with co-occurring water or soil acidification
- Multiple STRESSED parameters in combination at the same station, indicating compound ecological failure

*Carbon management implication at ALERT:* Carbon stocks at these sites should not be reported as stable in any carbon accounting framework. Sites may be net emitters rather than sinks. Immediate hydrological investigation and management action is required before any carbon crediting can be applied to these zones.

## 8.2 Cluster-by-Cluster Condition Scores — Keta Lagoon Complex

### 8.2.1 Assessment Cluster Network

The December 2025 baseline assessment at Keta Lagoon Complex established five ecological clusters, encompassing 27 water quality stations and 21 soil sampling stations. The cluster framework maps the ecological gradient from the Volta-influenced northwestern zone through the productive mangrove core to the evaporatively stressed main lagoon bank. Each cluster receives a water quality condition score, a soil quality condition score, an integrated overall condition, a carbon stock integrity rating, and a priority management action.

---

### Cluster 1: Anyanui-Dzita

**Water Quality Score: ALERT (Tunu Road stations) / STRESSED (remaining cluster)**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*pH at Anyanui-Tunu Road stations: 2.7.* This is the single most critical ecological finding in the entire monitoring dataset, across both sites. A pH of 2.7 is more than four logarithmic units below the lower threshold of the productive mangrove zone (pH 6.5), corresponding to more than ten thousand times the hydrogen ion concentration. It is 1.3 units below the defined Alert threshold of pH 4.0 and places the water chemistry at these two stations firmly in the range of dilute sulphuric acid. The mechanism is acid sulphate soil formation: pyrite-bearing sediments historically stabilised under anaerobic, waterlogged intertidal conditions have been exposed to atmospheric oxygen following the disruption of tidal exchange at the road corridor. The oxidation of iron sulphide is the active source of the acidity, and it is self-reinforcing — every dry-season cycle without hydrological restoration expands the zone of acidification and deepens the chemical damage to the sediment profile.

*Salinity at Dzita: 40 ppt.* This hypersaline reading at the main lagoon body station represents the upper limit of physiological tolerance for *Rhizophora racemosa*. At 40 ppt, mangrove growth rates decline, osmotic regulation consumes energy diverted from biomass production, and carbon sequestration rates fall below those achievable in the optimal 5–25 ppt range. *Avicennia germinans* retains broader salinity tolerance and may persist at this concentration, but the species-composition shift it represents reduces the carbon productivity of the site relative to the productive Saviatula-Galotse benchmark.

*Overall water chemistry at six remaining stations: pH 7.1–7.59, salinity 4.3–40 ppt.* The six stations outside the Tunu road anomaly fall within broadly acceptable ecological ranges, though the freshwater-influenced western stations (4.3 ppt salinity) are at risk of progressive salinisation as Volta River discharge continues to decline. The ORP at Dzita (160 mV) is consistent with functional mangrove habitat; the ORP regime at the Tunu road stations is, by contrast, actively oxidising — the chemical signature of pyrite oxidation rather than carbon-preserving reduction.

**Soil Quality Score: ALERT (Tunu Road dead mangrove site) / MODERATE (Agbledorne sub-zone) / STRESSED (Dzita)**

*Humus at Tunu Road dead mangrove site: 44.3%.* This is the highest humus value in the entire Keta dataset — and its location at the site of active acid sulphate formation transforms it from an indication of ecological success into an endangered carbon stock. The 44.3% humus is the legacy SOC capital built over decades or centuries of functional mangrove ecosystem operation. The mangroves that built it are dead. The acid sulphate chemistry is advancing toward the organic layer. The gap between those two facts defines the urgency of the management response.

*Moisture and pH at Tunu Road:* Quarterly tracking must determine whether moisture at these stations has dropped below the 25% anaerobic threshold and whether the acid sulphate chemistry documented in the water column has begun penetrating the soil profile (evidenced by soil pH below 5.5 at Tunu road stations). The December 2025 baseline recorded soil pH values within the site-wide range of 5.55–7.0, indicating the acid front had not yet reached the organic layer — but the water pH of 2.7 is the leading edge of a process that moves from water to soil over successive seasonal cycles.

*Agbledorne sub-zone:* The three Agbledorne soil stations, within the same cluster, retain partial tidal connectivity. Humus values fall within the moderate moist-site range; nutrient values (nitrate 37–73 mg/kg, phosphate 52–103 mg/kg) are consistent with partially functional mangrove soil chemistry. This sub-zone is stressed but not yet at Alert level.

**Overall Cluster Condition: ALERT (Anyanui-Tunu Road zone) / STRESSED (Dzita margin) / MODERATE (Agbledorne sub-zone)**

The Anyanui-Dzita cluster cannot be assigned a single condition score because it contains two co-occurring ecological states separated by a boundary that the December 2025 baseline has identified but not fully mapped. For management purposes, the ALERT classification governs: any cluster containing an Alert-level station requires Alert-priority management response, regardless of the condition of other stations within the same geographic grouping.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: CRITICAL (Tunu Road) / AT RISK (Dzita) / MEDIUM (Agbledorne)**

The 44.3% humus at the Tunu Road dead mangrove site constitutes the highest carbon stock value in the Keta network, co-located with the most acute active threat to carbon stability. It should not be reported as a stable carbon stock in any carbon accounting framework until hydrological restoration has been initiated and quarterly monitoring has confirmed that soil pH at the Tunu Road stations is not declining below 5.5. The Dzita carbon stock is limited by hypersalinity and associated reduced species composition and productivity. Agbledorne's carbon stock is moderate and partially stable.

**Priority Management Action:** Commission a field hydrological investigation at Anyanui-Tunu Road within 14 days of report receipt, specifically to identify the culvert failure, road blockage, or drainage modification responsible for tidal exchange disruption, and issue a formal engineering recommendation for tidal restoration — this is the network's highest management priority and must proceed independently of and ahead of all other site management activities.

---

*Narrative interpretation:* The Anyanui-Dzita cluster is the most analytically complex — and the most management-urgent — zone in the entire monitoring network. It contains, within a relatively small geographic footprint, both the highest legacy SOC value and the most acute active ecological threat. The pH 2.7 at the Tunu Road stations is not a data anomaly to be verified in a future monitoring round. It is the confirmed signature of an established, ongoing acid sulphate soil formation event. The combination of dead mangrove canopy, acid water, and the highest humus content in the dataset at the same coordinates is the most powerful carbon management signal in the assessment: it tells us exactly where the largest single intervention dividend lies, and exactly how little time there is to realise it. The organic carbon at 44.3% humus is warming — literally, as solar radiation heats the exposed, unshaded sediment surface — and it is acidifying, as the sulphuric acid generated by pyrite oxidation migrates through the soil profile. The science of what must happen here is not in doubt. The management decision required is a physical one: restore tidal exchange, arrest the acid sulphate process, and give the SOC the chance to stabilise. Every quarter without that intervention is a quarter in which irreplaceable blue carbon is converted to atmospheric CO₂.

### Cluster 2: Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi

**Water Quality Score: GOOD**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*pH: 6.5–7.6.* This is the optimal ecological range for *Rhizophora racemosa* productivity in the Keta system — the band within which all biochemical functions from root respiration to nutrient uptake to microbial decomposition cycling operate at full efficiency. The range reflects the stable interaction between tidal exchange, freshwater influence, and the buffering chemistry of well-developed mangrove organic soils in this zone.

*Salinity: 10.6–40 ppt.* The range encompasses the full productive span of the two dominant mangrove species in the complex, with the lower end (10.6 ppt, near the Salo and Genui stations) representing the conditions under which *Rhizophora racemosa* achieves its highest above-ground biomass and most vigorous below-ground root investment. This is the reference salinity benchmark for the entire complex.

*DO: High across open stations; ORP: mildly oxidising (water column), reducing (sediment profile inferred from humus data).* The co-occurrence of well-aerated surface water and deep-reducing sediment conditions — confirmed by the 40.6% humus at Galotse and 37.5% at Agortoe — is the defining geochemical signature of a functioning blue carbon system: oxygen available to support aquatic life and root-associated microbial communities at the surface, while anaerobic conditions in the sediment depth profile preserve the organic carbon that has accumulated there.

**Soil Quality Score: GOOD**

*Humus: 40.6% (Galotse), 37.5% (Agortoe); site-wide moist-station range 14.6–26.2%.* These values represent the endpoint of decades of undisturbed, anaerobic, continuously waterlogged organic matter accumulation in a high-productivity *Rhizophora* stand. They are the highest non-anomalous humus readings in the monitoring network and the benchmark against which all restoration and recovery targets are measured.

*Moisture: 40.4% (Galotse), consistently above 25% across the cluster.* A moisture value of 40.4% indicates essentially saturated soil — pore spaces filled with water, atmospheric oxygen exchange with the soil matrix at effectively zero, anaerobic reducing conditions fully established. The co-occurrence of 40.4% moisture and 40.6% humus at Galotse is the quantitative expression of the blue carbon mechanism itself.

*Soil pH: 6.2–7.0.* The optimal range for the simultaneous availability of phosphate, active nutrient cycling, and anaerobic suppression of aerobic bacterial decomposition. This pH band is the chemical sweet spot of blue carbon soil function — not so acidic as to limit nutrient bioavailability, not so alkaline as to activate oxidising decomposition pathways that would begin consuming the humus pool.

*Potassium: highest in the Keta network at Agbastivi and Galotse (78–201 mg/kg in moist-site range).* Elevated potassium is a direct indicator that the litter-fall and decomposition cycle is operating at high intensity — the trees are productive, the litter input is abundant, and the decomposition process is releasing nutrients at rates consistent with a high-functioning, high-SOC-accumulating ecosystem.

**Overall Cluster Condition: GOOD**

This cluster is functioning at or near the ecological optimum for blue carbon mangrove habitat. All nine measured water quality parameters and all seven soil parameters at the core stations are consistent with conditions that actively support mangrove productivity and SOC accumulation. The Salo Permanent Sample Plot (PSP-1) is located within this cluster, meaning that the carbon stock measurements at PSP-1 are being taken in a water and soil chemistry environment that is actively sustaining the growth and carbon accumulation processes the PSP data quantifies.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: HIGH**

This cluster holds the most important blue carbon soils in Ghana. Carbon stocks at the Galotse and Agortoe stations are actively accumulating, the conditions sustaining them are intact, and the ecological processes responsible for those conditions are operating at full function. This is the primary carbon reference zone for the entire national programme.

**Priority Management Action:** Maintain zero-tolerance protection of tidal exchange conditions in the Salo-Galotse-Agortoe corridor — any infrastructure proposal, drainage modification, or channel dredging activity in this zone must undergo a formal ecological impact assessment specifically evaluating the risk to the water chemistry balance before any approval is granted.

---

*Narrative interpretation:* The Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi cluster is the reason the Keta Lagoon Complex contains approximately 69% of Ghana's national mangrove resource. Everything that is working in this cluster — the pH balance, the salinity gradient, the tidal exchange that maintains soil saturation, the *Rhizophora* canopy that shades and feeds the soil — is the product of ecological conditions that have remained, over a long period, within the range that allows the blue carbon system to function. That these conditions have persisted is not inevitable. It is the result of geography, hydrology, and — at this stage in the assessment — the absence of the kind of hydrological disruption that produced the acid sulphate crisis two kilometres to the northwest. Management of this cluster must understand that its good condition is not a guarantee of future good condition: it is a current state that requires active protection. A single road construction project cutting across a tidal channel in this zone, without adequate culverts and tidal exchange infrastructure, could produce the same acid sulphate conditions at Galotse or Agortoe that have already produced them at the Tunu Road. The 40.6% humus that is being protected here is the carbon equivalent of a national natural heritage site. It must be managed accordingly.

### Cluster 3: Aborlorvi-Norlopi

**Water Quality Score: MODERATE**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*pH: 7.2–7.8.* The range falls within the acceptable zone for mangrove establishment, and critically, there is no acid sulphate emergency at these stations. The upper end (7.8) approaches the elevated values of the main lagoon bank corridor (7.8–8.2) and represents the boundary condition between optimal and sub-optimal chemistry for *Rhizophora* establishment. Whether pH at these stations stabilises within the productive range or drifts upward is a key restoration feasibility question that the quarterly monitoring programme will answer across the first two to three rounds.

*Conductivity and TDS: elevated relative to the benchmark cluster (Figure 3 y-axis extends to 600, vs. 180 for the benchmark cluster).* The elevated parameter values relative to the Saviatula-Galotse zone indicate that the water chemistry at the restoration sites is more concentrated in dissolved ions than the reference productive mangrove environment. This may reflect evaporative concentration, agricultural inputs, or domestic waste — distinguishing between these sources is a prerequisite for finalising species selection and planting design.

*DO: Above critical minimum at all stations.* The absence of hypoxic conditions at the restoration site stations confirms that the water chemistry is not actively hostile to seedling establishment. This is a necessary positive baseline finding.

**Soil Quality Score: MODERATE**

*Moisture: 25.4% at the most lagoon-proximate station.* This is the most important soil finding for restoration planning, and it is a boundary value. At 25.4%, the Aborlorvi site is at the edge of the soil moisture conditions associated with the anaerobic, SOC-preserving regime. It is above the 25% threshold that indicates water-table proximity, confirming tidal or lagoon water-table influence at this location — but it is substantially below the 40.4% at Galotse and 38.7% at Genui, both of which represent continuously saturated conditions. Restoration success here depends critically on whether this moisture value rises significantly in the wet season.

*Humus: lower transitional range.* The Aborlorvi soils are not organically depleted to the point of biological hostility for mangrove establishment, but they have not yet developed the deep SOC pool characteristic of mature mangrove habitat. The gap between the current Aborlorvi humus baseline and the Galotse benchmark of 40.6% is the carbon recovery opportunity that successful restoration at this site represents.

*Soil pH and nutrients: within acceptable ranges, no acid sulphate indicators.* The fundamental chemical preconditions for mangrove seedling establishment are met: no extreme acidification, no critical nutrient depletion. Nutrients at the upland stations (nitrate 1–23 mg/kg, phosphate 1–32 mg/kg, potassium 2–65 mg/kg) are at the lower end of the range, reflecting the absence of the self-reinforcing productivity cycle of mature mangrove habitat — but these are starting conditions, not disqualifying conditions.

**Overall Cluster Condition: MODERATE (Restoration Feasible with Monitoring-Guided Design)**

The Aborlorvi-Norlopi cluster is ecologically viable as a restoration site — the water quality presents no chemical barrier to replanting comparable to the Tunu Road acid emergency, and the soil chemistry, while at the boundary of optimal conditions, does not disqualify establishment. The feasibility question that the December 2025 baseline does not yet resolve — and that the quarterly monitoring programme is specifically designed to answer — is whether the 25.4% soil moisture at the most water-proximate station rises sufficiently in the wet season to confirm that the proposed planting locations experience adequate inundation depth and frequency for *Rhizophora racemosa* establishment.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: MEDIUM**

Carbon stocks at Aborlorvi are at a lower baseline level than the Galotse benchmark, reflecting the partially degraded, seasonally drying conditions of this transitional zone. The restoration opportunity, if hydrological validation supports it, is to build new SOC from this moderate baseline over the coming decades.

**Priority Management Action:** Conduct at minimum two full seasonal monitoring cycles (wet-season and dry-season moisture readings) at the proposed restoration sites before committing to planting at scale; prioritise planting in lagoon-proximate micro-locations where moisture values most closely approach the 30–35% range consistent with seasonally saturated intertidal conditions.

### Cluster 4: Genui Reference Station

**Water Quality Score: GOOD**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*pH: 6.5–7.6.* Grouped with the benchmark Saviatula-Galotse cluster in the source assessment — confirming that the optimal water chemistry conditions of the productive mangrove core extend to the northeastern reference point of the monitoring network.

*Salinity: 10.6–40 ppt.* Within the same productive range as the core mangrove cluster. The Genui station is not experiencing the hypersaline isolation of the Dzita station; it is positioned within or at the edge of the intermediate salinity zone that supports active mangrove development.

*DO and ORP: mildly oxidising surface water consistent with functioning mangrove habitat.* No indication of hypoxic stress or anomalous redox conditions.

**Soil Quality Score: GOOD**

*Moisture: 38.7%.* The second highest moisture reading in the Keta network, exceeded only by Galotse (40.4%). At 38.7%, the Genui soil is operating in the anaerobic, SOC-preserving regime. In terms of soil carbon function, Genui aligns with the Galotse cluster rather than the drier, upland-influenced stations of Aborlorvi. This confirms that the SOC-preserving conditions extend to the northeastern sector of the complex — the high-value soil carbon zone is broader than the core cluster alone.

*pH, nutrients, humus:* All within the acceptable range for productive mangrove soils, consistent with the water chemistry profile. No acid sulphate indicators.

**Overall Cluster Condition: GOOD**

As a single-station reference point, Genui cannot capture intra-cluster variation. Its value lies in confirming that the optimal water and soil chemistry conditions of the Saviatula-Galotse core extend to the northeastern reference point — and in establishing the northeastern anchor for early detection of progressive salinisation, which would appear first at this marginal station if the ecological space available for productive mangrove habitat begins to compress.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: HIGH**

The 38.7% soil moisture confirms that carbon preservation conditions at Genui are equivalent in quality to the core benchmark cluster. Whatever SOC has accumulated under these continuously saturated conditions is in an active preservation regime.

**Priority Management Action:** Maintain quarterly monitoring at this single northeastern station specifically to detect early upward trends in salinity or conductivity — the first signal of the hydrological deterioration that would reduce the productive zone's northeastern extent.

### Cluster 5: Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga

**Water Quality Score: STRESSED**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*pH: 7.8–8.2.* Consistently above the optimal range for *Rhizophora racemosa* productivity (6.5–7.6) and approaching the upper boundary of the acceptable range. The elevation is driven by the combined effects of reduced freshwater inflow to this main lagoon bank corridor, intense evaporative concentration, and the photosynthetic CO₂ drawdown of algal communities under high afternoon temperatures — which progressively alkalises the water column during daylight hours. At pH values above 8.0, phosphate availability to mangrove root systems begins to decline, seedling establishment becomes more difficult, and the chemical conditions for maximum SOC accumulation are sub-optimal. This is not a crisis of the scale of the Anyanui acid anomaly, but it is a chronic, multi-parameter stressor.

*Conductivity and TDS: highest values in the Keta network (y-axis extends to 1,100 in the cluster figure, vs. 180 for the benchmark cluster).* The main lagoon bank stations record conductivity and TDS values substantially above any other cluster in the network, reflecting the compound effect of the large, evaporation-exposed main lagoon body receiving limited freshwater input. These values indicate that the dissolved ion load in this water is driving salinity conditions that approach or reach the hypersaline range at the most exposed stations.

*Thermal loading: afternoon temperature peaks 31.19–37.3°C at open lagoon stations.* High temperatures at the main lagoon bank stations directly reduce dissolved oxygen solubility, elevate pH through CO₂ outgassing, and accelerate the rate of organic carbon mineralisation in any adjacent sediment. The open, unshaded character of the main lagoon bank — unlike the canopy-buffered interior stations of the Saviatula-Galotse core — means this thermal stress is experienced at maximum intensity throughout the dry season.

**Soil Quality Score: MODERATE**

*Afiadenyigba moisture: 26.4%.* Above the 25% anaerobic threshold, confirming periodic rather than continuous tidal inundation. SOC is partially exposed to decomposition during dry phases of the tidal cycle — the carbon storage efficiency of this station is lower than the continuously saturated core mangrove zone stations.

*Water-soil interaction risk:* The elevated water pH (7.8–8.2) at the main lagoon bank exerts a progressive alkalinising influence on soils flooded by that water. While the December 2025 soil pH at Afiadenyigba fell within the site-wide range of 5.55–7.0 (indicating the alkaline water influence had not yet shifted the soil beyond its buffering capacity), the trajectory must be tracked quarterly. A progressive upward drift in soil pH toward 7.5 and above would reduce phosphate bioavailability and slow productivity — the chain of consequences that progressively reduces the SOC accumulation rate at these stations.

**Overall Cluster Condition: STRESSED**

The Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga cluster presents a consistent and ecologically coherent picture of a thermally and salinically stressed main lagoon bank environment. The elevated pH, high conductivity and TDS, limited freshwater input, and temperature-driven alkalisation combine to describe a zone operating near or beyond the outer boundary of optimal mangrove habitat. This is not an emergency requiring immediate intervention of the type demanded at Anyanui, but it is a stressed system whose trajectory — under climate change projections of higher temperatures and reduced rainfall — points toward worsening conditions over the coming decade.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: AT RISK**

The limited mangrove fringe along this corridor is operating under chemical sub-optimality, reducing the rate of new SOC accumulation relative to the benchmark zone. Carbon stocks that do exist are partially exposed to aerobic decomposition during tidal dry phases. The direction of travel under projected climate trajectories — warmer, saltier, more alkaline — is toward further reduction in both SOC accumulation capacity and existing stock stability.

**Priority Management Action:** Implement paired morning and afternoon monitoring (to detect diel pH and DO oscillation) at the highest-risk stations (Woe landing bridge, Anloga main market), and enforce zero-tolerance management of mangrove harvesting along the main lagoon bank — the residual mangrove fringe here is the only carbon and coastal protection asset along the most heavily populated stretch of the lagoon, and it cannot sustain additional canopy loss under current conditions.

## 8.3 Cluster-by-Cluster Condition Scores — Ada-Songor Lagoon

### 8.3.1 Assessment Cluster Network

The December 2025 baseline assessment at the Ada-Songor Lagoon established four water quality cluster zones, encompassing 23 water quality monitoring stations and four primary soil sampling station areas. The cluster framework maps the ecological gradient from the Volta-influenced freshwater baseline in the north and northwest through the transitional creek system to the high-salinity, ORP-compromised interior of the lagoon.

---

### Cluster 6: Azizanya-Ayigbo

**Water Quality Score: GOOD**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*Conductivity: 6.23 µS (Azizanya-Kewunor bridge), 2.64 µS (Ayigbo bridge).* The lowest conductivity values in the entire Ada monitoring network, and the Ayigbo reading in particular sits close to the conductivity of the main Volta River itself. These are the fingerprint of an active, surviving freshwater pulse — confirming that the Volta's diluting influence remains measurable and ecologically significant at this cluster despite the Akosombo Dam's interception of more than 90% of historical sediment load.

*TDS: 1.95 ppm (Ayigbo) — the lowest in the entire Ada dataset.* An extraordinarily low concentration of dissolved material confirming that the water at Ayigbo bridge is essentially riverine in character, dominated by the Volta's freshwater discharge rather than by lagoon-origin dissolved ions.

*pH: 7.6–8.0.* Circumneutral to slightly alkaline — within the optimal range for mangrove establishment and consistent with carbonate-buffered water under moderate freshwater influence.

*ORP: 11–76.6 mV.* The lowest ORP values in the Ada network. This low, near-reducing ORP is consistent with the organic-rich, freshwater-influenced conditions of the river estuary margin and represents the geochemical baseline of what the Ada system's carbon storage conditions would look like under stronger freshwater influence. The contrast with the 832–1,547 mV at the interior lagoon could not be more stark.

**Soil Quality Score: GOOD**

Soil chemistry at the Azizanya-Ayigbo zone, assessed within the broader Ada monitoring framework, is consistent with the freshwater-influenced estuarine character of these stations. pH, moisture, and organic matter conditions reflect the stabilising influence of the Volta's freshwater input.

**Overall Cluster Condition: GOOD**

The Azizanya-Ayigbo cluster is the healthiest ecological zone in the Ada-Songor monitoring network and the historical reference baseline for the system as a whole — the closest available approximation of what the lagoon's water chemistry looked like before the Akosombo Dam reduced the Volta's freshwater discharge and sediment delivery. It functions as the monitoring system's canary for progressive salinisation of the entire Ada gradient: any sustained upward trend in conductivity or salinity at these two stations across successive quarterly rounds must be treated as a system-level alarm.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: HIGH**

The Volta-stabilised, near-reducing ORP conditions at Azizanya-Ayigbo are the most favourable carbon-preserving conditions in the Ada network. The near-zero salinity and low conductivity support the establishment and productivity of *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* in their most productive salinity range. SOC accumulation conditions are intact and actively maintained by the surviving Volta freshwater pulse.

**Priority Management Action:** Monitor conductivity and salinity at Ayigbo bridge as the primary early-warning indicator for Ada-system-wide salinisation — any upward trend sustained across two consecutive quarterly rounds must be escalated to the Ada East District Assembly as a hydrological system-level alert.

### Cluster 7: Patukope-Pute

**Water Quality Score: STRESSED/ALERT**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*Salinity: 28.6 ppt (Pute walkway lagoon intake), 28.2 ppt (Patukope creek).* The highest salinity values in the entire Ada dataset — approaching marine salinity and confirming the compound effect of reduced Volta freshwater dilution and salt pan brine seepage at the lagoon intake zone. At this salinity, the water chemistry is at or near the upper operational limit for productive *Rhizophora racemosa* establishment.

*TDS: 57.3 ppm (Pute lagoon intake) — the highest in the Ada network.* Consistent with the concentrated dissolved solid load of evaporatively concentrated lagoon water at the saline intake zone.

*Compound risk — water salinity over near-acid-sulphate soils:* When saline water intrudes over soils with a pH of 4.78 — as is occurring at Pute — the oxidation products generated can further acidify both the soil and any overlying water during drainage events. The monitoring protocol at Pute must specifically track water pH and conductivity together quarterly, watching for the acidification signal in the water column that would indicate active acid sulphate soil exposure. This joint water-soil risk puts Pute at the boundary between Stressed and Alert classifications.

**Soil Quality Score: ALERT**

*Soil pH: 4.78 (Pute lagoon, white mangroves).* The lowest soil pH in the entire monitoring network. At 4.78, aluminium and iron become soluble at concentrations toxic to plant roots; phosphate availability collapses through binding in aluminium and iron complexes that plant roots cannot access; the soil chemistry is approaching confirmed acid sulphate territory (threshold pH 4.5). The mechanism is pyrite oxidation following the disruption of waterlogged, anaerobic conditions — the same process as at Keta's Tunu Road, at a less extreme stage.

*Humus: 4.78% (Pute lagoon intake), 9.2% (Pute lagoon white mangroves).* The lowest humus values in the entire monitoring network — approximately 12% and 23% of the Galotse benchmark respectively. These severely depleted values reflect the long-term absence of mangrove canopy (no litter input), the loss of waterlogged conditions that would preserve organic matter, and the accelerating decomposition driven by the 38.4°C surface temperature at the exposed Pute walkway station.

*Nutrient depletion across all parameters: N = 3 mg/kg, P = 3 mg/kg, K = 7 mg/kg.* These are at or near the detection floor — a nutrient-depleted soil environment in which mangrove seedling establishment would fail regardless of other conditions, because there is no nitrogen for protein synthesis, no phosphate for root development, and no potassium for osmotic regulation in a saline environment.

*Soil temperature: 38.4°C.* The highest surface temperature in the Ada monitoring network — a direct marker of canopy absence. At this temperature, organic matter decomposition is proceeding at maximum rate, converting whatever residual humus remains at 4.78%–9.2% into CO₂ rather than allowing it to accumulate.

**Overall Cluster Condition: ALERT**

The Patukope-Pute cluster is the most degraded soil environment in the entire Ghana Blue Carbon monitoring network, and the compound vulnerability of near-acid-sulphate soils interacting with high-salinity lagoon water column conditions constitutes an active carbon loss process. This is an ecological and carbon accounting emergency. The site is not merely stressed — it is in active decline, losing the organic matter and carbon-preserving conditions that define its blue carbon value, and the trajectory without intervention is continued deterioration.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: CRITICAL**

Carbon stocks at Pute should not be reported as stable in any carbon accounting framework. The combination of near-acid-sulphate soil pH, severely depleted humus, nutrient depletion, maximum surface temperature, and strongly oxidising water column ORP (832–1,236 mV in the adjacent interior lagoon) describes a site that is currently a net emitter of CO₂ rather than a carbon sink. Active soil preparation and restoration investment — liming, organic matter addition, nutrient supplementation, hydrological management to reduce saline intrusion — is required before any carbon accumulation function can be re-established.

**Priority Management Action:** Commission a compound restoration assessment for Patukope-Pute covering soil pH amendment, organic matter addition, and hydrological management of the lagoon intake zone — the spatial comparison with the Obane restored mangrove site (Section 8.3.4), which demonstrates that Ada mangrove soils can achieve 26.0% humus under restoration, defines both the target condition and the achievability of the recovery pathway.

### Cluster 8: Futuenya-Big Ada Creek

**Water Quality Score: MODERATE**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*DO: 6.09 mg/L (Futuenya creek bridge), 6.91 mg/L (Luhue creek adjacent to Big Ada system) — the highest DO values in the entire Ada network.* The open, wind-agitated creek channels provide robust surface aeration that actively sustains aerobic water column conditions. High DO is the most positive ecological signal in the Ada water quality dataset and directly supports mangrove root function, fish populations, and the aerobic decomposition processes that sustain nutrient cycling in adjacent intertidal zones.

*Salinity: 0.2 ppt (Futuenya creek bridge).* Near-freshwater, confirming the continuing influence of Volta discharge in the transitional creek system. The very low salinity at this station places it well within the optimal range for *Rhizophora racemosa* establishment and productivity.

*pH: 8.1–8.6.* The highest alkaline values in the Ada network. While the water is ecologically functional at this pH, values above 8.0 represent a parameter that must be monitored for downward buffering capacity as ionic chemistry shifts. The alkalinity is likely driven by wind-mixed, carbonate-buffered creek conditions rather than by any pollution pressure — but the range warrants close attention in future rounds.

*Conductivity at Big Ada bridge: 50.4 µS* — the highest in the entire Ada network. This initially counterintuitive value (a creek draining toward freshwater sources recording the highest conductivity in the network) signals either tidal saltwater intrusion into the creek system beyond what the near-freshwater salinity at Futuenya suggests, or upstream land-use contributions to the ionic load. This anomaly must be tracked closely in future monitoring rounds: if sustained or increasing, it may be an early indicator of saltwater penetration into the transitional creek system.

**Soil Quality Score: MODERATE**

Soil chemistry in the transitional creek zone reflects the intermediate character of this cluster — not at the extreme depletion of Pute, not at the maximum benchmark of Galotse. Moisture, pH, and organic matter conditions are within functional ranges consistent with the partially freshwater, partially tidal character of this landscape position.

**Overall Cluster Condition: MODERATE (Most Ecologically Stable Zone in the Ada System)**

The Futuenya-Big Ada Creek cluster is currently the most ecologically stable part of the Ada water system: high DO, low salinity, functional pH, and no immediate geochemical emergency. It occupies the ecological middle ground that the two other clusters above bracket: less stressed than Pute, less pristine than Azizanya-Ayigbo. The management priority here is early warning detection — monitoring whether the high DO and low salinity persist as the Volta's freshwater influence potentially diminishes, and tracking whether the anomalous conductivity at Big Ada bridge represents the leading edge of a salinisation trend.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: MEDIUM**

The transitional creek system supports mangrove habitat in a functional, if not benchmark-condition, ecological environment. Carbon stocks here are present and partially stable, with the high DO confirming aerobic conditions that support aquatic life while — critically — the low salinity and functional pH maintain the conditions in which adjacent intertidal soils can support mangrove establishment and gradual SOC accumulation.

**Priority Management Action:** Track Big Ada bridge conductivity (50.4 µS) as the primary early-warning indicator for saltwater intrusion into the transitional creek system — flag any sustained increase above this baseline across two consecutive quarterly rounds for investigation.

### Cluster 9: Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane

**Water Quality Score: ALERT**

**Key water quality parameter justifications:**

*ORP: 832–1,547 mV across Wasakuse, Togbloku, and interior lagoon stations.* This is the headline finding of the Ada-Songor water quality assessment, and its implications for carbon management cannot be overstated. ORP values in this range represent strongly oxidising geochemical conditions — values that are not merely elevated but are among the highest that would be expected in any coastal wetland environment. At 832–1,547 mV, the water column at these stations is not suppressing decomposition; it is actively promoting it. The thermodynamic environment immediately above the sediment surface is fully compatible with the breakdown of organic compounds, meaning that carbon stored in Ada-Songor sediments beneath and adjacent to these stations is exposed to a geochemical regime that is actively hostile to its preservation.

The contrast with Keta is diagnostic. At comparable interior lagoon stations in the Keta complex, ORP was approximately 160 mV — mildly oxidising, consistent with functioning mangrove habitat with carbon-preserving reducing conditions in the sediment depth profile. The gap between 160 mV at Keta and 832–1,547 mV at the Ada interior lagoon is not a difference of degree. It is a qualitative difference in geochemical regime. Keta's lagoon interior is reducing; Ada's interior at these stations is strongly oxidising. The implications for comparative carbon storage function are severe.

*Salinity: 28.2–28.6 ppt.* High-salinity water confirms that the interior lagoon at these stations is receiving concentrated lagoon water from the Pute intake zone, with no significant freshwater dilution. The combination of high salinity and strongly oxidising ORP in a semi-enclosed water body indicates reduced freshwater inflow, evaporative concentration, and insufficient tidal exchange to maintain the reducing conditions required for carbon preservation.

**Soil Quality Score: ALERT**

*Nitrate at Wasakuse-ngwa: 984 mg/kg.* The highest nitrate reading in the entire Ada and Keta monitoring network — nearly double the MRV eutrophication alert threshold of 500 mg/kg. The source is unambiguous: concentrated cattle waste deposited directly into the wetland margin by herds that graze and rest at the lagoon edge. At concentrations approaching 984 mg/kg, nitrogen-stimulated microbial decomposition shifts the soil from a carbon accumulator to a carbon emitter — the same nitrogen that would, in small quantities, assist mangrove growth is, at these concentrations, actively undermining the soil's capacity to retain its organic carbon.

*Phosphate at Obane: 705 mg/kg; Potassium at Obane: 1,280 mg/kg.* Both values are the highest in the entire monitoring network — 705 mg/kg phosphate is 7–14 times the natural moist-site mangrove range recorded at Keta (52–103 mg/kg); 1,280 mg/kg potassium is nearly 183 times the 7 mg/kg at Pute and more than six times the highest natural values at the Keta benchmark stations (approximately 200 mg/kg). These are not indicators of soil fertility; they are markers of intense organic waste concentration from repeated cattle deposition at the wetland margin.

*Humus at Obane restored site: 26.0%.* Critically, within the same cluster, the Obane restored mangrove station demonstrates that recovery is achievable — and that the 26.0% humus under restored canopy is categorically better than the depleted conditions at Pute. This is both a management warning (the nutrient enrichment is threatening this recovery signal) and a management argument (the recovery pathway for degraded Ada sites is empirically demonstrated).

*Soil temperature at Obane: 34.2°C vs. 38.4°C at Pute walkway.* The 4.2°C canopy-cooling effect of the restored Obane stand is a measurable carbon management benefit: lower soil temperature directly slows microbial decomposition, protecting the 26.0% humus from the temperature-accelerated carbon loss visible at Pute.

**Overall Cluster Condition: ALERT**

The Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane cluster receives an Alert classification driven by two converging ecological crises: the ORP anomaly (832–1,547 mV) in the water column, which represents a direct and active threat to the carbon storage function of the interior lagoon sediments; and the extreme nutrient loading at the wetland margin (N 984 mg/kg, P 705 mg/kg, K 1,280 mg/kg), which represents a sustained anthropogenic pressure that is already exceeding defined alert thresholds and poses a documented risk of accelerating organic matter decomposition in the soils it affects. Neither crisis is self-correcting without management intervention.

**Carbon Stock Integrity Rating: CRITICAL**

Carbon stocks at the Wasakuse-Togbloku interior lagoon stations cannot be assumed to be stable. The ORP anomaly constitutes a direct material uncertainty in any carbon stock assessment for this zone — an uncertainty that must be explicitly disclosed in the Carbon Inventory Report and the MRV verification framework. The extreme nutrient loading at the Wasakuse-Obane wetland margin represents an additional, compounding risk of nitrogen- and phosphate-stimulated organic matter decomposition that would accelerate carbon loss from both the 984 mg/kg nitrate zone and the 26.0% humus at the Obane restored site. Immediate cattle exclusion from the wetland margin buffer is required.

**Priority Management Action:** (1) Investigate the root cause of the ORP anomaly (832–1,547 mV) across multiple seasonal monitoring rounds to determine whether values are sustained, episodic, or worsening — this finding alone requires explicit disclosure in the Carbon Inventory Report; (2) implement physical exclusion barriers or formal community agreements for cattle exclusion from the wetland margin at Wasakuse and Obane within the current monitoring cycle — the 984 mg/kg nitrate reading constitutes grounds for immediate formal escalation to the Ada East District Assembly under the MRV governance protocol.

---

*Narrative interpretation:* The Wasakuse-Togbloku-Obane cluster holds the most complex and analytically demanding condition picture in the Ada monitoring network. Two ecologically opposite stories are unfolding within the same cluster: at the interior lagoon, the ORP anomaly documents a geochemical environment that is actively hostile to carbon preservation, and no management intervention has yet been identified that could reverse it in the short term; at the Obane restored mangrove station, three kilometres away, the soil chemistry demonstrates that active restoration produces measurably better carbon conditions — and that the recovery pathway from degraded Ada soil to functioning blue carbon habitat is empirically achievable. The cattle-mediated nutrient enrichment (984 mg/kg N, 705 mg/kg P, 1,280 mg/kg K) is the pressure that most immediately threatens to convert the Obane recovery signal from an asset into a liability: if the enrichment intensifies, nitrogen-stimulated decomposition will begin consuming the 26.0% humus that restoration has built, and the carbon benefit of the Obane restoration investment will be reversed by a livestock management failure. Immediate action on cattle exclusion at this cluster is among the highest-priority and most feasibility-achievable management interventions in the entire Ada-Songor system.

## 8.5 Carbon Stock Integrity Implications

### 8.5.1 Connecting Condition Scores to Carbon Accounting

The condition scores in Section 8.4 are not merely ecological observations. They are carbon accounting inputs. Each tier carries specific implications for how the Carbon Inventory Report should treat the carbon stocks in these zones, and for how the MRV monitoring programme should calibrate its reporting frequency, uncertainty treatment, and intervention triggers at each site.

This section makes those implications explicit, following the tiered logic of the condition assessment framework and using the specific cluster data from Chapters 4 through 7 to ground the carbon accounting treatment in observed parameter values rather than generic methodological assumptions.

### 8.5.2 GOOD Condition Clusters — Stable Reference Zones

**Applicable zones:** Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi (Keta); Genui Reference Station (Keta); Azizanya-Ayigbo (Ada).

These zones meet the ecological criteria for stable, actively accumulating carbon stocks. The parameter evidence from the December 2025 baseline — 40.6% humus and 40.4% moisture at Galotse, pH 6.5–7.6 in water, ORP consistent with reducing conditions in the sediment profile, and 2.64 µS conductivity confirming intact Volta freshwater influence at Azizanya — supports treating these zones as primary carbon stock reference areas in the Carbon Inventory Report.

*Carbon accounting treatment:* Carbon stocks in these zones are suitable as primary carbon stock reference zones and stable baselines for project accounting. Stocks may be assumed stable and accumulating at background rates consistent with the observed ecological conditions. SOC monitoring at the standard quarterly frequency is appropriate. No additional uncertainty penalties are required. These are the zones against which the departure from the baseline, in degraded zones, is calculated.

*Monitoring obligation:* Maintaining the GOOD classification at these zones is itself a management deliverable. Any parameter drift — sustained decline in moisture at Galotse, upward pH trend at the Salo stations, conductivity increase at Ayigbo — must trigger a field investigation before the next quarterly round.

### 8.5.3 MODERATE Condition Clusters — Present but Reduced Carbon Function

**Applicable zones:** Agbledorne sub-zone (Keta); Aborlorvi-Norlopi (Keta); Futuenya-Big Ada Creek (Ada).

These zones contain carbon stocks at levels below the benchmark but within ranges consistent with partial functionality. The ecological evidence from the baseline — moisture at the boundary of the anaerobic threshold at Aborlorvi (25.4%), pH approaching the upper acceptable range at Futuenya (8.1–8.6), moderate humus at Agbledorne — supports a treatment that acknowledges carbon presence while recognising that stocks are subject to stresses that may be increasing.

*Carbon accounting treatment:* Carbon stocks in these zones are present but should be reported with wider uncertainty bands than the GOOD zones, reflecting the measured parameter variability and the risk of seasonal drying or elevated pH conditions reducing SOC accumulation rates. Monitoring at the standard quarterly frequency is appropriate, but the second and third monitoring rounds are particularly important for establishing whether the boundary conditions observed in December 2025 (moisture at 25.4%, pH at 7.8) are stable, improving, or worsening. Any downward trend in moisture below 20% at Aborlorvi, or upward pH trend above 8.5 at the transitional creek stations, should trigger reclassification to STRESSED and a corresponding revision of the carbon accounting treatment. For the Aborlorvi restoration zone specifically, carbon credit generation should not be claimed for replanted areas until at minimum two seasons of quarterly monitoring data confirm that the restored sites are maintaining soil moisture above 25% — the minimum threshold for the anaerobic conditions required for SOC accumulation.

### 8.5.4 STRESSED Condition Clusters — Carbon Stocks at Active Risk

**Applicable zones:** Dzita-Anyanui margin (Keta); Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga (Keta); Obane Creek restored mangrove (Ada).

These zones contain measurable carbon stocks but under conditions that place those stocks at active and ongoing risk of loss. The hypersaline conditions at Dzita reduce species productivity below the benchmark level; the thermal and saline stress at Afiadenyigba-Anloga limits SOC accumulation rates and partially exposes existing stocks to aerobic decomposition during tidal dry phases; the extreme nutrient enrichment at Obane (P 705 mg/kg, K 1,280 mg/kg) risks stimulating microbial decomposition of the 26.0% humus that restoration has built.

*Carbon accounting treatment:* Carbon stocks in these zones should not be assumed stable without active management intervention and confirmed improvement in the stressed parameters. Standard emission factors developed for productive mangrove conditions will overstate the carbon retention function of these sites. Stress-adjusted emission factors — reflecting the reduced productivity, partial seasonal aerobic exposure, or nutrient-stimulated decomposition risks documented in the baseline — are more appropriate. The Carbon Inventory Report should flag these zones as requiring active management intervention as a precondition for carbon credit generation, and should note that stocks reported from these zones carry a higher inherent uncertainty than GOOD zone stocks. For Obane specifically, the 26.0% humus value should be treated as a current upper bound that is at risk of declining if cattle management at the wetland margin is not implemented within the current monitoring cycle.

### 8.5.5 ALERT Condition Zones — Carbon Stocks Not to Be Reported as Stable

**Applicable zones:** Anyanui-Tunu Road (Keta); Patukope-Pute (Ada); Wasakuse-Togbloku interior lagoon (Ada); Ada Interior Lagoon (Togbloku station sub-zone).

These zones may be net emitters of CO₂ rather than carbon sinks. The evidence from the December 2025 baseline does not permit any of these zones to be classified as stable carbon stocks under any standard carbon accounting framework.

At Anyanui-Tunu Road, the combination of pH 2.7 water, dead mangrove canopy, and the established mechanism of acid sulphate soil formation advancing into a sediment layer with 44.3% humus means that the carbon decomposition process has already begun. This carbon loss is not hypothetical; it is the mechanistic consequence of the pyrite oxidation that produced the pH 2.7 reading. Until hydrological restoration is initiated and confirmed to have arrested the acid sulphate process, and until quarterly monitoring demonstrates that soil pH at the Tunu Road stations is stable above 5.5 across a minimum of two consecutive seasonal rounds, this zone cannot be credited as a carbon stock.

At Patukope-Pute, the near-acid-sulphate soil pH (4.78), severely depleted humus (4.78%–9.2%), and maximum surface temperature (38.4°C) in combination with strongly oxidising water column ORP (832–1,236 mV in the adjacent interior lagoon) confirm that this is an active carbon loss location. Any carbon accounting treatment of Pute must model expected further soil carbon loss, not assume stability from a fixed baseline.

At Wasakuse-Togbloku, the ORP values of 832–1,547 mV constitute a material uncertainty that must be explicitly disclosed in the Carbon Inventory Report and in any MRV verification documentation. This disclosure is not optional: Verra VM0033 and comparable verification frameworks require that baseline conditions be stable or predictably varying; an ORP anomaly of this magnitude in the interior lagoon fails that criterion. The extreme nutrient loading further compounds the disclosure requirement.

*Carbon accounting treatment for Alert zones:* (1) Do not treat as stable baseline carbon stocks. (2) Report explicitly as zones subject to active carbon loss processes. (3) Apply conservative modelled loss scenarios rather than flat baseline assumptions. (4) Flag as requiring management intervention before any carbon crediting can be extended to these zones. (5) Exclude from project-level carbon calculations until a minimum of four consecutive quarterly monitoring rounds confirms that the ALERT-level parameter anomalies have resolved.

## 8.6 Management Priority Matrix

### 8.6.1 Framework Logic

The management priority matrix organises all required interventions across the 13 assessment zones into a quadrant framework based on two axes: ecological stress severity (derived from the condition scores in Section 8.4) and management feasibility (the practical difficulty of intervention, accounting for technical complexity, institutional authority, cost, and community engagement requirements). The quadrant assignment is intended as a sequencing tool for MLNR and MESTI — not a substitute for the site-specific ecological assessments that detailed intervention planning requires.

The four quadrants are:

**Quadrant 1 — High Ecological Stress × High Management Feasibility:** Act immediately. The combination of urgent ecological need and achievable intervention means that delay is a management failure. These are the interventions that the programme should resource and commission within the current reporting cycle.

**Quadrant 2 — High Ecological Stress × Low Management Feasibility:** Plan and resource. The ecological need is as urgent as Quadrant 1, but the intervention requires technical complexity, governance negotiation, or external resourcing that cannot be resolved within a single reporting cycle. These must be formally programmed and tracked.

**Quadrant 3 — Moderate Ecological Stress × High Management Feasibility:** Maintain and monitor. Conditions are sub-optimal but not critical; the interventions required are achievable and should be initiated as a maintenance management activity alongside the core monitoring programme.

**Quadrant 4 — Good Condition × All Feasibility:** Protect. The best-condition zones require not active restoration but active protection of the conditions that make them ecologically productive. Protection failures here are the most damaging possible outcomes for the carbon programme.

---

### 8.6.2 Priority Matrix — All 13 Assessment Zones

**QUADRANT 1: Act Immediately**

*(High ecological stress + High management feasibility)*

**Anyanui-Tunu Road — hydrological restoration:** The acid sulphate emergency at the Tunu Road stations is caused by a physical infrastructure failure: a road corridor built across the tidal floodplain without adequate culverts for tidal exchange. The intervention required — hydrological investigation, culvert installation or tidal gate restoration — is technically well-understood, has clear institutional authority (Wildlife Division, Keta Municipal District Assembly), and has a defined escalation protocol already written into the MRV framework. This is the highest-priority management action in the entire programme. The 44.3% humus at this site — the largest single carbon stock value at risk of active mineralisation — gives the intervention a quantifiable carbon conservation rationale that can support resourcing at MLNR/MESTI level.

**Cattle exclusion at Wasakuse-Obane wetland margin:** The 984 mg/kg nitrate at Wasakuse and the 705 mg/kg phosphate and 1,280 mg/kg potassium at Obane are direct, measurable consequences of unmanaged cattle access to the wetland margin. The intervention — physical exclusion fencing or a formal community management agreement defining a buffer distance from the water's edge — is technically simple, financially modest, and within the governance authority of the Ada East District Assembly under the MRV Layer III escalation protocol. The MRV eutrophication alert threshold has already been exceeded by a factor of nearly two. Formal escalation to the District Assembly is immediately warranted.

**Mangrove harvesting prohibition on Keta main lagoon bank:** The residual mangrove fringe along the Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga corridor is the only carbon sequestration and coastal protection cover along the most densely populated stretch of the lagoon. It is operating under thermal and saline stress conditions that reduce its ecological resilience. Any additional canopy loss from harvesting in this stressed corridor would directly reduce the SOC accumulation rate and remove the coastal protection function. A formal harvesting moratorium — with Wildlife Division enforcement — is achievable within the current institutional framework.

---

**QUADRANT 2: Plan and Resource**

*(High ecological stress + Lower management feasibility)*

**Ada-Songor ORP anomaly — root cause investigation:** ORP values of 832–1,547 mV in the interior lagoon are the most significant carbon accounting finding in the Ada-Songor dataset, and their cause is not yet fully established. The investigation required spans hydrological modelling of the Songor interior lagoon circulation, seasonal sampling across the full wet and dry season cycle, sediment core analysis to determine the depth penetration of oxidising conditions, and assessment of the role of salt pan operations in suppressing freshwater input to the interior basin. This is a multi-quarter, multi-disciplinary investigation that requires specialist ecological and geochemical expertise beyond what the quarterly monitoring network can provide alone. It must be formally commissioned and resourced; it cannot be resolved through standard monitoring. Carbon accounting for the Ada system cannot be finalised until the ORP anomaly's temporal dynamics, spatial extent, and causal drivers are understood.

**Patukope-Pute compound restoration:** The restoration pathway for Pute — liming to correct the 4.78 soil pH, organic matter addition to begin rebuilding from 4.78%–9.2% humus, nutrient supplementation, hydrological management of the lagoon intake to reduce saline intrusion from the 28.6 ppt water column — is technically complex, multi-stage, and requires coordination between soil scientists, hydrologists, community engagement specialists, and the salt pan operators whose activities influence the Pute water chemistry. The Obane comparison (Section 8.3.4) demonstrates that Ada mangrove soils can achieve 26.0% humus under restoration — the recovery pathway is empirically demonstrated. But realising it at Pute requires a sequenced intervention plan that must be formally designed, resourced, and governed. It cannot happen within a single reporting cycle, but formal planning must begin in this one.

**Keta lagoon bank salinisation — climate adaptation planning:** The Afiadenyigba-Havedzi-Vodza-Woe-Anloga corridor's thermal and saline stress is partly the product of the lagoon's geography and the reduced Volta River freshwater input — factors that are not amenable to simple site-level intervention. Any meaningful management of progressive salinisation in this corridor requires engagement at the basin scale, including dialogue with the Volta River Authority (VRA) about seasonal discharge management, and the development of coastal adaptation scenarios for the main lagoon bank under climate change projections. These conversations are long-lead-time institutional processes. They must be initiated, but they cannot be resolved on a quarterly management cycle.

---

**QUADRANT 3: Maintain and Monitor**

*(Moderate ecological stress + High feasibility)*

**Dzita hypersaline margin — salinity trend monitoring and community engagement:** The hypersaline conditions at Dzita (40 ppt) are a chronic stress, not an acute emergency. The management response is structured monitoring — tracking whether minimum dry-season salinity at the western Anyanui stations is rising year-on-year, which would confirm that the freshwater influence gradient is contracting — combined with community education and engagement in the Dzita area about the relationship between lagoon salinity and mangrove community health. This is achievable within the standard quarterly monitoring cadence without additional resourcing.

**Aborlorvi-Norlopi restoration planning — hydrological validation:** The restoration feasibility question at Aborlorvi turns on whether wet-season moisture values rise sufficiently above the 25.4% dry-season baseline to confirm adequate tidal inundation depth and frequency. This validation is achievable through two to three additional quarterly monitoring rounds using the existing station network, without major additional investment. Species selection (whether *Rhizophora* at 5–25 ppt or *Avicennia* at higher salinities) and planting density decisions can then follow from the monitoring data. Within the current resourcing structure, this is a Quadrant 3 action — proceed with monitoring-led planning, and commit to planting when the data supports it.

**Futuenya-Big Ada Creek conductivity monitoring:** The anomalous 50.4 µS conductivity at Big Ada bridge is a Moderate-stress signal that requires surveillance rather than intervention at this stage. Standard quarterly monitoring at this station, with explicit tracking of whether the value is stable, increasing, or decreasing, will determine within two to three monitoring rounds whether this reflects a structural trend (saltwater intrusion advancing into the creek system) or a one-off measurement. The determination is achievable within the existing monitoring programme.

**Agbledorne — acid sulphate migration surveillance:** The Agbledorne sub-zone within the Anyanui-Dzita cluster retains partial tidal connectivity and currently records conditions within the Moderate range. The management priority is defensive monitoring: specifically tracking whether the acid sulphate chemistry at the adjacent Tunu Road stations is migrating toward the Agbledorne sampling positions across successive quarters (detected as a downward trend in water pH toward 5.0 or soil pH below 5.5 at the Agbledorne stations). If migration is detected, Agbledorne's classification escalates to STRESSED and the management priority escalates accordingly.

---

**QUADRANT 4: Protect**

*(Good condition + All feasibility levels — protection is the management action)*

**Saviatula-Galotse-Agortoe-Agbastivi core:** The most important single management obligation in the entire Ghana Blue Carbon programme is protecting the ecological conditions that sustain the 40.6% humus at Galotse, the 37.5% at Agortoe, and the optimal water chemistry across this corridor. Protection means zero-tolerance enforcement of any action that could alter tidal exchange in this zone — road construction, drainage modification, channel dredging, coastal development — with a formal ecological impact assessment requirement for any proposed infrastructure. It also means active community engagement to maintain the social licence for conservation in the Salo and Galotse communities, where mangrove product harvesting and fishing provide direct livelihoods.

**Azizanya-Ayigbo freshwater baseline:** The Volta-stabilised near-freshwater conditions at Azizanya and Ayigbo are the ecological foundation of the Ada monitoring system's reference benchmark. Protecting these conditions is primarily a hydrological governance matter — working with the VRA and water resources management institutions to ensure that Volta freshwater discharge reaching the estuary is not further reduced, and monitoring conductivity at Ayigbo bridge as the system-level canary for the Volta's ecological function at this estuary margin.

**Genui northeastern anchor:** Maintaining tidal connectivity and access to the Genui Road station for quarterly monitoring ensures that the northeastern extent of the productive mangrove zone is continuously observed and that any progressive salinisation of the gradient is detected at its earliest appearance.

## 8.7 Chapter Conclusions

The integrated condition assessment presented in this chapter transforms the December 2025 baseline parameter data from Chapters 4 through 7 into a structured management decision framework. Across both sites and all 13 assessment zones, the picture that emerges is one of significant spatial heterogeneity: the same landscape that contains some of the most productive blue carbon environments in West Africa — the Galotse benchmark, the Azizanya freshwater baseline — also contains some of the most acutely degraded ecological conditions documented in any systematic coastal wetland assessment in Ghana — the pH 2.7 acid emergency at Anyanui-Tunu Road and the combined ORP-nutrient crisis at Wasakuse-Togbloku.

This spatial heterogeneity is not an obstacle to management. It is the management framework's most important input. The condition assessment tells site managers and national programme coordinators exactly which zones need immediate intervention (the four ALERT zones), which need active monitoring to prevent deterioration (the STRESSED zones), which need monitoring-guided investment decisions (the MODERATE zones), and which need protection-focused governance (the GOOD zones). The distinction between these categories carries direct implications for how MLNR and MESTI allocate management resources, how the Carbon Inventory Report treats carbon stocks in each zone, and how the MRV monitoring system calibrates its alert thresholds and escalation protocols for the next three years of quarterly assessment.

The most important single conclusion from this assessment is this: the two highest-SOC zones in the monitoring network — the 44.3% humus at Anyanui-Tunu Road and the 40.6% at Galotse — represent opposite ends of the management spectrum. The Galotse carbon is intact, productive, and in need of active protection. The Tunu Road carbon is endangered, actively threatened by acid sulphate soil formation, and in need of emergency hydrological restoration. Those two facts, side by side, define the entire strategic logic of the Ghana Blue Carbon programme at Keta: the carbon to protect and the carbon to rescue exist in the same landscape, require different responses, and demand that both responses proceed simultaneously, without delay, and with the urgency that the data in these chapters justifies.
