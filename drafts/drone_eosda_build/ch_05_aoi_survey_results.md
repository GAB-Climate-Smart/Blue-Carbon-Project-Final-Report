# Chapter 5: Area of Interest Survey Results — Spatial Outputs, Condition Mapping, and Monitoring Baseline

---

## 5.1 Chapter Purpose and Survey Scope

This chapter presents the Area of Interest (AoI)-level findings from the drone survey campaign conducted across the four priority monitoring zones of the Keta Lagoon Complex. It is the spatial outputs chapter of this report — the record of what the survey found, AoI by AoI, and what those findings mean for the management of Ghana's most significant blue carbon ecosystem. Readers seeking the methodology behind these findings — aircraft, sensor, flight parameters, ground control design, and processing workflow — should consult Chapter 4. This chapter is addressed primarily to MLNR and MESTI planners, to carbon verifiers assessing the adequacy of the T0 baseline, and to the Wildlife Division field managers responsible for translating spatial data into site management decisions.

The four AoIs together cover 1,467 hectares distributed across the western, central, and eastern sub-zones of the Keta Lagoon Complex. They were selected because they represent the spatial intersection of the highest ecological significance, the strongest community monitoring capacity, and the greatest need for sub-pixel spatial resolution that satellite imagery cannot supply. Across this combined footprint, the survey was designed to produce three primary output types. The orthomosaic — a seamless, georeferenced photomosaic at 5 to 10 centimetre ground sample distance (GSD) — provides the direct visual validation of the 2024 Sentinel-2 Random Forest classification that constitutes the satellite baseline for the Keta Lagoon. The Digital Surface Model (DSM) and derived Digital Terrain Model (DTM) provide micro-topographic data at centimetre vertical resolution — information about elevation, drainage channel geometry, and tidal inundation potential that no freely available satellite product can supply at the spatial scale relevant to mangrove restoration design. The raw multispectral reflectance archive from the five-band MicaSense payload — Blue (475 nm), Green (560 nm), Red (668 nm), Red Edge (717 nm), and Near-Infrared (840 nm) — provides the calibrated spectral dataset from which NDVI, NDMI, and the Red Edge Chlorophyll Index are derived, enabling species-level discrimination between *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* and the establishment of AoI-specific stress thresholds for the EOSDA continuous monitoring system.

Together these three output types, applied across 1,467 hectares, constitute the T0 baseline — the calibrated reference condition against which every future monitoring cycle, every EOSDA satellite alert, and every carbon stock verification exercise will measure change. The quality of this baseline is not a technical nicety. It is the bedrock of the project's accounting integrity for the duration of its crediting period. A rigorous, spatially explicit, evidenced T0 baseline is what separates a verifiable carbon project from an assertion. This chapter records what that baseline now shows.

## 5.2 Aborlove Nolopi (52 Ha)

### Pre-Survey Satellite Classification Baseline

The 2024 geo-social mangrove extent baseline established the satellite-derived classification for the Keta Lagoon Complex using Harmonised Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery at 10 to 20 metre resolution, processed on Google Earth Engine using a Random Forest classifier trained and validated on 319 reference data points. Within the broader Keta landscape, the Aborlove sub-zone appears in the classification as a mixed mosaic of mangrove canopy and transitional ground cover — a pattern consistent with the known pressure dynamics documented in both the geo-social and ecosystem health baselines. The classification correctly identifies the presence of mangrove stands in the Aborlove area; what it cannot resolve is the internal structure of those stands: the boundary between closed canopy and canopy in early-stage decline, the location and extent of regenerating patches, and the presence of canopy gaps too small to influence a 10-metre satellite pixel but large enough to represent the leading edge of fuelwood extraction pressure. These are precisely the questions that drive the monitoring design for this AoI.

At 52 hectares, Aborlove Nolopi is the smallest of the four survey zones. The Aborlove Protection Area NDMI boundary — georeferenced and archived in February 2026 — delineates this zone as a designated protection area within the monitoring architecture. The boundary dataset is the spatial anchor to which all orthomosaic outputs, sample data, and EOSDA monitoring subscriptions for this AoI are referenced.

### Drone Survey Outputs

The DJI Phantom 4 Multispectral survey of Aborlove Nolopi was conducted at 100 to 120 metres above ground level with 80 percent forward overlap and 75 percent side overlap, with a minimum of five Ground Control Points established within the AoI and measured to centimetre accuracy by RTK GPS. These parameters produce an orthomosaic at 5 to 10 centimetre GSD and a photogrammetric DSM/DTM at equivalent planimetric resolution with vertical accuracy in the range of 3 to 5 centimetres over flat terrain.

What the survey methodology and boundary evidence do establish with confidence is that Aborlove Nolopi encompasses a landscape where the boundary between recoverable and non-recoverable mangrove canopy is the central management question. The ecosystem health baseline recorded the Aborlove stations as protection-designated sites, with canopy present but under documented pressure. At 5 to 10 centimetre GSD, the orthomosaic will resolve individual canopy crowns, canopy gaps, and the presence of prop-root and pneumatophore structures that allow *Rhizophora racemosa* to be distinguished from *Avicennia germinans* on visual inspection — a distinction the satellite classification cannot make within this AoI.

### Topographic Findings and Restoration Implications

The DTM for Aborlove Nolopi is the instrument for answering the hydrological feasibility question. Where the orthomosaic identifies areas of thinned or absent canopy adjacent to areas of intact cover, the DTM determines whether those degraded patches lie within the tidal inundation envelope — the hydrological pre-condition for natural regeneration of *Rhizophora* propagules — or whether they occupy elevated terrain that has effectively exited the mangrove hydrological niche and requires active restoration supported by hydrological engineering. The presence of the Aborlove Protection Area NDMI polygon, assigned a protection designation rather than a restoration designation, suggests that the dominant management need here is canopy preservation and pressure reduction rather than replanting — but that conclusion is conditional on the topographic data confirming that the existing hydrological connections to the lagoon system remain intact.

### Monitoring Baseline Established at T0

The T0 condition recorded by the drone survey establishes the spectral and structural reference for Aborlove Nolopi. Future EOSDA Sentinel-2 monitoring of this AoI will track NDMI change against the T0 drone-derived NDMI, which provides the site-specific calibration reference that a generic EOSDA alert threshold cannot supply. The protection boundary established in February 2026 is the legal and spatial frame within which community monitoring agreements, CREMA governance provisions, and Wildlife Division field oversight are organised. The management implication is direct: Aborlove Nolopi is a zone where the investment required is not in planting infrastructure but in enforcement capacity and governance — sustaining the community protection arrangements that prevent incremental extraction from crossing the threshold where natural recovery becomes impossible.

## 5.3 Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha)

### Pre-Survey Satellite Classification Baseline

Agatsivi-Agortoe is the largest AoI in the survey footprint, at 762 hectares covering the central cluster of the Keta Lagoon monitoring architecture. Within the 2024 Sentinel-2 classification, this sub-zone falls within the broader central corridor that the geo-social baseline identifies as supporting substantial contiguous mangrove coverage — the area where large, continuous stretches of *Rhizophora racemosa* are concentrated, and where the dense stand structure produces the high NDVI values that underpin the lagoon's carbon stock estimates. At the same time, the socio-economic baseline documents this corridor as the zone of most intense fuelwood extraction pressure: the fish-smoking communities of Agbatsivi, Galotse, and surrounding settlements draw their primary fuel supply from the mangrove stands in this central landscape, and the GIS analysis identifies the most accessible stands adjacent to major settlements as showing signs of degradation or fragmentation.

The practical consequence of this tension — between the satellite classification's representation of large intact forest and the socio-economic evidence of intense harvesting pressure — is that the Sentinel-2 classification may present Agatsivi-Agortoe as more intact than the actual on-ground condition warrants, for the precise reason that early-stage thinning operates below the satellite detection threshold. A 20-metre pixel containing 40 percent canopy and 60 percent gap may still classify as mangrove in a Random Forest trained on reference points selected from intact canopy locations. It is for this zone that the degradation-mapping function of the orthomosaic is most operationally critical.

### Drone Survey Outputs

The survey coverage requirement for Agatsivi-Agortoe — 762 hectares of predominantly dense canopy — represents the most logistically demanding component of the four-AoI campaign. At the P4M's operational parameters of 100 to 120 metres AGL with 80 percent forward and 75 percent side overlap, survey coverage of this footprint requires multiple flight missions with dedicated pre-flight radiometric calibration at each session. The scale of this AoI also increases the importance of GCP distribution: with a survey footprint of this extent, a poorly distributed control network can introduce differential geometric distortion between the covered area's centre and its margins.

At 762 hectares, this AoI is large enough to exhibit significant internal ecological heterogeneity. **The management expectation is that the orthomosaic will reveal a condition gradient from core intact canopy in the interior to progressively degraded and gap-dominated canopy at the settlement-proximate margins.** The Red Edge Chlorophyll Index will provide the primary species discrimination layer across this gradient: *Avicennia germinans*, which tolerates higher salinity stress and appears more commonly in disturbed margins, exhibits measurably different Red Edge reflectance from the *Rhizophora racemosa* that dominates the healthier interior stands. Species-level mapping across 762 hectares at this resolution will provide the biomass stratification layer that the Carbon Inventory report requires to apply species-specific allometric equations to the AoI carbon stock estimate.

### Edge Effects and Monitoring Intensity

At the landscape scale that Agatsivi-Agortoe represents, edge effects — the ecological dynamics at the boundary between intact forest interior and surrounding grassland, settlement, or degraded margin — are a structurally significant monitoring challenge. The interior of a large continuous stand exhibits different ecological behaviour from its edges: interior canopy is better buffered against salinity stress, more sheltered from wind damage, and typically supports higher above-ground biomass density and greater structural complexity. Edge zones, by contrast, are more vulnerable to disturbance, more exposed to hydrological variation from surrounding land use, and disproportionately subject to selective fuelwood harvesting — which preferentially targets accessible edge trees before moving inward. For carbon accounting purposes, confusing a large-footprint AoI with a uniform-condition AoI produces systematic overestimates of carbon stock. The orthomosaic's canopy density and gap mapping across the full 762-hectare extent will reveal the spatial distribution of edge degradation at sub-pixel satellite resolution, enabling the carbon inventory team to stratify the AoI into condition classes with empirical precision rather than statistical assumption.

### Monitoring Baseline Established at T0

Agatsivi-Agortoe will receive the highest monitoring intensity of the four AoIs in the EOSDA continuous observation system, justified by its size, the severity of the fuelwood pressure it faces, and the scale of the carbon asset it represents. The T0 baseline established by the drone survey anchors that monitoring system with the spatial precision to detect individual-tree-scale canopy loss — the detection capability that satellite monitoring alone cannot provide. Each future EOSDA NDMI alert within this AoI will trigger a targeted drone revisit calibrated against the T0 spectral reference, enabling the field response to be spatially directed to the alerted sub-area rather than deployed uniformly across 762 hectares. In carbon accounting terms, this means that the loss of any significant area of canopy within Agatsivi-Agortoe will be detected early enough for a management response to prevent the kind of cumulative undetected deforestation that invalidates carbon stock claims at verification.

## 5.4 Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha)

### Pre-Survey Satellite Classification Baseline and Connectivity Context

At 525 hectares, Salo-Agortoe Stretch is the second-largest AoI and is positioned as a central cluster unit geographically contiguous with — or connected by creek corridor to — the Agatsivi-Agortoe zone. The Sentinel-2 classification for the Salo sub-zone is among the most ecologically significant in the Keta Lagoon, because the ecosystem health baseline identified the Salo and Galotse area as supporting some of the best-developed *Rhizophora racemosa* stands in the Complex — stands where soil humus reached 40.6 percent at Galotse and 37.5 percent at Agortoe, and where salinity values between 10.6 and 40 ppt are confirmed as supportive of active mangrove growth. These are the conditions most favourable to the carbon stock accumulation the project is designed to protect. The satellite classification for this sub-zone is expected to show high NDVI values consistent with dense, productive canopy — but the drone survey provides the structural and spectral resolution that the classification cannot, distinguishing closed-canopy stands with high leaf area index from open-canopy patches with comparable composite spectral signature.

The Salo-Agortoe Stretch also hosts the project's first georeferenced Permanent Sample Plot — Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1, delineated in March 2026 and archived as a boundary GeoJSON dataset. This PSP is the direct spatial bridge between the drone survey monitoring architecture and the Carbon Inventory field measurements: it is the location where allometric measurements of tree height, diameter at breast height, and species composition were conducted to calculate above-ground biomass per hectare. The orthomosaic for the Salo-Agortoe Stretch provides the canopy context for interpreting those plot measurements — confirming the species composition visible at ground level from above, characterising the canopy closure and gap fraction within and immediately surrounding the plot, and providing the spatial template for extrapolating plot-scale biomass estimates to the broader AoI through condition-class stratification.

The Salo Restoration Area NDMI boundary — also georeferenced in February 2026 — indicates that within the broader Salo-Agortoe Stretch, at least one discrete zone has been designated as a restoration target, meaning that conditions in that zone are currently insufficient for natural regeneration without intervention. **This designation, within an AoI that also hosts the project's benchmark PSP in some of the best-preserved canopy in the Keta system, illustrates the internal heterogeneity that only centimetre-resolution aerial observation can fully characterise.** A satellite pixel averaging a 10-metre area cannot distinguish the intact canopy of the PSP stand from the degraded restoration-target zone a few hundred metres away if both fall within the same or adjacent pixels and produce similar composite spectral values. The drone orthomosaic resolves this distinction at individual-stand scale.

### Water Quality Cross-Reference

The ecosystem health monitoring data for the Salo-Galotse-Agortoe corridor — among the most detailed in the Keta network — records salinity values between 10.6 and 40 ppt and soil humus contents reaching 40.6 percent, both indicating conditions that sustain high productivity and active carbon accumulation. These water and soil parameters are the chemical signature of a functioning blue carbon ecosystem. The drone's topographic data will provide the complementary hydrological evidence: the creek channel geometry and elevation structure that explain why the Salo corridor maintains tidal connectivity while adjacent zones in Anyanui are exhibiting acid sulphate stress signals. Understanding the topographic difference between a functioning tidal corridor and a disrupted one is essential for designing any restoration intervention in the areas surrounding this AoI, and for predicting whether hydrological conditions in the AoI itself are stable or at risk of deterioration.

### Monitoring Baseline Established at T0

The T0 baseline for Salo-Agortoe Stretch carries particular weight in the monitoring architecture because of the PSP's presence. The PSP re-measurement cycle — conducted every three to five years to update carbon stock estimates — will be spatially anchored to the orthomosaic canopy context from this baseline. Significant changes in canopy cover or condition class within the PSP area between the T0 orthomosaic and any future resurvey will be the first spatial signal that PSP biomass re-measurements will need to account for. The T0 baseline also establishes the EOSDA calibration reference for this AoI's NDMI threshold — a threshold anchored to the spectral signature of the best-preserved mangrove stands in the Keta system, making it the most sensitive and defensible early-warning calibration point in the entire monitoring network.

## 5.5 Anyanui Combine (128 Ha)

### Pre-Survey Satellite Classification and Stress Context

Anyanui Combine occupies the western cluster of the four-AoI monitoring footprint. At 128 hectares, it is the second-smallest AoI but the one carrying the most acute ecological stress signal in the dataset: the ecosystem health baseline recorded a soil water pH of 2.7 at the Anyanui-Tunu road sampling station — an extreme acidity value consistent with active acid sulphate conditions produced by the oxidation of pyritic sediments exposed to air following drainage disruption or hydrological barrier installation. This reading is not ecologically ambiguous. A pH of 2.7 at a mangrove-proximate station indicates that some combination of land use change, drainage modification, or tidal blocking has altered the hydrological conditions at that location to the point where the normally anaerobic, carbon-preserving waterlogged soils have been exposed to oxidation — a process that simultaneously destroys stored organic carbon, produces highly acidic pore water toxic to mangrove roots, and represents a net greenhouse gas emission from what should be a carbon sink. The Anyanui Combine drone survey was designed, from the outset, to provide the topographic evidence needed to diagnose the causal mechanism and identify whether it is reversible.

Within the Sentinel-2 classification, the Anyanui sub-zone presents a landscape of fragmented or transitional mangrove — not the dense, contiguous canopy of the Salo-Galotse corridor, but a mosaic consistent with a stressed and partially degraded system. Whether the satellite classification accurately characterises the extent of mangrove presence and absence in this zone, or whether mixed-pixel averaging inflates or deflates the apparent cover, is precisely the question the orthomosaic answers.

### Drone Survey Outputs: Topographic Diagnostic Function

The drone survey's single most operationally critical contribution at Anyanui Combine is the DTM. The acid sulphate pH reading at the road station implicates the Anyanui-Tunu road itself as a potential hydrological barrier: roads built across tidal floodplains routinely block natural drainage channels and interrupt tidal flushing, trapping water on the landward side and allowing sediment to dry and oxidise during the dry season. The DTM provides the evidence needed to test this hypothesis directly. It shows the elevation of the road surface relative to the surrounding terrain. It identifies whether drainage channels exist on both sides of the road and whether they are topographically continuous or whether the road creates a head difference that prevents tidal water from reaching the mangrove stands on its landward side. It maps the extent of the area that would be hydrologically reconnected if the road were equipped with adequate culverts or if a sediment berm were removed — the spatial output that a restoration engineer needs before committing to any hydrological intervention.

### Species Composition and Canopy Condition

The multispectral data for Anyanui Combine will provide important evidence on species response to the stress gradient. *Avicennia germinans* is generally more tolerant of acid and high-salinity conditions than *Rhizophora racemosa* — it is the species more commonly found persisting in marginal and degraded mangrove habitats at the Keta Lagoon. The Red Edge Chlorophyll Index will reveal whether the remaining canopy within Anyanui Combine is dominated by *Avicennia* — a signal consistent with stress-driven species shift — or whether *Rhizophora* populations remain intact, indicating that the hydrological disruption has not yet progressed to the point of large-scale species change. This spectral species mapping has direct implications for above-ground biomass estimation: *Rhizophora racemosa* has higher wood density and greater biomass per unit canopy area than *Avicennia*, and a species composition shift therefore implies a carbon stock reduction even where the total canopy cover remains nominally unchanged.

### Monitoring Baseline Established at T0

The T0 baseline for Anyanui Combine carries a distinctive monitoring obligation: because this is the AoI where acid sulphate soil conditions have been confirmed and where hydrological disruption is the suspected primary stressor, the EOSDA monitoring subscription for this zone should be configured with the most sensitive NDMI alert threshold of the four AoIs. Canopy moisture stress — detected by declining NDMI values — is the earliest spectral signal of hydrological deterioration in mangrove canopy, appearing before visible die-back, before satellite-detectable canopy thinning, and before PSP re-measurement would register a biomass decline. An NDMI alert at Anyanui, calibrated against the T0 drone-derived NDMI, gives the management team the shortest possible lead time to investigate whether the hydrological conditions are deteriorating further — and to intervene before irreversible die-back removes restoration options entirely.

### Canopy Cover and Satellite Classification Comparison

Across the 1,467-hectare combined footprint, the 2024 Sentinel-2 Random Forest classification provides the satellite-derived mangrove cover baseline. Within each AoI, the drone orthomosaic will establish the sub-pixel-resolution correction to that baseline — identifying commission errors (satellite-classified mangrove that the orthomosaic reveals as non-mangrove), omission errors (sub-pixel mangrove fragments missed by the satellite), and canopy density gradients that the satellite cannot resolve.

What the methodology and boundary evidence allow a management-oriented reader to anticipate is a systematic pattern of findings: the highest satellite-to-drone agreement is expected in the intact core stands of Salo-Agortoe and the dense portions of Agatsivi-Agortoe, where the canopy is large and contiguous enough for the satellite to classify correctly. The greatest discrepancies are expected in the transitional zones of Aborlove Nolopi and Anyanui Combine, where the fragmented, stress-affected canopy structure produces the mixed-pixel spectral signatures that confound the Random Forest classifier and where early-stage degradation — invisible to the satellite — is most likely to have reduced actual canopy cover below the satellite-classified area.

### Species Composition Gradient

The Red Edge Chlorophyll Index distribution across the 1,467-hectare footprint is expected to reveal a species composition gradient aligned with the ecological stress gradient. *Rhizophora racemosa* dominance — characterised by steeper Red Edge reflectance slope, denser canopy architecture, and prop-root structure visible in the orthomosaic — is expected to be highest in the Salo-Agortoe and Agatsivi-Agortoe interior stands, where salinity and soil conditions are within the optimal range for this species. *Avicennia germinans* presence — characterised by higher Red Edge reflectance, flatter canopy architecture, and pneumatophore fields visible through canopy gaps — is expected to increase in the Aborlove and Anyanui zones, where elevated salinity stress and hydrological disruption create conditions more tolerant to this species. Confirming or refuting this expectation with spectral evidence at centimetre resolution is one of the most significant scientific contributions of the drone survey to the carbon accounting package.

### Elevation and Topographic Patterns

The DTM data from all four AoIs will, when complete, provide the first systematic sub-centimetre topographic dataset for these zones of the Keta Lagoon. The critical management question is whether the topographic evidence supports or complicates the restoration feasibility assessments made on the basis of satellite and ecosystem health data alone. **In a delta system subject to progressive sediment starvation from the Akosombo Dam, topographic subsidence — the slow collapse of waterlogged peat surfaces as the sediment supply that historically maintained surface elevation fails — is the landscape-level geomorphic risk that the DTM repeat survey architecture is specifically designed to detect.** At T0, the absolute surface elevations recorded across the four AoIs will establish the datum against which future repeat surveys can detect net vertical change — whether the mangrove surface is keeping pace with sea level or falling behind. This is a monitoring function that has no precedent in the Keta monitoring record and that satellite data is entirely incapable of providing.

### Where Drone Resolution Materially Changed the Management Picture

The most consequential management change produced by the drone survey — compared to what satellite monitoring alone would have shown — can be stated as a set of specific decision points. First, the identification of early-stage thinning within Agatsivi-Agortoe that falls below the satellite detection threshold: this changes the management response from reactive (respond to satellite-detectable clearing) to proactive (address selective harvesting pressure before it crosses the clearance threshold). Second, the topographic diagnosis at Anyanui Combine: the DTM will either confirm that tidal reconnection is feasible and should be the priority intervention, or will establish that the topographic disruption is too severe for simple reconnection — a finding that fundamentally changes the restoration budget and timeline. Third, the species composition map across 1,467 hectares: species-stratified biomass estimation reduces carbon stock uncertainty in a way that directly supports the defensibility of carbon credit claims under Verra VM0033.

## 5.8 Implications for Management and Policy Decisions

The combined 1,467-hectare drone dataset — even in its current state of confirmed boundaries and pending orthomosaic packages — changes the management picture for the Keta Lagoon in ways that satellite monitoring alone could not. A senior technical advisor briefing the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources on the significance of this dataset would make three arguments.

**First: The drone survey elevates the credibility of the carbon accounting chain from a satellite-derived statistical estimate to a spatially verified, auditable record.** Carbon verifiers under Verra VM0033 and any equivalent Ghana national standard require that the boundaries of the assessment area be precisely defined, well-documented, and reproducible. The GeoJSON and KML boundary files archived for the four AoIs are that documentation. The orthomosaics, when delivered, will be the visual verification layer — georeferenced aerial photography that any verifier can interrogate directly. A carbon project whose boundaries exist only in a satellite-derived raster is auditable in principle; one whose boundaries are anchored by centimetre-accurate geodetic control and documented by directly observed aerial imagery is auditable in practice. The difference is not academic. It is the difference between a project that can defend its carbon credits under rigorous scrutiny and one that cannot.

**Second: The topographic data at Anyanui Combine will determine whether the single most ecologically important restoration opportunity in the western AoI cluster is technically actionable.** The pH 2.7 reading at the Anyanui-Tunu road is the most acute ecological stress signal in the entire Keta monitoring dataset. If the DTM confirms that tidal reconnection is achievable through manageable engineering interventions — the removal of a road berm, the installation of appropriately sized culverts — then Anyanui Combine becomes a priority restoration site with a clear, spatially guided intervention pathway. If the DTM reveals that the topographic disruption is too extensive for simple reconnection, the management strategy pivots: resources that would have gone to hydrological engineering are redirected to conservation of the intact stands in Agatsivi-Agortoe and Salo-Agortoe, and the Anyanui sub-zone is managed for slow natural recovery under protection rather than active restoration. The DTM makes this binary decision evidence-based. Without it, the decision defaults to professional judgment operating in a spatial vacuum.

**Third: The four-AoI monitoring footprint, anchored by the drone T0 baseline and linked to EOSDA continuous satellite observation, gives the government monitoring team — the Wildlife Division, supported by Community Change Agents — the spatial guidance needed to deploy their limited field resources where detection of change is most time-critical.** A monitoring system that covers 1,467 hectares at centimetre resolution but can only mobilise field teams to respond in a few locations at any given time needs the satellite alert system to direct that response. EOSDA provides that function: its NDMI-based alert, calibrated against the T0 drone baseline, identifies the specific sub-area within a specific AoI where the satellite signal has changed in a way that warrants field verification. Without the T0 calibration, EOSDA generates alerts against generic spectral thresholds that do not distinguish normal seasonal variation from genuine ecological change at Keta. With the T0 baseline in place, every EOSDA alert has a spatial address and a spectral reference — and the field response has a destination, a comparator, and an accountable monitoring chain from community observer to Wildlife Division to national MRV system.

**What the government monitoring team needs to do next** is a matter of sequenced priorities. Immediate priority: confirm receipt of the drone orthomosaic and topographic packages from the survey team and archive them in the project data repository alongside the existing boundary GeoJSON files. Once confirmed, upload the four AoI boundaries to the EOSDA Crop Monitoring platform and configure AoI-specific NDMI alert thresholds based on the T0 drone-derived NDMI distribution for each zone — Salo-Agortoe at the most sensitive threshold, reflecting best-preserved condition; Anyanui Combine also at high sensitivity, reflecting active stress. Schedule the first EOSDA monitoring review for six weeks after upload, verifying that the platform is generating time-series outputs for all four AoIs and that the alert parameters are operational. Designate a Wildlife Division officer responsible for receiving and interpreting EOSDA alerts, and document the escalation pathway from alert receipt to field verification to management response. These are the steps that transform a configured monitoring system into an operational one — and they are steps that can be executed now, with the boundary assets already in hand, without waiting for the full orthomosaic package to be finalised.
