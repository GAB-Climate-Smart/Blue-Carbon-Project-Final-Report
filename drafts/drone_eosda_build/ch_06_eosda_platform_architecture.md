## 6.1 Chapter Purpose: From Survey to Continuous Observation

The drone survey described in Chapters 4 and 5 of this report accomplishes something that no satellite instrument at currently available commercial resolution can match: it records the condition of four priority Areas of Interest across 1,467 hectares of the Keta Lagoon Complex at a moment in time, with centimetre-resolution imagery, species-level spectral discrimination, and sub-centimetre topographic precision. That record — the T0 baseline — is indispensable. But it is, by definition, a snapshot. A mangrove canopy that is intact on the day of a drone survey can be selectively harvested within weeks. A zone showing early-stage hydrological stress in February 2026 may either stabilise or accelerate into visible die-back before the next scheduled drone revisit. The survey establishes where Ghana's blue carbon assets stand at the start of this programme; it cannot, on its own, tell managers what is happening to those assets between now and the next time a drone takes to the air.

The purpose of this chapter is to describe the architecture of the system that closes that gap. The EOSDA Crop Monitoring platform — configured here for a mangrove ecosystem monitoring application — provides the continuous, automated, satellite-derived observation layer that complements the drone's periodic high-resolution ground truth. Where the drone delivers precise structural and spectral data on specific dates, the EOSDA platform delivers Sentinel-2-based vegetation and moisture indices across all four Areas of Interest every three to five days, generating an unbroken time series that alerts managers when conditions within any AoI deviate materially from the T0 baseline. Together, drone survey and EOSDA monitoring are not two separate activities with separate reporting chains; they are two components of a single, integrated observation system, each compensating for what the other cannot provide.

This chapter documents how the EOSDA system was configured for that purpose: the reasons it was selected over alternative monitoring approaches, the specific logic by which Areas of Interest were defined and ingested, the technical basis of the NDVI and NDMI indices it monitors, the alert threshold architecture and its operational meaning, the institutional access arrangements that determine who receives and acts on monitoring outputs, and the governance implications of committing a national ministry to a continuous monitoring programme with a defined reporting chain to international carbon standards. It is written for a senior technical audience at MESTI and MLNR who need to understand what the platform does, why specific design choices were made, and what management obligations flow from those choices — not merely that a monitoring system has been set up.

## 6.2 Platform Selection Rationale

The selection of EOS Data Analytics Crop Monitoring as the operational satellite monitoring platform for this programme was not a default choice or a consequence of prior contractual relationships. It was a considered decision based on the specific technical requirements of mangrove ecosystem monitoring in a lower-middle-income country context, evaluated against the practical capabilities of the available alternatives.

The most fundamental requirement was access to a dense, current Sentinel-2 time series without per-image cost. Sentinel-2, operated by the European Space Agency under the Copernicus programme, delivers multispectral imagery at 10-metre spatial resolution in its primary vegetation bands, with a revisit frequency at any given location of three to five days (combining the Sentinel-2A and Sentinel-2B satellites). At the Keta Lagoon Complex — a site where the difference between recoverable early-stage thinning and irreversible canopy loss may unfold over weeks — three-to-five-day revisit frequency is not a convenience; it is a minimum specification for detecting change fast enough to allow a management response. Landsat 8/9, the alternative free multispectral archive, provides 30-metre resolution and a 16-day revisit period. At 30 metres, a pixel covering 900 square metres can conceal significant sub-pixel variation in canopy condition; at 16-day revisit, a rapid degradation event triggered by an intensive fuelwood harvesting episode may progress through its most critical early phase before a new image is acquired. Sentinel-2 at 10 metres and three to five days avoids both limitations. The EOSDA platform provides direct, pre-processed access to this archive without requiring the project team to operate a Google Earth Engine account or manage image compositing workflows independently.

The second requirement was built-in calculation and time-series tracking of the vegetation and moisture indices most relevant to mangrove monitoring — specifically the Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) and the Normalised Difference Moisture Index (NDMI). Both indices are calculated and archived automatically within the EOSDA platform for each cloud-filtered Sentinel-2 acquisition, generating a continuous record for each defined Area of Interest. The significance of this automation is institutional rather than purely technical: it means that the Wildlife Division officer or MESTI staff member monitoring the system does not need to understand remote sensing processing workflows or write image analysis code. They read dashboard outputs displaying index values over time, compare them against the T0 baseline, and receive automated alerts when the platform detects a threshold exceedance. The platform converts satellite data into management information — which is the operational form in which monitoring data actually changes decisions.

The third requirement was the ability to import Areas of Interest from existing GIS polygon datasets. The drone survey programme had already generated boundary GeoJSON files for the four priority monitoring zones; the EOSDA platform accepts shapefile uploads that define named AoIs to which all subsequent analysis, alert configuration, and index time-series reporting are tied. This workflow continuity — from drone boundary export to EOSDA AoI upload — means that the monitoring system inherits the same spatial frame as the drone survey and the carbon inventory, without any intermediate coordinate transformation or manual polygon re-entry. The boundary datasets archived in February and March 2026 for Aborlove Nolopi, Agatsivi-Agortoe, Salo-Agortoe Stretch, and Anyanui Combine are the direct source geometries for the EOSDA workspace.

The fourth consideration, which carries particular weight in a government programme that must outlast its founding consultancy engagement, is the platform's accessibility to non-specialist users. EOSDA's web interface requires no GIS software installation, no satellite data processing infrastructure, and no specialist remote sensing knowledge to operate at the monitoring level. MESTI, EPA, and Wildlife Division staff with standard office computing access can log in, view current AoI status, examine NDMI trends, and receive email or dashboard alerts when threshold conditions are triggered. This accessibility is not incidental to the platform's monitoring value; it is a precondition for institutional sustainability. A monitoring platform that requires a GIS specialist to interrogate is a monitoring platform that stops functioning when that specialist leaves. A platform with a navigable dashboard that a trained but non-specialist officer can use is a monitoring platform that can survive staff turnover — the most predictable governance risk in any long-term monitoring programme.

The fifth consideration is the platform's continuity with the Sentinel-2 analytical framework already established by the GIS team in the 2024 geo-social mangrove extent baseline. That assessment processed Harmonised Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery on Google Earth Engine using a Random Forest classifier trained on 319 reference data points, producing a 10-to-20-metre-resolution classification of mangrove extent across the 1,068,361-hectare coastal landscape. The EOSDA platform monitors the same satellite constellation at the same spatial resolution over the same landscape units — the four AoIs falling entirely within the Keta Lagoon Complex classified in that baseline. There is therefore no conceptual or spatial discontinuity between the satellite baseline established in 2024 and the satellite monitoring beginning in 2026. The EOSDA NDVI and NDMI time series are tracking the same pixels, at the same resolution, as the pixels that underpin the national mangrove extent estimate. This methodological coherence strengthens the defensibility of any future claim that the monitoring system is detecting change relative to a documented baseline.

## 6.3 AoI Ingestion and Boundary Configuration

The four Areas of Interest registered in the EOSDA monitoring workspace correspond exactly to the four priority monitoring zones identified in the drone survey methodology and confirmed through the community engagement process completed under FPIC protocols. They represent the spatial intersection of ecological significance — mangrove stands with high carbon stock value or acute restoration potential — and community monitoring capacity, where Community Change Agents are trained and the Wildlife Division has established field access. The combined footprint of 1,467 hectares covers the western, central, and eastern sub-zones of the Keta Lagoon Complex monitoring architecture.

The boundary source geometry for each AoI originates from the drone survey shapefile outputs, which were cross-validated against community resource-mapping polygons developed during the participatory engagement sessions. This cross-validation matters for two reasons. First, it ensures that the monitoring boundary reflects the actual extent of community-managed and community-agreed monitoring zones rather than an externally imposed analytical unit. Second, it reduces the risk of boundary disputes — either within the community or between communities — that might otherwise complicate the governance of the monitoring system. Where the drone survey boundary and the community mapping polygon were in agreement, the drone-derived geometry was adopted as the definitive AoI boundary; the drone's geodetic control network provides centimetre-level coordinate accuracy that participatory mapping cannot match. All boundary files are registered in the WGS 84 Geographic Coordinate Reference System (EPSG:4326), consistent with the coordinate reference system used in the 2024 geo-social baseline and the EOSDA platform's native ingestion format.

## 6.4 Index Selection and Monitoring Logic

The EOSDA platform monitors two spectral indices for each AoI: the Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) and the Normalised Difference Moisture Index (NDMI). The selection of these two indices, rather than a broader suite of spectral metrics, was deliberate. Together, NDVI and NDMI provide complementary diagnostic coverage of the two ecological conditions most relevant to early detection of mangrove stress: changes in photosynthetic activity and changes in canopy water content. Understanding precisely why each index matters — and what its values mean at the Keta Lagoon sites — is essential for any institution relying on EOSDA alerts to make management decisions.

### NDVI: Canopy Greenness and Photosynthetic Vigour

NDVI is calculated from the ratio of near-infrared (NIR) and red-band reflectance in the Sentinel-2 sensor:

**NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red)**

In Sentinel-2 terms, this is Band 8 (NIR, 842 nm) minus Band 4 (Red, 665 nm), divided by their sum. Healthy, photosynthetically active vegetation absorbs strongly in the red band — where chlorophyll absorbs light for photosynthesis — and reflects strongly in the near-infrared band, where leaf cell structure scatters radiation back outward. The NIR-minus-Red numerator is therefore large and positive for healthy canopy, producing NDVI values approaching 1.0. Stressed or dying vegetation, which loses chlorophyll and structural leaf integrity, absorbs less red light and reflects less NIR, compressing the ratio toward zero; bare soil and open water produce values near zero or slightly negative.

In mangrove ecosystems, NDVI is the primary indicator of canopy greenness — the aggregate photosynthetic health of the canopy as seen from above. For the Keta Lagoon Complex, where the 2024 geo-social baseline used NDVI as one of the spectral indices incorporated into the Random Forest classification to distinguish dense mangrove from degraded cover classes, the index has an established site-specific performance record. Dense, structurally intact *Rhizophora racemosa* stands — such as those documented in the Salo-Galotse corridor, where soil humus reaches 40.6 percent and salinity is within the species' optimal growth range — are expected to produce NDVI values in the range of 0.55 to 0.80, with the higher end of the range corresponding to the densest, most stratified canopy. Degraded or thinned canopy, where selective fuelwood harvesting has opened gaps in the canopy and reduced leaf area index, will produce lower NDVI values, with the reduction proportional to the severity and spatial extent of the thinning. Completely bare or deforested areas will produce NDVI values below 0.20.

The management implication of this interpretation range is direct. A sustained decline in NDVI at Agatsivi-Agortoe — the largest AoI and the zone of most intense fuelwood harvesting pressure — is the remote-sensing signature of progressive canopy thinning. It will appear in the EOSDA time series before it is visible to a drone re-survey, before it registers in Permanent Sample Plot re-measurement, and before any community observer notices the cumulative effect of selective harvesting at the landscape scale. The platform's role is to surface this signal early enough for a management response — enforcement engagement, community dialogue, alternative fuel provision — to prevent the thinning from crossing the threshold into detectable clearance.

### NDMI: Canopy Moisture and Hydrological Stress Detection

NDMI is calculated from the near-infrared and shortwave-infrared bands:

**NDMI = (NIR − SWIR) / (NIR + SWIR)**

In Sentinel-2 terms, this is Band 8 (NIR, 842 nm) minus Band 11 (SWIR, 1,610 nm), divided by their sum. Liquid water in plant tissue absorbs strongly in the shortwave-infrared, so high canopy moisture produces a large positive NIR-minus-SWIR difference and a high NDMI value. As plant water stress increases — driven by drought, salinity stress, hydrological disruption, or root system damage — the shortwave-infrared reflectance increases and NDMI declines toward zero and below.

NDMI is not simply a secondary verification index for NDVI. It measures a physiologically distinct and earlier indicator of vegetation stress. A mangrove canopy under progressive hydrological stress — for example, one experiencing increasing salinity because a road has blocked tidal flushing, as the evidence from the Anyanui-Tunu road site suggests — will begin to lose canopy moisture content before it begins to lose chlorophyll. The spectral consequence is that NDMI declines before NDVI does. A monitoring system tracking only NDVI would detect the ecological deterioration later, at a stage when the stress is more advanced and the management window is narrower. Tracking NDMI in parallel with NDVI at Anyanui Combine — where acid sulphate soil conditions recorded at pH 2.7 indicate that hydrological disruption is already operating — means that any further deterioration in the hydrological regime will appear in the moisture index first, giving managers a lead-time advantage of weeks to months over what NDVI alone would provide.

This distinction is clinically important at Keta. The ecosystem health baseline identified the Akosombo Dam's disruption of Volta River sediment and freshwater delivery as the landscape-scale existential risk to the lagoon system. Progressive salinisation — driven by reduced freshwater input — would appear in the monitoring record as a gradual multi-year NDMI decline across the Anyanui cluster, where freshwater influence from the Volta historically moderated lagoon salinity. This is precisely the kind of slow, landscape-driven trend that requires a continuous satellite time series to detect: it is below the detection threshold of quarterly field visits alone, invisible to annual satellite classifications, and only reliably apparent in a multi-year NDMI record that captures the year-on-year drift rather than seasonal oscillation.

### Alert Threshold Architecture: The 15% Deviation Rule

The operational alert threshold established for this monitoring programme is a **15% deviation from the T0 baseline value** for either NDVI or NDMI within any registered AoI. This threshold is not an arbitrary figure. It was selected to sit above the range of normal intra-seasonal spectral variation — the oscillation between Ghana's wet and dry season phenological cycles — while remaining sensitive enough to detect the onset of genuine ecological change before it becomes irreversible.

To understand the practical meaning of the 15% threshold, consider the specific case of Salo-Agortoe Stretch, where the best-preserved mangrove stands in the Keta monitoring footprint produce the highest NDVI values in the four-AoI system. If the T0 drone-calibrated NDVI baseline for Salo-Agortoe is established at 0.70 — a value consistent with dense *Rhizophora racemosa* canopy under conditions documented in the ecosystem health baseline — then the alert threshold is 0.70 minus 15 percent of 0.70, equalling 0.595. Any sustained period — defined in the monitoring protocol as two consecutive cloud-filtered Sentinel-2 acquisitions showing values at or below this threshold — would trigger a platform alert. The two-acquisition requirement prevents single-image artefacts from generating false alerts, while still providing a response time of six to ten days from the onset of a genuine change event.

For NDMI, where values at healthy mangrove sites are typically lower in absolute terms than NDVI, the 15% threshold operates on the same relative basis. A T0 NDMI value of 0.30 — illustrative of a healthy but moisture-variable site — would trigger an alert at 0.255. For Anyanui Combine, where the T0 values are expected to be suppressed relative to Salo-Agortoe due to existing hydrological stress, the alert threshold will be calibrated against that AoI's own drone-derived T0 NDMI distribution, not against a system-wide generic value. This per-AoI calibration is one of the critical operational advantages of grounding the alert system in drone survey data rather than in generic satellite-derived baselines.

### Seasonal Adjustment and Ghana's Mangrove Phenology

Ghana's coastal mangroves experience two climatically distinct seasons whose spectral signatures must be distinguished from genuine ecological change in the monitoring record. The wet season — approximately April to October, with peak rainfall in June–July and a secondary peak in September–October — delivers freshwater inflow from the Volta system and from direct precipitation, moderating lagoon salinity, enhancing soil moisture, and sustaining high canopy productivity. NDVI and NDMI values are characteristically higher in the wet season. The dry season, dominated by the Harmattan — the dry northeast trade wind that typically strengthens between November and March — reduces rainfall, increases evaporation, concentrates lagoon salinity, and produces seasonal reductions in NDVI and NDMI that reflect physiological stress under elevated salinity and reduced freshwater supply, rather than permanent ecological change.

The T0 baseline established by the drone survey, combined with the first Sentinel-2 composite generated after FPIC clearance, must be assessed in the context of its seasonal timing. The boundary datasets archived in February and March 2026 fall within the Harmattan-influenced dry season — a period when NDVI and NDMI values will be at or near their annual minimum for the lagoon system. An alert threshold calibrated against a dry-season T0 must therefore account for the fact that wet-season values will be systematically higher than the T0 reference, rather than interpreting a wet-season NDVI above the T0 as an anomaly. The EOSDA platform's time-series display allows the monitoring officer to observe the seasonal cycle as it develops, building a multi-year phenological record against which genuine decline trends — visible as progressive downward drift of the seasonal minimum — can be distinguished from normal intra-annual variation.

Practically, this means that the first complete wet-dry cycle recorded after T0 establishment will be the most informative calibration period in the monitoring programme: it will establish the seasonal amplitude of NDVI and NDMI variation at each specific AoI, inform any necessary adjustment of the alert threshold, and provide the year-one baseline against which year-two conditions are compared. The monitoring officer reviewing the EOSDA dashboard in April 2026 should therefore not interpret the expected wet-season rise in NDVI as a departure from the February 2026 T0; they should record it as the first phenological data point in the multi-year seasonal record.

## 6.5 Monitoring Cycle and Reporting Intervals

The operational monitoring cycle flows from the Sentinel-2 acquisition cadence through a four-level reporting structure — monthly, quarterly, annual, and event-triggered — each producing a defined output for a defined institutional audience. This structure is not bureaucratic procedure; it is the architecture that ensures monitoring data translates into management decisions at the appropriate timescale.

Sentinel-2 acquires new imagery over any given location every three to five days under clear-sky conditions. In a coastal West African environment subject to cloud cover — particularly during the wet season — cloud masking is applied automatically within the EOSDA processing workflow. Cloud-affected pixels are identified using the Sentinel-2 Scene Classification Layer (SCL), which distinguishes cloud shadow, thin cloud, thick cloud, and cloud-adjacent pixels, and these are excluded from the composite calculation. On any given acquisition date, the EOSDA platform calculates NDVI and NDMI for cloud-free pixels within each AoI boundary; acquisitions where more than a defined proportion of AoI pixels are cloud-masked are flagged in the time series as low-confidence data points. This cloud-masking workflow means that during peak wet-season months, when cloud cover is most persistent over the coast, the effective monitoring frequency may drop from every three to five days to every ten to fourteen days in practice. This is not a system failure; it is an expected characteristic of optical satellite monitoring in tropical West Africa, and it is one of the reasons that alert thresholds require two consecutive cloud-filtered acquisitions — rather than a single observation — to trigger a response. The quarterly field monitoring network, described in Chapter 7 of the companion MRV Standalone Report, provides the in-person ground-truth layer that fills the cloud-affected observation gaps.

**Monthly review** is the foundational monitoring cycle. Each calendar month, the designated monitoring officer — nominated from the Wildlife Division with oversight from MESTI — reviews the EOSDA dashboard for all four AoIs. This review assesses whether any alert conditions have been triggered, whether the NDVI and NDMI trends for the month are within the expected seasonal range, and whether cloud masking has created observation gaps that reduce confidence in the month's data. The monthly review generates an internal one-page status note for each AoI, filed in the project's monitoring archive. This is the monitoring system's ground-level operational record — the document that will allow a future carbon verifier to reconstruct the observation history of any AoI for any month of the programme.

**Quarterly reporting** produces the primary external-facing outputs. At the end of each calendar quarter, NDVI and NDMI trend data for all four AoIs are aggregated into a quarterly monitoring report, structured around the standard format agreed with MESTI and the carbon programme oversight team. This report documents the average and minimum NDMI and NDVI values for each AoI in the quarter, identifies any alert events that occurred and the responses they triggered, notes cloud-masking data quality issues, and compares the quarter's readings against the equivalent period in prior years as the time series builds. Quarterly reports are distributed to MESTI, EPA, the relevant district Wildlife Division offices, and CCA representatives at the site level. Distribution to CCA representatives is not ceremonial; it is the mechanism by which the community monitoring network receives feedback on the satellite-level observation of the same zones where they conduct quarterly field measurements. When an EOSDA alert corresponds with a CCA field observation of unusual canopy condition, the convergence of satellite and field evidence constitutes the strongest possible case for management escalation.

**Annual trend analysis** is conducted at the end of each programme year, drawing on the complete four-quarter NDMI and NDMI record for all AoIs. The annual analysis is specifically designed to detect multi-season trends — the progressive drift in seasonal minimum or maximum values that characterises a system under sustained ecological pressure, as distinct from the within-year oscillation of normal phenological cycles. A downward shift in the wet-season NDMI maximum at Anyanui Combine between year one and year two, for example — if the dry-season minimum remains stable — would suggest that canopy moisture recovery during the wetter season is being impaired, a pattern consistent with slowly worsening salinity stress or progressive canopy thinning at the margin. Detecting such a trend requires a multi-year record, and the annual analysis is the reporting mechanism that makes it visible. Annual trend analysis outputs are submitted to MLNR and MESTI as part of the national blue carbon programme's annual performance review, and form part of the evidentiary package submitted to Verra or Gold Standard verification bodies at each periodic verification event.

**Alert-triggered response** operates outside the scheduled reporting cycle. When the EOSDA platform generates an alert — two consecutive cloud-filtered acquisitions showing NDVI or NDMI 15% or more below the T0 baseline for a given AoI — the response protocol is as follows. First, the designated monitoring officer is notified by the platform (via email or dashboard notification, depending on the alerting configuration) within 24 hours of the second triggering acquisition. Second, the officer conducts a remote desktop review within five working days, examining the spatial distribution of the alert within the AoI boundary to determine whether the deviation appears to be spatially localised (suggesting a discrete disturbance event) or spatially diffuse (suggesting a broader physiological stress). Third, based on that desktop assessment, the officer either initiates a field verification request to the relevant Wildlife Division district office within ten working days, or documents the assessment conclusion — for example, that the deviation reflects a cloud artefact not fully captured by the cloud mask — in the monthly monitoring note. Fourth, any field verification finding that confirms a genuine ecological change triggers the formal escalation pathway to MLNR and MESTI within 30 days of the field visit.

## 6.6 Integration with Drone Ground Truth

The EOSDA platform and the drone survey do not operate as parallel but disconnected monitoring systems. They are architecturally linked through the T0 baseline and operationally linked through the alert-to-field-response workflow. Understanding the nature and limits of that linkage is essential for anyone tasked with operating either component.

The drone orthomosaic provides the spatial and spectral reference at a scale that the EOSDA platform's 10-metre pixel cannot approach. A single EOSDA pixel — 10 metres by 10 metres, covering 100 square metres — integrates the spectral reflectance of whatever combination of canopy crown, canopy gap, shadow, and understorey occupies that 100 square metres at the time of acquisition. In a dense, closed-canopy *Rhizophora racemosa* stand, a single pixel may encompass multiple canopy crowns and produce a spectrally coherent signal. In a partially thinned stand where selective harvesting has opened gaps of 2 to 5 metres between crowns, the same pixel integrates crown signal and gap signal, producing a composite value lower than an intact crown value but higher than an open-ground value — a signal that correctly records the degradation but cannot tell the monitoring officer how large the gaps are, where they are located within the pixel, or whether they are actively expanding. The drone orthomosaic at 5 to 10 centimetre GSD answers all of those questions within the same spatial footprint.

When the EOSDA system triggers an alert in an AoI, the drone survey T0 imagery is the primary reference for visual comparison. The monitoring officer reviewing the alert begins with the desktop assessment: the spatial distribution of the EOSDA deviation pattern within the AoI boundary is overlaid, in a GIS viewer, against the T0 orthomosaic for the same zone. If the EOSDA deviation is concentrated in the northeast margin of Agatsivi-Agortoe, and the T0 orthomosaic for that zone shows the edge-zone canopy that was already exhibiting the lowest canopy closure density at the time of survey, the alert is interpreted as potential early-stage thinning at the settlement-proximate margin — the ecologically most plausible scenario for that AoI. If the EOSDA deviation is diffuse across the full extent of an AoI, but the T0 orthomosaic shows intact canopy uniformly, the deviation more likely reflects a seasonal or meteorological cause — an unusually dry spell, a cloud artefact, or a sensor calibration anomaly — and warrants a lower escalation priority.

Re-flight triggers for drone resurveys are formally defined within the monitoring protocol. A drone resurvey should be commissioned when any of three conditions are met: first, when an EOSDA alert triggers a field verification visit that confirms genuine ecological change affecting more than five hectares within any AoI; second, when the annual trend analysis reveals a systematic multi-season NDVI or NDMI decline exceeding the T0 alert threshold at AoI-average level; third, on a scheduled three-to-five-year cycle coinciding with Permanent Sample Plot re-measurement, to provide updated canopy context for the carbon inventory update. Outside these triggers, drone resurveys are not the first-line response to EOSDA alerts — they are the highest-resolution confirmation tool, deployed when the satellite-field evidence combination warrants centimetre-resolution spatial verification.

The drone survey's Digital Surface Model and derived Digital Terrain Model play a further role in interpreting NDMI patterns. At Anyanui Combine, where the micro-topographic evidence from the DTM will eventually show the precise elevation relationship between the Anyanui-Tunu road surface and the surrounding tidal floodplain, the DTM will provide the hydrological explanation for any spatially patterned NDMI anomaly. Low-lying zones that are topographically confined below the tidal flushing threshold — cut off from seasonal freshwater input by a road berm or blocked drainage channel — will exhibit characteristically lower NDMI than topographically elevated zones with equivalent canopy structure. Mapping NDMI deviation against topographic position, using the T0 DTM as the elevation reference, allows the monitoring team to distinguish hydrologically stressed zones from mechanically disturbed zones — a distinction that is invisible to the satellite alone but becomes analytically tractable when the DTM and NDMI data are used together.

## 6.7 Institutional Access and Data Governance

The institutional architecture of the EOSDA monitoring system must be as carefully designed as its technical configuration, because the most sophisticated monitoring platform cannot protect an ecosystem if the people responsible for acting on its outputs lack the access, the training, or the institutional authority to do so.

**Account administration** sits with Ecological Nest Ltd. as the project's lead technical implementation partner during the programme's active implementation phase. The account administrator holds the master credentials for the EOSDA workspace, is responsible for AoI ingestion and alert configuration, and is the first point of contact for any technical issues with the platform. **The transfer of account administration to a Ghana government institution — specifically to MLNR's GIS unit, with secondary access held by MESTI — is a mandatory milestone in the programme's handover protocol,** and must be completed before the programme's implementation phase closes. Until that transfer is executed and confirmed in writing, the monitoring system is technically operational but institutionally incomplete: it has no government-resident custodian capable of maintaining it after the consultancy engagement ends.

**Read-only viewer access** is provisioned to the following institutional categories: MESTI's Climate Change Division, the EPA's Coastal Management Unit, the Wildlife Division at regional and district level (Keta and Ada districts specifically), and the relevant Municipal and Metropolitan District Assemblies with jurisdiction over the monitoring zones. Read-only access allows these institutions to view the EOSDA dashboard, examine the current NDVI and NDMI status of each AoI, and review the alert history — but not to modify AoI boundaries, change alert thresholds, or alter workspace configuration. This access-tier architecture ensures that monitoring data is institutionally visible — reducing the risk that an alert is acted on by only one institution when a coordinated multi-agency response is needed — while protecting the monitoring configuration from inadvertent or unauthorised alteration.

Community monitoring teams — the Community Change Agents trained during the December 2025 baseline assessment and operating under the FPIC community monitoring framework — receive monitoring outputs through a different channel. CCAs do not, in general, have the computing access or technical orientation to navigate the EOSDA web interface independently; nor is it appropriate to provision individual CCA accounts for a complex platform dashboard. Instead, the quarterly monitoring report — prepared by the Wildlife Division monitoring officer from EOSDA data — is shared with CCA representatives in a simplified format: a map of the AoI showing the previous quarter's NDMI or NDVI distribution (rendered as a colour-graded image rather than a raw data table), accompanied by a one-paragraph plain-language summary of whether conditions are stable, improving, or declining. This format was designed specifically to be legible to a field monitor with secondary school-level education and no GIS background. Where EOSDA issues an alert between quarterly reports, the alert summary is communicated to the relevant CCA through the Wildlife Division district officer, who serves as the institutional intermediary between the satellite platform and the community monitoring network.

**Data sovereignty** is unambiguous within this programme's governance framework. All AoI boundary polygons uploaded to the EOSDA platform are owned by MLNR and MESTI as the commissioning institutions; they were generated under a contract between the World Bank WACA ResIP 2 programme and the Government of Ghana, and their legal status is not affected by their temporary hosting on a commercial platform's servers. All NDMI and NDVI time-series data generated for the four AoIs are project-specific outputs that can be exported from the EOSDA platform in standard formats (CSV for time-series data, GeoTIFF for spatial index layers) at any time and archived in the national project data repository. The government's right to export its own monitoring data is not subject to EOSDA's commercial terms and should be exercised at least annually as part of the standard monitoring archive process. Dependence on a single commercial platform for the only copy of multi-year monitoring data would be a governance vulnerability; the export-and-archive protocol ensures that the monitoring record is independent of the platform's continuity.

**Handover protocol** requires completion of the following items before the account is transferred to Ghana government stewardship: (1) written documentation of the full AoI configuration, including named AoIs, boundary source files, alert threshold values, and institutional access list; (2) a platform orientation session for the nominated MLNR GIS officer, covering dashboard navigation, time-series review, alert interpretation, and data export procedures; (3) transfer of account administrator credentials and verification that the new administrator can log in and navigate the workspace; (4) at least one complete quarterly monitoring cycle completed under the new administrator's management, with the quarterly report reviewed and approved by MESTI; and (5) a signed handover record confirming all five steps have been completed to the satisfaction of MLNR and MESTI.

## 6.9 Policy Implications: What Continuous Monitoring Means for Ghana's Blue Carbon Commitments

A senior technical advisor briefing MESTI leadership on the policy significance of the EOSDA monitoring system would make the following argument.

**The monitoring system is not a technical add-on to the blue carbon programme. It is the operational mechanism through which Ghana's climate commitments become verifiable in practice.** Ghana's Nationally Determined Contribution under the Paris Agreement includes explicit commitments to conserve and restore coastal ecosystems as part of the country's adaptation and mitigation strategy. Those commitments, stated in general terms at the national level, can only be substantiated at the international level if they are backed by a credible, continuous, documented observation record showing whether the ecosystems they refer to are being protected, are declining, or are recovering. The EOSDA NDMI and NDVI time series for the four Keta Lagoon AoIs are, in the most literal sense, the evidence that will be presented to international reviewers assessing whether Ghana's blue carbon commitments are being met. A gap in that evidence record — caused by an account lapse, a staffing change, or a failure to maintain the alert workflow — is not a technical inconvenience. It is an evidentiary gap in Ghana's NDC compliance record.

The quarterly reporting cycle that flows from EOSDA monitoring is also the primary evidence generation mechanism for Verra VCS and Gold Standard carbon credit verification. Under both frameworks, projects must demonstrate at each verification event that the accounting area remained intact and protected during the crediting period, that no deforestation or material degradation occurred that was not detected and reported, and that the monitoring system was operational and documented throughout. The alert history, monthly status notes, and quarterly monitoring reports generated by the EOSDA workflow are the documents that a third-party verifier will examine to answer those questions. A programme that can demonstrate three years of continuous, documented, alert-verified monitoring across 1,467 hectares of the Keta Lagoon Complex is a programme positioned to defend its carbon credits under rigorous scrutiny. A programme that cannot produce that documentation — because the monitoring system was set up but not maintained — is a programme whose credits are at risk of reversal.

**The governance risk of monitoring interruption must be quantified and mitigated.** The most predictable cause of monitoring interruption is not technical failure but institutional discontinuity: a staff member who leaves without transferring their platform access, an account subscription that lapses because the renewal invoice was sent to an email address no longer active, or a change in government administration that disrupts the institutional memory of what the monitoring system is and why it matters. These risks are known, and their mitigations are straightforward. Account administrator credentials must be held by an institutional email address, not a personal one, so that a staff departure does not sever access. The EOSDA subscription must be tied to a government budget line — however modest — rather than to a consultancy project budget that closes at programme end. The handover orientation session must produce written documentation sufficient for a successor officer, arriving with no prior knowledge of the system, to resume monitoring without external assistance. None of these mitigations is technically complex. All of them require deliberate institutional commitment from MLNR and MESTI before the programme's implementation phase concludes.

**The EOSDA monitoring system positions Ghana at the frontier of operational blue carbon monitoring capacity among WACA coastal nations.** The West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project spans multiple West African countries with coastal blue carbon assets, few of which have yet established continuous satellite-based monitoring systems with defined alert architectures, institutional access frameworks, and integration with drone ground truth at the site level. The architecture described in this chapter — drone-calibrated T0 baseline, EOSDA continuous monitoring, per-AoI alert thresholds, quarterly reporting to identified institutional recipients, CCA community feedback loop, and defined government handover protocol — represents a monitoring design that is both more rigorous and more institutionally sustainable than most national-level monitoring programmes in the WACA region. Ghana has the opportunity, through this programme, to demonstrate not only that its mangroves are being protected but that its monitoring system is capable of proving it. That demonstration is itself a carbon finance asset: it reduces the risk premium that carbon credit buyers and verifiers assign to projects in jurisdictions with uncertain monitoring capacity, and it supports Ghana's credibility as a partner in international carbon markets that are increasingly requiring rigorous MRV as a condition of market access.

The practical immediate action for MESTI leadership is not to review the technical configuration of the EOSDA platform — that is the technical team's domain. It is to confirm two institutional commitments: first, that a named officer at the Wildlife Division, supported by MESTI oversight, is formally designated as the monitoring system's responsible custodian with the time and institutional backing to execute the monthly review and quarterly reporting cycle; and second, that the account administrator handover from Ecological Nest to MLNR is scheduled with a confirmed target date before the programme's implementation phase closes. With those two commitments in place, the technical architecture described in this chapter becomes an operational monitoring system. Without them, it remains a well-designed capability waiting for the institutional ownership that will make it function.

> **Chapter 6 Summary — for Senior Ministry Officials**
>
> The EOSDA Crop Monitoring platform provides automated, continuous Sentinel-2 satellite monitoring of four priority mangrove Areas of Interest across 1,467 hectares of the Keta Lagoon Complex: Aborlove Nolopi (52 Ha), Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha), Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha), and Anyanui Combine (128 Ha). Monitoring tracks two vegetation indices — NDVI (canopy greenness) and NDMI (canopy moisture) — updated every three to five days at 10-metre resolution. A 15% deviation from the drone-calibrated T0 baseline in either index triggers an alert requiring field verification within ten working days. The monitoring cycle runs monthly (internal review), quarterly (formal report to MESTI, EPA, Wildlife Division, and district offices), and annually (multi-year trend analysis for NDC and carbon standard reporting). **The platform architecture is complete; account opening, AoI upload, alert configuration, and institutional access provisioning are pending immediate action by the project team.** The two non-negotiable institutional commitments required of MESTI are: (1) designation of a named monitoring officer with protected time for platform management; and (2) a confirmed date for account administrator handover to MLNR before the consultancy phase closes. These commitments determine whether a well-designed monitoring system becomes a functioning one.
