# Chapter 3: Free, Prior and Informed Consent and Community Engagement

---

## 3.1 Why FPIC is Inseparable from Drone Monitoring in This Context

A drone survey over the Keta Lagoon Complex is not a neutral technical exercise conducted above an empty landscape. It is an aerial survey of community land — land whose management, use, and ownership are structured by a tripartite governance system that has no analogue in most of the contexts where drone monitoring is treated as a routine remote sensing operation. Authority over the Lagoon's mangrove zones is distributed across customary institutions (chiefs, queen mothers, councils of elders who hold the social licence to grant or withhold access), statutory agencies (the Wildlife Division, District Assemblies holding legal jurisdiction over Ramsar site management), and the emerging community-based structures through which the WACA Blue Carbon initiative is being operationalised — Community Change Agents, Community Resource Management Committees, and the women's groups and VSLAs that represent the most functionally effective governance organisations at the grassroots level. Any survey aircraft transiting over the four Areas of Interest that constitute the primary monitoring footprint of this report is flying over land that these institutions govern.

The data collected by that aircraft — centimetre-resolution orthomosaics, Structure from Motion topographic models, multispectral canopy condition imagery — will not remain a scientific record. It will be used to define the spatial boundaries of carbon credit accounting under Verra VM0033 or a comparable voluntary market methodology. Those boundaries will determine restoration zones. Restoration zones will impose access restrictions on the fuelwood collectors, fishers, and aquaculturists whose livelihoods depend on the mangrove stands being monitored. The monitoring outputs will support carbon credit claims that carry market value, and the financial flows from those credits will be distributed — equitably or inequitably — to the communities from whose land they were generated. Every orthomosaic produced by this workstream is, in this sense, a financial instrument in embryo.

Communities whose land and livelihoods are inscribed in that instrument have a right to know it exists, to understand what it records, to consent to its production, and to participate in governance decisions about how it is used and disclosed. Under the World Bank's Environmental and Social Framework, that right is operationalised through two binding standards. ESS7 — which applies to Sub-Saharan African Historically Underserved Traditional Local Communities, the standard that governs this project's engagement with Ghana's coastal fishing and farming communities — requires that projects affecting these groups pursue Free, Prior, and Informed Consent as a condition of activities with significant implications for resource access, livelihoods, or cultural heritage. ESS10 — Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure — requires that meaningful engagement with affected communities precedes operational decisions and that information is disclosed in accessible, culturally appropriate formats. Both standards apply to the drone survey and continuous monitoring system described in this report.

The WACA Blue Carbon initiative's own Risk Assessment and Safeguards Report, prepared as part of the August 2025 baseline package, identifies FPIC failure as a top-tier institutional risk: the determination that inadequate consent processes "could jeopardize project funding and the international validation of any carbon credits generated." That is not a social development caveat appended to a technical report. It is the project's own diagnosis of the consequences of conducting drone monitoring without documented social licence. This chapter records what has been done to establish that licence and what remains to be completed.

## 3.2 The FPIC Principle in the WACA Context

**Free** consent, in this project context, requires more than the absence of direct coercion. It requires structural care about the difference between the consent of institutions and the consent of communities. Traditional authority figures — chiefs, queen mothers, elders — hold genuine social legitimacy and their engagement is an indispensable precondition for any field activity. Chief approval was a mandatory first step for the research team's entry into communities including Dzita, Sota, Pute, and Sankor during the August 2025 baseline fieldwork, and that precedent applies equally to drone survey operations. But the consent of a traditional council, even when freely given, does not constitute the consent of the community as a whole. The Governance, Institutions, and CREMA Readiness Report documents a governance system where traditional decision-making bodies are "almost always male-led" and where women, youth, migrants, and persons with disabilities have "limited to no representation." A consent process that routes exclusively through these structures produces documented agreement from gatekeepers without meaningful engagement with the primary users of the land being monitored. Separate, targeted consultation with women's groups, youth organisations, and migrant populations is not a courtesy addition to the FPIC process — it is a compliance requirement.

**Prior** consent means that drone flights are conducted only after community engagement is completed, not in parallel with it or after the fact. In practice for this workstream, it means that the AoI boundary files were not uploaded to the EOSDA platform for continuous monitoring configuration, and no orthomosaic imagery has been formally disclosed or used in any carbon accounting calculation, before the communities adjacent to each AoI had been engaged about the purpose and implications of that monitoring. It also means that if EOSDA alert signals from a monitored AoI are to be used in future verification submissions, the FPIC process must have been completed — and documented — before those submissions are made. The prior requirement extends across the project lifecycle: communities that consented to baseline mapping activities during the June–August 2025 fieldwork have not thereby consented to the specific boundary configurations of the AoIs established in February–March 2026, to the access restrictions that may be associated with designated restoration zones, or to the terms of any future carbon credit arrangement. Each of these represents a materially distinct intervention requiring its own documented FPIC process.

**Informed** consent in communities where the concept of carbon markets has no established local referent — where carbon rights, credit verification, and benefit-sharing schedules are not familiar frameworks — requires sustained, multilingual, and multi-format information disclosure. The Governance Report documents a landscape shaped by "community fatigue from previous, unfulfilled development promises." In that context, information disclosure that overstates certainties, glosses over timelines, or neglects to explain the conditions under which carbon credits are validated and payments made will generate initial agreement that dissolves into passive non-compliance when reality fails to match expectation. Informed consent in this project must communicate honestly: what drones are and what they photograph; who will hold and access the imagery; how monitoring data supports carbon credit claims; what financial benefits communities might receive and over what timeframe; and what restrictions on land use, if any, are associated with monitored zones. That communication must be delivered in Ewe (the dominant language of the Keta Lagoon communities), Fante (where applicable for adjacent communities), and through visual formats, participatory discussion, and community radio where literacy barriers are documented.

## 3.3 The FPIC Process Conducted for the Drone Survey AoIs

The engagement process supporting the drone survey and EOSDA configuration was initiated as an extension of the community engagement conducted during the June–August 2025 socio-economic baseline fieldwork, which itself operated under FPIC-compliant protocols aligned with the project's Environmental and Social Safeguards Framework. The baseline engagement established the foundational relationships, institutional contacts, and consent documentation framework upon which the drone survey FPIC process has built. What follows documents the AoI-specific engagement activities conducted between the conclusion of the baseline fieldwork and the completion of the AoI boundary datasets in February–March 2026.

**AoI 1: Aborlove Nolopi (52 Ha) — Eastern Cluster**

The Aborlove Nolopi AoI covers a mixed landscape of degraded and recovering mangrove in the eastern sector of the Lagoon. Community engagement for this AoI was conducted with the Aborlove-Norlopi traditional council, whose jurisdiction encompasses the land within and adjacent to the monitoring boundary. Chief consultation established the foundational social licence for survey operations over this zone, with the project team providing a structured briefing on the purpose of drone overflights, the nature of the orthomosaic and multispectral data to be collected, and the connection between aerial monitoring and the carbon inventory work underway at the adjacent baseline plots. Community information meetings were facilitated by the CCA assigned to this cluster, who provided Ewe-language translation and subsequent community follow-up to ensure that the information presented in the formal meeting was understood at the household level.

Women's group engagement for AoI 1 was conducted through a separate consultation session with the fuelwood collector networks whose harvesting activity occurs within the AoI boundary. Participants were informed that the AoI covers zones identified as priorities for ecological assessment and potential restoration planning, and that the drone imagery may be used to inform access restrictions in future restoration zones. The distinction between the monitoring phase (which does not itself impose restrictions) and the restoration planning phase (which will require its own consent process) was explained and documented. Youth engagement at Aborlove was conducted informally through the CCA network rather than through a formal youth consultation session; this gap is recorded in the FPIC status table at Section 3.8 as requiring completion before restoration zone designations can proceed.

**AoI 2: Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha) — Central Cluster**

The Agatsivi-Agortoe AoI is the largest of the four monitoring zones, spanning contiguous and fragmented canopy across a landscape subject to sustained fuelwood pressure. Multiple community units fall within or adjacent to this AoI — Agatsivi, Agortoe, and Agbastivi — making this the most complex of the four FPIC processes in terms of the number of separate governance structures requiring engagement.

Traditional authority engagement was conducted sequentially with the councils governing each of the three adjacent communities, recognising that a single consolidated meeting convened by one chief would risk the appearance of that chief speaking for communities over whom their jurisdiction is limited or contested. Separate consultation meetings with each council were documented individually. The Agbastivi community cluster includes Galotse as an adjacent settlement — the Governance Report identifies Galotse as a community where "Queen Mothers were instrumental in mobilizing female participation" for project activities during the baseline. That precedent was carried forward into the drone survey FPIC process: Queen Mothers in the Galotse area were engaged directly and invited to facilitate women's consultation sessions within their social networks. Their involvement substantially increased the participation of women in the information disclosure process for this AoI.

The multi-community character of this AoI required that CCA facilitators from each adjacent community participated in the FPIC process, rather than relying on a single CCA whose community affiliation might create perceptions of partiality. Three CCA facilitators supported the engagement across Agatsivi, Agortoe, and Agbastivi. Focus group discussions included mixed-gender and gender-separate sessions; gender-separate sessions for women in the fish processing economy were conducted at times that accommodated the working schedules of women managing post-harvest activities — a logistical adaptation recommended by the CCAs based on their knowledge of community daily rhythms.

**AoI 3: Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha) — Central Cluster**

The Salo-Agortoe Stretch encompasses the creek system that is most directly connected to the Salo community's livelihood and land-use patterns. The Salo community has an historically intimate relationship with this mangrove zone: the Salo mangrove market operates within or adjacent to this AoI, and Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1 — the first georeferenced carbon inventory anchor in the monitoring system, delineated in March 2026 — falls within the stretch's monitoring boundary. That inventory plot is a direct material link between the drone survey AoI and the carbon accounting work to which monitoring outputs will contribute, and it was central to the information disclosed to the Salo community during the FPIC process for this AoI.

The Salo FPIC engagement was shaped by one of the most consequential findings in the Governance and Risk Assessment reports: a female respondent from Salo stated directly, "We use the land, but we don't decide anything about it." This documented exclusion of women from resource governance decisions within the community whose land hosts the most carbon-significant monitoring zone in the central cluster made gender-inclusive engagement not only a compliance requirement but an ethical imperative specific to this site. The FPIC process for AoI 3 included a dedicated women's consultation session convened with fish processor associations and VSLA members in Salo. The session covered the same information as the community-wide meeting — drone purpose and operations, data access, connection to carbon crediting — and added a specific discussion of how restoration access restrictions might affect the daily fuelwood collection routes currently used by women in this community. The outcome of that discussion, including the concerns raised about fuelwood access during any monitoring or restoration period, is documented in the FPIC log for AoI 3 and must be addressed in the benefit-sharing and access arrangement negotiations that will follow the monitoring phase.

**AoI 4: Anyanui Combine (128 Ha) — Western Cluster**

The Anyanui Combine AoI occupies the estuary mouth, where the Keta Lagoon meets tidal marine influence. Ecosystem health monitoring conducted for the companion Wetland Ecosystem Health Report identified a pH anomaly of 2.7 at monitoring stations within this AoI — a severe acidification signature consistent with tidal blockage by road infrastructure. The road responsible for that blockage is not an external intrusion into the landscape; it is a community access route serving Anyanui, Tunu, and Fuveme. Any management recommendation for tidal restoration at this site — including the recommendation already documented in the Ecosystem Health Report — is simultaneously a governance decision about a piece of infrastructure on which community members depend for daily movement and economic activity.

The FPIC process for AoI 4 was therefore structured to include explicit disclosure of this connection. Communities were informed not only about the drone survey and EOSDA monitoring configuration but about the ecosystem health findings that make AoI 4 the AoI most likely to generate a management recommendation in the near term — and about the fact that any tidal restoration measure at the estuary mouth would require community consultation and consent on the trade-offs between hydrological restoration benefits and potential impacts on road access. This framing — honest about the downstream implications of current monitoring — is consistent with the FPIC principle that communities must understand the full implications of what they are consenting to, not merely the immediate technical activity.

Traditional authority engagement was conducted with the councils governing Anyanui, Tunu, and Fuveme. CCA facilitators from each community participated, with particular attention to ensuring that Tunu and Fuveme — which are smaller and less institutionally prominent than Anyanui — were not treated as appendages of the larger community's consent process. Youth engagement at the Anyanui cluster included a specific discussion of the road infrastructure context, recognising that access to transport routes disproportionately affects the mobility of young people who travel for work, education, and market access.

## 3.4 Community Change Agents as FPIC Facilitators

The Community Change Agents trained during the December 2025 baseline assessment fieldwork served as the primary implementation mechanism for the drone survey FPIC process across all four AoIs. Their role was not administrative. It was culturally embedded facilitation: translating technical concepts into accessible community language, situating the drone survey within the broader Blue Carbon initiative that communities had already been introduced to during baseline activities, and providing the social context that determines whether an information meeting produces genuine understanding or performed agreement.

The effectiveness of CCAs in this role was demonstrated concretely during the baseline engagement from which the drone survey FPIC process draws. In Sankor, CCAs leveraged existing social networks — church groups, football clubs — to disseminate project information through trusted peer channels rather than formal announcements. In Dzita, CCAs successfully facilitated a dialogue between landowners and youth to resolve a dispute over a potential restoration site. These are not examples of CCAs delivering a message on behalf of an external team; they are examples of CCAs functioning as trusted community actors whose social embeddedness allows them to navigate the power dynamics and relationship histories that external facilitators cannot access.

For the drone survey FPIC process, CCAs played three specific roles. First, they prepared communities for formal consultation sessions by conducting prior household-level engagement that allowed community members to arrive at information meetings with foundational awareness rather than encountering the concept of drone monitoring for the first time in a formal setting — a practice that substantially improves the quality of consent obtained in the meeting itself. Second, they provided Ewe-language facilitation that ensured conceptual accuracy was maintained in translation, rather than the simplification or distortion that occurs when technical concepts are filtered through an interpreter whose accountability is to the external team rather than to the community. Third, they conducted post-meeting follow-up to identify community members who had not attended formal sessions, to ensure that consent documentation did not systematically exclude those whose participation was lowest in formal meeting formats — typically women with young children, persons with disabilities, and elderly community members.

## 3.5 Inclusive Engagement: Women, Youth, and Marginalized Groups

The central governance finding of the August 2025 baseline across all three Ramsar sites is unambiguous: the primary users of mangrove resources — women who depend on fuelwood for their livelihoods in the fish processing economy — have no formal voice in the development of rules for mangrove management. This finding is not incidental to the drone survey FPIC process. Women conducting fuelwood collection traverse the same AoI zones that the drone orthomosaics now document. Their daily routes, harvesting sites, and use patterns are, in effect, inscribed in the multispectral canopy condition data collected by this survey. Yet the governance structures through which their communities interface with the carbon project — traditional councils, interim CRMCs — are "almost always male-led." A consent process that routes exclusively through those structures will document the consent of the institutions that govern land on paper without the consent of the people who govern it in practice.

The FPIC process for each AoI therefore required separate, dedicated women's consultation sessions as a non-negotiable protocol step, not as a supplementary engagement for women willing to attend mixed-format meetings. The logistics of those sessions were designed to be accessible: scheduled to avoid the peak hours of fish smoking and market activity, convened at accessible locations within the community rather than at formal meeting halls, and facilitated by female CCAs or female community liaisons where available. The information package delivered in women's sessions covered the same substantive content as community-wide meetings, with specific attention to two topics that are most directly relevant to women's livelihoods: the possible future access restrictions on fuelwood collection zones that may follow from restoration planning based on drone survey outputs, and the mechanism through which women's groups would receive a share of any carbon finance benefits — specifically the commitment that a defined portion of co-benefits will be channelled through established VSLAs and fish processor associations rather than aggregated at the CREMA committee level where male gatekeeping risks intercepting the flow.

Youth engagement for the drone survey FPIC process drew on the warning documented in the baseline: a youth respondent in Totope stated, "If they don't see us, we will stop seeing the forest." That sentence is a precise articulation of the risk that youth disengagement from governance creates for carbon permanence. Young people are the primary labour force for any restoration activity that follows from the monitoring work documented in this report. If they experience the drone survey and its associated governance processes as activities conducted over their communities without their participation, the passive non-compliance that the Risk Assessment identifies as the Keta landscape's primary social risk will have been actively recruited. Youth-specific engagement sessions were conducted at the Agatsivi-Agortoe and Anyanui AoI clusters, with discussion focused on the employment and skills development opportunities associated with the blue carbon programme — a tangible entry point for a demographic whose engagement with conservation messaging depends on visible and credible links to their own economic futures. At Aborlove, formal youth consultation remains pending and is flagged at Section 3.8.

Migrant populations — Togolese and Beninois fishers present in significant numbers across the Keta Lagoon Complex — face a specific exclusion risk that requires dedicated attention. These groups are among the most resource-dependent in the landscape, are excluded from customary tenure systems, and have no formal representation in traditional councils or emerging CRMCs. Their consent cannot be obtained through institutional channels. The FPIC process attempted to reach migrant fishers through CCA networks with social connections that cross community-membership boundaries, and through informal outreach at fishing landing sites. The degree to which this outreach achieved meaningful engagement — as opposed to information delivery to individuals without the social context to evaluate what they were hearing — is documented honestly in the FPIC logs and represents an area requiring sustained attention as the project moves from monitoring to restoration planning.

## 3.6 Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM)

The Risk Assessment and Safeguards Report establishes that formal grievance mechanisms are "informal or non-existent in over 80% of communities" profiled across the three Ramsar sites. For the drone survey and EOSDA monitoring system, this baseline absence of grievance infrastructure creates a specific and immediate risk: if a community member objects to a drone overflight, raises concerns about who is accessing the imagery, or believes their community was not properly consulted before an AoI boundary was registered in the monitoring platform, there is currently no standardised, accessible pathway through which that concern can be received, documented, and resolved. The absence of that pathway does not prevent grievances from arising; it redirects them into informal channels where they are less visible, less accountable, and more likely to manifest as passive non-compliance or active opposition.

The GRM established under the WACA Blue Carbon initiative, and which applies to the drone survey and EOSDA monitoring workstream, operates through four escalation tiers designed to match the severity and complexity of grievances with the appropriate resolution authority.

At the first tier, CCAs and designated CRMC Grievance Officers serve as the accessible community-level point of contact for any concern arising from monitoring activities. This tier is designed to handle the majority of drone and EOSDA monitoring grievances: confusion about what the drone photographed or who has access to the imagery, concerns about whether a particular community was adequately consulted before its AoI was activated, or requests for information about what happens if a change alert is detected. The CCA receives and logs the concern in a standardised GRM logbook — the documentation requirement applies regardless of how the grievance is ultimately resolved — and attempts informal resolution within seven days. The CCA network's effectiveness in this role derives from the same quality that makes them effective FPIC facilitators: they are peer-nominated, locally trusted, and accountable to the community rather than to the project team.

At the second tier, grievances that the CCA cannot resolve within seven days, or that involve concerns about the conduct of the FPIC process itself or the use of monitoring data in ways communities did not consent to, are escalated to the CREMA Executive Committee with the participation of traditional leaders. This tier handles concerns that require mediation between parties, or that involve substantive decisions about community governance of the monitoring process — for example, a community's request to withdraw consent for a specific monitoring boundary, or a dispute between adjacent communities about which traditional council's jurisdiction applies to a shared AoI zone.

At the third tier, concerns that cannot be resolved at CREMA level within thirty days, or complaints against CREMA leadership or project staff, are escalated to a joint Safeguards Desk comprising representatives from the District Assembly and the Wildlife Division. This tier carries statutory authority and provides the escalation mechanism that gives the first two tiers credibility — without the backstop of a body with genuine legal standing, community-level grievance processes can be managed by powerful community actors to their own advantage.

At the fourth tier, where systemic failure of the GRM is documented — grievances that have exhausted the local and district processes without fair resolution, or evidence that the GRM is being systematically subverted — the World Bank's Accountability Mechanism provides the ultimate recourse pathway. This tier is not anticipated to be activated for routine monitoring concerns, but its existence is disclosed to communities as part of the FPIC information package. Communities have a right to know the full scope of the accountability infrastructure they are operating within, including their right to seek redress from the project's financing institution where local processes fail.

Surveillance-specific concerns — fear that drone imagery includes personal property, domestic spaces, or individuals in ways that constitute a privacy violation — were raised in consultations at multiple AoI clusters and are addressed explicitly in the data access arrangements documented in Section 3.7. The GRM provides the channel through which community members can raise these concerns after monitoring activities have occurred.

## 3.7 Data Sovereignty and Community Access to Monitoring Data

The previous drone report that this chapter supplements did not address data sovereignty. That omission is a substantive gap, not a minor oversight. Communities have a right to access and understand the data collected about their land — a right that flows directly from the FPIC principles that grant consent in the first place. Consent to monitoring is not the same as consent to unrestricted third-party access to monitoring outputs. The governance framework for drone survey data and EOSDA monitoring outputs must specify who holds the data, who can access it and under what conditions, what community actors can see their AoI's monitoring record, and how alert signals are communicated.

The data governance arrangement for this workstream operates on the principle that community-level actors are the first responders to monitoring signals within their AoI. When the EOSDA platform detects a deviation of 15% or more in NDMI values within an AoI — the change detection threshold that triggers a vegetation condition alert — the notification should reach the CCA assigned to the relevant community before it reaches any national-level platform or reporting cycle. CCAs, as the culturally embedded local monitors trained in the project's ecological interpretation framework, are best placed to conduct rapid ground-truth assessment of an alert signal: to determine whether an NDMI deviation reflects anthropogenic disturbance, storm impact, or data artefact before an alert is escalated through district and national channels. Reversing this sequence — delivering alerts first to national authorities who then commission community investigation — treats communities as subjects of surveillance rather than participants in monitoring.

Community access to the orthomosaic products generated by the drone survey is a separate question that requires explicit documentation. The resolution and scope of centimetre-scale orthomosaics means that they are not appropriate for unrestricted public distribution — they may capture details of private property, community members, and livelihood infrastructure. But communities should have access to community-level data summaries derived from the orthomosaics: canopy condition maps, extent measurements, and the boundary files that define their AoI within the national monitoring system. These derived products allow community governance structures — CREMA committees, CCAs, traditional councils — to verify that the monitoring boundaries registered in the platform correspond to the boundaries discussed and consented to during the FPIC process, and to track the ecosystem condition of their land over time without requiring technical access to the raw orthomosaic files.

The EOSDA platform dashboard and its configuration for the four Keta Lagoon AoIs is currently accessible at the project team level. Extending CCA-level access to the monitoring dashboard — allowing designated community monitors to view the NDMI time series and alert status for their AoI — is a technical step that has not yet been completed and is flagged in the status table below as a pending action. This access is not a luxury; it is the operational expression of the principle that local communities are co-custodians of their own landscape monitoring system.

This documentation is outside the scope of the drone survey report but must be flagged here because the data generated by this workstream is the direct upstream input to that process. A monitoring system without a documented benefit-sharing framework is a system that collects data about community land without community economic participation in the value that data enables. Completing that documentation is the next sequential obligation in the FPIC chain that this chapter has opened.
