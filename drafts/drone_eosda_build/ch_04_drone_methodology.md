# Chapter 4: Drone Survey Methodology

---

## 4.1 The Three-Output Survey Architecture

A drone survey of the Keta Lagoon Areas of Interest is not, in the first instance, a photography exercise. It is a multi-output data collection mission whose three primary products — an orthomosaic, a topographic dataset, and a calibrated multispectral reflectance archive — are designed to answer fundamentally different questions about the mangrove ecosystem and its trajectory. Understanding the survey's methodology requires understanding, at the outset, that all three outputs are produced from a single flight mission using the same aircraft and the same sensor payload. They are not the products of three separate campaigns with separate costs and schedules. They are three analytical lenses derived from one body of raw imagery, each processed differently to extract different categories of information.

The first output — the orthomosaic — is a georeferenced, seamless photomosaic of the entire AoI at 5 to 10 centimetre ground sample distance (GSD), assembled by structure-from-motion photogrammetry from the raw imagery archive. As a visual product, the orthomosaic is the aerial equivalent of a satellite true-colour image, but at a resolution approximately 1,000 to 2,000 times finer than the 10 to 20 metre Sentinel-2 imagery used by the GIS team to produce the national mangrove extent baseline. Where the Sentinel-2 pixel is a single spectral average across a square area that may contain several tree crowns, a dirt path, and a shallow water margin, the orthomosaic resolves individual canopy crowns, canopy gaps, root exposure, and water-surface texture within the same area. This resolution difference is not incidental — it is the basis of the orthomosaic's primary analytical function: the direct validation and, where necessary, supersession of the satellite classification within the four AoIs.

The second output — the digital surface model and derived topographic dataset — is a three-dimensional point cloud and associated terrain model capturing micro-elevation differences in the range of 5 to 20 centimetres. These differences are entirely invisible to satellite sensors, which measure spectral reflectance and cannot infer ground elevation directly. At the scale of a mangrove restoration site, micro-topographic variation of this magnitude is operationally decisive: a 15-centimetre elevation difference determines whether a given patch is inundated at mean high tide, which in turn determines whether a newly planted propagule survives its first wet season. The topographic output is therefore the restoration design tool — the dataset that answers questions about drainage channel geometry, tidal connectivity, and micro-topographic feasibility that no other instrument available to the project can answer at the spatial resolution required.

The third output — the raw multispectral reflectance data from all five bands of the MicaSense sensor — is the species identification and vegetation health index dataset. Calibrated reflectance values across the Blue (475 nm), Green (560 nm), Red (668 nm), Red Edge (717 nm), and Near-Infrared (840 nm) channels are the inputs for NDVI, NDMI, and Red Edge chlorophyll index calculations that enable discrimination between *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* canopy at individual tree-crown scale, quantification of canopy moisture stress as a precursor to die-back, and calibration of the EOSDA continuous monitoring system to the specific spectral signature of Keta Lagoon mangroves at baseline condition.

These three outputs together serve two strategic functions within the broader monitoring architecture. First, they validate and, within the AoI footprints, supersede the satellite-derived mangrove classification produced by the GIS team. Second, they provide the T0 calibration anchor — the baseline against which all future monitoring outputs are referenced — for the EOSDA continuous monitoring system described in Chapter 6.

## 4.2 Aircraft and Sensor Platform

The primary survey platform for this workstream is the DJI Phantom 4 Multispectral (P4M), an integrated drone and multispectral sensor system developed specifically for vegetation mapping and precision forestry applications. The integration of airframe and sensor in a single commercially available platform eliminates the alignment and synchronisation errors that affect multi-component systems where a third-party sensor is mounted on a generic drone airframe. The P4M carries a downward-facing multispectral camera array incorporating five individual 1.6 MP sensors with fixed-focus lenses centred on the Blue (475 nm), Green (560 nm), Red (668 nm), Red Edge (717 nm), and Near-Infrared (840 nm) wavebands — the same five bands provided by the MicaSense RedEdge and Altum sensors used in professional vegetation mapping, here delivered in a more operationally compact configuration.

The critical technical feature of the P4M for the purposes of photogrammetric accuracy is its integrated RTK (Real-Time Kinematic) positioning system. RTK positioning provides centimetre-level GPS accuracy by receiving real-time correction signals from a fixed ground station or network, eliminating the positional drift that accumulates in conventional GPS-guided survey aircraft. For a mangrove monitoring programme that requires direct comparison between orthomosaics produced in different survey epochs — comparing T0 against T1, T1 against T2 — the absolute positional accuracy of each mission is as important as its internal resolution. A 10 centimetre positional error between two survey epochs, which would be invisible in the 10-metre Sentinel-2 data and entirely irrelevant to the national classification workflow, can produce false change signals at 5 to 10 centimetre GSD orthomosaic resolution. The RTK system, combined with the ground control point network described in Section 4.3, prevents positional drift from being misread as ecological change.

Survey operations across the four AoIs were conducted at a flight altitude of 100 to 120 metres above ground level (AGL), producing the 5 to 10 centimetre GSD that the monitoring design requires. Flight plans were configured with 80 percent forward image overlap and 75 percent side overlap — overlap parameters that provide the redundant image coverage necessary for robust structure-from-motion reconstruction and minimise the risk of data gaps in the photogrammetric model. The total surveyed area across all four AoIs is 1,467 hectares, distributed as follows: Aborlove Nolopi (52 Ha, eastern cluster), Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha, central cluster), Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha, central cluster including Salo Permanent Sample Plot 1), and Anyanui Combine (128 Ha, western cluster). Each AoI was planned and executed as a separate flight mission, with dedicated pre-flight calibration, ground control point occupation, and post-flight quality assessment.

The integrated downwelling light sensor (DLS 2) mounted on the top surface of the aircraft records solar irradiance continuously throughout each flight mission. This measurement is required for accurate radiometric calibration of the multispectral imagery: because solar irradiance varies with cloud cover, atmospheric conditions, and sun angle during the mission, calibrating pixel reflectance values against an irradiance record — rather than assuming constant illumination — is the difference between a dataset that is scientifically comparable across time and one that reflects the weather conditions on a particular morning.

## 4.3 Ground Control Point Design and RTK Correction

Ground Control Points (GCPs) are the physical anchors that tie the photogrammetric model to real-world geographic coordinates. A minimum of five GCPs per AoI were established at the time of survey, with points distributed across the AoI extent to provide even spatial coverage of the model constraint. Each GCP consists of a highly visible physical marker — typically a checkerboard panel or painted target — positioned at a location whose coordinates have been measured by RTK GPS to centimetre-level accuracy. The GCP coordinates are introduced into the photogrammetric processing workflow as control constraints, forcing the reconstructed three-dimensional model to conform to real-world geometry rather than floating in a self-consistent but positionally arbitrary reference frame.

For continuous monitoring purposes, the permanence and resolvability of the GCP network across survey epochs is as important as its initial accuracy. The same GCPs will be reoccupied in every future drone revisit of each AoI — whether that revisit is part of the annual resurvey schedule or triggered by an EOSDA anomaly alert. By reoccupying the same control network, each future orthomosaic is constrained to the same real-world coordinate system as the T0 baseline, enabling direct pixel-to-pixel comparison between missions without the systematic positional offsets that would otherwise accumulate between surveys conducted months or years apart. This is not a photogrammetric formality. The ability to attribute spectral changes in a future orthomosaic to genuine ecological change — rather than to differential registration error — depends entirely on the consistency of the control network across survey epochs.

The RTK measurements for GCP positions were carried out using a survey-grade receiver in conjunction with a network RTK correction service or a co-located base station, achieving horizontal accuracies of better than 2 centimetres and vertical accuracies of better than 3 centimetres. These accuracy levels are consistent with the requirements of Verra VM0033 for spatial data used in carbon stock boundary delineation and with the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework's standards for georeferenced community asset mapping.

## 4.4 Primary Output 1 — Orthomosaic: Validation of the Satellite Classification

The orthomosaic is the output that connects the drone survey most directly to the existing scientific record for the Keta Lagoon. The geo-social mangrove extent baseline report, produced by the project's GIS team in 2024, classified 20,717.95 hectares of mangroves within the Keta Lagoon Complex using Harmonised Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery at 10 to 20 metre resolution, with a Random Forest classifier trained and validated on 319 reference data points. That report is explicit about the capabilities and the limits of this approach. In the section describing the methodological rationale for Sentinel-2 over earlier Landsat data, the report states that in a heterogeneous landscape like Ghana's coast, where mangroves often exist as narrow fringes along lagoons or in small, fragmented patches, the higher resolution of Sentinel-2 is critical — it allows for the detection and accurate classification of smaller stands that would have been missed or misclassified as mixed pixels in the coarser 30-metre Landsat data. This candid acknowledgement that Sentinel-2, for all its improvement over Landsat, still generates mixed-pixel classification uncertainty in fragmented mangrove landscapes defines the validation function that the drone orthomosaic performs.

The 102.8 MB `keta_lagoon.tif` classification raster archived in the project repository is the spatial output of that Sentinel-2 Random Forest analysis. It is the master spatial reference layer against which the drone orthomosaics are overlaid within the four AoIs. The validation workflow compares the satellite-derived binary classification — mangrove versus non-mangrove — at each location within the AoI footprints against the orthomosaic's centimetre-resolution visual evidence of what is actually present at that location. This comparison identifies four categories of classification discrepancy that the satellite workflow cannot self-diagnose.

Commission errors are locations the Random Forest model classified as mangrove that the orthomosaic reveals are not mangrove. In a fragmented coastal landscape like the Keta system, these can arise from spectral confusion between mangrove canopy and other dark, dense vegetation types, from water-shadow effects in pixels straddling open-water and canopy boundaries, or from the averaging of a mixed-canopy pixel whose reflectance falls within the training-data envelope for mangrove even though the actual ground cover is a mosaic of mangrove and mudflat. At 10 to 20 metres, the classifier makes a binary decision about a pixel whose area might contain only 40 percent mangrove canopy. At 5 to 10 centimetre GSD, the orthomosaic shows exactly what is inside that pixel — and the verification is immediate and unambiguous.

Omission errors are small mangrove stands — fragments smaller than one Sentinel-2 pixel, that is, smaller than 100 to 400 square metres — that the satellite classification missed entirely but that are visible in the orthomosaic. In the Keta landscape, where the socio-economic baseline documents the progressive fragmentation of mangrove stands under fuelwood pressure from adjacent fish-smoking communities, these small fragments represent a significant category of unmapped carbon stock. The geo-social report itself identified previously undocumented mangrove stands through participatory resource mapping with community members, and some of these stands may fall below the satellite detection threshold. The orthomosaic provides the definitive answer.

Canopy density is a third dimension of validation that the satellite cannot supply directly. The NDVI derived from Sentinel-2 provides a proxy for vegetation vigour, but it cannot distinguish a closed-canopy stand with high leaf area index from an open-canopy stand with patchy regeneration — both can produce similar composite NDVI values at 10-metre resolution. The orthomosaic delivers a direct canopy cover percentage per hectare, enabling stratification of the classified mangrove area into condition classes that inform carbon stock estimation and restoration prioritisation.

Degradation mapping is the fourth and arguably most operationally critical validation function. Early-stage thinning — the removal of individual trees for fuelwood, producing scattered canopy gaps not yet large enough to register as non-mangrove at satellite resolution — is the leading edge of the deforestation process documented in the geo-social baseline. At 5 to 10 centimetre GSD, canopy gaps produced by individual tree removal are directly visible. Within the Agatsivi-Agortoe AoI, which the monitoring plan identifies as subject to sustained fuelwood pressure from adjacent fish-smoking operations, detection of early-stage thinning events between survey epochs is the primary early-warning function of the orthomosaic monitoring workflow.

Where the orthomosaic reveals significant discrepancies with the satellite classification — commission errors that inflate the classified mangrove area, or omission errors and gap patterns that reveal structural degradation invisible to the satellite — the drone-derived interpretation supersedes the Sentinel-2 result within the AoI footprints. For the purposes of carbon accounting under Verra VM0033, the higher-resolution direct observation takes precedence over the modelled classification.

## 4.5 Primary Output 2 — Topographic Data: The Restoration Design Tool

The digital surface model (DSM) and derived digital terrain model (DTM) produced from the structure-from-motion photogrammetric processing of the raw imagery are products that have no equivalent in the satellite toolkit. No freely available satellite product can resolve micro-elevation differences in the 5 to 20 centimetre range relevant to mangrove hydrology. LiDAR surveys can produce comparable vertical precision, but LiDAR campaign costs and logistical requirements are prohibitive at the site scale and operational tempo required for this monitoring programme. The drone-derived topographic dataset is therefore a unique and irreplaceable output for restoration planning purposes.

The ecological rationale for topographic precision is straightforward. In mangrove ecosystems, tidal inundation frequency — the primary driver of species zonation, seedling recruitment success, soil redox conditions, and ultimately carbon accumulation rate — is determined by elevation relative to tidal datums. An area lying 15 centimetres above mean high water experiences a fundamentally different inundation regime from an area lying 15 centimetres below it, even though the two locations may be separated horizontally by only a few metres. A restoration planting design based on topographic data at satellite resolution — which offers no useful elevation information at these scales — is equivalent to planting without any consideration of hydrology. Most propagule mortality in mangrove restoration projects is attributable to incorrect hydrological positioning: propagules planted in areas that are either too frequently inundated for their stage of development or too rarely inundated to sustain their physiological requirements.

For the Aborlove Nolopi and Anyanui Combine AoIs specifically, the topographic dataset answers a suite of restoration design questions that cannot be addressed by any other instrument available to the project. Within Anyanui Combine, the ecosystem health baseline has recorded soil pH values in the range of 2.7 at certain measurement stations — an extreme acidity signal that is consistent with oxidation of pyrite-rich sediments exposed by disrupted tidal flow. The causal diagnosis — whether the acidity results from a natural sediment chemistry anomaly, from the blockage of tidal channels by sedimentation or human disturbance, or from historical pond construction — requires an understanding of the micro-topographic drainage network that only the DTM can provide. Where are the natural drainage channels relative to the pH anomaly locations? Is there a topographic barrier impeding tidal flushing that would otherwise dilute and buffer the acidified soil water? Can tidal reconnection be achieved by the low-intervention removal of a sediment berm, or does the elevation mismatch between the drainage channel and the target restoration area require more substantial hydrological engineering? The DTM answers these questions spatially and precisely, enabling the restoration design to be evidence-based rather than speculative.

For Aborlove Nolopi, where the orthomosaic identifies the boundary between recoverable and non-recoverable canopy as the central restoration question, the topographic data provides the hydrological context needed to interpret that boundary. Areas where canopy thinning is associated with low-lying, frequently inundated terrain may be naturally self-recovering once the pressure of direct extraction is reduced; areas where thinning corresponds to elevated, rarely inundated terrain may require active restoration to return to productive mangrove condition.

## 4.6 Primary Output 3 — Raw Multispectral Data: Species Identification and Vegetation Health Indices

The five-band MicaSense sensor calibrated reflectance dataset is the input for the spectral analysis layer that bridges the drone survey to the satellite monitoring system. Three categories of spectral index are calculated from the raw data for the T0 baseline.

The Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), derived from the Red and Near-Infrared bands, is the most direct comparison point between the drone survey data and the EOSDA satellite monitoring product. EOSDA generates NDVI time series for each AoI from Sentinel-2 imagery on its recurring observation cycle. The drone-derived NDVI for each AoI at T0 provides the calibration reference against which the EOSDA NDVI baseline is anchored: systematic differences between the drone NDVI and the satellite NDVI at the same locations quantify the calibration offset that must be accounted for when interpreting satellite-derived change signals against the drone baseline condition.

The Normalised Difference Moisture Index (NDMI), derived from the Near-Infrared and applicable Short-Wave Infrared channels, measures canopy moisture content. In the Keta landscape, NDMI is the primary stress indicator used by the project's GIS team in the `keta_lagoon.tif` classification raster and in the named monitoring polygon datasets for the five boundary areas (Dzita, Salo, Avu, Aborlove, and Anyanui). Declining NDMI values are a physiological precursor to visible die-back — canopy moisture stress emerges before leaf discolouration, canopy thinning, or structural collapse become visible in a true-colour image. The drone-derived NDMI at T0 establishes the healthy-condition reference for each AoI, enabling future revisit surveys and EOSDA alerts to be calibrated against a site-specific stress threshold rather than a generic spectral threshold derived from a different landscape.

The Red Edge Chlorophyll Index, derived from the Red Edge and Near-Infrared bands, is the key input for species-level discrimination. The geo-social mangrove extent baseline report identified both *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* as the dominant species within the Keta Lagoon system, but acknowledged that species-level discrimination from Sentinel-2 imagery at 10 to 20 metre resolution is unreliable across the spatial complexity of the lagoon complex. At 5 to 10 centimetre GSD, the canopy architecture of the two species is individually distinguishable: *Rhizophora* presents a rounded, dense, stilt-rooted canopy structure with characteristic prop-root visible at the canopy edge; *Avicennia* presents a flatter, more open canopy with pneumatophore fields visible in gaps. More fundamentally, the two species have measurably different Red Edge reflectance profiles — *Avicennia germinans* exhibits a higher Red Edge reflectance associated with its succulent, salt-tolerant leaf morphology, while *Rhizophora racemosa* shows a steeper Red Edge slope associated with higher chlorophyll concentration in its thicker leaves. At drone survey resolution, these differences allow the construction of species distribution maps within each AoI that a satellite sensor simply cannot produce, enabling carbon stock estimates to be stratified by species with the greater accuracy that above-ground biomass allometrics require when applied to species with different wood density and growth form.

## 4.7 How the Three Outputs Serve Continuous Monitoring

The three survey outputs described in Sections 4.4 through 4.6 are designed as T0 baselines — the temporal reference point against which all future states of the Keta AoIs are measured. Their monitoring value compounds over time as the system produces updated versions of the same three products at each resurvey, whether that resurvey occurs on the annual scheduled cycle or is triggered by an anomaly alert from the EOSDA satellite monitoring workflow.

The orthomosaic differencing workflow — comparing the T0 orthomosaic against a T1 orthomosaic produced at the next drone resurvey — provides the change detection product at the highest spatial resolution available to the project. Canopy loss events of individual-tree scale, new deforestation clearances, and the progressive closure of canopy in areas subject to active restoration management are all detectable at this resolution. The sensitivity of the comparison depends on the positional accuracy of the GCP network: because the same GCPs are reoccupied at each survey epoch, the two orthomosaics are registered to the same real-world coordinate system, and pixel-to-pixel change comparisons are valid rather than contaminated by registration error.

The topographic time series — the comparison of DTMs produced at successive survey epochs — detects geomorphic changes in the AoIs that cannot be measured by any other instrument. Sedimentation or erosion of creek channels, changes in micro-topographic connectivity associated with restoration earthworks, and subsidence of formerly waterlogged areas following hydrological rehabilitation are all processes that operate at the 5 to 20 centimetre vertical scale that the drone photogrammetry resolves. In a delta system subject to the sediment starvation imposed by the Akosombo Dam, detecting subsidence trends in the mangrove surface elevation is a monitoring requirement that only the repeat topographic survey can fulfil.

The spectral index time series — tracking NDVI, NDMI, and Red Edge Chlorophyll Index at each survey epoch — provides the bridge between the drone survey and the EOSDA satellite monitoring workflow. Between annual drone flights, EOSDA monitors the AoIs using Sentinel-2 imagery at its three-to-five-day revisit interval. When an EOSDA NDMI alert flags an anomalous spectral change within an AoI, the alert triggers a targeted drone revisit — an additional survey of the alerted area to confirm or refute the satellite signal at centimetre resolution before a field response is mobilised. The drone survey is therefore not a standalone annual event but an integral component of a multi-resolution monitoring system in which satellite frequency and drone precision are complementary rather than competing.

Together, these three time-series products make the drone survey a genuine multi-temporal monitoring system, not a one-time baseline photography exercise. The T0 condition documented in this baseline is not merely a snapshot; it is the calibrated reference against which the entire future trajectory of the Keta Lagoon AoIs is measured.

## 4.8 Data Processing Workflow

Raw imagery from the P4M flight missions enters a six-stage processing chain before survey products are ready for delivery to the MRV platform and EOSDA.

Stage one is radiometric calibration. Before each flight mission and after its completion, the MicaSense calibration panel — a reflectance target with known spectral properties — is photographed under the same illumination conditions as the survey. These calibration images, combined with the continuous downwelling light sensor record from the DLS 2, are used to convert the raw digital number values in each band to absolute surface reflectance values. This step is mandatory for scientific comparability between survey epochs: without radiometric calibration anchored to a known reflectance reference, the same mangrove stand will produce different digital number values on a sunny day versus a cloudy day, making multi-temporal spectral change analysis impossible.

Stage two is structure-from-motion photogrammetric processing, carried out in Agisoft Metashape or equivalent photogrammetric software. The processing workflow aligns overlapping images by identifying common feature points across multiple photographs, reconstructs the three-dimensional geometry of the surveyed surface, incorporates the GCP coordinates as geometric constraints, and produces a dense three-dimensional point cloud of the AoI.

Stage three is point cloud processing to generate the DSM and DTM. The DSM captures the surface as seen from above — canopy height, branch structure, and any ground surface visible through gaps. The DTM isolates the ground surface by filtering out the above-ground vegetation returns, using vegetation height classification to distinguish ground-level returns from canopy returns. In a dense mangrove canopy, DTM accuracy depends on the availability of ground-return points through canopy gaps and root spaces; GCP density and distribution are critical to constraining the model where ground points are sparse.

Stage four is orthomosaic generation. The photogrammetric model is used to orthorectify each raw image — correcting for camera tilt, lens distortion, and terrain relief — and the orthorectified images are mosaicked into the seamless, georeferenced orthomosaic product at the survey's achieved GSD.

Stage five is spectral index calculation. NDVI, NDMI, and Red Edge Chlorophyll Index rasters are calculated from the calibrated reflectance bands and clipped to each AoI boundary. These index rasters are the inputs for the comparison with the `keta_lagoon.tif` Sentinel-2 classification raster and for the EOSDA calibration workflow.

Stage six is validation and output preparation. The orthomosaic and spectral index products are overlaid against the `keta_lagoon.tif` master raster and the AoI boundary GeoJSON files to produce the validation comparison described in Section 4.4. The completed product package — orthomosaic, DSM, DTM, and index rasters for each AoI — is then assembled for upload to the MRV platform data repository and for integration into the EOSDA continuous monitoring configuration.
