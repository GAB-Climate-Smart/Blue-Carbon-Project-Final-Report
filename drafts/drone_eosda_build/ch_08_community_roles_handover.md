# Chapter 8: Community Roles, Institutional Handover, and Data Sovereignty

---

## 8.1 Chapter Purpose: Sustainability Beyond the Survey

Drone surveys and EOSDA continuous monitoring are technical investments. They produce orthomosaics, NDMI-derived canopy condition outputs, alert-ready Area of Interest (AoI) polygons, and a real-time platform for detecting forest change. But technology without institutional ownership decays quickly. Platform subscriptions lapse. Trained staff move on. Community monitors who were engaged during the field campaign disengage when no one follows up. And the first signal of canopy loss — the early warning that justified the entire monitoring architecture — goes undetected for eighteen months while a carbon credit buyer believes the forest is intact.

This chapter exists because the technical outputs described in Chapters 4 through 7 are only as durable as the human and institutional systems that receive, use, and sustain them. It documents the handover architecture for the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment monitoring system: who receives what, with what formal authority, and how data sovereignty — the right of communities and their designated government representatives to hold, query, and act on monitoring data — is maintained at the appropriate institutional level. It draws on the governance, risk, and participatory mapping assessments prepared across the project's 37 communities, and should be read alongside the FPIC engagement record in Chapter 3 and the EOSDA platform architecture described in Chapter 6. The central argument of this chapter is simple: monitoring without community and institutional ownership is not monitoring. It is a technical exercise with a shelf life.

## 8.2 The 37-Community Monitoring Network

The Area of Interest boundaries that define the EOSDA monitoring workspaces were not drawn by consultants from satellite imagery alone. They were refined through participatory mapping exercises conducted with communities across the Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site and the Songor Ramsar Site (Ada East District). A total of 37 communities participated in this process, contributing generations of spatial knowledge about mangrove extent, harvesting zones, degraded patches, sacred groves, and waterway access points that remote sensing at any resolution cannot capture. The NDMI-derived AoI boundaries for Dzita, Salo, Avu, and Aborlove are anchored to ground-truth reference data that communities helped generate. This originating contribution is not a formality. It is the basis on which communities have a rightful and legitimate claim to ongoing participation in the monitoring system those boundaries support.

The community layer of the monitoring system operates through **Community Change Agents (CCAs)** — individuals nominated by their peers and trained by the project team in data collection, ecological observation, and community liaison functions. CCAs have already demonstrated effectiveness at scale across the project: mobilising diverse community groups for baseline consultations, providing nuanced translation of technical concepts into Ewe, Dangme, and Fante, and mediating misunderstandings between external teams and residents. Under the monitoring handover architecture, CCAs take on a formal monitoring role at the community level, functioning as the primary human interface between the automated EOSDA alert system and the communities those alerts serve.

At the operational level, each CCA designated as a community monitor is responsible for three core functions. First, they conduct quarterly site observations at designated locations within or adjacent to the AoI, recording visible changes in mangrove canopy condition, evidence of harvesting or encroachment, and any hydrological events (flooding, channel blockage, storm damage) that may affect vegetation health. These observations are recorded using a standardised field data sheet and, where mobile connectivity allows, a simple mobile-reporting application — recognising that digital literacy varies significantly across the 37 communities and that the reporting pathway must be accessible to monitors operating with basic handsets on low-bandwidth networks. Second, CCAs receive automated alert summaries from the EOSDA platform — routed through the District Assembly or CCA WhatsApp communication channel where feasible — and are responsible for conducting a field verification visit within fourteen days of any alert flagging significant canopy change within their designated AoI. Third, CCAs log their observations and verification findings in a community-level monitoring register, which is submitted to the District Assembly and Wildlife Division quarterly alongside the EOSDA platform's automated monitoring outputs.

**Training requirements** before community monitors can participate meaningfully in this system include: orientation to the EOSDA dashboard (what the platform shows, what an alert means, how to access quarterly summaries); standardised field observation and data recording protocols; GPS waypoint recording for field verification visits; and community-level GRM procedures for escalating disputes or concerns arising from monitoring findings. **[PENDING: Delivery of community monitor training programme across all 37 communities — training schedule, facilitator organisation, and completion verification documentation to be confirmed by WACA PIU.]**

## 8.3 CCA as the Institutional Anchor

The governance report prepared under this assessment makes clear that the CREMA — the Community Resource Management Area — is the intended long-term institutional anchor for community-based resource governance at each project site. Under the Wildlife Resources Management Act, 2023 (Act 1115), a gazetted CREMA becomes a legal entity capable of entering contracts, enforcing by-laws, and holding assets on behalf of its member communities. For the monitoring system, the CREMA Management Committee (CRMC) is the institutional body that receives, holds, and acts on AoI boundary data, monitoring outputs, and alert notifications at the community level.

The CRMC composition under the CREMA model includes elected community representatives, a traditional authority liaison (typically a member of the chief's council designated for resource management matters), and government observers from the Wildlife Division at district level. This tripartite structure — community voice, customary legitimacy, and statutory oversight — mirrors the governance architecture required for monitoring handover: no single actor holds data or decision-making authority in isolation.

**The CRMC's specific mandate in relation to drone and EOSDA monitoring outputs is as follows.** The CRMC is the designated community-level custodian of the T0 (time-zero) drone survey outputs for its AoI, including the shapefile boundary, the associated orthomosaic extent, and the initial NDMI-derived canopy condition composite. These materials constitute the baseline against which all future monitoring rounds are compared; their integrity and accessibility at community level is non-negotiable. The CRMC holds the formal right to request a drone re-survey if, in its assessment, the current monitoring data does not adequately capture conditions on the ground — for example, if an alert has been triggered but the EOSDA composite cannot differentiate between storm damage and active illegal clearing. The CRMC is also the recipient of the quarterly EOSDA monitoring summary at community level and is responsible for tabling that summary at the quarterly CREMA general meeting, ensuring that monitoring data reaches all community members rather than being held only by technically literate individuals.

**Quarterly reporting obligation** from the CRMC to the Keta Municipal Assembly or Ada East District Assembly covers monitoring summary findings, any alert verification visits conducted by CCAs, grievances or disputes related to monitoring findings, and a brief statement of whether monitoring activities were completed as planned. This reporting obligation runs upward to MESTI through the District Assembly, forming the community tier of the national annual monitoring report submitted to the World Bank.

A critical governance gap must be acknowledged honestly. The CREMA Legislative Instrument (LI) required to operationalise the provisions of Act 1115 had not been finalised or passed by Parliament as of the date of this report. Until the LI is in force, CREMAs cannot be formally gazetted, and the CRMC cannot operate as a legal entity. **[PENDING: Finalisation and parliamentary passage of the CREMA Legislative Instrument — MLNR and Wildlife Division to confirm timeline and interim governance arrangements for CCA monitoring roles during the pre-LI period.]** In the interim, CCA-led monitoring should be documented under a formal memorandum of understanding between the WACA Project Implementation Unit and community representatives, providing a functional but legally provisional basis for monitoring activities to continue without interruption.

## 8.4 Traditional Authority Integration in Monitoring

The governance assessment confirmed that traditional authorities — chiefs, queen mothers, and councils of elders — are the most immediately legitimised decision-making structures across all 37 project communities. Their endorsement during the FPIC process was a precondition for baseline field access in communities including Dzita, Sota, Pute, and Sankor. That endorsement, once granted, does not automatically extend to all future monitoring activities. It must be actively maintained.

**Traditional authority endorsement of the EOSDA monitoring workspace** is the formal act by which a chief or traditional council acknowledges that satellite-derived monitoring of their customary territory is legitimate, that the data generated belongs to the community, and that alerts generated by the platform will be shared with and acted on by community institutions. This endorsement provides the customary legitimacy for ongoing observation — an important counterpart to the statutory authorisation provided by the CREMA structure and the government agencies described below. Without it, monitoring may be technically operational but socially contested.

The stool land implications of remote sensing monitoring deserve explicit acknowledgement. In Ghanaian customary law, stool land is the collective property of the traditional community, held in trust by the chief. Drone surveys conducted over stool land, and the EOSDA monitoring of vegetation on stool land, generate data about community-owned assets. If EOSDA detects significant canopy loss within an AoI that overlies stool land, **the traditional authority must be notified alongside government agencies before any external enforcement action is taken.** The alert response protocol should therefore route through the following sequence: EOSDA generates alert → EOSDA administrator at MESTI receives notification → CCA conducts field verification → CRMC is formally notified → traditional authority liaison within CRMC informs chief and council → if verified loss confirmed, District Assembly Safeguards Desk is activated. External intervention — enforcement action, re-survey request, escalation to EPA — follows only after the traditional authority has been informed and has had an opportunity to respond through customary channels.

The gender dimension of traditional authority integration requires specific attention. In Keta and parts of the Songor landscape, **queen mothers are recognised as custodians of women's livelihood zones** — the areas where fish processing, fuelwood collection, and other female-dominated economic activities are concentrated. These zones frequently overlap with mangrove-adjacent areas within the AoIs. If an EOSDA alert is triggered for canopy loss in a zone that women identify as a livelihood resource, the monitoring response must be gender-responsive: it must engage women's groups directly, seek their account of what has happened and why, and ensure that any management response does not restrict women's access to resources without providing viable alternatives. Queen mothers are the appropriate customary interlocutor for this dimension of alert response.

The legal question of data sharing also requires clarity. **Drone data collected over stool land is, by the principles of customary tenure, community property.** Sharing those data outputs — orthomosaics, NDMI composites, AoI boundary files — with government agencies constitutes a transfer of community-held information to the state. This transfer should be governed by a formal data sharing agreement between the CRMA (or its CRMC), the traditional authority, and MESTI, specifying what data is shared, for what purposes, and under what conditions access by third parties (including carbon standard verifiers) is permitted. **[PENDING: Negotiation and execution of community-level data sharing agreements covering all active AoIs — legal support from MLNR or project legal counsel required before handover.]**

## 8.5 Women and Youth in Monitoring

The governance assessment documented a finding that demands direct response in this chapter, not a footnote. A female community member in Salo, speaking during a focus group discussion, described the relationship between women and resource governance with precision: "We use the land, but we don't decide anything about it." This sentence captures a structural condition, not an anecdote. Women across the 37 project communities are the primary collectors of mangrove fuelwood, the principal operators of fish smoking kilns, and the backbone of post-harvest processing value chains that make the Keta Lagoon's fisheries economically functional. They are not peripheral users of the resources the monitoring system is designed to protect. They are central to the social-ecological system those resources sustain. And they are, by the governance assessment's own finding, almost entirely absent from the formal decision-making structures that will govern monitoring findings and management responses.

This is a carbon credit integrity issue, not merely a social equity concern. A monitoring system that detects canopy loss and routes the alert response exclusively through male-dominated traditional councils and government agencies is a monitoring system that systematically excludes the people with the most detailed knowledge of how and why that loss is occurring. Women who collect fuelwood daily know where harvesting pressure is intensifying, which stands are being selectively cleared, and which areas are recovering. Their observations are primary evidence. Excluding them from the monitoring network means the system is operating with half its sensor capacity.

**Specific monitoring roles designed for women's groups** within the handover architecture include the following. Women's groups — particularly fish processor associations and VSLAs, which the governance assessment identified as the most cohesive and accountable community institutions across all three Ramsar sites — are the designated community partners for shoreline patrol in coastal-fringe AoI zones, mangrove seedling recruitment observation at designated quarterly observation points, and water and sediment sample collection at stations identified under the Keta and Ada MRV wetland monitoring network. These roles leverage existing patterns of territorial knowledge and daily resource interaction without requiring high levels of digital literacy or technical training beyond basic field observation protocols.

**Youth roles** in the monitoring system are positioned at the digital interface: drone data processing assistance (where equipment permits), EOSDA dashboard review and quarterly summary preparation at CCA level, mobile reporting of field observations, and maintenance of the community monitoring register. Youth groups in communities including Dzogborve and Totope expressed both the digital competence and the willingness to engage in monitoring if offered meaningful and compensated roles — the risk assessment identified "if they don't see us, we will stop seeing the forest" as the precise expression of what disengagement looks like and what it costs.

**The positive obligation on the project is formally documented here.** Women's participation in monitoring activities must be resourced — meaning that women's travel costs, time compensation, and childcare arrangements for monitoring participation are treated as legitimate project costs, not optional goodwill expenditure. It must be tracked — with monitoring participation data disaggregated by gender and reported quarterly to the WACA PIU. And it must be reported to the World Bank — with the annual monitoring report including a specific section on gender-disaggregated participation in monitoring activities, consistent with the ESS10 requirement for ongoing stakeholder engagement in project monitoring. **[PENDING: Development of gender-responsive monitoring participation budget and tracking template — to be integrated into the PIU safeguards monitoring framework.]**

## 8.6 Government Institutional Handover Architecture

The transition from consultancy-led monitoring to permanent government stewardship requires a clear mapping of which institution receives what function, with what authority, and at what point in the handover sequence. The following describes the designated role of each national and district institution in the post-handover monitoring system.

| Institution | Monitoring Role | Authority | Reporting Obligation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MESTI** | Policy oversight of EOSDA outcomes; primary EOSDA account holder; annual monitoring report to World Bank | National policy mandate | Annual report to World Bank; notification to Cabinet if national-level carbon integrity risk |
| **EPA** | Environmental compliance integration; enforcement authority for illegal clearing detected within AoIs | Environmental Protection Agency Act | Enforcement action report to MESTI; inclusion in EPA annual environmental compliance report |
| **Wildlife Division (Forestry Commission)** | Species composition integration from drone survey; consultation when re-survey is triggered; CREMA oversight | Wildlife Resources Management Act 2023 | Quarterly field validation reports to District Assembly and MESTI |
| **Keta Municipal Assembly** | Local government recipient of quarterly monitoring reports; spatial data custodian for Keta AoIs; GRM escalation point | Local Governance Act 2016 | Incorporation of monitoring findings into District MTDP environmental monitoring reports |
| **Ada East District Assembly** | Local government recipient of quarterly monitoring reports; spatial data custodian for Ada/Songor AoIs; GRM escalation point | Local Governance Act 2016 | Incorporation of monitoring findings into District MTDP environmental monitoring reports |

**MESTI** is designated as the primary policy oversight body for EOSDA monitoring outcomes and the institutional recipient of alert notifications at the national level. Annual monitoring reports — synthesising EOSDA outputs, community monitoring observations, and field verification records — are prepared by MESTI and submitted to the World Bank as the primary accountability document for monitoring system performance. MESTI is the appropriate body to hold the EOSDA platform administrator credentials after handover, given its mandate for environmental science and technology coordination and its role as national counterpart to the World Bank for the WACA ResIP 2 programme.

**EPA** is designated as co-holder of EOSDA administrator access — a second institutional account with read and alert-review permissions — and as the enforcement authority if EOSDA detects illegal clearing within AoIs. EPA's enforcement mandate under the Environmental Protection Agency Act provides the legal instrument for action where canopy loss is attributable to unlawful activity: illegal logging, sand mining encroachment, or deliberate land-use conversion within restoration or protection zones. EPA's involvement in monitoring also ensures that blue carbon conservation is integrated into Ghana's broader environmental compliance framework rather than operating as a parallel, project-specific system.

**The Wildlife Division** is the designated technical body for integrating species composition data from the drone survey into national wildlife habitat maps, and is the statutory body consulted when a drone re-survey is triggered by an EOSDA alert. As the lead agency for CREMA establishment, the Wildlife Division provides the connecting tissue between community-level monitoring (through CCAs and CRMCs) and national-level government institutions — a function that requires adequate field staff capacity, which the governance assessment identified as currently insufficient. **[PENDING: Wildlife Division field staff capacity assessment and resource plan for sustained community-facing monitoring support — to be included in WACA PIU capacity-building programme.]**

**District Assemblies at Keta and Ada** are the local government recipients of quarterly monitoring reports and the designated custodians of AoI spatial data at district level for land-use planning integration. Their role as GRM escalation points — the Tier 3 level in the three-tier grievance mechanism — means they are also the bodies responsible for formal adjudication of disputes arising from monitoring findings that cannot be resolved at the CRMC level.

## 8.7 EOSDA Account Handover Protocol

The EOSDA platform is a subscription-based commercial monitoring service. The project has configured AoI boundaries, established alert thresholds, and generated T0 monitoring composites for the named sites. All of this configuration is held within an EOSDA account currently administered by the consultancy team. Before project close, the account — and the institutional knowledge required to operate it — must be formally transferred to Ghana government stewardship. The following table documents the minimum requirements for handover completion.

| Handover Requirement | Responsible Party | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Administrator credentials transferred from consultancy team to designated MESTI official | WACA PIU + MESTI | **[PENDING]** |
| Training completed for named MESTI staff (minimum two staff, platform administration and alert response) | WACA PIU | **[PENDING]** |
| Training completed for named EPA staff (minimum one staff, alert review access) | WACA PIU | **[PENDING]** |
| Training completed for at least one CCA per active AoI (community monitoring dashboard summary) | WACA PIU + Wildlife Division | **[PENDING]** |
| Succession plan documented: if trained staff leave, named successor role and access re-grant procedure confirmed in writing | MESTI + EPA | **[PENDING]** |
| EOSDA subscription budget line identified in MESTI or MLNR post-project operational budget | MESTI/MLNR Finance | **[PENDING]** |
| All AoI boundary shapefiles backed up to MLNR server and confirmed accessible | Consultancy team + MLNR ICT | **[PENDING]** |
| T0 composite exports for all active AoIs backed up to MLNR server | Consultancy team + MLNR ICT | **[PENDING]** |
| Alert records and monitoring log archived and transferred | Consultancy team + MESTI | **[PENDING]** |
| Handover completion certificate signed by MESTI, WACA PIU, and consultancy team | All parties | **[PENDING]** |

The question of who pays for the EOSDA subscription after project close is not a minor administrative detail. It is a structural sustainability question. If no budget line is identified within MESTI or MLNR's recurrent operational expenditure, the EOSDA account will lapse when project funding ends. All configured AoI boundaries, alert thresholds, and monitoring history will be inaccessible. The monitoring system will cease to function at the precise moment — after consultancy engagement ends and before the next project cycle begins — when continuity matters most. **[PENDING: Formal confirmation from MESTI or MLNR of the budget line designated for EOSDA subscription renewal in post-project operational expenditure. This is a project closure prerequisite, not a recommended good practice.]**

The succession plan for trained staff requires the same level of seriousness. Natural staff turnover at government agencies means that any individual trained on the EOSDA platform may leave their post within the project's operational horizon. The succession plan must specify: the role designation (not the named individual) responsible for EOSDA administration, the procedure for granting account access to a new holder of that role, and the minimum retraining requirement before a new staff member can operate the platform independently.

## 8.8 GRM Integration for Monitoring Disputes

The Grievance Redress Mechanism documented in the risk and safeguards assessment was designed with land access, benefit-sharing, and FPIC disputes in mind. Its three tiers — community CCA intake, CRMC formal mediation, and District Assembly adjudication — apply directly and without modification to monitoring disputes, which the project must anticipate as a realistic category of grievance.

The most foreseeable category of monitoring dispute arises when EOSDA alerts trigger enforcement action over a parcel whose tenure or usage rights are contested. If canopy loss is detected within an AoI at Avu or Aborlove, and enforcement action is initiated against a community member who claims legitimate access to that parcel under customary tenure, that community member has a right to contest the monitoring finding and the enforcement response through the GRM. **This is not an administrative inconvenience. It is the mechanism that distinguishes a monitoring system with social legitimacy from one that functions as an instrument of exclusion.**

The GRM escalation pathway for monitoring disputes is: **community level** (CCA receives complaint, logs it, attempts informal resolution by clarifying what the monitoring data shows and its limitations) → **CRMC level** (formal mediation, involving traditional authority liaison, to assess whether the monitoring finding reflects an actual violation or a data artefact, and to propose a resolution acceptable to all parties) → **District Assembly Safeguards Desk** (formal adjudication if CRMC resolution is contested) → **MESTI** (if District Assembly determination is contested or involves a matter of national policy significance) → **independent review** (if the complainant disputes MESTI's determination, and where the dispute has implications for the validity of carbon credit claims, independent third-party review is required).

All GRM complaints related to monitoring activities must be logged in the standardised GRM register, disaggregated by type (monitoring finding dispute, access restriction complaint, enforcement action complaint, benefit-sharing dispute related to monitoring outcomes), and reported to the World Bank in the annual monitoring report. This is an ESS10 requirement and a carbon standard compliance obligation.

**The FPIC re-engagement trigger** applies where monitoring reveals new activity that materially changes the project's scope or impact. If EOSDA alerts consistently identify an area of active land use within an AoI that was not identified as a conflict zone at the time of initial FPIC clearance — for example, a new artisanal salt pan expanding into a protection zone — the project cannot respond with enforcement action alone. FPIC must be re-engaged with the communities affected, the monitoring data must be shared transparently, and any modification to AoI boundaries or management restrictions must be negotiated with community consent rather than imposed unilaterally.

## 8.9 Data Sovereignty and Benefit Sharing

The data generated by drone surveys and EOSDA continuous monitoring is not neutral technical output. It is information about community-managed landscapes, generated on community-held land, by a project funded through international climate finance mechanisms in which communities are the named beneficiaries. Questions of ownership, access, and benefit sharing must be answered explicitly rather than defaulting to the assumption that government or consultancy holding constitutes appropriate stewardship.

The following framework governs data ownership and access for the primary outputs of the monitoring system.

**AoI boundary data** — the shapefile polygons defining the Dzita restoration areas, Salo restoration area, Avu protection area, and Aborlove protection area — are **community property registered with the traditional authority** of the relevant community. MESTI, the Wildlife Division, and the District Assembly hold copies for operational and planning purposes. No third party — including carbon standard verifiers, research institutions, or commercial entities — may access or use AoI boundary data without community consent expressed through the CRMC.

**Drone orthomosaics** — the high-resolution aerial composites generated from the drone survey — are **government assets held by MESTI** as the national body responsible for environmental spatial data under MLNR's mandated functions. Orthomosaics are accessible to communities on request through the CCA or CRMC, and MESTI is obligated to provide accessible summary products (printed maps, simplified visual outputs) when requested. Full-resolution orthomosaic data is not held at community level by default, given data storage and technical capacity constraints, but communities retain the right to receive it.

**EOSDA monitoring outputs** — quarterly alert summaries, canopy change composites, and trend analysis reports — are **shared data**: MESTI holds primary copies, and the CRMC for each AoI receives quarterly summaries in accessible format. Alert notifications are shared simultaneously with MESTI, the District Assembly, and the relevant CRMC, with no institutional hierarchy in access to alert information. Early warning is not a privilege of government; it belongs equally to the communities the alerts are designed to protect.

**Carbon credit revenue sharing** is the downstream dimension of data sovereignty. If the monitoring system supports the generation and sale of verified carbon credits, the communities that participated in the participatory mapping, consented to the monitoring workspaces, and maintain the ongoing legitimacy of the project through their FPIC and CCA engagement are the first beneficiaries of the revenue those credits generate. The principle is not merely one of equity: it is one of logical coherence. Communities who bear the governance burden and accept the restrictions on resource access that conservation monitoring implies must receive a proportionate share of the economic benefits it produces. The precise revenue-sharing formula — what percentage flows to the 37 communities, through what mechanism, managed by whom — is a matter for negotiation under the benefit-sharing plan and the CREMA constitution, not a technical decision to be made unilaterally. **[PENDING: Finalisation of community benefit-sharing formula for carbon credit revenue — requires MLNR national carbon rights framework guidance and participatory negotiation with CRMC representatives across all active AoI communities.]**

## 8.10 Policy Imperative: Monitoring Without Community Is Not Monitoring

A senior technical advisor writing this assessment would be failing in their professional duty if they allowed the following risk to sit quietly in a risk register without naming it plainly. The greatest operational threat to the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment's monitoring system is not a technical failure. It is not a platform outage, a sensor malfunction, or a missed quarterly field round. It is the following scenario: the EOSDA account subscription lapses because no post-project budget line was confirmed. The CCAs who were trained as community monitors receive no follow-up, no stipend, and no feedback on their field verification reports, and they disengage. The first genuine alert — canopy loss in the Salo AoI from accelerating fuelwood harvesting pressure — fires into an account no one is monitoring. The loss continues for eighteen months. A carbon credit buyer in Europe holds credits against a forest that is actively degrading. When the next re-survey reveals the change, the project is in breach of its monitoring obligations and its carbon credit verification collapses.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the documented failure mode of satellite monitoring systems whose institutional handover was not planned as rigorously as their technical design. The World Bank's ESS10 — Environmental and Social Standard 10 on Stakeholder Engagement and Information Disclosure — requires that stakeholder engagement is sustained throughout project implementation, not merely at inception and closure. For a monitoring system, this means that the engagement architecture described in this chapter — CCA roles, CRMC custodianship, traditional authority notification protocols, women's and youth monitoring participation — must be treated as an ongoing programme obligation, not a one-time capacity-building input.

**The project closure report must demonstrate, in verifiable terms, the following.** That all EOSDA accounts are held by named government officials with documented training records and succession plans. That CCA monitors in all active AoI communities have completed training and have submitted at least one quarterly monitoring report. That CRMC representatives in each AoI community have received and formally acknowledged receipt of T0 boundary data and monitoring summary access. That the EOSDA subscription is funded through a confirmed budget line in MESTI or MLNR's post-project operational expenditure. That the community-level data sharing agreements have been signed by traditional authority representatives. And that the GRM is operational — not merely designed — with at least one full quarter of grievance log data available for review.

If these conditions are not met at project close, the monitoring system has not been handed over. It has been abandoned in place. A monitoring system without community engagement is not a monitoring system. It is a platform that generates alerts no one reads, about a forest that communities no longer believe the project cares about, producing data that will not withstand scrutiny when a carbon credit buyer's verifier arrives and asks to see the community monitoring register.

Ghana's communities have participated in this assessment in good faith. They shared their spatial knowledge. They navigated FPIC processes. They nominated CCAs. They allowed drone surveys over their stool lands. The institutional handover described in this chapter is the project's good faith response: ensuring that the monitoring system they helped design continues to serve them, long after the consultancy team has left.

> **Summary for World Bank Social Development Specialists:** This chapter documents the institutional handover architecture for the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment's drone survey and EOSDA continuous monitoring system, serving 37 communities across the Keta Lagoon Complex and Songor Ramsar sites. Monitoring sustainability depends on four interlocked conditions: (1) CCA-led community monitors are formally resourced, trained, and reporting within a CREMA governance structure, with women's groups and youth assigned specific, compensated monitoring roles; (2) EOSDA administrator credentials are transferred to MESTI, with EPA as co-holder, and a confirmed post-project subscription budget in government operational expenditure; (3) AoI boundary data and T0 monitoring composites are registered with traditional authorities as community property and backed up to MLNR servers; and (4) the three-tier GRM is operational for monitoring disputes, with FPIC re-engagement triggered if monitoring reveals new scope-altering activities. Critical pending items — CREMA LI finalisation, community data-sharing agreements, benefit-sharing formula for carbon revenue, and EOSDA subscription budget confirmation — are explicitly flagged for resolution before project close. The core risk is that community disengagement from monitoring functions creates an eighteen-month detection gap for canopy loss — a carbon credit integrity failure that no amount of platform sophistication can prevent if the human architecture that sustains the system is allowed to atrophy.
