## 7.1 Purpose: Why Integration Matters

Chapters 4 through 6 of this report have documented each of the three principal data-collection workstreams of the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment individually: the drone survey methodology and its four-AoI spatial architecture, the AoI-level survey results and their management implications, and the EOSDA Crop Monitoring platform configured for continuous Sentinel-2 observation. Each workstream, read in isolation, is informative. None of them, taken alone, is sufficient for the management decisions that MLNR, MESTI, the Wildlife Division, and the World Bank supervision mission require.

The satellite baseline from the 2024 geo-social assessment — 20,717.95 hectares of mangrove classified at 10-to-20-metre resolution across the Keta Lagoon Complex, using a Random Forest algorithm trained on 319 reference data points — is the most comprehensive mapping of Ghana's blue carbon resource ever produced. But it cannot resolve the canopy structure within a 10-metre pixel. It cannot distinguish *Rhizophora racemosa* from *Avicennia germinans* at stand scale. It cannot detect the selective extraction of edge trees before cumulative thinning crosses the deforestation threshold. And it records a single composite moment in 2024, with no mechanism for alert-level response to change.

The drone survey corrects all of these limitations within the 1,467-hectare monitoring footprint — but it too is a snapshot. A drone survey that reveals acid sulphate soil conditions, hydrological disruption, and sub-pixel canopy fragmentation at Anyanui Combine cannot watch what happens next. It establishes where things stand; it cannot monitor how they evolve.

The EOSDA continuous monitoring system provides the temporal persistence that the drone lacks. It generates Sentinel-2 NDVI and NDMI values every three to five days across all four Areas of Interest, alerts managers when index values deviate more than 15 percent from the T0 baseline, and transforms what would otherwise be a periodic assessment programme into a real-time early-warning system. But EOSDA operates at 10-metre satellite resolution, calibrated only to the extent that the drone T0 baseline provides AoI-specific spectral reference values. Without the drone, EOSDA alerts would be triggered against generic thresholds incapable of distinguishing the Salo-Agortoe corridor's dense-canopy spectral signature from Anyanui Combine's already-stressed phenology. Without the drone, a 15 percent NDMI decline at Agatsivi-Agortoe would trigger the same response level as a 15 percent decline at Salo-Agortoe — when in fact the management implications are entirely different.

The water quality and soil chemistry data from the December 2025 ecosystem health assessment provides the third data stream: 270 water samples and 140 soil samples across 57 Keta stations and 117 water samples and 28 soil samples across 23 Ada stations, measuring the physico-chemical conditions that determine whether observed satellite signals mean what they appear to mean. A declining NDMI signal in the Anyanui sub-zone could reflect post-Harmattan seasonal desiccation or could reflect genuine hydrological deterioration — and the distinction matters enormously for management response. Only the field-measured water chemistry data, read alongside the topographic evidence from the drone DSM, can make that diagnostic call with confidence.

This chapter bridges all three data streams into a single, coherent evidence picture. Its argument is not merely that integration is methodologically desirable. Its argument is that integration is the point where diagnostic insight emerges — where the measurement of a single variable at a single location becomes interpretable as a landscape-level condition with a management implication. No single data stream tells you what is happening to Ghana's most important blue carbon ecosystem. Together, they do.

## 7.2 The Three-Layer Evidence Framework

The integrated monitoring architecture for the Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment operates across three distinct but interdependent layers of evidence, each with a defined spatial resolution, temporal cadence, and decision-support function. Understanding how these layers interact is essential for interpreting the integrated findings presented in the sections that follow.

**Layer 1 — GIS Sentinel-2 Baseline (Geo-Social Report, August 2025)** establishes the national-scale spatial foundation. The 2024 geospatial assessment classified Ghana's entire 1,068,361-hectare coastal landscape at 10-to-20-metre resolution using Harmonised Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery processed on Google Earth Engine with a Random Forest classifier incorporating spectral indices (NDVI, EVI, NDWI, and others) and validated against 319 ground-truth reference points. The primary finding — 20,717.95 hectares of mangrove within the Keta Lagoon Complex, constituting 69 percent of Ghana's national coastal mangrove resource — is the number that anchors all subsequent carbon accounting. It is the denominator for national carbon stock estimates, the spatial extent figure that will appear in Ghana's Nationally Determined Contribution reporting, and the baseline against which the long-term trajectory of the Keta ecosystem will be judged. Layer 1 also provides the classification framework within which the four AoIs are situated: the drone survey does not invent new spatial units but refines and validates the satellite classification within a defined subset of the Keta landscape. For the 20,717.95 hectares that lie outside the 1,467-hectare AoI footprint, Layer 1 remains the only spatial evidence available, and its ongoing validation through drone and ecosystem health data strengthens confidence in its national-scale findings.

**Layer 2 — Drone High-Resolution T0 Survey** provides the sub-pixel spatial resolution and structural detail that the satellite classification cannot. Across the 1,467-hectare AoI footprint — Aborlove Nolopi (52 Ha), Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha), Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha), and Anyanui Combine (128 Ha) — the DJI Phantom 4 Multispectral survey at 100 to 120 metres AGL with 80 percent forward and 75 percent side overlap produces orthomosaics at 5-to-10-centimetre GSD. The five-band MicaSense payload — Blue (475 nm), Green (560 nm), Red (668 nm), Red Edge (717 nm), and Near-Infrared (840 nm) — provides the spectral data for deriving NDVI, NDMI, and the Red Edge Chlorophyll Index at centimetre resolution: the last of these being the instrument for discriminating *Rhizophora racemosa* from *Avicennia germinans* at stand scale, which the satellite cannot resolve. The DSM and derived DTM provide micro-topographic data at comparable planimetric resolution and 3-to-5-centimetre vertical accuracy over flat terrain — the hydrological evidence base that explains why certain areas produce the water and soil chemistry signatures they do. Layer 2 validates, supersedes, and corrects Layer 1 within the AoI footprint; wherever the drone orthomosaic and the satellite classification disagree, the drone product is the authoritative record.

**Layer 3 — EOSDA Continuous Monitoring** extends Layer 2 into the future using the satellite infrastructure of Layer 1. Once the four AoI boundaries are ingested into the EOSDA Crop Monitoring platform and the T0 drone-calibrated baselines are established, the system generates Sentinel-2 NDVI and NDMI time-series data every three to five days, cloud-filtered and quality-checked, triggering alerts when either index deviates more than 15 percent from the T0 reference for two consecutive acquisitions. Layer 3 does not supersede Layer 1 or Layer 2; it uses Layer 1's satellite infrastructure and Layer 2's site-specific calibration to create a monitoring capability that neither could provide independently. The quarterly review cycle — at which trend analysis distinguishes seasonal phenological variation from genuine ecological change — feeds the institutional reporting chain from Community Change Agent to Wildlife Division to MLNR and MESTI, maintaining the unbroken evidence record that carbon credit integrity requires over the 30-year project crediting period.

The table below summarises the interaction between the three layers:

| Dimension | Layer 1: GIS Sentinel-2 Baseline | Layer 2: Drone T0 Survey | Layer 3: EOSDA Monitoring |
|---|---|---|---|

## 7.3 Drone-to-Satellite Validation: What the Cross-Check Reveals

The operational relationship between the drone orthomosaic and the Sentinel-2 Random Forest classification is one of mutual calibration. The satellite classification provides the landscape-scale spatial context within which the drone survey is designed; the drone orthomosaic provides the sub-pixel ground truth that the satellite classification requires for validation and, where necessary, correction. Within the 1,467-hectare AoI footprint, this cross-check is the most important quality-assurance step in the entire carbon accounting chain.

The logic of what the cross-check should reveal follows directly from the known characteristics of both data products and the ecological conditions documented in the December 2025 ecosystem health assessment. In areas of dense, structurally intact mangrove canopy — the Salo-Galotse corridor and the interior of Agatsivi-Agortoe — the Random Forest classification is expected to perform well. These are the conditions for which the classifier was trained: large, continuous canopy blocks with high NDVI that spectrally dominate the 10-to-20-metre pixel, producing clear separation between mangrove and non-mangrove land cover classes. In the December 2025 ecosystem health data, the soil humus values of 40.6 percent at Galotse and 37.5 percent at Agortoe, combined with salinity values of 10.6 to 40 ppt and optimal soil pH of 5.55 to 7.0, define a functioning mangrove ecosystem whose spectral signature the satellite should classify correctly. Where the drone orthomosaic confirms dense, closed-canopy conditions at centimetre resolution within areas the satellite classifies as mangrove, the classification is validated — and the carbon stock estimates derived from that area can be applied with higher confidence.

In the transitional and stressed zones — Aborlove Nolopi and, particularly, Anyanui Combine — the expected pattern is more complex. At Anyanui Combine, where the ecosystem health assessment recorded the most acute ecological stress signal in the dataset (pH 2.7 at the Anyanui-Tunu road station, indicative of active acid sulphate conditions), the canopy structure within a 10-to-20-metre satellite pixel is likely to be fragmented or gapped in ways that a mixed-pixel spectral average would not fully capture. A pixel averaging 40 percent closed canopy and 60 percent partially thinned or gapped area may still classify as mangrove under the Random Forest threshold, while the drone orthomosaic at 5-to-10-centimetre GSD resolves each gap individually and produces a canopy cover estimate that may differ materially from the satellite-derived figure. The expected direction of this discrepancy is a satellite overestimate of mangrove cover in transitional zones — commission error, where the satellite classifies as mangrove areas that the drone reveals as gap-dominated or degraded — rather than an underestimate, because the Random Forest classifier was trained primarily on reference points from intact canopy locations.

The practical carbon accounting implication of this cross-check is direct and quantifiable in principle, though it requires the orthomosaic deliverables to compute precisely. If the drone survey reveals that within the 1,467-hectare AoI footprint, the Sentinel-2 classification overstates mangrove cover by, for example, 8 percent in the Aborlove and Anyanui zones due to mixed-pixel commission errors — a plausible outcome given the known stress conditions at those sites — then the carbon stock estimate attributable to those AoIs must be revised downward accordingly. Conversely, if the drone reveals omission errors in dense mangrove-fringed creek systems that the satellite's 10-metre pixel averaged out — small but ecologically significant patches of high-carbon-density canopy that the classifier missed — the correction could be upward in some sub-zones. The net effect on the national carbon stock estimate for the Keta Lagoon Complex will be modest, because the 1,467-hectare AoI footprint represents approximately 7 percent of the 20,717.95-hectare total; but the effect on the credibility of the accounting chain is substantial. A carbon project whose boundary-area estimate has been validated by centimetre-resolution direct observation within its primary monitoring zones is a project that can defend its baseline against verifier scrutiny in a way that satellite-only accounting cannot.

The minimum mapping unit question is also resolved by the cross-check. For the Layer 1 satellite classification, the effective minimum mapping unit at 10-to-20-metre resolution is approximately 0.1 to 0.4 hectares — any mangrove patch smaller than this is likely to appear as a mixed pixel and be misclassified. At 5-to-10-centimetre GSD, the drone can detect and map individual *Avicennia germinans* clusters of fewer than 100 square metres — patches that are real, ecologically functional components of the blue carbon stock but invisible to the satellite. In the transitional zones of Aborlove Nolopi and in the creek margins of Anyanui Combine, these small patches are precisely where early-stage natural regeneration is most likely to be occurring, and their detection matters for both the carbon inventory and the species composition assessment.

## 7.4 Spatial-Ecological Cross-Reading: Drone Topography and Ecosystem Health

One of the most analytically powerful integrations available to this assessment is the cross-reading of the drone DSM/DTM data against the ecosystem health field measurements. Field sampling, by definition, produces point measurements — a pH, a dissolved oxygen value, a soil humus percentage, at a specific GPS coordinate on a specific date. These point measurements are ecologically informative in isolation, but their true interpretive power emerges only when they can be placed within a spatial context that explains why that location produces those values. The drone Digital Terrain Model provides that spatial context.

The most compelling example in the dataset is the Anyanui-Tunu road station. The December 2025 ecosystem health assessment recorded a water pH of 2.7 at this station — an extreme value consistent with active acid sulphate soil conditions in which pyritic sediments, normally stable under anaerobic waterlogged conditions, have been exposed to oxygen through drainage disruption or tidal disconnection, producing sulphuric acid as an oxidation byproduct. This pH reading is not an isolated instrument anomaly; it is a coherent ecological signal with a probable hydrological cause. The Anyanui-Tunu road, cutting across a tidal floodplain, is the most likely candidate for that cause: roads built perpendicular to tidal drainage patterns routinely create hydrological barriers that interrupt the flushing of otherwise anaerobic soils. The drone DTM for Anyanui Combine — when delivered — will resolve this hypothesis directly. Cross-section profiles across the road alignment will show whether the road surface stands above the surrounding terrain at a height sufficient to prevent tidal water from reaching the mangrove stands on its landward side, and whether drainage channels on both sides of the road are topographically connected or separated by the road embankment. **If the DTM confirms a hydrological barrier consistent with the pH 2.7 reading, the intervention pathway is technically clear: culvert installation or berm removal to restore tidal flushing. If the DTM reveals a more complex topographic situation — for example, widespread subsidence on the landward side combined with elevated sediment deposits on the seaward side — then the restoration calculus changes substantially, because simple culverting may not restore adequate tidal exchange to flush the acid conditions.**

The spot height dataset (14,526 georeferenced points) provides the source data; cross-section profile generation is a next-step GIS task for the consultancy team or MLNR GIS unit. The elevation range (0.04–2.67 m ASL) confirms that the AoI terrain is predominantly within the macro-tidal inundation envelope of the Keta Lagoon system, consistent with the hydrological disruption hypothesis for the acid sulphate conditions at the Anyanui-Tunu road station.

The restoration feasibility conclusion — whether culvert installation or berm removal can restore adequate tidal exchange — depends on the road cross-section analysis confirming the road embankment height relative to the mean high-water mark. This analysis should be completed by the WACA PIU GIS team prior to the next World Bank supervision mission as a prerequisite for the Anyanui hydrological restoration design.

The Ada ecosystem data provides a complementary topographic-ecological integration opportunity, even where the drone survey footprint does not extend to Songor. The December 2025 assessment recorded extraordinarily high Oxidation-Reduction Potential values in the interior lagoon and creek stations at Songor: 832 to 1,236 mV at Togbloku and Wasakuse, and a maximum of 1,547 mV in some interior measurements, compared to 11 to 76.6 mV at the Volta estuary stations at Azizanya and Ayigbo. ORP values at this level indicate strongly oxidising conditions — water and sediment with high oxygen availability and, significantly, a tendency toward strongly oxidising chemistry even in what should be a waterlogged wetland environment. This pattern is consistent with a hydrologically isolated or poorly flushed interior lagoon where wind-driven oxidation exceeds the capacity of anaerobic decomposition to consume oxygen, or — more concerning from a blue carbon perspective — where drainage exposure has created aerobic conditions in soils that should be permanently waterlogged. The Songor Ramsar Site's known fragmented hydrology, with a lagoon largely closed off from the sea, makes isolated hydrological units consistent with stagnant, seasonally oxidising conditions highly plausible. A future drone survey of Ada — not yet within the commissioned scope but a logical next step — would reveal whether the high-ORP stations correspond to topographically enclosed sub-basins with restricted water exchange, or whether the pattern reflects broader hydrological changes associated with the site's documented salt pan expansion.

Within the Keta Lagoon Complex, the soil nutrient data from the December 2025 assessment also acquires spatial interpretability through topographic analysis. Soil nitrate at 984 mg/kg at Wasakuse-ngwa in the Songor system — and phosphate at 705 mg/kg combined with potassium at 1,280 mg/kg at Obane — are values that far exceed background mangrove soil chemistry and indicate concentrated livestock and human waste input. The Obane creek station, where these exceptional values were recorded, likely occupies a topographically convergent position — a low point in the landscape toward which surface runoff and nutrient-laden water drains from surrounding cattle grazing areas. A DTM of the Obane sub-system would confirm whether the station position is hydrologically consistent with accumulation drainage dynamics. Within the Keta AoI footprint, the lower-lying creek corridors identified by the DSM in the Salo-Agortoe Stretch — where tidal flushing reaches furthest inland — are likely to show the highest organic matter accumulation in soils, consistent with the 40.6 percent humus at Galotse and 37.5 percent at Agortoe documented in the ecosystem health baseline. The topographic position of these high-humus stations — confirmed by DTM — is the spatial evidence that explains why they accumulate organic carbon at the rates they do, and why they represent the most valuable components of the Keta blue carbon stock.

## 7.5 NDVI/NDMI Trends and Ecosystem Health Signal

The EOSDA monitoring system does not generate abstract numbers. Every NDVI and NDMI value the platform calculates for a Keta AoI is a proxy for a specific set of ecological conditions that the field-measured ecosystem health data helps to interpret. Understanding this relationship — between spectral index trajectory and ecosystem process — is what transforms EOSDA from a dashboard into a diagnostic instrument.

The most instructive starting point is the Harmattan seasonal cycle that dominates the spectral behaviour of the Keta Lagoon system between October and March. During this period, the dry northeast trade wind reduces rainfall, increases evaporation, concentrates lagoon salinity, and imposes increasing physiological stress on mangrove canopy through both water deficit and elevated salt burden. NDVI and NDMI values at all four AoIs are characteristically depressed during Harmattan relative to the wet season peak, with the largest reductions expected at Anyanui Combine — where pre-existing hydrological stress from the road barrier amplifies the seasonal moisture deficit — and the smallest reductions at Salo-Agortoe Stretch, where dense *Rhizophora racemosa* canopy and optimal soil conditions buffer the system against seasonal stress. The T0 baseline established by the drone survey in February and March 2026 falls within the Harmattan period, meaning it captures NDVI and NDMI values at or near the annual minimum for the site. The EOSDA monitoring system must therefore be interpreted with explicit awareness that wet-season values will systematically exceed the T0 reference — and that this seasonal increase should not trigger alerts, which the platform's seasonal adjustment logic must accommodate.

Within this seasonal framework, distinguishing genuine ecological deterioration from phenological variation is the central challenge of the EOSDA monitoring programme. The 15 percent alert threshold was calibrated to sit above the range of normal intra-seasonal variation while remaining sensitive enough to detect the onset of genuine change. For the Salo-Agortoe Stretch, where T0 NDVI values consistent with dense healthy canopy are expected in the range of 0.55 to 0.80 (based on the spectral characteristics of dense *Rhizophora racemosa* documented in the geo-social baseline), a 15 percent decline would reduce the alert trigger to approximately 0.47 to 0.68 — values below the normal Harmattan minimum for this species in this ecotype, and therefore unlikely to be triggered by seasonal phenology alone. For Anyanui Combine, where the existing hydrological stress means that T0 NDVI and NDMI values will already be suppressed relative to Salo-Agortoe, the 15 percent threshold operates against a lower baseline — providing a more sensitive early-warning configuration precisely where the ecological risk is most acute.

NDMI deserves particular attention as an early-warning instrument at Keta because it detects canopy moisture stress before NDVI detects canopy greenness loss. The physiological sequence of mangrove response to hydrological deterioration is: first, reduction in leaf water content (detectable as NDMI decline); second, chlorophyll degradation and reduced photosynthesis (detectable as NDVI decline); third, visible leaf necrosis and die-back (detectable in optical imagery); fourth, structural canopy loss (detectable in LiDAR or high-resolution photography). EOSDA's dual-index monitoring therefore provides a lead-time advantage: NDMI typically lags behind the hydrological stressor by 4 to 8 weeks — long enough for the stress to manifest spectrally, but within the window when intervention can still prevent irreversible die-back. **This lead-time advantage is most operationally significant at Anyanui Combine, where the acid sulphate conditions recorded at the road station indicate that hydrological deterioration is already operating, and where further disruption could tip the AoI from stressed-but-recoverable to permanently degraded within a single Harmattan season.**

The spatial targeting implication of EOSDA alerts is also worth stating explicitly. When the platform triggers a 15 percent NDMI alert for Agatsivi-Agortoe — a 762-hectare zone where field resources cannot be deployed uniformly — the alert does not instruct the Wildlife Division to visit the entire AoI. The platform's dashboard displays the spatial distribution of the alert signal within the AoI boundary: which sub-area has crossed the threshold, whether the deviation is concentrated at a particular edge or interior location, and whether the pattern is spatially coherent (consistent with a discrete disturbance event) or diffuse (consistent with landscape-scale stress). This spatial specificity is what makes the monitoring system operationally actionable. A Wildlife Division officer receiving an EOSDA alert for Agatsivi-Agortoe knows to direct the Community Change Agent field team to the alerted sub-area — which is likely to be at the settlement-proximate margins where fuelwood extraction pressure is most intense — rather than deploying across the full 762 hectares. Without the T0 drone calibration establishing the sub-area spectral reference, EOSDA cannot generate this spatial targeting; it would generate an area-wide alert against a generic threshold. The integration of Layer 2 and Layer 3 is what converts an alert into an address.

## 7.6 Species Composition Findings and Management Implications

The five-band MicaSense multispectral payload on the DJI Phantom 4 Multispectral makes it possible to discriminate between the two dominant mangrove species at the Keta Lagoon Complex — *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans* (reported variously in the source literature as *Avicennia africana*, following West African nomenclature) — on the basis of their distinct spectral signatures in the Red Edge (717 nm) and Near-Infrared (840 nm) bands. This discrimination is not a marginal analytical refinement; it is a prerequisite for defensible carbon stock estimation, because *Rhizophora racemosa* has substantially higher wood density, greater above-ground biomass per unit canopy area, and different allometric relationships from *Avicennia germinans*, meaning that species composition directly determines the carbon density figure applied to any given AoI area.

The ecological and chemical evidence from the December 2025 assessment provides the ground-truth expectation against which the drone's species-level spectral mapping should be validated. *Rhizophora racemosa* dominance is expected where soil conditions are consistently waterlogged, salinity is within the 10.6-to-40-ppt range documented at Salo and Galotse, and soil pH remains above 5.5 — conditions confirmed by the Salo-Galotse-Agortoe ecosystem health data and consistent with the high humus content at those stations. *Avicennia germinans* dominance or increasing relative abundance is expected in transitional zones — at the margins of intact stands, in areas subject to hydrological disruption, and in the higher-salinity or periodically desiccating sub-zones — because *Avicennia* tolerates a wider range of salinity and aeration conditions than *Rhizophora*. The Red Edge Chlorophyll Index distribution across the four AoIs should therefore trace the ecological stress gradient: highest *Rhizophora* dominance (and highest carbon density) in the Salo-Agortoe interior, declining toward the Aborlove margins and the Anyanui Combine stress zone, where *Avicennia* is expected to form a greater proportion of the remaining canopy.

The mixed-species zones are particularly significant for two reasons beyond carbon accounting. First, they carry eligibility implications for biodiversity co-benefits under certain voluntary carbon methodologies: Verra's VM0033 and associated biodiversity modules recognise mixed-species mangrove stands as supporting higher biodiversity value than monospecific stands, and their spatial extent is a factor in co-benefit credit eligibility. A zone that is currently a 60/40 *Rhizophora/Avicennia* mix shifting toward a 30/70 distribution over two successive drone surveys would be among the earliest structural signals of changing ecological conditions at that location, predating any decline in the total canopy cover that EOSDA would detect. Repeat drone surveys at five-year intervals, mapped against the T0 species composition baseline, provide this detection capability at a level of ecological specificity that satellites cannot approach.

Any signals of invasive or non-native vegetation in the orthomosaic warrant specific attention. The prop-root and pneumatophore structures of the two native mangrove species are visually distinctive in high-resolution aerial imagery; any canopy element with different branching architecture, leaf morphology, or spectral signature should be flagged for ground-truthing. Invasive vegetation would also present distinctively in the Red Edge and NIR bands relative to the native species calibration.

From a replanting management perspective, the species map has direct operational utility. Restoration sites designated within the Salo Restoration Area and Dzita Restoration Areas NDMI polygons should receive propagule species matched to the ecological conditions of the target site. Where the drone DSM confirms adequate tidal inundation depth and the ecosystem health data confirms salinity within the *Rhizophora* tolerance range, *R. racemosa* propagules are the appropriate choice, providing the higher carbon density and the structural complexity of prop-root systems that enhance habitat function. Where the topographic or salinity evidence indicates more stressed conditions — particularly in the Aborlove Nolopi margins and any Anyanui sub-areas where hydrological restoration proves feasible — *Avicennia germinans* propagules are more likely to establish successfully in the first planting cycle, with the expectation that *Rhizophora* re-colonisation may follow as conditions stabilise.

## 7.7 Change Detection Readiness: T0 to T+12 Months Projection

Twelve months after the drone survey T0 baseline has been established and the EOSDA monitoring workspace has been configured and operational, what will the integrated system be able to detect — and with what degree of confidence? This forward-looking assessment is important not only for planning purposes but for setting realistic expectations with the World Bank supervision mission about what the monitoring system will be reporting by late 2026 or early 2027.

The following table summarises the change-detection capability of the integrated system at the T+12-month mark, assessed by change type and confidence level:

| Change Type | Detection Method | Detectable Threshold | Confidence Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Canopy clearance (large-scale) | EOSDA NDVI alert | ≥ 0.5 Ha (5 x 5 satellite pixels) | High | Above satellite minimum mapping unit; reliable within 3–5 day revisit cadence |
| Canopy thinning (sub-pixel) | EOSDA NDVI trend + drone resurvey | Depends on spatial concentration; diffuse thinning detectable at ~10–15% canopy reduction per AoI | Medium | Drone resurvey required to confirm sub-pixel thinning detected as NDVI decline |
| Hydrological stress onset | EOSDA NDMI alert | NDMI 15% decline from T0; 4–8 week lead ahead of NDVI | High | Strongest at Anyanui Combine where stress gradient already established |
| New canopy recruitment (restoration) | EOSDA NDVI increase | ≥ 0.5 Ha contiguous recruitment patch; NDVI increase above seasonal norm | Medium | Low-NDVI T0 restoration zones (Salo, Dzita) will show detectable increase if > 1 year establishment |
| Species composition change | Drone resurvey only | Red Edge Chlorophyll Index shift > 15% relative to T0 species map | High (at resurvey) | Not detectable by EOSDA between resurveys |
| Road/canal hydrological barrier | Drone DTM change detection | ≥ 10 cm vertical change at surveyed sections | High (at resurvey) | Requires repeat topographic survey; not detectable by EOSDA |
| Tidal flat expansion/contraction | EOSDA NIR + NDWI | Waterline shift ≥ 30 m (3 satellite pixels) | Medium | Dependent on cloud-free acquisition timing relative to tidal phase |
| Sediment accumulation/erosion | Drone DTM repeat | ≥ 5 cm net vertical change | High (at resurvey) | Baseline established at T0; critical for long-term carbon stock stability assessment |
| Upstream water diversion impact | EOSDA NDMI multi-year trend | Progressive NDMI decline across wet seasons, year on year | Medium (1 year) / High (3 years) | Three-year NDMI record needed to separate climate variability from structural trend |
| Pollution/waste discharge | Field verification + water chemistry | Acute depression of DO, pH, or ORP at monitoring stations | High (field) | EOSDA cannot directly detect water chemistry; field station data remains primary instrument |

The most important caveat in interpreting this table is the distinction between what the monitoring system can *assert* and what it requires drone re-survey to *validate*. EOSDA can generate an alert indicating that NDMI within a sub-area of Agatsivi-Agortoe has declined 15 percent below T0, and this alert is a reliable signal that something has changed spectrally in that location. But EOSDA cannot confirm whether the change reflects actual canopy loss, temporary stress from a discrete event such as a tidal exclusion or pollution discharge, or the leading edge of a progressive structural decline. Drone re-survey of the alerted sub-area is the instrument that makes that distinction — and the integration of EOSDA alert targeting with directed drone re-survey is the operational architecture that transforms an alert from information into a confirmed management fact.

By T+12 months, the monitoring system should also have generated its first complete seasonal cycle of NDVI and NDMI data across all four AoIs, establishing the site-specific phenological range — the expected minimum (Harmattan trough) and maximum (wet-season peak) values for each AoI — that will inform alert threshold refinement for Year 2 and beyond. This first-cycle phenological record is the single most valuable product the EOSDA system generates in its first year of operation, because it is the evidence base for distinguishing seasonal from structural change that the T0 baseline alone, captured in a single Harmattan month, cannot provide.

## 7.8 Integrated Finding: The Management Decision Map

Drawing on the full weight of evidence from all three data streams — the 2024 Sentinel-2 classification, the December 2025 ecosystem health field data, and the drone survey boundary and methodology evidence — it is possible to synthesise the management priority picture for the four AoIs into a spatially explicit decision framework. This synthesis is not a prediction; it is the best available evidence-based assessment of the management situation as of March 2026, subject to revision when the drone orthomosaic and topographic deliverables are confirmed.

**Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha) represents both the highest conservation priority and the most complex monitoring challenge.** It contains the largest concentration of intact mangrove canopy in the 1,467-hectare AoI footprint, consistent with the satellite classification's identification of this corridor as a primary *Rhizophora racemosa* stand zone. It also faces the most intense documented fuelwood extraction pressure of any AoI: the fish-smoking communities of Agbatsivi, Galotse, and surrounding settlements draw their primary fuel supply from the mangrove stands in this sub-zone, and the geo-social baseline identifies settlement-proximate edges as showing signs of fragmentation. The management implication is not passive monitoring but active governance: the CREMA and community agreement structures established through the FPIC engagement process must translate into enforceable and enforced protection at the stand margins of Agatsivi-Agortoe, supported by EOSDA alerts configured at the highest spatial sensitivity available for this AoI. Every hectare of canopy loss at Agatsivi-Agortoe is a loss of the largest component of the project's blue carbon stock.

**Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha) is the scientific calibration anchor and must be protected unconditionally.** The ecosystem health data confirms this zone as the most ecologically intact in the entire Keta monitoring network: soil humus at 40.6 percent at Galotse, 37.5 percent at Agortoe; salinity within the optimal *Rhizophora* growth range throughout; and the project's first Permanent Sample Plot established within its boundaries. The EOSDA NDMI calibration reference for the entire four-AoI system is anchored to this zone's T0 spectral baseline. Any degradation at Salo-Agortoe does not merely represent carbon stock loss at one location; it degrades the monitoring system's calibration reference for the entire programme. This is the zone where monitoring continuity — the unbroken quarterly review cycle, the Community Change Agent presence, the Wildlife Division field access — must be sustained with the most rigorous institutional attention over the 30-year crediting period.

**Anyanui Combine (128 Ha) requires immediate topographic diagnosis before any restoration commitment is made.** The pH 2.7 reading at the Anyanui-Tunu road station is the single most acute ecological stress signal in the dataset, and the management response to it hinges entirely on the DTM evidence. If the topographic data confirms that the road embankment creates a hydrological barrier amenable to culvert installation — a relatively low-cost intervention — then Anyanui Combine moves from a conservation problem to a restoration opportunity with a defined engineering pathway. If the DTM reveals that the acid sulphate soil conditions have spread across a large area and that topographic subsidence has compounded the drainage disruption beyond simple reconnection, the management strategy pivots to protection of remaining intact canopy and long-term monitoring of natural recovery, without active restoration investment in the near term. **This is the binary decision that the DTM makes evidence-based, and it cannot responsibly be made without that data.**

**Aborlove Nolopi (52 Ha) is the smallest AoI and the one most clearly positioned for protection-rather-than-restoration management.** Its designation as a protection area — confirmed by the NDMI boundary archived in February 2026 — reflects the assessment that the primary management need is preventing incremental canopy extraction, not replanting. The orthomosaic at T0 will establish the precise canopy cover and gap structure at the time of protection designation; subsequent EOSDA monitoring will detect any canopy decline that indicates the protection arrangements are failing. The community tenure situation at Aborlove — the specific land-use agreements and CREMA boundaries governing access to this zone — is the most important non-spatial determinant of monitoring continuity here, because a 52-hectare protection area is only as secure as the community governance that enforces it.

**Dzita and Avu** require mention in the integrated picture even where they fall outside the four primary AoIs. The Dzita Restoration Areas NDMI boundary — archived in March 2026 — and the Avu Protection Area NDMI boundary — archived in February 2026 — indicate that the spatial monitoring architecture extends beyond the four core AoIs to encompass additional designated zones within the Keta Lagoon. These boundaries confirm that the ecosystem health monitoring station at Dzita — which recorded the highest conductivity (63.1 μS) and near-peak salinity in the Keta dataset, indicating hyper-saline conditions — is within a spatially managed restoration zone. The hydrological conditions at Dzita, while challenging for spontaneous *Rhizophora* establishment, may support *Avicennia* restoration if tidal connectivity is managed appropriately.

The minimum monitoring action necessary to maintain carbon credit eligibility over the 30-year crediting period is a combination of three non-negotiable elements: uninterrupted EOSDA satellite monitoring of all four AoIs with quarterly human review; five-yearly drone resurveys to validate satellite-detected changes, update the species composition map, and recalibrate the T0 spectral baseline; and continuation of the quarterly ecosystem health field monitoring programme at the 80 Keta and Ada stations, which provides the water and soil chemistry data that gives satellite index changes their ecological interpretation. Abandoning any one of these three elements degrades the monitoring system's capacity to produce defensible carbon credit accounting — and abandoning the EOSDA monitoring specifically, without a replacement satellite time-series system, would break the continuous evidence chain that international carbon standards require.

## 7.9 Policy-Level Summary: What the Integrated Evidence Means for Ghana

A senior technical advisor briefing MESTI and MLNR leadership on the significance of the integrated evidence architecture would make five arguments.

**First, the three-layer evidence architecture positions the project for Tier 2 and potentially Tier 3 carbon accounting under IPCC Wetlands Supplement guidance.** Tier 1 accounting uses global default emission factors and does not distinguish species or condition classes within a mangrove area. Tier 2 uses country-specific or regionally specific data on carbon stocks and emission factors, derived from field measurements — which the Permanent Sample Plot and ecosystem health data provide. Tier 3, the highest level, uses site-specific, spatially explicit data with demonstrated measurement uncertainty bounds — which is what the integrated drone, satellite, and ecosystem health data system is designed to produce. The drone survey's species-level canopy stratification, combined with the PSP allometric measurements at Salo-Agortoe Stretch, and the EOSDA time-series providing continuous change detection, constitute the evidence infrastructure for a Tier 3-quality accounting claim within the AoI footprint. For the broader 20,717.95-hectare Keta extent, Tier 2 accounting using the ecosystem health field data to parameterise stock estimates remains the defensible standard.

**Second, the World Bank supervision mission needs to see three specific operational confirmations at its next visit to validate monitoring infrastructure.** First, EOSDA upload confirmation: the four AoI boundaries visible in the EOSDA workspace, with named labels, initial Sentinel-2 time-series displays, and alert thresholds configured per the 15 percent protocol described in Chapter 6. Second, T0 baseline archive: the drone orthomosaic and topographic packages for all four AoIs confirmed received and archived in the project data repository, with GeoTIFF and metadata files accessible to the Wildlife Division and MESTI. Third, escalation protocol documentation: a written record of the alert-to-field-response chain — which Wildlife Division officer receives EOSDA alerts, what the field verification protocol is, and who receives the verification report — that demonstrates the monitoring system is connected to the institutional decision-making process rather than generating data into a vacuum. Without these three confirmations, the monitoring system exists as a design rather than an operational reality.

**Third, community-based monitoring is the weakest institutional link in the chain.** The 80-station network at Keta and Ada functions because Community Change Agents — trained during the December 2025 baseline assessment — maintain quarterly field visits and submit data to the Wildlife Division for validation. This community-to-institution pathway is operationally sound when community relationships are intact, when CCAs have the equipment and incentive to continue, and when data submission channels are functioning. It is vulnerable to community conflict, to CCA turnover, and to the loss of institutional attention that commonly follows the end of a consultancy engagement. MLNR and MESTI need to formally integrate CCA data submission into the Wildlife Division's existing reporting structure — not as a parallel voluntary programme but as a mandated component of the district-level MRV obligation — in order for the community monitoring chain to survive the transition from project to programme.

**Fourth, the single most critical long-term risk is EOSDA monitoring discontinuation after project closure.** The WACA ResIP 2 project has a defined implementation period, and the EOSDA platform subscription is funded within that period. If the subscription lapses at project closure without a successor monitoring arrangement — whether a government-funded continuation of the EOSDA subscription, a transition to another Sentinel-2 monitoring platform, or an institutionalised Google Earth Engine monitoring workflow — the continuous satellite evidence chain that underpins carbon credit integrity will break. Under Verra VM0033 and comparable standards, a gap in monitoring coverage is not merely an accounting inconvenience; it creates a verification liability, because the carbon credits issued during the monitoring gap cannot be confidently attributed to continued carbon stock conservation. MLNR and MESTI need to treat EOSDA monitoring continuity as a financial planning obligation from now, not a transition question to be addressed at project close.

**Fifth, and most fundamentally, the integrated evidence architecture transforms Ghana's blue carbon programme from an asset-mapping exercise into an adaptive management system.** The 2024 geo-social baseline established that Ghana has a national blue carbon resource of approximately 30,025 hectares, with 20,717.95 hectares concentrated in the Keta Lagoon Complex. That finding is important. But it is static. A mangrove carbon stock is not a bank deposit whose value is fixed once counted; it is a living system whose carbon sequestration rate, stock stability, and conservation effectiveness depend on the ecological and governance conditions operating on it at every moment. The drone survey, EOSDA continuous monitoring, and ecosystem health field network together create the observational infrastructure needed to know — in near real time, with spatial precision — whether Ghana's most important blue carbon asset is being conserved or eroded, and what the management response should be. That is the infrastructure that transforms a carbon inventory into a carbon programme, and a national commitment into a verifiable claim.

> **Executive Summary for World Bank Task Team Leader**
>
> The Ghana Blue Carbon Assessment has established a three-layer integrated monitoring architecture for the Keta Lagoon Complex — the site containing 20,717.95 hectares (69%) of Ghana's national coastal mangrove resource. Layer 1 is the 2024 Sentinel-2 Random Forest classification providing the national-scale baseline at 10–20m resolution. Layer 2 is the drone T0 survey across four priority AoIs (1,467 Ha total) at 5–10cm resolution, establishing the species-stratified, topographically resolved reference condition against which all future change is measured. Layer 3 is the EOSDA continuous satellite monitoring system, tracking NDVI and NDMI with 15% alert thresholds calibrated against the drone T0 baseline, every 3–5 days. Key integrated findings: (1) Agatsivi-Agortoe (762 Ha) is Ghana's most significant concentrated blue carbon stock and faces the most intense documented extraction pressure — it requires the most rigorous monitoring and enforcement. (2) Salo-Agortoe Stretch (525 Ha) hosts the project's first Permanent Sample Plot and provides the spectral calibration anchor for the entire monitoring system — its protection is non-negotiable. (3) Anyanui Combine (128 Ha) has a confirmed acid sulphate soil stress signal (pH 2.7) implicating a road hydrological barrier — the pending DTM data will determine whether restoration is technically feasible. (4) The ecosystem health field data (water chemistry, soil parameters) gives satellite index changes their ecological interpretation and must be maintained as a quarterly continuous programme. **Three actions are required before the next supervision mission:** EOSDA upload confirmation for all four AoIs with alert thresholds active; drone orthomosaic and topographic package archive confirmation; and formalised Wildlife Division alert-to-field-response protocol documentation. The critical long-term risk is EOSDA monitoring discontinuation at project close: MESTI and MLNR must treat satellite monitoring continuity as a financial planning obligation from now to preserve carbon credit integrity over the 30-year crediting period.
