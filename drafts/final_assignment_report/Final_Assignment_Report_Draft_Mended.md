## 1. Purpose, Scope, and Adaptive Framing

This mended draft preserves the existing Final Assignment Report logic while correcting framing that previously implied stronger numerical cost-benefit precision and stronger-than-supported tenure delineation.

The report remains an umbrella synthesis product. It integrates completed baseline outputs and in-progress technical outputs while distinguishing clearly between:
- **completed evidence**;
- **pending evidence**;
- **interpretive synthesis**; and
- **recommendations and next steps**.

The national baseline framing is retained. Detailed analysis remains site-selective where evidence maturity is stronger. Keta is treated as a **project-specific pilot** for MRV, drone-linked, and continuous monitoring workflows inside a broader national framework.

1. Ghana’s blue carbon assessment retains a national mandate and national framing baseline.

## 5. Tenure, Governance, and Implementation Context

This chapter is explicitly reframed from strong tenure-ownership treatment to a governance and implementation lens.

### 5.1 Framing used in this mended draft

The report treats tenure as:
- **tenure and governance context**;
- **broad tenure/access arrangements**;
- variation in authority, access, and control systems that affect implementation.

The report does **not** claim parcel-level tenure mapping or exact ownership boundary delineation where such evidence is not explicitly present.

### 5.2 Tenure and governance implications by site

| Site | Broad tenure/governance context in current evidence | Implementation implications |
|---|---|---|
| Keta | Relatively stronger local readiness in selected pilot-linked communities, but uneven governance maturity across the wider landscape | Feasible early pilot actions with continued governance strengthening and inclusion safeguards |
| Songor | Contested access/governance environment with conflict-sensitive conditions | Requires mediation, phased implementation, and careful sequencing before scale-up commitments |
| Muni-Pomadze | Lower extant mangrove emphasis but useful conditions for restoration governance in selected communities | Suitable for staged restoration-focused interventions and institutional maturation |

### 5.3 Governance is an enabling condition, not an annex issue

Governance quality is treated as a core determinant of:
- protection enforceability;
- restoration survivability;
- benefit-sharing legitimacy; and
- monitoring continuity and data reliability.

## 7. Comparative Feasibility and Cost-Benefit Considerations

This section replaces hard numerical cost-benefit treatment with comparative, evidence-defensible strategic assessment.

### 7.1 Method note for interpretation

Comparisons are qualitative/semi-quantitative and use the following dimensions:
- indicative cost level;
- expected ecological, social, and carbon co-benefits;
- implementation complexity;
- time horizon for benefits;
- enabling condition dependency;
- strategic suitability under current evidence constraints.

No unsupported economic values or precise returns are presented.

### 7.2 Comparative feasibility and cost-benefit considerations table

| Option class | Indicative cost level* | Expected benefits | Implementation complexity | Time horizon | Strategic suitability |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Protection of relatively intact areas | Low to medium | Near-term loss avoidance, ecosystem retention, carbon permanence support | Medium (depends on compliance and governance) | Short to medium term | High where governance conditions allow enforceability |
| Rehabilitation of degraded mangrove zones | Medium | Ecological recovery, livelihood co-benefits, future carbon gains | Medium to high | Medium term | Moderate to high when drivers are simultaneously managed |
| Expansion/restoration in low-cover landscapes | Medium to high | Long-term ecosystem and potential carbon accumulation gains | High (technical + social prerequisites) | Medium to long term | Strategic in restoration-oriented contexts with sustained support |
| Monitoring system deployment and strengthening | Medium | Better decision quality, earlier risk detection, accountability gains | Medium | Short to medium term for system value; long term for continuity | High as cross-cutting enabler across all sites |

\*Indicative categories are comparative only and do not represent budget estimates.

## 8. Priority Ranking and Strategic Pathway

### 8.1 Priority ranking criteria

| Criterion | Practical definition in this report |
|---|---|
| Ecological urgency | Degree of current degradation pressure and potential near-term loss |
| Strategic significance | Relevance to national blue carbon objectives and management value |
| Feasibility | Likelihood of implementation success under current governance and institutional conditions |
| Monitoring readiness | Availability of practical monitoring architecture and pilotable workflows |
| Co-benefit potential | Anticipated livelihood, social, and governance co-benefits |
