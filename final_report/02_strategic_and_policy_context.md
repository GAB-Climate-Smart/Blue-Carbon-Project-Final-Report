# Chapter 2. Strategic and Policy Context

## 2.1 Why a strategic frame matters before the evidence

A baseline assessment is only useful if the people who will use it can locate the findings inside the policies, financing windows, and reporting cycles that they already operate within. Ghana's blue carbon ecosystems do not sit in a policy vacuum. They sit at the intersection of seven distinct international and national instruments, each of which carries reporting obligations, financing pathways, or enforcement mechanisms that this Final Report's findings can directly inform. This chapter sets out that policy environment so that every recommendation in Part IV can be read as an action against a named instrument, not as an abstract proposal.

The instruments that frame this assignment are, in order of strategic weight:

1. The Paris Agreement and Ghana's Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)
2. The Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF)
3. The Ramsar Convention on Wetlands of International Importance
4. The World Bank West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project Phase II (WACA ResIP II, P175525)
5. Ghana's national climate, biodiversity, and forestry strategies (NCCP, NBSAP, Ghana REDD+ Strategy, National Adaptation Plan)
6. The voluntary carbon market standards (Verra VCS, VM0033, Plan Vivo) and Article 6 of the Paris Agreement
7. The legal and institutional architecture for coastal land tenure and protected area management within Ghana

Each is treated below in turn. The chapter closes with a summary of how this Final Report's structure speaks back to each instrument.

## 2.2 The Paris Agreement and Ghana's Nationally Determined Contributions

Ghana submitted its first Nationally Determined Contribution to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change in 2015 and updated it in 2021. The updated NDC identifies the land sector as the country's largest source of potential carbon dioxide removals. Energy-sector emissions dominate Ghana's gross greenhouse gas profile, but it is the land sector, and within it the forests, wetlands, and coastal ecosystems, that holds the greatest national capacity to draw carbon out of the atmosphere and retain it at scale (Standalone MRV Report, §2.1).

Within the land sector, blue carbon ecosystems occupy a disproportionately large role. Mangroves and tidal salt marshes store up to ten times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial forests. The mechanism is well documented in the IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement: the permanently waterlogged soils on which mangroves grow accumulate organic matter at exceptional rates, and the sustained absence of oxygen prevents the decomposition that would otherwise return that carbon to the atmosphere. The Final Report's Chapter 8 quantifies this stock for Ghana's priority sites against IPCC Tier 2 standards. Chapter 12 then translates the stock into the emissions and removals language that an NDC submission requires.

The implication for this Final Report is direct. Every quantitative finding in Chapter 8 should be readable as a contribution to Ghana's NDC accounting. Every monitoring protocol described in Chapter 11 should be readable as the mechanism by which that contribution is verified across reporting cycles. The Final Report is not a parallel exercise to NDC reporting; it is intended to be a primary input into it.

## 2.3 The Convention on Biological Diversity and the Global Biodiversity Framework

Ghana is a Party to the Convention on Biological Diversity, and the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework adopted in December 2022 has reset the targets against which the country reports. The most consequential of the new targets for this assignment is Target 3, the so-called 30 by 30 target, which commits Parties to ensure that at least thirty percent of terrestrial, inland water, and coastal and marine areas are effectively conserved and managed through ecologically representative, well-connected, and equitably governed systems of protected areas and other effective area-based conservation measures by 2030.

The 30 by 30 target places Ghana's three Ramsar-listed coastal wetland complexes (the Keta Lagoon Complex, the Songor Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, and the Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site) at the centre of the country's biodiversity reporting commitments. These are not peripheral policy objects. They are designated sites at which the Government of Ghana has made formal international commitments to sustain ecological integrity. The tenure analysis in Chapter 10 of this Final Report quantifies the share of each priority landscape that already sits within a recognised protected area, and the priority ranking in Chapter 12 explicitly incorporates 30 by 30 status as one of the ranking criteria.

The National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP) is Ghana's national instrument for translating CBD obligations into domestic action. The NBSAP is currently being revised to reflect the GBF; the findings of this Final Report are sequenced so that they can feed directly into the revision cycle for the coastal wetland sections of the next NBSAP.

## 2.4 The Ramsar Convention and Ghana's wetland obligations

The Ramsar Convention on Wetlands of International Importance, ratified by Ghana in 1988, requires Parties to maintain the ecological character of designated Ramsar Sites and to report on changes in that character under Article 3.2. Ghana has six Ramsar Sites along its coast: Keta Lagoon Complex, Songor, Muni-Pomadze, Densu Delta, Sakumo Lagoon, and the Greater Amanzule Wetlands. Five of the six contain mangrove or tidal salt marsh ecosystems within scope of this assignment; the assignment focuses on the three sites where commercial-scale carbon, biodiversity, and livelihood pressures coincide most acutely (Keta, Songor, Muni-Pomadze) and includes the Ada estuary as a comparison system (Inception Report, §2.3).

The Ramsar obligation is operationally specific. It requires not only that ecological character is maintained but also that any change in character triggers a formal reporting and management response. The Ecosystem Health Standalone Report (Phase 5 deliverable, finalised April 2026) provides the baseline ecological character measurement against which future change will be detected. The continuous monitoring system described in Chapter 11 is the mechanism by which Article 3.2 reporting will be operationalised on a quarterly cycle, replacing the current ad hoc reporting regime that has limited Ghana's ability to demonstrate compliance to the Ramsar Secretariat.

## 2.5 The World Bank WACA ResIP II programme

This assignment was commissioned within the World Bank West Africa Coastal Areas Resilience Investment Project Phase II (WACA ResIP II), Project ID P175525. WACA is a regional programme covering six West African coastal countries (Benin, Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Mauritania, São Tomé and Príncipe, and Togo) and is structured around four pillars: physical investment in coastal protection, integrated coastal zone management, regional dialogue, and project management. Ghana's allocation funds the assessment, monitoring, and restoration of priority blue carbon ecosystems, with the assignment under this contract delivering the assessment and monitoring layer that the subsequent restoration consultancy will build on.

Three implications follow from the WACA framing. First, the Final Report should be readable by the WACA results framework, which means the priority ranking and the spatial intervention table in Chapters 12 and 13 are designed to map onto the WACA reporting indicators. Second, the carbon-related findings should be quantified in units that the World Bank carbon finance pipeline can use. Third, the safeguards architecture (Environmental and Social Framework, ESF) that governs WACA-financed work flows through into how community-facing recommendations are framed in Chapter 14.

## 2.6 Ghana's national climate, biodiversity, and forestry strategies

Ghana's domestic policy environment for blue carbon sits across four overlapping instruments, each housed in a different ministry and each carrying its own reporting cycle.

The **National Climate Change Policy (NCCP)**, led by MESTI, is the umbrella domestic instrument under which the NDC is implemented. It identifies wetlands as a priority adaptation asset and a priority mitigation source.

The **National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP)**, led by MESTI through the Ghana Wildlife Division of the Forestry Commission, is the domestic instrument for the CBD and is currently being revised against the Global Biodiversity Framework.

The **Ghana REDD+ Strategy**, hosted within the Forestry Commission's Climate Change Directorate, governs forest-related emissions reductions. Although the formal REDD+ scope in Ghana has historically focused on the high forest zone of the south-west, the inclusion of mangroves under the IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement and the country's interest in expanding eligible carbon assets means that blue carbon ecosystems are now firmly within the REDD+ analytical perimeter.

The **National Adaptation Plan (NAP)**, also led by MESTI, identifies coastal protection and ecosystem-based adaptation as priority programmes. The mangrove and salt marsh ecosystems in scope here are explicitly named as adaptation assets in the NAP framing.

The Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR), through which this assignment is executed, holds the lead institutional mandate for forestry, lands, and natural resources, and is the focal point for the Wildlife Division and the Forestry Commission. The Ministry of Fisheries and Aquaculture Development (MoFAD) holds the parallel mandate over fisheries and aquaculture, which is consequential because the lagoon and estuarine fisheries that depend on the mangroves cross the MLNR / MoFAD jurisdictional boundary. The institutional crosswalk for the recommendations in Chapter 14 is set out in Chapter 10.

## 2.7 Voluntary carbon market standards and Article 6

A growing share of the financing that this assignment is designed to unlock will flow through the voluntary carbon market or through Article 6 of the Paris Agreement. Both mechanisms require methodological standards that this Final Report is designed to meet.

The most relevant voluntary carbon market standard for Ghana's blue carbon ecosystems is the **Verra Verified Carbon Standard (VCS)**, and within VCS the **VM0033 methodology for tidal wetland and seagrass restoration** is the operative methodology for restoration-based crediting. VM0033 sets the rules for stratification, baseline determination, additionality demonstration, leakage assessment, permanence, and monitoring. The Carbon Inventory Report being prepared by the carbon team is structured against VM0033 v2.1 requirements, and Chapter 8 of this Final Report carries the alignment status. The High Quality Blue Carbon Practitioners Guide (Ocean Risk Alliance, 2024) is used as a complementary best-practice reference for project boundary definition and community benefit-sharing.

**Article 6 of the Paris Agreement** offers a parallel pathway through bilateral and multilateral cooperative arrangements. Article 6.2 enables internationally transferred mitigation outcomes (ITMOs); Article 6.4 establishes a centralised mechanism overseen by the UNFCCC Supervisory Body. Ghana has signalled its intent to participate in both, and the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation has set up a domestic infrastructure for ITMO authorisation and corresponding adjustment. The MRV Framework described in Chapter 11 is designed to satisfy the rigour requirements that an Article 6 buyer or supervisory body will apply.

The implication for this Final Report's recommendations is concrete. Any carbon market or Article 6 financing pathway that the Government of Ghana chooses to pursue requires the data, the monitoring architecture, and the safeguards system that this assignment has produced. Chapter 14 sets out the readiness assessment and the next steps required to convert this evidence base into a financeable pipeline.

## 2.8 The legal and institutional environment for coastal tenure

The Terms of Reference require this Final Report to establish the percentage split of blue carbon ecosystems falling within state-owned land, formal protected areas, private lands, and communal lands. That requirement only makes sense if the legal categories themselves are defined. In Ghana, coastal land tenure is governed by an overlapping set of statutes, customary tenure systems, and special area designations that together produce the four-way split the ToR asks about.

**State land** is land vested in the President of the Republic for and on behalf of the people of Ghana under Article 257 of the 1992 Constitution and the Lands Act 2020 (Act 1036). Most formally protected areas, including Ramsar Sites and the few legally gazetted Wildlife Reserves, fall under state ownership.

**Protected areas** in the coastal zone include the Ramsar Sites listed earlier, several Wildlife Sanctuaries managed by the Forestry Commission's Wildlife Division, and the marine and coastal components of the proposed Marine Protected Area network (MPA), which is at design stage at the time of writing.

**Private land** along Ghana's coast is comparatively limited but is increasing in extent through registered leaseholds, particularly in peri-urban Keta and Ada where tourism, salt-pan development, and aquaculture have driven a wave of land acquisitions over the past two decades.

**Communal land**, also referred to as customary land or stool land, is held by traditional authorities (paramount chiefs, divisional chiefs, and family heads) under the customary tenure system that the 1992 Constitution recognises. The majority of the coastal mangrove ecosystems in scope of this assignment sit on customary land, even where the wetland itself has been designated as a Ramsar Site, because designation does not in itself transfer underlying tenure. This produces the central governance challenge that Chapter 10 returns to: the designated protector and the legal land-holder are usually different parties.

The Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) instrument, established under the Wildlife Resources Management Bill and operationalised by the Forestry Commission, is the principal Ghanaian mechanism for resolving this tension by formalising community-led management arrangements over customary land. The CREMA Readiness Assessment (Phase 2 deliverable, finalised 2025) identifies the priority sites within this assignment where CREMA establishment is feasible and where it is not. Chapter 10 of this Final Report integrates the CREMA findings into the tenure split analysis required by the ToR.
