# Chapter 11. Spatial Monitoring System (Drone, EOSDA, MRV)

## 11.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter is Part III in its entirety. It describes the continuous monitoring system that the assignment has built and operationalised, integrating three observation layers (high-resolution drone, satellite-based change detection, and Community Change Agent ground observation) into a single architecture that connects the cluster-level reading to the national MRV submission. The intent is operational: by the end of the chapter the reader should be able to see how a quarterly CCA reading at a Keta cluster, a Sentinel-2 alert generated by the EOSDA platform, and an entry in Ghana's national MRV submission are linked in one continuous data flow.

The chapter is not a technical manual. The flight plans, the polygon coordinate registers, the MRV data dictionary, the QA/QC protocols, and the platform configuration are in the Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring Report and the National MRV Framework Standalone Report. This chapter is the management-facing summary that the recommendations in Chapter 14 will draw on.

## 11.2 Why a continuous system rather than a one-off baseline

A baseline measured once and shelved is of limited value to a Ramsar reporting cycle, an NDC verification cycle, or a Verra crediting cycle. Each of those instruments requires not the measurement of state at a single point in time but the detection of change between points in time. The continuous monitoring system is the mechanism by which the baseline established in Part II is kept current and turned into a stream of detectable change rather than a static snapshot.

Three operational requirements drive the design. First, the system must detect change at a spatial resolution that is meaningful for management response, which means cluster-level rather than national-level resolution. Second, it must detect change quickly enough to enable an enforcement response while the disturbance is still in progress, which means sub-monthly latency for high-priority change types. Third, it must produce an output that satisfies the rigour requirements of the national MRV framework, which means defensible chain-of-custody from observation to submission.

## 11.3 The three observation layers

The first observation layer is the **high-resolution drone survey** of fixed polygons at the priority sites. The polygons are co-located with the carbon sample plots and the ecological condition stations established in Phases 4 and 5, so that the drone imagery time series can be cross-walked to the carbon and ecological measurements at the same location. Drone flights are scheduled on a defined revisit cycle (named in the Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring Report) and produce orthomosaics at sub-metre resolution. The drone layer is the highest-resolution source in the system and is used for ground-truth validation of the satellite layer and for the closest detection of small-area disturbance.

The second observation layer is the **EOSDA satellite monitoring platform**. EOSDA ingests Sentinel-2 and other satellite data on the standard revisit cycle, applies change-detection algorithms across the priority site polygons, and generates automated alerts when a change exceeds a configured threshold. The platform is the operational backbone of the system because it is the only layer that can cover all priority sites continuously and at low marginal cost. The drone and CCA layers exist to validate and contextualise the satellite alerts, not to substitute for them.

The third observation layer is the **Community Change Agent (CCA) network**. The CCA network is the human ground layer of the system. CCAs are recruited from the communities surrounding the priority sites, trained in a standardised observation protocol, and report on a quarterly cycle. They detect change types that the satellite cannot see (sub-pixel disturbance, illegal cutting at small scales, water quality anomalies, fishery anomalies) and they validate the satellite alerts on the ground when an alert is triggered. The CCA network is gender-balanced by structural design (Chapter 9 §9.5) and is supported by a defined channel through the district assemblies and the Forestry Commission's Wildlife Division.

## 11.4 How the three layers integrate

The integration logic is straightforward but worth stating explicitly because it is what turns three disconnected observation streams into a single architecture.

A typical change-detection pathway begins with an EOSDA alert flagging a potential disturbance at a named cluster. The alert is routed to the CCA assigned to that cluster, who conducts a ground visit within a defined response window, confirms or rejects the alert, and reports the cause where the alert is confirmed. Where the disturbance is significant or the cluster is in a high-priority polygon, a drone overflight is scheduled to capture sub-metre imagery that supports the formal record. The confirmed disturbance is then logged into the MRV data system with the satellite, drone, and CCA evidence linked as a single record. From the MRV data system, the record flows into Ghana's national MRV submission cycle and, where relevant, into the Ramsar Article 3.2 reporting cycle and the Verra monitoring report for any future crediting project.

The system is designed so that no single observation layer is a single point of failure. The satellite layer provides coverage but not certainty; the CCA layer provides certainty but not coverage; the drone layer provides forensic resolution at lower frequency. Together they form a triangulated system in which each layer compensates for the limitations of the others.

## 11.5 The MRV framework in one paragraph

The National MRV Framework Standalone Report sets out the data dictionary, the reporting templates, the QA/QC protocols, and the institutional arrangements through which the monitoring outputs feed Ghana's UNFCCC reporting cycle. This Final Report does not reproduce that framework. The point to take from it for management purposes is that the MRV architecture has been designed to satisfy the rigour requirements of three downstream consumers simultaneously: Ghana's NDC reporting cycle under the Paris Agreement, the Ramsar Article 3.2 reporting cycle, and the verification requirements of the voluntary carbon market and Article 6.

## 11.6 Operational status at the time of writing

At the time of writing (April 2026), the continuous monitoring system is operational across the priority sites with the following status. The drone polygon network is established at Keta, Songor, and Ada with the Muni-Pomadze polygon network in final configuration. The EOSDA monitoring platform is configured with the priority polygons and is generating routine change-detection outputs. The CCA network is recruited and trained at the priority sites and is on a quarterly reporting cycle, with the first full quarterly cycle completed and reviewed under Phase 6.

Chapter 14 names the operational sustainment requirements (financing, institutional hosting, succession planning for the CCA network) that are needed to keep the system running beyond the assignment term.
