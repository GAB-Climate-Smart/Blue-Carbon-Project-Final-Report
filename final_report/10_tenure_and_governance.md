# Chapter 10. Tenure, Governance, and Institutional Landscape

## 10.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter delivers the tenure split mandated by the Terms of Reference, sets out the institutional landscape that governs the priority blue carbon ecosystems, and reports the CREMA readiness assessment that determines where community-led management arrangements are feasible. It is the chapter at which the legal and customary architecture of coastal Ghana is reconciled with the spatial baseline established in Chapter 6, so that the priority ranking in Chapter 12 and the spatial intervention plan in Chapter 13 can be drafted against a defensible tenure picture.

The chapter does not restate the legal categories themselves; those were defined in Chapter 2 §2.8. It applies them.

## 10.2 The four-way tenure split mandated by the Terms of Reference

The Terms of Reference require this Final Report to establish the percentage of blue carbon ecosystems falling within state-owned land, formal protected areas, private land, and communal land. The categories are not always mutually exclusive: a Ramsar Site (a protected area designation) typically sits on land that is simultaneously owned by the state in cadastral terms and held under customary tenure in operational terms. This Final Report resolves the overlap by reporting the **dominant operational tenure category** for each parcel, defined as the category that determines who can authorise or restrict activity on the ground today.

Under that convention, the headline tenure split for the three priority sites is reported in the table below. The full per-cluster tenure register is in Annex B; this table carries the site-level summary that the priority ranking in Chapter 12 will use as one of its ranking criteria.

| Site | State (cadastral, non-protected) | Formal protected area | Private (registered leasehold) | Communal (customary, stool/family) | Tenure unresolved |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Keta Lagoon Complex | Reported in Annex B | Ramsar designation overlay | Increasing in peri-urban Keta and Anloga | Dominant operational category for the mangrove parcels | Flagged where cadastral and customary registers cannot be reconciled |
| Songor Lagoon | Reported in Annex B | Ramsar + UNESCO Biosphere overlay | Salt-pan leaseholds in places | Dominant for the mangrove fringes | Flagged at the salt-pan and lagoon-margin interface |
| Muni-Pomadze | Reported in Annex B | Ramsar designation overlay | Limited; peri-urban Winneba pressure | Dominant for the lagoon fringe | Flagged at the urban edge |

Three points about this table matter for the chapters that follow. First, the **communal (customary) category is the dominant operational category for the majority of the mangrove parcels at all three priority sites**, even where the wetland itself is designated as a Ramsar Site, because designation does not transfer the underlying customary tenure. Second, **private leasehold is small in absolute terms but increasing**, particularly in the peri-urban stretches of Keta, Anloga, and Winneba, and the trend matters for the medium-term protection picture. Third, the **tenure unresolved category is non-trivial**: where a parcel could not be definitively classified, it has been flagged rather than imputed, and the recommendations in Chapter 14 name the cadastral reconciliation work that would close the gap.

## 10.3 The central governance challenge

The central governance challenge that this chapter exposes is straightforward to state and difficult to resolve. **The legal duty-bearer for ecological character (the state, through the Ramsar designation) and the operational land-holder (the customary authorities, on behalf of the community) are usually different parties, with different incentives, different reporting lines, and different time horizons.** This is not a Ghana-specific problem; it is the structural condition of most West African coastal wetlands. It is, however, the condition that any recommendation in Chapter 14 has to contend with directly, because a protection or restoration plan that is signed by the Forestry Commission but not endorsed by the relevant chief and CCA network will not hold on the ground.

The Community Resource Management Area (CREMA) instrument exists precisely to resolve this tension by formalising community-led management arrangements over customary land within the framework of the Wildlife Resources Management regime. CREMA establishment is not a panacea, but it is the most operationally tested mechanism Ghana has for bringing the legal duty-bearer and the operational land-holder into a single management arrangement.

## 10.4 CREMA readiness across the priority sites

The CREMA Readiness Assessment (Phase 2) applied a structured readiness matrix to the priority sites and the surrounding communities. The matrix covers six readiness dimensions: traditional authority engagement, community organisation capacity, prior experience with natural resource management, intra-community cohesion, alignment with district assembly priorities, and the presence of an entry-point intervention that the CREMA could be built around. Each dimension is scored on a four-band scale and the overall readiness is reported as a composite.

The headline CREMA readiness picture across the priority sites is uneven and is consequential for the spatial intervention plan in Chapter 13.

At **Keta**, CREMA readiness is highest in the clusters where the traditional authority has previously engaged on coastal protection or fisheries management, and lowest in the clusters where intra-community cohesion is fractured by recent land disputes. The CREMA Readiness Assessment names the specific clusters in each band. The intervention plan in Chapter 13 sequences CREMA establishment to start with the highest-readiness clusters and build outward.

At **Songor**, CREMA readiness is shaped by the salt-winning economy and the dual Ramsar / Biosphere designation. Several of the clusters score well on traditional authority engagement but moderately on alignment with district assembly priorities, because the salt economy creates a structural tension between the conservation logic of the designations and the livelihood logic of the salt-pan operators. The intervention plan returns to this tension.

At **Muni-Pomadze**, CREMA readiness is constrained by the peri-urban character of the surrounding population and by the institutional weight of the Effutu Traditional Council, which holds a coordinating role that does not always align with the district assembly's planning cycle. The intervention plan recommends a different entry-point at Muni-Pomadze than at the larger sites.

The cluster-by-cluster CREMA readiness scores are in Annex B. The full readiness matrix and the methodological note are in the CREMA Readiness Assessment.

## 10.5 The institutional landscape

The institutional landscape that surrounds the priority blue carbon ecosystems involves seven principal actors, each with a defined statutory mandate or operational role. The mapping below is the navigational reference that Chapter 14 uses when naming implementation partners against each recommendation.

The **Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR)** holds the lead institutional mandate for forestry, lands, and natural resources, and is the focal point for the Forestry Commission and its Wildlife Division. MLNR is the contracting authority for this assignment.

The **Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI)** is the political and reporting home for the National Climate Change Policy, the National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan, and the National Adaptation Plan. MESTI is the commissioning ministry under the WACA ResIP II framing.

The **Forestry Commission**, through its **Wildlife Division** and its **Climate Change Directorate**, is the operational agency for protected area management and for the REDD+ portfolio. The Wildlife Division is the relevant agency for Ramsar Site management on the ground.

The **Ministry of Fisheries and Aquaculture Development (MoFAD)** holds the parallel mandate over fisheries and aquaculture. MoFAD's mandate is consequential because the lagoon and estuarine fisheries that depend on the mangroves cross the MLNR / MoFAD jurisdictional boundary, and the recommendations in Chapter 14 name where coordination across this boundary is needed.

The **Environmental Protection Authority (EPA)** is the regulatory agency for environmental impact assessment, pollution control, and the safeguards screening that any WACA-financed intervention will require.

The **District Assemblies** with jurisdiction over the priority sites (Keta Municipal, Anloga District, Ada East, Effutu Municipal) are the local government interface for any spatial intervention and the principal channel through which CREMA arrangements are aligned with district planning cycles.

The **Traditional Authorities** at each site (paramount chiefs, divisional chiefs, family heads, chief fishermen) are the customary land-holders and the operational gatekeepers for any community-facing intervention. They are not a homogeneous category; the governance report names the specific institutions at each site.
