# Chapter 4. Methodology Overview

## 4.1 Why a methodology chapter sits here

This chapter is a navigational instrument, not a methods manual. The methods used in this assignment are documented at full technical depth in the four standalone reports (Ecosystem Health, Carbon Inventory, Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring, National MRV Framework). Reproducing them here would duplicate hundreds of pages and break the editorial rule that each fact lives in one place. Instead, this chapter sets out the methodological architecture: which method answers which question, which standalone report carries the full protocol, and how the methods connect across thematic strands so that the integrated findings in Part II can be read with confidence.

The reader who needs the field protocol for soil organic carbon sampling should turn to the Carbon Inventory Report (§3.7). The reader who needs the drone flight plan and ground control point design should turn to the Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring Report (§4). The reader who needs the water quality station register should turn to the Ecosystem Health Report (Appendix A). The reader who wants to know how those three protocols fit together to produce a single defensible baseline for Keta, Songor, and Muni-Pomadze should read this chapter.

## 4.2 The six methodological strands

The assignment uses six methodological strands, one for each thematic objective set out in Chapter 3. Each strand has its own primary standard, its own field or desk protocol, and its own quality assurance regime.

The **spatial extent strand** uses remote sensing as its primary tool. Sentinel-2 imagery at 10 metre resolution provides the regional-scale mapping; PlanetScope and high-resolution drone orthomosaics provide the validation and the cluster-scale measurement. Land cover classification follows a supervised approach with locally collected training points, and accuracy is reported using the standard confusion matrix and kappa coefficient (Geospatial Baseline Report, §4). The spatial extent strand is the source for Chapter 6.

The **ecological condition strand** uses in situ measurement of physico-chemical water quality parameters, vegetation structure, and macrofaunal indicators across stratified sampling stations. Water quality parameters include dissolved oxygen, pH, salinity, electrical conductivity, total dissolved solids, oxidation-reduction potential, and nutrient concentrations. Vegetation structure is measured through plot-based species composition, canopy cover, regeneration density, and pneumatophore density where relevant. The full station register and parameter set are in the Ecosystem Health Report. The ecological condition strand is the source for Chapter 7.

The **carbon stock strand** follows the IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement Tier 2 approach. Above-ground biomass is estimated through diameter-at-breast-height measurements applied through species-specific allometric equations. Below-ground biomass is estimated through root-to-shoot ratios appropriate to each species. Soil organic carbon is measured through cores to one metre depth with laboratory analysis of bulk density and carbon fraction. Deadwood is captured through visual survey at the plot. The carbon stock strand is the source for Chapter 8 and is the subject of the Carbon Inventory Report being finalised by the carbon team.

The Carbon Inventory Report, now substantially integrated, implements this approach through a two-tier sampling architecture. At the national baseline level, 3,757 individual trees were measured across 11 sampling communities using Temporary Sample Plots (TSPs): six communities at the Songor Ramsar Site (Wasa Kuse, Azizanya, Totope, Luhuese, Alavanyo, Patukope; N = 1,100 trees) and five communities at the Keta Lagoon Complex (Tunu/Gblipe, Atiteti/Anyanui, Agbatsivi, Atorkor, Galotse/Sota; N = 2,657 trees). Above-ground biomass was estimated through diameter-at-breast-height measurements applied through species-specific allometric equations for the dominant species (*Rhizophora racemosa*, *Avicennia germinans*, *Laguncularia racemosa*). Below-ground biomass was estimated through root-to-shoot ratios (fine root and coarse root fractions). Soil organic carbon was sampled to a depth of 30 cm at each plot, with laboratory analysis of bulk density and organic carbon fraction. The 30 cm depth is appropriate for a national snapshot baseline; the Carbon Inventory Report and the MRV Framework specify that any project-level assessment for carbon trading must sample to a minimum of 100 cm (IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement recommended range: 0-100 cm) using Permanent Sample Plots (PSPs) established by the project proponent. Songor carbon results are complete; Keta above-ground biomass is reported, with belowground components (fine root, coarse root, and soil organic carbon) currently in laboratory processing.

The **socio-economic strand** uses a mixed-methods design that combines household survey data, focus group discussions, key informant interviews, and gendered livelihood mapping. The household survey samples were drawn at the cluster level around each priority site, with disaggregation by gender, age, and primary livelihood activity. The full sampling frame is in the Socio-Economic Baseline Report. The socio-economic strand is the source for Chapter 9.

The **tenure and governance strand** uses cadastral analysis, protected-area register reconciliation, customary tenure mapping through traditional authority interviews, and institutional mapping across the relevant ministries, commissions, and district assemblies. The CREMA Readiness Assessment sits inside this strand and uses a structured readiness matrix applied site by site. The tenure and governance strand is the source for Chapter 10.

The **continuous monitoring strand** is the operational integration of three observation layers: drone survey of fixed polygons on a defined revisit cycle, EOSDA satellite monitoring with automated change-detection alerts, and CCA-led ground observation on a quarterly cycle. The Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring Report and the National MRV Framework Report together carry the full design. The continuous monitoring strand is the source for Chapter 11.

## 4.3 How the strands connect

The six strands are designed to interlock rather than to run as parallel silos. Three connection points are worth naming because they determine how Part II can be read as an integrated whole rather than as six separate baselines bound between the same covers.

The first connection point is the **shared spatial frame**. All six strands work to the same set of priority site boundaries, and within each site to the same cluster delineation. A water quality station, a carbon plot, a household survey cluster, a tenure parcel, and a monitoring polygon at Keta can all be located against the same map. This shared frame is what makes the integrated synthesis in Chapter 12 possible at all.

The second connection point is the **stratification logic**. Within each priority site the landscape is stratified into ecologically meaningful units (intact mangrove, degraded mangrove, salt marsh, mudflat, converted land) and the same stratification is used by the carbon, ecological, and spatial strands. This means a finding in one strand can be cross-checked against a finding in another at the same stratum without translation losses.

The third connection point is the **temporal anchor**. The baseline year for all six strands is taken as the composite of the field campaigns conducted between 2024 and early 2026. Where a strand draws on historical imagery or archival data (notably the Keta deforestation analysis), the historical reference period is named explicitly. The Final Report does not mix baselines from different years without flagging the difference.

## 4.4 Quality assurance, uncertainty, and limitations

Each strand carries its own quality assurance regime, documented in the corresponding standalone report. This section names the cross-cutting quality assurance principles that apply across the assignment, so that the reader can read every numerical claim in Part II with a consistent expectation of rigour.

The first principle is **traceability**. Every numerical claim in this Final Report is traceable to a specific table or section in one of the standalone reports. Annex D lists the source register; the in-line citation convention is set out in Chapter 3.

The second principle is **named uncertainty**. Where a finding has a quantified uncertainty range it is reported with that range. Where a finding has a known qualitative limitation but no quantified range it is reported with the limitation named in the surrounding text. The Final Report does not present point estimates as if they were exact measurements when they are not.

The third principle is **independent validation where feasible**. Spatial extent measurements are validated against drone orthomosaics and ground control points. Water quality readings are validated through duplicate sampling at a subset of stations. Carbon plots will be validated through a quality control re-measurement protocol described in the Carbon Inventory Report. Socio-economic findings are triangulated across household survey, focus group, and key informant data.

Three limitations apply across the assignment and should be stated up front so that the reader can weigh the findings appropriately.

The first limitation is the **partial carbon integration**. The Carbon Inventory Report has been substantially integrated into this Final Report: Songor ecosystem carbon is fully reported (297,453 Mg total, 638.64 MgC/ha) and Keta above-ground biomass is documented (716,220 Mg, 34.57 MgC/ha). The remaining gap is Keta belowground carbon (fine root, coarse root, and soil organic carbon), which is currently in laboratory processing. When those results are received, they will be inserted into the few remaining tagged placeholders in Chapters 8 and 12. The Final Report structure ensures this is a single editorial pass, not a rewrite.

The second limitation is the **tenure resolution ceiling**. Cadastral and protected-area registers in Ghana are not always reconciled at the parcel level along the coast, and customary tenure does not always map cleanly onto state cadastral categories. Where a parcel could not be definitively classified, Chapter 10 reports it as "tenure unresolved" rather than imputing a category.

The third limitation is the **single-baseline-year constraint**. The assignment produces a baseline at one point in time. Trend analysis is only possible where historical data exists (notably for the Keta deforestation series). For all other parameters, trend detection is the job of the continuous monitoring system described in Chapter 11, not of this baseline.

## 4.5 Reading guide for Part II

Part II runs from Chapter 5 to Chapter 10. The chapters are sequenced to build the integrated picture in stages.

Chapter 5 sets the national coastal context and introduces the four study areas in their geographic and ecological setting. Chapter 6 establishes the spatial extent of mangrove and tidal salt marsh at each site. Chapter 7 establishes the ecological condition of those same sites against the parameter set described in §4.2. Chapter 8 establishes the carbon stock held within them. Chapter 9 establishes the socio-economic dependency that surrounds them. Chapter 10 establishes the tenure and governance arrangements that determine who can do what to them, and concludes with the tenure split mandated by the Terms of Reference.

A reader who wants only the spatial picture can read Chapters 5 and 6. A reader who wants the integrated ecological and carbon picture can read Chapters 6, 7, and 8. A reader who wants the management-facing picture should read Chapters 9 and 10 alongside the strategic chapters in Part IV. Each Part II chapter opens with a one-paragraph orientation that names what the chapter delivers and what it does not.
