# Chapter 8. Carbon Stocks and Inventory

## 8.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter quantifies the carbon stock held in the mangrove ecosystems at the two principal study landscapes, Keta Lagoon Complex and Songor Ramsar Site, against IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement Tier 2 standards. It draws on the Carbon Inventory and Stock Assessment Report (Standalone Report 3), which documents the field measurement of 3,757 individual trees across 11 sampling communities and the laboratory analysis of soil organic carbon.

The carbon inventory serves a dual purpose. First, it provides a national baseline snapshot of carbon stocks across Ghana's most significant mangrove landscapes. Second, it validates the field measurement workflow described in the companion MRV Framework Report, demonstrating that the methodology can be replicated by project proponents operating within the national monitoring architecture. The inventory employs Temporary Sample Plots appropriate to a national baseline survey; the MRV Framework specifies the Permanent Sample Plot requirements for any project-specific assessment targeting carbon credit generation.

## 8.2 Carbon pools assessed

The chapter reports on four carbon pools consistent with the IPCC Tier 2 framework for tidal wetlands: above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (fine root carbon, coarse root carbon), and soil organic carbon (SOC). The methods for each pool are summarised in Chapter 4 and documented in full in the Carbon Inventory Report (Chapter 3). SOC sampling was conducted to 30 cm depth for this national baseline; the implications of this conservative depth relative to the IPCC 100 cm recommendation are discussed in Section 8.5.

## 8.3 Above-ground biomass

The carbon inventory measured 3,757 individual trees across the two Ramsar sites, representing the most comprehensive vegetation structure assessment of Ghanaian mangroves to date. Nine tree species were recorded at Songor (N = 1,100 trees) and ten at Keta (N = 2,657 trees).

**Table 8.1: Above-ground carbon stock by site**

| Site | Mean AGB (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total AGB storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Songor (465.76 Ha) | 30.19 ± 15.93 | -10.76 to 71.14 | 129.30 | 14,061 |
| Keta (20,717.95 Ha) | 34.57 ± 17.63 | -8.56 to 77.70 | 134.90 | 716,220 |
| **Combined** | | | | **730,281** |

All four vegetation structure parameters (diameter, height, wood density, basal area) differ significantly between the two sites (p < 0.0001). Keta trees are taller (mean 6.22 m vs 4.20 m) with higher mean wood density (0.92 vs 0.67 g/cm3), driven by the dominance of *Rhizophora racemosa* (wood density 1.14 g/cm3). Songor trees have larger mean diameter (7.58 cm vs 4.60 cm), reflecting the different species composition: *Avicennia germinans* dominates Songor, while Keta supports three true mangrove genera (*Rhizophora*, *Avicennia*, *Laguncularia*).

The wide confidence intervals and high coefficients of variation (129 to 135%) reflect the inherent spatial heterogeneity of mangrove landscapes where closed-canopy, coppicing, harvested, and grassland strata coexist. This variability is typical of mangrove AGB inventories globally and underscores the importance of stratified sampling in future project-specific assessments.

Keta's total AGB storage (716,220 Mg) is 51 times that of Songor (14,061 Mg), driven by the 44-fold difference in mangrove area. This concentration of above-ground carbon in a single landscape reinforces the strategic importance of Keta for both conservation and carbon finance.

## 8.4 Below-ground biomass

**Table 8.2: Below-ground carbon stock (Songor)**

| Component | Mean (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fine root carbon (< 2 mm) | 9.83 ± 5.53 | -4.39 to 24.05 | 137.00 | 4,578 |
| Coarse root carbon (>= 2 mm) | 0.62 ± 0.30 | -0.15 to 1.39 | 119.00 | 289 |

Fine root carbon at Songor (9.83 MgC/ha) substantially exceeds coarse root carbon (0.62 MgC/ha), consistent with the dominance of *Avicennia germinans*, which develops extensive pneumatophore and fine cable root networks rather than the coarse prop root structures characteristic of *Rhizophora*.

**Keta below-ground biomass:** Laboratory analysis of Keta belowground samples is currently in progress. Fine root and coarse root carbon values will be incorporated upon completion. Given that Keta's species composition is dominated by *Rhizophora racemosa* (with its substantial prop root systems), the coarse-root-to-fine-root ratio at Keta may differ from the Songor pattern.

## 8.5 Soil organic carbon

**Table 8.3: Soil organic carbon (Songor, 0-30 cm)**

| Component | Mean (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SOC (0-30 cm) | 598.00 ± 49.33 | 471.20 to 724.80 | 30.62 | 278,524 |

Soil organic carbon is the dominant carbon pool at Songor, accounting for 93.6% of total measured ecosystem carbon. This finding is consistent with the global mangrove literature, which consistently reports SOC as the primary carbon reservoir, typically comprising 50 to 90% of total ecosystem carbon.

The relatively lower coefficient of variation for SOC (30.6%) compared to AGB (129%) and root carbon (119 to 137%) confirms that soil carbon is the more spatially stable pool. This spatial regularity is characteristic of mangrove SOC globally: while tree biomass varies sharply with disturbance history, the organic-rich soil matrix accumulates over centuries and varies more gradually across the landscape.

The Songor SOC density of 598 MgC/ha at 0-30 cm places this site in the upper range of global mangrove SOC values for this depth interval, comparable to values from Southeast Asian and Caribbean systems. If the standard global ratio of approximately 2 to 2.5 times the 0-30 cm value applies, total SOC to 1 metre depth at Songor could be in the range of 1,200 to 1,500 MgC/ha.

**Depth limitation and the two-tier logic:** SOC for this national baseline was sampled to 30 cm. The IPCC Wetlands Supplement recommends sampling to a minimum of 100 cm for mangrove soils, recognising that a substantial proportion of SOC is stored below the 30 cm horizon. The SOC values reported here are therefore conservative minimum estimates. The companion MRV Framework specifies that any project proponent conducting a carbon assessment for credit generation must sample SOC to 100 cm minimum, with depth intervals at 0-15, 15-30, 30-50, and 50-100 cm. The national baseline provides the reference; project-level assessments must extend to the full depth profile.

**Keta SOC:** Values are currently in laboratory processing. Given that SOC accounts for over 90% of total ecosystem carbon at the comparable Songor site, the Keta SOC result will be the single most consequential number in the national inventory. If Keta SOC density is comparable to Songor, total SOC storage for Keta's 20,718 Ha could be approximately 12.4 million Mg of soil carbon alone.

## 8.6 Total ecosystem carbon stock

**Table 8.4: Total ecosystem carbon stock (Songor)**

| Component | MgC/ha | % of total | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Above-ground biomass | 30.19 | 4.7% | 14,061 |
| Fine root carbon | 9.83 | 1.5% | 4,578 |
| Coarse root carbon | 0.62 | 0.1% | 289 |
| Soil organic carbon (0-30 cm) | 598.00 | 93.6% | 278,524 |
| **Total** | **638.64** | **100%** | **297,453** |

**Key finding:** The total ecosystem carbon stock at Songor is 297,453 Mg (638.64 MgC/ha). The overwhelming dominance of SOC (93.6%) has two critical management implications. First, the most important carbon conservation action is preventing soil disturbance; even total loss of above-ground biomass would affect only 6.4% of the stock. Second, carbon assessments based solely on biomass would underestimate the true carbon value by a factor of approximately 16.

**Keta total:** Keta AGB is 716,220 Mg. Belowground components are pending. Combining the measured AGB with an indicative SOC estimate (using Songor density as a proxy), total ecosystem carbon at Keta could exceed 13 million Mg, which would represent one of the largest documented mangrove carbon stocks in West Africa. This figure will be confirmed or revised when the laboratory results are received.

## 8.7 Deforestation, degradation, and the additionality context

The Keta Lagoon Complex experiences an annual deforestation rate of 2.15%, driven primarily by the harvesting of *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Rhizophora mangle* for commercial fish smoking. This rate is derived from multi-temporal analysis and corroborated by the Socio-Economic Baseline Report.

At this deforestation rate, Keta loses approximately 445 hectares of canopy cover per year. Using the measured AGB carbon density of 34.57 MgC/ha, this translates to an annual above-ground carbon emission of approximately 15,400 Mg of carbon, or approximately 56,500 Mg CO2e, from biomass loss alone. When SOC emissions from deforested areas are included (as required under both IPCC and Verra methodologies), the annual emission from Keta deforestation is likely to be several times higher. This figure will be quantified precisely when the Keta SOC data is received.

At Songor, the degradation trajectory differs: salt pan expansion rather than fuelwood extraction is the primary driver, and the remaining 465.76 Ha represents a near-terminal landscape more appropriately framed as a restoration candidate than a conservation baseline.

Carbon permanence faces two categories of risk. Project-addressable risks include fuelwood harvesting (addressable through improved cookstove interventions), salt pan expansion (addressable through CREMA governance), and unsustainable aquaculture. Non-project structural risks include the Akosombo Dam sediment deficit, coastal erosion from sea-level rise, and extreme weather events. Any carbon project must account for both categories and may need to apply a permanence buffer (typically 10-20% of credits) for non-addressable reversal risk.

## 8.8 Uncertainty and quality treatment

Uncertainty in carbon stock estimates arises from allometric equation selection (can shift AGB by 15-30%), DBH and height measurement error, wood density database uncertainty, spatial variability within strata, SOC depth limitation (0-30 cm captures approximately 40-60% of the 0-100 cm total), and laboratory analytical precision.

The wide confidence intervals reported for AGB (crossing zero at the lower bound) reflect high spatial heterogeneity rather than unreliable methodology. Coefficients of variation of 129-135% are within the range commonly reported for mangrove AGB inventories in heterogeneous landscapes. SOC shows substantially lower variability (CV 30.6%), confirming soil carbon as the more reliable basis for landscape-level stock estimation.

Formal uncertainty propagation across all carbon pools will be completed when the Keta belowground values are received. The allometric equation citation is pending confirmation from the Carbon Assessment Team and is required for verification compliance.

## 8.9 Carbon market readiness

This national baseline inventory establishes the four elements required for carbon market development: quantified stocks at two Ramsar sites, a documented deforestation baseline (2.15%/year at Keta), a validated field methodology, and species and structural data sufficient for project-level allometric equation selection.

However, a project proponent seeking Verra VM0033 or Gold Standard certification must go beyond this baseline: establishing Permanent Sample Plots (with tagged trees and documented subplot coordinates), sampling SOC to 100 cm minimum, applying and citing specific allometric equations, conducting formal uncertainty analysis, and establishing a community-owned re-measurement protocol at 3-5 year intervals within the MRV Framework. The national baseline is the proof of concept; the project-specific assessment is the market-ready instrument. The full readiness assessment and pathway recommendations are in Chapter 14 Section 14.2.4.
