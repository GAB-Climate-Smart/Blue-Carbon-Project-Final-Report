# Chapter 8. Carbon Stocks and Inventory

## 8.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter quantifies the carbon stock held in the mangrove ecosystems at the two principal study landscapes, Keta Lagoon Complex and Songor Ramsar Site, against IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement Tier 2 standards. It draws on the Carbon Inventory and Stock Assessment Report (Standalone Report 3), which documents the field measurement of 3,757 individual trees across 11 sampling communities and the laboratory analysis of soil organic carbon across all pools at both sites.

The carbon inventory serves a dual purpose. First, it provides a national baseline snapshot of carbon stocks across Ghana's most significant mangrove landscapes. Second, it validates the field measurement workflow described in the companion MRV Framework Report, demonstrating that the methodology can be replicated by project proponents operating within the national monitoring architecture. The inventory employs Temporary Sample Plots appropriate to a national baseline survey; the MRV Framework specifies the Permanent Sample Plot requirements for any project-specific assessment targeting carbon credit generation.

## 8.2 Carbon pools assessed

The chapter reports on four carbon pools consistent with the IPCC Tier 2 framework for tidal wetlands: above-ground biomass (AGB), below-ground biomass (fine root carbon, coarse root carbon), and soil organic carbon (SOC). The methods for each pool are summarised in Chapter 4 and documented in full in the Carbon Inventory Report (Chapter 3). SOC sampling was conducted to 30 cm depth for this national baseline; the implications of this conservative depth relative to the IPCC 100 cm recommendation are discussed in Section 8.5.

## 8.3 Above-ground biomass

The carbon inventory measured 3,757 individual trees across the two Ramsar sites, representing the most comprehensive vegetation structure assessment of Ghanaian mangroves to date. Nine tree species were recorded at Songor (N = 1,100 trees) and ten at Keta (N = 2,657 trees).

**Table 8.1: Above-ground carbon stock by site**

| Site | Mean AGB (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total AGB storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Songor (465.76 Ha) | 30.19 ± 15.93 | -10.76 to 71.14 | 129.30 | 14,061 |
| Keta (20,717.95 Ha) — incl. grass | 14.65 ± 2.68 | — | — | 303,518 |
| Keta (20,717.95 Ha) — excl. grass | 6.14 ± 2.52 | — | — | 127,208 |

All four vegetation structure parameters (diameter, height, wood density, basal area) differ significantly between the two sites (p < 0.0001). Keta trees are taller (mean 6.22 m vs 4.20 m) with higher mean wood density (0.92 vs 0.67 g/cm³), driven by the dominance of *Rhizophora racemosa* (wood density 1.14 g/cm³). Songor trees have larger mean diameter (7.58 cm vs 4.60 cm), reflecting the different species composition: *Avicennia germinans* dominates Songor, while Keta supports three true mangrove genera (*Rhizophora*, *Avicennia*, *Laguncularia*).

Keta's AGB carbon density (14.65 MgC/ha including grass contributions, 6.14 MgC/ha excluding grass) reflects the younger, shorter, and more disturbed canopy structure at Keta relative to the larger-stemmed trees recorded at Songor. The higher wood density of *Rhizophora racemosa* (1.14 g/cm³) at Keta is offset by the smaller mean diameter (4.60 cm vs 7.58 cm at Songor), yielding a lower per-hectare AGB density. The total above-ground storage at Keta (303,518 Mg including grasses) is 22 times that of Songor (14,061 Mg), driven entirely by the 44-fold difference in mangrove area. When species-specific allometric equations (rather than the generalised Kauffman and Donato 2012 model) are applied, Keta AGB increases to 13.4 ± 5.04 MgC/ha, reflecting stronger biomass representation for *Rhizophora racemosa*.

## 8.4 Below-ground biomass

**Table 8.2: Below-ground carbon stock — Songor Lagoon**

| Component | Mean ± S.E. (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fine root carbon (< 2 mm) | 9.83 ± 5.53 | -4.39 to 24.05 | 137.00 | 4,578 |
| Coarse root carbon (≥ 2 mm) | 0.62 ± 0.30 | -0.15 to 1.39 | 119.00 | 289 |

Fine root carbon at Songor (9.83 MgC/ha) substantially exceeds coarse root carbon (0.62 MgC/ha), consistent with the dominance of *Avicennia germinans*, which develops extensive pneumatophore and fine cable root networks rather than the coarse prop root structures characteristic of *Rhizophora*.

**Table 8.2b: Below-ground carbon stock — Keta Lagoon Complex**

| Component | Mean ± S.E. (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fine root carbon (< 2 mm) | 0.28 ± 0.11 | 0.01 to 0.57 | 115.50 | 5,801 |
| Coarse root carbon (≥ 2 mm) | 5.40 ± 2.31 | -0.05 to 10.86 | 120.70 | 111,877 |

The pattern at Keta is the inverse of Songor: fine root carbon (0.28 MgC/ha) is far lower while coarse root carbon is substantially higher (5.40 MgC/ha). This reversal directly reflects the species composition difference. Keta is dominated by *Rhizophora racemosa*, which invests heavily in coarse prop-root and anchor-root structures; coarse root carbon was estimated using size-mass allometric models (Addo-Danso et al. 2016) with a carbon factor of 0.39. Fine root carbon at Keta was determined using the soil core method with oven-drying at 70°C, applying a root carbon concentration factor of 0.39 (Kauffman and Donato 2012). Both BGB pools show high spatial variability (CV: 116–121%), indicating that belowground carbon distributions are strongly influenced by local stand structure and disturbance history.

## 8.5 Soil organic carbon

**Table 8.3: Soil organic carbon — Songor Lagoon (0–30 cm)**

| Component | Mean ± S.E. (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SOC (0–30 cm) | 598.00 ± 49.33 | 471.20 to 724.80 | 30.62 | 278,524 |

Soil organic carbon is the dominant carbon pool at Songor, accounting for 93.6% of total measured ecosystem carbon. The relatively lower coefficient of variation for SOC (30.6%) compared to AGB (129%) and root carbon (119–137%) confirms that soil carbon is the more spatially stable pool. The Songor SOC density of 598 MgC/ha at 0–30 cm is within the upper range of global mangrove SOC values for this depth interval, comparable to values from Southeast Asian and Caribbean systems. If the standard global ratio of approximately 2 to 2.5 times the 0–30 cm value applies, total SOC to 1 metre depth at Songor could be in the range of 1,200 to 1,500 MgC/ha.

**Table 8.3b: Soil organic carbon — Keta Lagoon Complex (0–30 cm)**

| Component | Mean ± S.E. (MgC/ha) | 95% CI | CV (%) | Total storage (Mg) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SOC (0–30 cm) | 801.50 ± 119.50 | 519.00 to 1,084.00 | 42.16 | 16,605,437 |

Soil organic carbon at Keta totals 16,605,437 Mg — the single most consequential number in this inventory. SOC accounts for 97.5% of all carbon stored in the Keta system. The mean density of 801.50 MgC/ha exceeds Songor's 598 MgC/ha, consistent with the high clay content of the Keta Lagoon substrate (Finlayson et al. 2000), which supports strong positive clay–carbon associations documented in mangrove literature (Li et al. 2024). The Keta SOC estimate (16,605,437 Mg at 0–30 cm) is strikingly similar to a previous independent estimate of 16,833,091 Mg from the South Tongu District mangrove assessment (Addo-Danso and Amissah 2024), providing external validation of this assessment's results.

Laboratory analysis was conducted using a LECO CN628 Elemental Analyzer for total carbon concentration. Bulk density was determined from oven-dried subsamples at 105°C for 4 hours, with stone-free corrections applied following Kauffman and Donato (2012).

**Depth limitation and the two-tier logic:** SOC for this national baseline was sampled to 30 cm. The IPCC Wetlands Supplement recommends sampling to a minimum of 100 cm for mangrove soils, recognising that a substantial proportion of SOC is stored below the 30 cm horizon. The SOC values reported here are therefore conservative minimum estimates. The companion MRV Framework specifies that any project proponent conducting a carbon assessment for credit generation must sample SOC to 100 cm minimum, with depth intervals at 0–15, 15–30, 30–50, and 50–100 cm. The national baseline provides the reference; project-level assessments must extend to the full depth profile.

The Keta Lagoon Complex holds 17,026,633 Mg of total ecosystem carbon including grasses, or 16,850,323 Mg on an excl.-grass basis — one of the largest documented mangrove carbon stocks in West Africa. SOC at 0–30 cm alone (16,605,437 Mg) is approximately 55 times larger than the above-ground biomass pool. The conservation value of this landscape is overwhelmingly driven by soil carbon, which is mobilised by soil disturbance and drainage alteration rather than by above-ground canopy removal alone.

## 8.7 Deforestation, degradation, and the additionality context

The Keta Lagoon Complex experiences an annual deforestation rate of 2.15%, driven primarily by the harvesting of *Rhizophora racemosa* and *Rhizophora mangle* for commercial fish smoking. This rate is derived from multi-temporal analysis and corroborated by the Socio-Economic Baseline Report.

At this deforestation rate, Keta loses approximately 445 hectares of canopy cover per year. Using the measured AGB carbon density of 14.65 MgC/ha, this translates to an annual above-ground carbon emission of approximately 6,500 Mg of carbon from biomass loss alone. The more consequential emission pathway, however, is through SOC disturbance: at the measured SOC density of 801.50 MgC/ha, each year of unmitigated deforestation threatens to release on the order of 356,500 Mg of soil carbon — an annual carbon emission risk approximately 55 times larger than the above-ground loss alone. This asymmetry is the primary case for including SOC emissions in any Keta carbon finance project baseline, as required under Verra VM0033 and the IPCC Wetlands Supplement Tier 2.

At Songor, the degradation trajectory differs: salt pan expansion rather than fuelwood extraction is the primary driver, and the remaining 465.76 Ha represents a near-terminal landscape more appropriately framed as a restoration candidate than a conservation baseline.

Carbon permanence faces two categories of risk. Project-addressable risks include fuelwood harvesting (addressable through improved cookstove interventions), salt pan expansion (addressable through CREMA governance), and unsustainable aquaculture. Non-project structural risks include the Akosombo Dam sediment deficit, coastal erosion from sea-level rise, and extreme weather events. Any carbon project must account for both categories and may need to apply a permanence buffer (typically 10–20% of credits) for non-addressable reversal risk.

## 8.8 Uncertainty and quality treatment

Uncertainty in carbon stock estimates arises from allometric equation selection (can shift AGB by 15–30%), DBH and height measurement error, wood density database uncertainty, spatial variability within strata, SOC depth limitation (0–30 cm captures approximately 40–60% of the 0–100 cm total), and laboratory analytical precision.

The wide confidence intervals for AGB reflect high spatial heterogeneity rather than unreliable methodology. Coefficients of variation of 129–135% are within the range commonly reported for mangrove AGB inventories in heterogeneous landscapes. SOC shows substantially lower variability (Songor CV 30.6%; Keta CV 42.2%), confirming soil carbon as the more reliable basis for landscape-level stock estimation. The Keta SOC estimate has been independently validated against the Addo-Danso and Amissah (2024) South Tongu District assessment, which returned a nearly identical figure (16,833,091 Mg at 0–30 cm).

Allometric equations applied: generalised model from Kauffman and Donato (2012) for AGB primary estimates; Komiyama et al. (2008) and Adu-Bredu (2014) for Keta coarse root carbon; Addo-Danso et al. (2016) for size-mass coarse root allometry; species-specific allometric cross-check at 13.4 ± 5.04 MgC/ha for Keta AGB.

## 8.9 Carbon market readiness

This national baseline inventory establishes the four elements required for carbon market development: quantified stocks at two Ramsar sites across all four IPCC Tier 2 carbon pools, a documented deforestation baseline (2.15%/year at Keta), a validated field methodology, and species and structural data sufficient for project-level allometric equation selection.

However, a project proponent seeking Verra VM0033 or Gold Standard certification must go beyond this baseline: establishing Permanent Sample Plots (with tagged trees and documented subplot coordinates), sampling SOC to 100 cm minimum, applying and citing specific allometric equations, conducting formal uncertainty analysis, and establishing a community-owned re-measurement protocol at 3–5 year intervals within the MRV Framework. The national baseline is the proof of concept; the project-specific assessment is the market-ready instrument. The full readiness assessment and pathway recommendations are in Chapter 14 Section 14.2.4.
