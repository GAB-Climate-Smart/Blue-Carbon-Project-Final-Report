# Chapter 9. Socio-Economic Conditions and Drivers

## 9.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter establishes the human dimension of the blue carbon ecosystems at the four study landscapes. It reports the livelihood profile of the surrounding communities, the ways in which they depend on the mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems for food, fuel, income, and protection, and the gendered structure of that dependency. It also reports the principal direct drivers of ecosystem change that originate in human activity: fuelwood cutting, fish smoking, salt-pan and farmland conversion, pollution, and the slower-moving driver of demographic and urban pressure. The intent is not an ethnographic monograph; the intent is a decision-relevant evidence base that the priority ranking in Chapter 12 and the recommendations in Chapter 14 can act on.

## 9.2 Livelihood profile across the four sites

The communities surrounding the Keta Lagoon Complex, Songor Lagoon, and Muni-Pomadze are coastal subsistence and small-trade economies in which several livelihood activities are typically combined within a single household across the agricultural year. The dominant livelihood activities, in approximate order of household participation, are lagoon and inshore fishing, fish smoking and trading, salt harvesting (especially at Songor), small-scale crop farming, fuelwood collection, and petty trade. Migrant labour to Accra, Tema, and the cocoa belt is a recurring secondary income source for many households.

Three livelihood patterns are common across all three priority sites and are consequential for the management discussion later in the report. First, the livelihood portfolio is **diversified but shallow**: most households participate in multiple activities, but no single activity is sufficient for resilience against shocks, which means that pressure on the mangrove ecosystem cannot be reduced by substituting one activity for another in isolation. Second, the **fish smoking economy is the principal channel through which fuelwood demand reaches the mangrove**, because fish smoking is a high-volume, year-round activity that consumes large quantities of mangrove wood as the preferred fuel. Third, **women carry a disproportionate share of the fish smoking, salt processing, and fuelwood collection workloads**, which makes any intervention that changes fuelwood access or smoking practice a gendered intervention by definition.

## 9.3 Direct dependency on the mangrove ecosystem

Direct dependency takes five principal forms across the priority sites.

The first form is **fuelwood**. Mangrove wood, particularly *Rhizophora* species, is the preferred fuel for fish smoking because it burns hot, long, and clean. The Cookstove Interventions Baseline (Phase 2) quantifies the per-household and per-smoker consumption rates and identifies the cost-effective entry points for improved cookstove interventions that can reduce demand without disrupting the underlying livelihood.

The second form is **lagoon and channel fisheries**. The mangrove root systems function as nursery habitat for the juvenile stages of several commercially important species harvested in the lagoon. The fishery and the mangrove are not separable as economic categories; loss of mangrove translates over time into loss of fishery, and the loss is felt by the same households on both sides.

The third form is **salt harvesting**, which is the historical and continuing economic backbone of the Songor system. Salt-pan construction and operation has reshaped the hydrology of parts of the Songor Ramsar Site, and the management discussion in Chapter 12 returns to the trade-offs that this creates.

The fourth form is **building materials**, including poles for housing and fencing, drawn at smaller volumes than the fuelwood demand but persistent across all sites.

The fifth form is **non-timber products**, including medicinal plants and the food-system uses of mangrove-associated species. These are smaller in aggregate volume but are culturally significant and are part of the legitimacy case that any community-facing intervention must respect.

## 9.4 Indirect dependency: storm surge, erosion, and the regulating services

Beyond the direct extractive uses, the surrounding communities depend on the mangrove ecosystem for the regulating services that buffer the coast against storm surge, salt-water intrusion into freshwater wells, and shoreline erosion. These services are not metered, are not paid for, and are typically taken for granted until they are lost. They are nonetheless the principal reason the WACA programme exists: the alternative to maintaining the natural buffer is investing in much more expensive engineered coastal defences.

The regulating service value is not separately quantified in this Final Report. It is named here because the cost-benefit analysis in Chapter 12 §12.5 carries a regulating service line that draws on the WACA programme's avoided-damage estimates and the Ghana coastal vulnerability literature.

## 9.5 The gendered structure of dependency

The gendered structure of dependency is not a side note. It is a first-order determinant of whether any management intervention will hold over time, because the people who interact with the mangrove on a daily basis are not the people who typically sit at the formal management table.

Women dominate the fish smoking, fuelwood collection, and salt processing labour at all three priority sites. They are also the primary food security actors in the household. The introduction of improved cookstoves (the focus of the Cookstove Interventions Baseline) is a gendered intervention because it changes the daily labour pattern of the women who do the smoking. Restoration interventions that restrict access to mangrove wood are similarly gendered, and unless they offer a substitute fuel pathway they are likely to fail or to displace the pressure to a different stretch of mangrove.

Men dominate the inshore and lagoon fishing crews, the salt-pan construction labour, and the migrant labour streams. Decisions about fishing gear, fishing days, and access to the lagoon are typically made through the men's chief fisherman institutions at each site, which are recognised under customary law and which Chapter 10 picks up in the governance discussion.

Two implications follow for the recommendations in Chapter 14. First, any community-facing intervention must engage both the women's and the men's institutional channels at each site, not one as a proxy for the other. Second, the Community Change Agent (CCA) network described in Chapter 11 is designed with gender-balanced representation as a non-negotiable structural feature, not a reporting line item.

## 9.6 The cookstove pathway

The Cookstove Interventions Baseline (Phase 2) is the most operational of the socio-economic standalones because it identifies a defined intervention with a measurable demand-reduction logic. The baseline finds that a high proportion of fish smokers at the priority sites currently use traditional smoking ovens that are inefficient by an order of magnitude relative to commercially available improved alternatives. The baseline names the unit cost, the willingness-to-pay envelope, the distribution channels, and the supply-side capacity that an improved cookstove rollout would need.

The cookstove pathway is one of the levers that the recommendations in Chapter 14 prioritise. It is not a substitute for mangrove protection, restoration, or enforcement, but it is the most cost-effective single demand-side intervention available to the assignment, and it has the additional advantage of being a gender-positive intervention by design.

## 9.7 Direct drivers of ecosystem change in one paragraph

The direct drivers of ecosystem change at the priority sites are, in approximate order of magnitude, fuelwood cutting for fish smoking, clearance for salt-pan and farmland conversion, pollution from urban runoff and aquaculture effluent (most acute at Muni-Pomadze given its peri-urban setting), unregulated sand and clay mining at smaller spatial scales, and the slower-moving driver of population growth and coastal urbanisation. Climate-related drivers (sea level rise, changes in tidal regime, shifts in storm intensity) are real but operate on a longer timescale than the assignment horizon and are taken up explicitly in Chapter 11 as the rationale for continuous monitoring rather than as a baseline-period driver.

## 9.8 Bridge to Chapter 10

The socio-economic dependency picture established here is necessary but not sufficient for designing a management response. It tells us who depends on the ecosystem and how, but it does not tell us who has the legal and customary right to manage it. Chapter 10 takes that question up, delivers the tenure split mandated by the Terms of Reference, and sets out the institutional landscape against which the recommendations in Chapter 14 will be drafted.
