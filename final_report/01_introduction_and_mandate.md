# Chapter 1. Introduction and Mandate

## 1.1 Why this report exists

Ghana's coastal wetlands hold one of the densest and most underestimated carbon assets in West Africa. The Keta Lagoon Complex alone contains 20,717.95 hectares of mangrove cover, with the Songor system, the Muni-Pomadze system, and the Ada estuary adding further hectares of intact, degraded, and restorable wetland under the Ramsar Convention's national obligations. These ecosystems sequester atmospheric carbon at rates that exceed terrestrial forests by an order of magnitude, they buffer the coastline against the storm surge and erosion that the West Africa Coastal Areas (WACA) programme was set up to address, and they sustain the fisheries, fuelwood economies, and salt production systems on which tens of thousands of households depend. Until now, Ghana has not had an integrated, evidence-grounded baseline that quantifies these systems at the resolution required for management decisions, climate finance access, or international reporting against the country's Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs).

This report closes that gap. It is the consolidated output of the assignment titled *Assessment of Blue Carbon Ecosystem Potentials of Ghana (Mangrove and Tidal Salt Marsh)*, executed under Contract ID GR-MESTI-441726-CS-CQS within the World Bank's WACA Resilience Investment Project Phase II (WACA ResIP II, P175525). The assignment was commissioned by the Ministry of Environment, Science, Technology and Innovation (MESTI), with technical oversight by the Ministry of Lands and Natural Resources (MLNR), and was delivered in seven phases over the contract term. This Final Report is the deliverable for Phase 7 (assignment completion).

## 1.2 What the Terms of Reference require

The Terms of Reference issued by MLNR / MESTI (RFP No. GR-MESTI-441726 CS-CQS) define the Phase 7 deliverable in unambiguous strategic terms. The Final Report must:

1. consolidate the findings of all previous phases into a single decision-oriented document;
2. establish the percentage split of blue carbon ecosystems falling within state-owned land, formal protected areas, private lands, and communal lands, and use that tenure analysis to inform the protection approach;
3. provide policy and management advice on protection, rehabilitation, and expansion of the different blue carbon ecosystems investigated;
4. include a cost-benefit analysis of the proposed implementation;
5. rank each blue carbon ecosystem by priority from highest to lowest, with the ranking criteria explicitly stated;
6. provide a master spatial table that lists each priority site with the recommended interventions, the implementation timeframe (short, medium, long-term), and the possible implementation partners.

The output that the Terms of Reference name is, in the language of the contract, "a Final strategic document containing policy, management advice, and a table with spatial locations where interventions can be implemented, level of priority, and possible implementation partners." This Final Report meets that standard. Chapter 12 carries the priority ranking, the criteria, and the cost-benefit analysis. Chapter 13 carries the spatial intervention table. Chapter 10 carries the tenure analysis. Each ToR requirement is mapped to its delivering chapter in Annex A.

## 1.3 What this report is, and what it is not

This Final Report is:

- a synthesis document that integrates seven phases of evidence into one coherent narrative for decision-makers;
- a baseline reference that quantifies extent, condition, carbon stock, socio-economic dependency, governance setting, and pressure intensity for each priority site;
- a strategic instrument that translates that baseline into ranked priorities, costed interventions, and an implementation calendar;
- a foundation for the continuous monitoring system (drone, satellite, MRV) that will outlast the consultancy and feed Ghana's NDC reporting cycle.

This Final Report is not:

- a replacement for the four standalone technical reports that sit beneath it. The Ecosystem Health Report, the National MRV Framework Report, the Drone Survey and EOSDA Continuous Monitoring Report, and the Carbon Inventory and Stock Assessment Report each remain the authoritative reference for their respective technical detail. This Final Report distils them into management-ready evidence and points the reader to the standalones for plot-level data, station registers, and full methodological annexes.
- a project implementation document. It identifies what should be done, where, by when, and with whom, but it does not substitute for the project preparation, safeguards screening, and procurement that each prioritised intervention will require.
- a static deliverable. The monitoring architecture described in Part III of this report is designed to keep the baseline current. The findings here are the starting point of an active management cycle, not the closing position of a one-off study.

## 1.4 Who this report is for

The audience is deliberately mixed because the decisions that flow from the findings sit across institutions. The report is written so that a senior policy reader, a technical reviewer, and a community-facing implementer can each find what they need without having to read every chapter.

| Audience | What they will use this report for |
|---|---|
| Government of Ghana (MLNR, MESTI, Forestry Commission, Wildlife Division, Fisheries Commission) | National baseline for NDC reporting, Ramsar Article 3.2 tracking, and the integration of blue carbon into the National Adaptation Plan, the National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP), and the National REDD+ Strategy. |
| World Bank WACA ResIP II programme | Evidence base for the Phase 4 carbon-focused interventions, the restoration consultancy ToR that follows this assignment, and the WACA results framework. |
| Civil society and community stakeholders | Transparent record of where their landscapes sit on the priority ranking, what interventions are recommended in their area, and how the Community Change Agent (CCA) network connects local monitoring to district and national reporting. |
| Development partners and climate finance institutions | Investment-ready evidence on carbon stocks, deforestation baselines, additionality context, and project boundary readiness for VCS / VM0033 alignment. |
| Academic and technical reviewers | Methodologically traceable baseline, with every quantitative claim citing the originating standalone report and section. |

## 1.5 Scope of evidence and known constraints

The Final Report draws on a defined evidence package built across the seven phases of the assignment. The four standalone technical reports listed above are the primary evidence base. They are complemented by a literature review (Phase 2), the geospatial extent baseline (Phase 3), socio-economic and gender baselines, the governance and CREMA readiness assessment, the cookstove interventions baseline, the plastics and pollution review, the environmental pressures and community dependency analysis, the livelihood diversification assessment, and the risk assessment and safeguards register. Each source is listed in Annex D with its file path in the project repository.

One constraint should be stated in front, not buried in a footnote. The tenure split analysis in Chapter 10 relies on the most recent cadastral and protected-area registers obtainable at the time of writing. Where a parcel could not be definitively classified, the report flags it as "tenure unresolved" rather than imputing a category. The Carbon Inventory Report has been fully integrated (April 22 2026), with both sites complete across all four carbon pools; no carbon data gap remains.

## 1.6 How to read this report

The report is divided into four parts and fourteen chapters, plus front matter, an executive summary, and six annexes. Each part has a single job.

**Part I (Foundations, Chapters 1 to 3)** explains why the report exists, the strategic and policy environment that frames it, and the objectives, scope, and adaptive approach used during execution.

**Part II (The Evidence Base, Chapters 4 to 10)** presents the integrated baseline. Chapter 4 sets out the methodology overview. Chapter 5 establishes the study areas and the national coastal context. Chapters 6 to 10 then walk through the spatial baseline, the ecosystem health condition, the carbon stock inventory, the socio-economic conditions, and the tenure and governance landscape. These chapters present the evidence; they do not yet make recommendations.

**Part III (Continuous Monitoring Architecture, Chapter 11)** describes how the baseline will be kept current. It consolidates the drone survey, the EOSDA satellite monitoring system, and the National MRV Framework into a single operational story so that the reader can see how a CCA's quarterly reading, a Sentinel-2 alert, and a national MRV submission connect.

**Part IV (Strategic Direction, Chapters 12 to 14)** is the decision-support core. Chapter 12 carries the integrated synthesis, the priority ranking with criteria, and the cost-benefit analysis. Chapter 13 carries the spatial intervention plan with the master table required by the Terms of Reference. Chapter 14 closes with the recommendations, the next steps, and the conclusion. A reader who has time only for the strategic section can read Part IV and the executive summary and still leave with the full set of decisions and the evidence trail to support them.

The annexes carry the cross-walk to the ToR, the detailed methods, the data tables that would otherwise interrupt the narrative, the source register, the carbon insertion register, and the acronyms list.

## 1.7 A note on language and editorial standards

This report is written for decision-makers. Sentences are short. Numbers are named, not summarised. Sites are named, not aggregated. Where a finding is uncertain, the uncertainty is stated. Where a recommendation depends on a future input, the dependency is stated. The report does not use the em dash. It does not repeat content across chapters; each finding lives in one chapter, and other chapters cross-reference. Every quantitative claim cites the standalone report and section it is drawn from. The intent is a document that a Minister can read to take a decision, that a technical reviewer can audit line by line, and that a community partner can use to see what the project means for their landscape.
