# Chapter 13. Spatial Intervention Plan

## 13.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter delivers the master spatial intervention table that the Terms of Reference name as the principal Final Report deliverable. The Phase 7 ToR requires "a table with spatial locations where interventions can be implemented, level of priority, and possible implementation partners." This chapter provides that table, with the implementation timeframe (short, medium, long-term) added as a fourth column on the basis of the priority ranking and CBA in Chapter 12.

The chapter also explains the intervention typology used in the table, the timeframe definitions, and the partnering logic, so that the table can be read without ambiguity by any of the audience groups named in Chapter 1 §1.4.

## 13.2 Intervention typology

Five intervention types are used in the master table. They are defined here so that the table cells can be read against a consistent vocabulary.

**Protection** means the formal designation, demarcation, and operational safeguarding of an intact or fair-condition cluster. In practice this combines a CREMA arrangement (Chapter 10) with monitoring coverage (Chapter 11) and an enforcement channel through the Wildlife Division and the relevant district assembly. Protection is the highest-leverage and lowest-risk intervention type.

**Assisted natural regeneration (ANR)** means the removal of barriers to natural mangrove recolonisation at degraded clusters where the propagule supply and the hydrological setting still support recovery. ANR is cheaper than active replanting and is the preferred restoration mode where the site conditions support it.

**Active replanting** means the propagation, transplanting, and establishment of mangrove seedlings at clusters where natural regeneration is not viable. Active replanting is more expensive per hectare than ANR and carries higher early-year mortality risk; it is used only where the site conditions require it.

**Demand-side livelihood intervention** means the rollout of improved cookstoves, alternative fuels, fish-smoking efficiency support, and the paired livelihood diversification activities that reduce extractive pressure on the mangrove. The cookstove pathway is the most operationally tractable component and is the lever that pairs with restoration in the recommendations.

**Hydrological reconnection** means the engineering and management work needed to restore tidal exchange at clusters where channelisation, salt-pan construction, or other historical modification has cut off the natural hydrology. This is the most uncertain and most site-specific intervention type and is named in the table only at clusters where Phase 6 site assessment supports it.

## 13.3 Timeframe definitions

The timeframe column uses three bands consistent with standard project planning practice.

**Short term** means 0 to 18 months from the adoption of this Final Report. Short-term interventions are those that can be initiated with the institutional and financing arrangements that already exist around the WACA ResIP II programme and the assignment's standalone outputs.

**Medium term** means 18 to 48 months. Medium-term interventions require an institutional or financing decision that has not yet been taken (for example, the establishment of a Verra-aligned crediting project or the formal CREMA gazetting at clusters where readiness is currently moderate).

**Long term** means 48 months and beyond. Long-term interventions are conditional on outcomes from the short and medium-term phases (for example, expansion of protection coverage to additional sites once the priority sites are stabilised).

The Carbon Inventory Report provides the basis for a carbon classification overlay on the master table. Both assessed sites fall in the "high" carbon density band: Songor at 638.64 MgC/ha total ecosystem carbon, and Keta at a minimum of 34.57 MgC/ha AGB (with total density expected to be comparable to Songor when belowground results are received). The carbon classification confirms rather than changes the priority ranking: the Tier 1 Keta clusters hold the largest absolute carbon stock at risk due to the combination of high density and large area, while the Tier 2 Songor clusters hold high per-hectare density in a much smaller area. All Tier 1 and Tier 2 clusters are classified as "high carbon" for intervention prioritisation purposes.

## 13.5 How to read the master table

Three reading conventions apply.

First, the **priority tier** is the band from the Chapter 12 ranking and is the primary sequencing variable. The table reads from highest priority (Tier 1) to lowest (Tier 5), with the comparison-system row for Ada at the bottom.

Second, the **intervention type** is drawn from the typology in §13.2. Where two intervention types are paired in a single cell (for example, "Protection + cookstove rollout"), the pairing is intentional and reflects the integrated logic from Chapter 12: protection of high-condition clusters is durable only if the demand-side pressure is reduced in parallel.

Third, the **lead implementation partner** is the institution that holds the operational lead, not the only institution involved. The supporting partners column names the additional institutions whose participation is required for the intervention to hold. The institutional crosswalk in Chapter 10 §10.5 supports the partner naming and explains the role of each institution.

## 13.6 Sequencing logic

The sequencing logic that runs through the table is straightforward. **Protect first, restore second, expand third.** Tier 1 and Tier 2 protection arrangements are sequenced into the short-term column because the rising pressure trajectory at Keta erodes the asset that the rest of the strategy depends on. Tier 3 restoration is sequenced into the medium-term column because it depends on the Tier 1 and Tier 2 protection arrangements being in place first. Hydrological reconnection and expansion are sequenced into the medium and long-term columns because they require site-specific feasibility work that has not yet been completed across all candidate clusters.

This sequencing is not arbitrary; it reflects the cost-benefit finding in Chapter 12 §12.5 that protection of high-condition clusters is the most favourable benefit-cost intervention category and that restoration is sensitive to the same pressure trajectory that produced the degradation.
