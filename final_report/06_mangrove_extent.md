# Chapter 6. Mangrove Extent and Spatial Baseline

## 6.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter establishes the spatial baseline against which every other quantitative chapter in Part II is anchored. It reports the area of mangrove and tidal salt marsh at each of the four study landscapes, sets out the cluster-level subdivisions used by the carbon, ecological, and socio-economic strands, and names the change-detection findings where historical imagery supports them. It does not report carbon stock (that is Chapter 8), ecological condition (Chapter 7), or socio-economic dependency (Chapter 9). It is the spatial frame on which those subsequent chapters hang.

## 6.2 Approach in brief

The spatial baseline integrates three data layers. Sentinel-2 imagery at 10 metre resolution provides the regional mapping. PlanetScope and high-resolution drone orthomosaics provide the validation and the cluster-scale measurement. Local ground-truth points collected by field teams and CCAs anchor the supervised classification. The full classification protocol, accuracy assessment, and confusion matrix are in the Geospatial Baseline Report (Phase 3); the methodological summary appears in Chapter 4 of this Final Report and is not repeated here.

## 6.3 Mangrove area at the four landscapes

The headline area finding for Keta has been named at several points already and is reported here as the canonical figure. The Keta Lagoon Complex contains **20,717.95 hectares** of mangrove cover at the most recent measurement (Geospatial Baseline, Phase 3). This is the single largest contiguous mangrove area within the assignment scope.

Songor mangrove cover is concentrated along the northern and eastern fringes of the lagoon and along the channels that connect the lagoon to the lower Volta. Muni-Pomadze mangrove cover is a narrow fringe along the lagoon margin, sharply bounded by the urban edge of Winneba. The Ada estuary mangrove cover is mixed estuarine fringe along the lower Volta. The detailed per-site, per-class area tables produced by Phase 3 are in the Geospatial Baseline Report; the headline numbers are reproduced in Annex B of this Final Report so that the priority ranking in Chapter 12 can be retraced without leaving the document.

The reader should note one editorial point. This Final Report does not multiply estimates of total area by ecological condition or carbon density at this stage. Those compound numbers belong in the synthesis chapter (Chapter 12), where they can be reported with the uncertainty propagation that they require.

## 6.4 Cluster delineation

Within each priority site, the landscape is subdivided into clusters that serve as the operational unit for field sampling, intervention planning, and monitoring. A cluster is defined as a contiguous block of mangrove or tidal salt marsh that shares a coherent ecological setting, a coherent tenure profile, and a single dominant pressure regime. The clusters are named after the closest settlement or geographic feature (for example, Anyanui-Dzita at Keta).

Cluster delineation matters for three downstream uses. First, the ecological condition baseline in Chapter 7 reports water quality and vegetation parameters at the cluster level. Second, the carbon inventory in Chapter 8 will report stock estimates at the cluster level. Third, the spatial intervention plan in Chapter 13 names interventions against named clusters rather than against whole sites, so that implementation partners can act on a defined geographic unit.

The full cluster register, with coordinates, area, and the standalone reports that reference each cluster, is in Annex B.

## 6.5 Change detection where historical data supports it

Change detection is feasible only where a historical imagery archive of sufficient quality exists. For Keta, the Landsat archive supports a multi-decadal change analysis that the Phase 3 baseline used to derive the deforestation rate reported in Chapter 8. For Songor and Muni-Pomadze, the historical archive is patchier and the change analysis is bounded to the period for which Sentinel-2 imagery is available (2015 onwards). For Ada, the comparison-system status means change analysis is not a primary deliverable.

The Keta deforestation finding is the spatial baseline's single most consequential result for the carbon-finance pathway, because it establishes the additionality context that any future protection-based crediting project will need. The full series and the per-period rate are reported in Chapter 8 §8.3, where they sit alongside the carbon stock numbers that turn the spatial loss into a quantified emission.

## 6.6 What the spatial baseline does not yet tell us

The spatial baseline tells us where the mangroves are and how much area they occupy. It does not tell us how healthy they are, how much carbon they hold, who lives next to them, or who has the legal right to manage them. Each of those questions is taken up in the next four chapters in turn. The spatial frame established here is the common reference that links them.

## 6.7 Bridge to Chapter 7

Chapter 7 takes the cluster delineation established here and reports the ecological condition of each cluster against the parameter set defined in Chapter 4. It is the first chapter in which condition, rather than extent, becomes the primary measurement.
