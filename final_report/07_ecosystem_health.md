# Chapter 7. Ecosystem Health Baseline

## 7.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter establishes the ecological condition, or health, of the mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems at the four study landscapes. It is the management-facing distillation of the Ecosystem Health Standalone Report finalised in April 2026. The standalone report carries the full station register, the parameter-by-parameter readings, and the appendix tables; this chapter carries the cluster-level conclusions that the priority ranking in Chapter 12 will draw on. It also carries the baseline ecological character measurement that operationalises Ghana's Ramsar Article 3.2 reporting obligation, a connection that Chapter 11 picks up for the continuous monitoring system.

## 7.2 The condition framework

Ecological condition in this assignment is assessed against three layered measurement domains. The first is the **physico-chemical water quality** domain, which captures the abiotic envelope within which the mangrove and salt marsh ecosystems function. The second is the **vegetation structure** domain, which captures the standing condition of the mangrove forest itself. The third is the **biological indicator** domain, which captures the macrofaunal and avifaunal signatures that reflect ecosystem function.

Each domain has its own parameter set, its own measurement protocol, and its own reference range. The reference ranges are taken from the Ramsar Strategic Framework, the IPCC Wetlands Supplement, and the comparable West African mangrove literature. Where a parameter falls inside the reference range it is reported as within ecological character. Where it falls outside, it is reported as a deviation and flagged for the management discussion in Chapter 12.

## 7.3 Physico-chemical water quality

The water quality parameter set covers dissolved oxygen, pH, salinity, electrical conductivity, total dissolved solids, oxidation-reduction potential, temperature, turbidity, and a nutrient panel covering nitrate, nitrite, ammonium, and phosphate. Stations are distributed across each cluster on a stratified design that captures the seaward edge, the landward edge, and the channel interior. Sampling was conducted across both wet and dry season campaigns so that the seasonal envelope can be characterised, not just a single snapshot.

The headline finding from the water quality strand is that **dissolved oxygen and salinity are the two parameters that distinguish the four landscapes most sharply**. Keta exhibits the widest seasonal swing in salinity, reflecting its restricted sea connection and its hypersaline dry-season regime. Songor sits at consistently higher salinity than Keta, reflecting the dual pressure of its salt-winning history and its shallower water column. Muni-Pomadze exhibits the highest variability in dissolved oxygen at the cluster level, reflecting both its peri-urban pressure profile and the input of organic loading from the adjacent Winneba urban area. The Ada estuary sits within the expected estuarine envelope on most parameters and is not flagged for ecological-character deviation.

Cluster-level deviations from reference range, with the implications for management, are tabulated in Annex B and reported against priority in Chapter 12. The full per-station readings are in the Ecosystem Health Standalone Report (Appendix A).

## 7.4 Vegetation structure

Vegetation structure is measured through plot-based species composition, basal area, canopy cover, regeneration density, and pneumatophore density at *Avicennia*-dominated stations. The plot design is consistent with the carbon stock plot design (Chapter 8), so that ecological structure measurements and carbon stock measurements can be cross-walked at the same plot location.

The headline finding from the vegetation structure strand is that **regeneration density is the most actionable indicator for management purposes**. Where regeneration density is high, restoration or assisted natural regeneration interventions are likely to be cost-effective. Where regeneration density is low, even in the absence of acute deforestation pressure, the system is not self-renewing and active replanting becomes necessary. The cluster-level regeneration map underpinning this finding is in the Ecosystem Health Standalone Report and is summarised at the cluster level in Annex B.

A second vegetation structure finding worth naming here is that **species composition is shifting at Keta in a direction consistent with chronic disturbance**. The proportion of *Avicennia germinans* relative to *Rhizophora racemosa* is higher at clusters with a history of fuelwood cutting, because *Avicennia* recovers more readily under the disturbance regime. This is not a positive ecological signal even though it preserves some mangrove cover, and Chapter 12 returns to it in the priority ranking discussion.

## 7.5 Biological indicators

The biological indicator strand covers macrofaunal indicators (benthic invertebrate community composition), avifaunal counts (especially the wading and migratory bird populations that are central to the Ramsar listing rationale), and where data exist, juvenile fish density in the lagoon channels. The full indicator set is reported in the Ecosystem Health Standalone Report.

The headline finding from the biological indicator strand is that **the avifaunal populations remain consistent with the Ramsar listing rationale at all three priority sites**, but that the benthic invertebrate community at the most pressured Keta clusters shows signs of community simplification consistent with chronic organic loading and fuelwood disturbance.

## 7.6 Ecological character: the Ramsar Article 3.2 anchor

The Ramsar Convention requires Parties to maintain the ecological character of designated Ramsar Sites and to report any change in that character to the Ramsar Secretariat. Ghana has historically reported on an ad hoc basis because the underlying baseline measurement has not existed at the resolution required. The Ecosystem Health Standalone Report changes that. The composite of physico-chemical, vegetation structure, and biological indicator measurements at the cluster level constitutes a defensible baseline ecological character measurement for each of the three priority Ramsar Sites covered by this assignment.

Two operational consequences follow. First, future Article 3.2 reporting can be made against this baseline rather than against an unstated implicit reference. Second, the continuous monitoring system described in Chapter 11 is the mechanism by which any change in ecological character will be detected on a quarterly cycle, replacing the ad hoc regime that has constrained Ghana's Ramsar reporting to date.

## 7.7 Cluster-level condition summary

The cluster-level summary table that the priority ranking in Chapter 12 will draw on is held in Annex B rather than reproduced here, in order to preserve the editorial rule that each fact lives in one place. The reader should know two things about it. First, it classifies each cluster on a four-band condition score (good, fair, degraded, severely degraded) derived from the composite of the parameters described above. Second, the condition score is one of the explicit criteria in the Chapter 12 priority ranking, alongside carbon stock weighting (now populated with measured densities from the Carbon Inventory Report), socio-economic dependency, and tenure feasibility.

## 7.8 What the ecological baseline does not yet tell us

The ecological baseline tells us how healthy the systems are at the cluster level and where the deviations from reference range sit. It does not tell us how much carbon those systems hold (Chapter 8), who depends on them (Chapter 9), or who has the legal right to manage them (Chapter 10). Those three chapters complete the evidence base.
