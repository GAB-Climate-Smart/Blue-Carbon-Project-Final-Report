# Chapter 5. Study Areas and National Coastal Context

## 5.1 Ghana's coast in one paragraph

Ghana's coastline runs for approximately 550 kilometres along the Gulf of Guinea, from the Côte d'Ivoire border in the west to the Togolese border in the east. It is a low-lying, sediment-rich coast shaped by the Volta River discharge in the east, the longshore drift that moves sand westward, and the seasonal upwelling regime that drives the country's marine fisheries. Six Ramsar Sites sit along this coast, of which five contain mangrove or tidal salt marsh ecosystems within scope of the global blue carbon literature. Four landscapes are addressed in this assignment: the Keta Lagoon Complex, the Songor Lagoon, Muni-Pomadze, and the Ada estuary as a comparison system. Their selection rationale is set out in Chapter 3 and is not repeated here.

## 5.2 The Keta Lagoon Complex

The Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site occupies the south-eastern corner of the Volta Region, between the Volta River mouth at Ada and the Togolese border. It is the largest coastal lagoon in Ghana and the largest of the four landscapes covered by this assignment. The lagoon is shallow, hypersaline in the dry season, and connected intermittently to the sea through a narrow channel near the town of Keta. Its mangrove cover totals 20,717.95 hectares at the most recent measurement (Geospatial Baseline, Phase 3), distributed across a mosaic of intact stands, degraded fringes, and recently cleared parcels.

Three features of the Keta system are consequential for everything that follows in this Final Report. First, the dominant species are *Rhizophora racemosa* on the seaward edge and *Avicennia germinans* on the more landward, hypersaline flats. Second, the lagoon is bounded by densely settled communities whose livelihoods depend on lagoon fisheries, salt harvesting, fuelwood collection, and, increasingly, smallholder vegetable farming on reclaimed land. Third, the historical rate of mangrove loss at Keta is the highest of the three priority sites, driven primarily by fuelwood cutting for fish smoking and by clearance for salt-pan and farmland conversion. The deforestation analysis in Chapter 8 quantifies this rate against the historical Landsat archive.

The Keta complex is the site at which the protection-based carbon-finance opportunity is largest, the tenure picture is most complex, and the socio-economic dependency is most acute. Each of those statements is supported with the underlying numbers in the chapter that delivers it (Chapters 8, 10, and 9 respectively).

## 5.3 The Songor Lagoon

The Songor Lagoon Ramsar Site, located in the Ada East District of the Greater Accra Region, sits immediately west of the Volta River mouth. It is also designated as a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, which gives it a dual international standing that no other site in this assignment carries. Songor is shallower and more hypersaline than Keta and is the historical centre of Ghana's traditional salt-winning industry. Its mangrove cover is concentrated along the northern and eastern fringes of the lagoon and along the channels that connect the lagoon to the Volta.

The dual designation matters operationally. The Ramsar listing imposes the Article 3.2 reporting obligation that the continuous monitoring system in Chapter 11 is designed to operationalise. The Biosphere Reserve designation imposes a separate UNESCO reporting cycle and a separate zoning logic (core, buffer, transition). The two designations are not always perfectly aligned at the parcel level, and the tenure analysis in Chapter 10 returns to the practical consequences of that misalignment.

The dominant species at Songor are similar to Keta (*Rhizophora racemosa* and *Avicennia germinans*), but the relative abundance shifts toward *Avicennia* because of the higher salinity of much of the lagoon margin. The condition picture in Chapter 7 reflects this difference.

## 5.4 Muni-Pomadze

The Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site sits in the Effutu Municipal District of the Central Region, immediately adjacent to the town of Winneba. It is the smallest of the three priority sites by area and the most peri-urban in character. The lagoon is fed by the Muni and Pomadze streams, has a narrow and intermittent connection to the sea, and supports a fringe of mangrove that is best understood as the most western expression of the lagoon-fringe mangrove system that runs east through Songor and Keta.

The site is smaller than Keta or Songor but no less important for two reasons. First, it sits inside the active footprint of a growing coastal town, which means the urban pressure profile is sharper than at the larger sites. Second, the governance configuration involves the Effutu Traditional Council in a way that is materially different from the chiefdoms surrounding Keta or Songor, and the tenure analysis in Chapter 10 reports on the implications.

## 5.5 The Ada estuary

The Ada estuary, comprising the lower Volta and the wetlands at Ada Foah immediately west of the Volta River mouth, is included in this assignment as a comparison system rather than a priority intervention site. It anchors the eastern boundary of the study area, hosts several of the continuous monitoring polygons established under Phase 6, and provides an estuarine reference point against which the more lagoon-dominated systems at Keta, Songor, and Muni-Pomadze can be contextualised. Findings for Ada appear in the relevant Part II chapters but are not weighted into the Phase 7 priority ranking in Chapter 12.

## 5.7 Why these four sites, in one sentence

Keta is the largest blue carbon opportunity, Songor is the most internationally exposed through its dual designation, Muni-Pomadze is the most peri-urban and therefore the test case for managing wetlands inside a growing coastal town, and Ada is the comparison anchor that lets the other three be read in context.
