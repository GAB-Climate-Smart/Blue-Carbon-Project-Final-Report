# Chapter 3. Objectives, Scope, and Adaptive Approach

## 3.1 The objectives the assignment was set to meet

The Terms of Reference (RFP No. GR-MESTI-441726 CS-CQS) frame the assignment around a single overarching objective: to assess the blue carbon ecosystem potential of Ghana's mangroves and tidal salt marshes in a way that supports national climate, biodiversity, and coastal resilience commitments, and to translate that assessment into actionable management direction. That overarching objective decomposes into six specific objectives, each of which has driven the design of one or more phases of the work.

The first specific objective is to establish the spatial extent of mangrove and tidal salt marsh ecosystems at the priority sites, using a mix of high-resolution drone survey and freely available satellite imagery, at a precision sufficient to support intervention planning at the cluster level.

The second is to characterise the ecological condition, or health, of those ecosystems against measurable parameters that can be monitored over time, so that change in ecological character can be detected and reported under Ghana's Ramsar Article 3.2 obligations.

The third is to quantify the carbon stocks held in the above-ground biomass, below-ground biomass, deadwood, and soil organic carbon pools, against IPCC Tier 2 standards and Verra VM0033 v2.1 methodological requirements.

The fourth is to characterise the socio-economic dependency of coastal communities on these ecosystems, including the gendered dimensions of access, use, and benefit, so that any management or carbon-finance pathway designed on the back of this baseline can be made fair, durable, and locally legitimate.

The fifth is to map the tenure and governance landscape across the priority sites, so that the percentage split of blue carbon ecosystems falling within state-owned land, formal protected areas, private land, and communal land can be quantified and used to design protection arrangements that match the legal and customary reality on the ground.

The sixth is to design and operationalise a continuous monitoring system, integrating drone, satellite, and community-based observation, that will outlast the assignment and feed Ghana's national MRV reporting cycle.

These six objectives drive the structure of Parts II and III of this Final Report. Part IV then translates the resulting evidence base into the strategic outputs required by the Phase 7 deliverable.

## 3.2 Geographic scope

The assignment covers four coastal landscapes along Ghana's eastern and central coast. Three are designated Ramsar Sites at which commercial-scale carbon, biodiversity, and livelihood pressures coincide most acutely; the fourth is included as a comparison system that anchors the eastern boundary of the study area.

The **Keta Lagoon Complex Ramsar Site** in the Volta Region is the largest of the three priority sites and the location of the most extensive mangrove cover quantified by this assignment. The Keta complex contains 20,717.95 hectares of mangrove cover at the most recent measurement (Geospatial Baseline, Phase 3). It is also the site at which deforestation pressure has been most acute over the past decade and at which the carbon-related opportunity for protection-based crediting is therefore the largest.

The **Songor Lagoon Ramsar Site and UNESCO Biosphere Reserve** in the Greater Accra Region is the second priority site. Songor is distinguished by its dual designation, by the salt-pan economy that has historically reshaped its hydrology, and by its proximity to the Densu and Volta deltaic systems. Its mangrove and salt marsh ecosystems sit at the interface of one of Ghana's most active coastal economies.

The **Muni-Pomadze Ramsar Site** in the Central Region is the third priority site and the smallest of the three by area. It is included because it represents a different ecological and socio-economic profile to Keta and Songor: smaller mangrove fringes, a tighter relationship between the wetland and the adjacent town of Winneba, and a different governance configuration with respect to the Effutu Traditional Council.

The **Ada estuary**, comprising the lower Volta and the Songor-adjacent wetlands at Ada Foah, is included as a comparison system rather than a priority intervention site. Ada anchors the eastern boundary of the study area, provides a contrast point for the Keta and Songor measurements, and is the location at which several of the continuous monitoring polygons established under Phase 6 sit (Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring Report, §3).

The geographic scope deliberately excludes the high-mangrove systems of the western Greater Amanzule wetlands. Greater Amanzule is recognised as a strategically important blue carbon system in the western Ghana coastal zone but was outside the contractual scope of this assignment. Chapter 14 of this Final Report identifies Greater Amanzule as the most strategically important next site for follow-on assessment.

## 3.3 Thematic scope and what is deliberately out of scope

The assignment covers six thematic strands: spatial extent, ecological condition, carbon stocks, socio-economic dependency, tenure and governance, and continuous monitoring. Each strand is addressed in its dedicated chapter in Parts II and III. Two thematic boundaries should be named explicitly because they shape what the reader should and should not expect to find in this Final Report.

Seagrass ecosystems are in scope of the global blue carbon literature but are not in scope of this assignment. Ghana's coast does not host extensive seagrass meadows, and the Terms of Reference are explicit that the focus is mangroves and tidal salt marshes. References to seagrass appear only where the methodological literature being cited treats the three habitats together (notably in VM0033 v2.1).

Marine fisheries assessment is also out of scope. The lagoon and estuarine fisheries that depend on the mangroves are covered in Chapter 9 because they are part of the socio-economic dependency picture, but the open-water fisheries managed by the Ministry of Fisheries and Aquaculture Development sit outside the assignment boundary. The institutional crosswalk in Chapter 10 names where this boundary creates a coordination gap.

## 3.4 The seven phases of the assignment

The assignment was executed in seven sequential phases over the contract term. Each phase produced a defined deliverable, and each deliverable feeds one or more chapters of this Final Report. The phasing is set out below for orientation; the full delivery register, with submission dates and acceptance status, is given in Annex C.

**Phase 1 (Inception)** produced the Inception Report, which fixed the scope, the methodology, the work plan, the risk register, and the safeguards screening. The Inception Report was revised once (Rev 2) following the initial stakeholder engagement and remains the procedural reference for the assignment.

**Phase 2 (Literature and policy review)** produced the literature review and the policy crosswalk that underpins Chapter 2 of this Final Report. It also produced the CREMA Readiness Assessment and the governance and institutional landscape report that Chapter 10 integrates.

**Phase 3 (Geospatial baseline)** produced the spatial extent measurements for the priority sites, using a combination of Sentinel-2 imagery, historical Landsat archives, and local validation. The Geospatial Baseline Report is the source for Chapter 6 and the deforestation analysis in Chapter 8.

**Phase 4 (Carbon inventory and stock assessment)** produced the Carbon Inventory and Stock Assessment Report, substantially integrated into this Final Report in April 2026. Songor ecosystem carbon is fully reported and Keta above-ground biomass is documented; Keta belowground components are in laboratory processing and will be inserted into the remaining tagged placeholders upon receipt. The Carbon Inventory Report is the source for Chapter 8 and for the carbon-weighted overlays in Chapters 12, 13, and 14.

**Phase 5 (Ecosystem health baseline)** produced the Ecosystem Health Standalone Report, finalised in April 2026. It is the source for Chapter 7 and provides the baseline ecological character against which the continuous monitoring system will detect change.

**Phase 6 (Drone survey and continuous monitoring)** produced the Drone Survey and Continuous Monitoring Report and the operational EOSDA monitoring polygons. It is the source for Chapter 11.

**Phase 7 (Consolidation and Final Report)** is the present deliverable. It does not generate new primary data; it integrates the evidence produced in Phases 2 to 6 into the decision-oriented synthesis required by the Terms of Reference.

The sequencing matters for one reason in particular. Phases 3, 5, and 6 (geospatial, ecological, and monitoring) were delivered before Phase 4 (carbon). The Final Report has therefore been designed so that the non-carbon evidence base can stand on its own as a management instrument, with the carbon layer integrating into it on a defined schedule rather than holding it up.

## 3.5 The adaptive approach used during execution

The assignment was not executed as a linear waterfall. Three adaptive moves were made during the contract term, each in response to a specific operational reality, and each has shaped the structure and the findings of this Final Report.

The first adaptive move was to **decouple the ecological baseline from the carbon inventory**. The original work plan in the Inception Report assumed that ecological and carbon field campaigns would run in parallel. In practice, the carbon team's field protocols required deeper-soil sampling, more intensive plot installation, and laboratory turnaround times that could not be synchronised with the ecological campaigns without delaying the latter. The decision was taken to run the ecological campaigns on their own schedule and to allow the carbon inventory to follow on its own timeline. The Final Report was designed with tagged insertion points so that the carbon results could be integrated without rewriting the surrounding prose; the Songor data and Keta above-ground biomass have now been integrated, with only the Keta belowground values (currently in laboratory processing) remaining to be inserted.

The second adaptive move was to **integrate Community Change Agents (CCAs) into the monitoring design earlier than originally planned**. The Inception Report envisaged CCAs as a Phase 6 deliverable layered on top of the drone and satellite system. As the Phase 5 ecological campaigns progressed it became clear that the CCA network was not just a monitoring delivery vehicle but a critical channel for local validation, ground-truthing, and trust-building. CCAs were therefore brought into the Phase 5 fieldwork and into the Phase 6 monitoring design simultaneously. Chapter 11 carries the resulting integrated architecture.

The third adaptive move was to **bring forward the tenure and governance analysis** so that it could inform the Phase 6 monitoring design and the Phase 7 prioritisation rather than sit as an afterthought at the back of the report. The tenure split mandate from the Terms of Reference is now resolved in Chapter 10, before the synthesis chapter draws on it in Chapter 12. This means the priority ranking can take tenure complexity into account as a first-order criterion rather than as a footnoted caveat.

These adaptive moves are documented here for two reasons. First, they explain why the Final Report's structure differs in places from the chapter sequence indicated in the original Inception Report. Second, they make transparent to the client and to future readers that the methodology was actively managed against operational realities, not held rigidly to a pre-contract design.

## 3.6 Quality standards and traceability

Every quantitative claim in this Final Report cites the standalone report and the section from which it is drawn. The convention used throughout is the short form *(Standalone Report Name, §Section)* in line. The full citation, with file path in the project repository and the date of the version cited, is given in Annex D.

Three standards underpin the quality framework. The IPCC 2013 Wetlands Supplement governs the carbon accounting approach. Verra VM0033 v2.1 governs the methodological alignment for any future crediting pathway. The Ramsar Strategic Framework and Guidelines for the Future Development of the List of Wetlands of International Importance govern the ecological character framing in Chapter 7. Where a finding sits inside more than one standard, the chapter in which it appears names all of them.

The traceability principle has one practical consequence for the reader. No paragraph in this Final Report makes a numerical claim that cannot be retraced to a specific table, plot, or section in one of the standalone reports. Where a finding is uncertain, the uncertainty is named in the text rather than buried in a footnote. Where an estimate depends on Keta belowground carbon data still in laboratory processing, it is held in a tagged placeholder so that the values can be inserted in a single editorial pass without disturbing the surrounding text.
