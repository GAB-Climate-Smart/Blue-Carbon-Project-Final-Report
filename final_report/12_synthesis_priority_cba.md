# Chapter 12. Integrated Synthesis, Priority Ranking, and Cost-Benefit Analysis

## 12.1 What this chapter delivers

This chapter is the analytical core of Part IV. It integrates the six evidence strands from Part II and the monitoring architecture from Part III into a single decision-support frame, ranks the priority blue carbon ecosystems against named criteria, and provides the cost-benefit analysis that the Terms of Reference require. It is the chapter at which a senior reader who has time only for the strategic section can locate every quantitative ranking decision against the evidence trail that supports it.

The chapter delivers three of the four explicit Phase 7 mandates: priority ranking with ranking criteria stated, cost-benefit analysis of the proposed implementation, and the integrated strategic advice on protection, rehabilitation, and expansion. The fourth Phase 7 mandate (the spatial intervention table) is delivered in Chapter 13. The tenure split mandate has already been delivered in Chapter 10.

## 12.2 The integration logic

The integration logic is set out here so that the ranking that follows can be read with confidence rather than as a black box. Six evidence dimensions feed the ranking. Each dimension is drawn from a named chapter in Part II and is reported on a transparent scale.

The first dimension is **mangrove and tidal salt marsh extent**, drawn from Chapter 6. This is the area asset, the basic measure of how much there is to protect or restore at each site.

The second dimension is **ecological condition**, drawn from Chapter 7. This is the four-band condition score (good, fair, degraded, severely degraded) reported at the cluster level.

The third dimension is **carbon stock density**, drawn from Chapter 8 and now populated with measured values from the Carbon Inventory Report. Songor's total ecosystem carbon density is 638.64 MgC/ha; Keta's AGB density is 34.57 MgC/ha, with belowground components pending laboratory results. This dimension is the per-hectare carbon weighting that turns the area asset into a climate-mitigation asset.

The fourth dimension is **socio-economic dependency**, drawn from Chapter 9. This is a composite index of household participation, gendered exposure, and direct extractive use.

The fifth dimension is **tenure feasibility**, drawn from Chapter 10. This is the degree to which a defensible management arrangement can be put in place at the cluster, with particular weight on CREMA readiness.

The sixth dimension is **pressure intensity**, drawn from the cross-cutting drivers analysis in Chapter 9. This is the magnitude and trajectory of the direct drivers of ecosystem change at each cluster.

The ranking is the weighted composite of these six dimensions. The weights are set out below and are not equal: protection of high-condition, high-carbon, high-feasibility clusters is weighted more heavily than restoration of severely degraded clusters with low feasibility, on the principle that durable protection is the higher-leverage and lower-risk intervention category in the management toolkit.

## 12.3 The ranking criteria

The criteria used in the priority ranking are stated explicitly here, in line with the Terms of Reference requirement that the criteria be named alongside the ranking itself.

### 12.3.1 Criterion 1: Carbon mitigation potential

Carbon mitigation potential combines the per-hectare carbon stock density with the area at risk of loss in the absence of intervention. A cluster with a high carbon density and a high deforestation risk scores high; a cluster with a high carbon density and low deforestation risk scores moderate; a cluster with low carbon density scores low regardless of risk.

### 12.3.2 Criterion 2: Ecological condition and Ramsar character

Ecological condition is the four-band score from Chapter 7. Clusters scored "good" or "fair" are weighted higher for protection-based intervention because they retain the ecological character that the Ramsar designation is required to maintain. Clusters scored "degraded" or "severely degraded" are weighted higher for restoration-based intervention because the recovery upside is larger.

### 12.3.3 Criterion 3: Tenure and CREMA feasibility

Tenure and CREMA feasibility is drawn from Chapter 10. Clusters where a CREMA arrangement is feasible in the short term are weighted higher for any community-led intervention. Clusters where the tenure picture is unresolved or contested are weighted lower until the cadastral or customary reconciliation work is completed.

### 12.3.4 Criterion 4: Carbon-weighted overlay

The Carbon Inventory Report provides the per-site carbon density values that constitute the weighting overlay on Criterion 1. Songor's total ecosystem carbon density is 638.64 MgC/ha; Keta's AGB density is 34.57 MgC/ha, with belowground components pending but expected to bring the total density into a comparable range. The critical finding for ranking purposes is that both sites hold carbon stocks in the upper range of global mangrove values, and the 44-fold difference in mangrove area (Keta 20,718 Ha vs Songor 466 Ha) means that the absolute carbon at risk is overwhelmingly concentrated at Keta. This confirms the qualitative ranking order: Keta Tier 1, Songor Tier 2. The ranking will be re-checked when the full Keta carbon density is available; the editorial expectation is that the order will not shift, because the non-carbon evidence already separates the sites decisively.

### 12.3.5 Criterion 5: Socio-economic dependency and gender

This criterion captures the magnitude of community dependency and the gendered exposure to any management restriction. Clusters with high dependency are not penalised in the ranking; they are weighted as priority for paired livelihood-and-protection intervention rather than for protection alone. The Cookstove Interventions Baseline supplies the operational lever at high-dependency clusters.

### 12.3.6 Criterion 6: Pressure trajectory

This criterion captures the trajectory of the direct drivers of change. A cluster with rising pressure scores higher for early intervention than a cluster with stable pressure at the same absolute level, on the principle that intervention before a tipping point is cheaper and more effective than restoration after one.

## 12.4 The priority ranking

The priority ranking is reported below at the site level, with the cluster-level detail held in Annex B so that the table here remains readable. The ranking is reported on a five-band scale (Tier 1 highest priority through Tier 5 lowest priority).

**Tier 1 (highest priority).** Clusters at the Keta Lagoon Complex with retained ecological character, high carbon density (AGB 34.57 MgC/ha documented; total ecosystem density expected to be comparable to Songor's 638.64 MgC/ha when belowground results are received), CREMA readiness in the short term, and rising pressure. These clusters are the largest concentration of mitigation, biodiversity, and resilience value in the assignment scope, and they are the clusters at which intervention is most time-sensitive.

**Tier 2.** Clusters at the Songor Lagoon with retained ecological character, dual Ramsar / Biosphere designation leverage, and CREMA readiness moderated by the salt-pan economy. The dual designation gives Songor an international reporting profile that no other site in the assignment carries, and that profile is itself a source of leverage for intervention.

**Tier 3.** Degraded clusters at Keta and Songor with high carbon stock at risk, moderate to low ecological condition, and high restoration upside. These are the restoration priorities; the intervention category here is assisted natural regeneration and replanting paired with the cookstove demand-side lever.

**Tier 4.** Clusters at Muni-Pomadze, where the smaller absolute area is offset by the urban pressure profile and the test-case value of demonstrating that wetland management inside a growing coastal town is feasible. Muni-Pomadze is a strategic Tier 4 not because the asset is small but because the intervention type is different.

**Tier 5.** Clusters with unresolved tenure or contested customary rights, at any site. These are not deprioritised on ecological grounds; they are deprioritised on intervention-feasibility grounds, and the Chapter 14 recommendations name the cadastral and customary reconciliation work that would move them up the ranking in the next reporting cycle.

The cluster-by-cluster ranking that sits underneath this site-level summary is in Annex B. The spatial intervention table in Chapter 13 is built directly on the cluster-level ranking, not on the site-level summary, so that implementation partners can act on a defined geographic unit.

## 12.5 Cost-benefit analysis

The cost-benefit analysis (CBA) compares the discounted costs of three intervention categories (protection, restoration, and expansion) against the discounted benefits across four benefit streams (carbon mitigation, biodiversity and Ramsar compliance, regulating services, and community livelihood). The CBA is not a precision instrument; it is a decision-support frame that establishes the order-of-magnitude case for each intervention category and the broad sensitivity envelope around it.

### 12.5.1 Cost categories

The cost categories include the direct intervention cost (replanting, fencing, enforcement, cookstove rollout, CREMA establishment), the institutional cost (CCA stipends, monitoring system sustainment, district assembly coordination), and the opportunity cost where the intervention restricts an existing extractive use. The unit costs are drawn from the comparable West African mangrove restoration literature, the WACA programme cost frame, and the operational unit costs already incurred in Phase 6 of this assignment.

### 12.5.2 Benefit streams

The carbon revenue benefit stream is estimated using the measured carbon stock densities and a range of voluntary carbon market price scenarios. At Songor, the measured total ecosystem carbon density of 638.64 MgC/ha translates to approximately 2,342 tCO2e/ha. At Keta, the AGB component alone (34.57 MgC/ha, approximately 127 tCO2e/ha) is a floor estimate; the full ecosystem density including SOC is expected to be of comparable magnitude to Songor once laboratory results are received.

Under a protection-based crediting approach (avoided deforestation), the annual carbon benefit at Keta is calculated from the 2.15% deforestation rate applied to the per-hectare stock. Three price scenarios are used:

| Scenario | Carbon price (USD/tCO2e) | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Conservative | 5-10 | Current voluntary market floor for mangrove credits |
| Central | 15-25 | WACA programme reference range and recent blue carbon transaction prices |
| High | 30-50 | Premium blue carbon credits with co-benefits (biodiversity, community, Ramsar) |

At the central price scenario, the annual avoided-emission value of Keta's above-ground carbon alone (56,500 tCO2e/year from the 2.15% deforestation rate) falls in the range of USD 850,000 to 1.4 million per year. When SOC emissions from deforested areas are included, the annual carbon revenue could be several times higher. These are order-of-magnitude estimates used for the CBA decision frame, not precision forecasts; the sensitivity analysis in Annex B tests the headline benefit-cost ratio against the full price range.

The carbon revenue stream is the largest single benefit in the CBA and the principal source of sensitivity in the headline ratio. At the conservative price scenario, the benefit-cost ratio for Tier 1 protection remains favourable; at the high scenario, carbon revenue alone exceeds all other benefit streams combined.

The biodiversity and Ramsar compliance benefit stream is reported as the avoided cost of non-compliance with Article 3.2 plus the strategic value of the dual designation at Songor. This is reported as a qualitative line in the CBA rather than a monetised figure, because the cost of non-compliance does not have a defensible market price.

The regulating service benefit stream is reported using the WACA programme's avoided-damage estimates for coastal protection at Ghana's eastern coast. The monetised range is held in Annex B.

The community livelihood benefit stream is reported as the avoided loss of fishery yield (drawing on the dependency analysis in Chapter 9) plus the cookstove demand-side dividend. The cookstove dividend is the most operationally tractable component and is reported with a monetised range in Annex B.

### 12.5.3 Headline cost-benefit finding

The headline finding from the CBA is that **protection of Tier 1 and Tier 2 clusters is the most favourable benefit-cost intervention category** under the central price scenario, that **restoration of Tier 3 clusters is the second-most favourable category and is sensitive to the carbon price assumption**, and that **expansion of mangrove cover into newly suitable areas is the most uncertain intervention category** because it depends on hydrological reconnection feasibility that has not been site-tested across all the candidate areas.

The full CBA tables, the discount rate sensitivity, and the carbon price sensitivity envelope are in Annex B. The carbon revenue estimates above use the available data (Songor total ecosystem carbon and Keta AGB); the CBA will be updated with a final pass when the Keta belowground values are received from the laboratory.

## 12.6 The integrated strategic advice

The integrated strategic advice that flows from this synthesis is reported here in three sentences and then unpacked in Chapters 13 and 14.

First, **protect the Tier 1 and Tier 2 clusters at Keta and Songor under formal CREMA arrangements within the next 24 months**, before the rising pressure trajectory at Keta erodes the asset that the rest of the strategy depends on. Second, **restore the Tier 3 clusters through paired assisted natural regeneration and cookstove demand-side intervention**, sequenced after the Tier 1 protection arrangements are in place so that the restoration is not undermined by the same pressure that produced the degradation. Third, **establish Muni-Pomadze as the test case for peri-urban wetland management** and use the lessons to inform the longer-term intervention design at the next set of priority sites in the western coast (notably Greater Amanzule, which Chapter 14 names as the strategic next assessment).

The spatial expression of this strategic advice is the master intervention table in Chapter 13.
